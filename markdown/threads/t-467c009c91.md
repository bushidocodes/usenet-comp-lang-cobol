restore process. The customers only lost data from the last 'ENTER' before
close-down.

Try real hard to make the excursion. Prepare all your questions and send
them in advance. That will enable the company, you are visiting, to get the
answers for you. Time is money, and if you respect that, I think, you would

There have already been plenty of responses about the comparative
RAM & processors.

But for me, possibly the biggest difference is in the system software.
A mainframe will often run multiple operating systems such as VM or
MVS, nested inside each other, with applications running in different
logical places.

The most common would be where a mainframe has a "live" and
"test" environment. Also, there are multiple environments, controlled
by database operational software such as IMS or CICS. My current
client has perhaps as many as 50 different IMS environments. This
is a large company, with many different projects going on simultaneously.
But, This also highlights another important difference between PCs
and mainframe - software release/version control.

Having said all this, with the growth in client/server & distributed
platforms
the differences are diminishing. But hopefully the client/server world won't
be reduced the state of the mainframe, where software releases can be a
truly painful experience - once the applications programmer has completed
his testing, the pain has only just begun!

The actual size of a mainframe is not related to it's processing capacity
and storage.

Older mainframes were quite large.  We had an old 3384 4 processor mainframe
and it was the size of about 8 large refrigerators bolted together (in light
blue cabinets; thus comes "Big Blue").

Our 3390-300E had 6 large refrigerator size cabinets, only 3 processors, and
kicked the 3384 around the block on processing power (50% increase).  It had
128MB of central storage (effectively RAM), and 128MB Expanded Storage (RAM
used as a phantom hard drive for high speed data caching, although it had
(and still has, I think, though IBM is trying to bypass this) the problem
that data set to Expanded Storage must be first pulled back to central
storage in order to actually move it back to a real hard disk; in other
words, central storage stood between expanded storage and the hard disks).
We got this machine used in 1994, and it was probably built back in the
early 80s.

Both of these mainframes required several large auxilliary components,
amounting to about 8 further large cabinets, including two Power
Distribution Units, and a water cooler, all three were each the size of two
very large fridges side by side.

The last mainframe we got was an IBM 9672 RB4 (later upgraded to R24) with 1
gigabyte of storage divided up between three "partitions" each running a
copy of MVS/XA 2.2.3 (yes, we were still running that late 80's version in
the late 90's).

The 9672 RB4 is the size of a single fridge, was jet black, and had a red
stripe down the middle of the front cabinet door, quite a departure from the
old hardline blue cabinets.  It also kicked the the 3390 around the block on
processing power, although it was running the old MVS/XA system in
"emulation", being by hardware design meant to run the MVS/ESA and later
software, and would have run much faster on that operating system.

The 9672 RB4 saved us roughly $2000 a month in electricity alone, and
required no external support units of any kind (no water cooler for this air
cooled mainframe).  Overall, it was much cheaper to run than old mainframes,
and wasn't a whole lot bigger than our big NCR and SUN unix servers, and
unlike those two machines, the mainframe ran 80% of a $3 billion dollar a
year retail enterprise.  It took one 6 processor NCR server (at about 80%
the size of the 9672) just to run check-clearance.  The mainframe ran
accounts receivable and payable, general legder, warehouse supply chain and
purchasing (for three warehouse complexes and 150 stores), retail pricing,
payroll, EDI, a DB2 region, a 500 user online CICS system for access to all
these and many other subsystems, plus two more CICS and DB2 regions for
development and modeling.  In short, we got far more for our money on that
mainframe than we did on any other platform, all while it had one hand tied
behind it's back by an obsolete operating system.

That 649 MB figure has got to be the result of misinterpretation of the
3350 specs. The 3350's were packaged as two drives in a single box, but
the maximum effective capacity of a single logical drive was only 19069
x 30 x 555, or about 317.5 MB (that's "real Greek" 10**6 MB, not funny
2**20 "MB", but in any event this was late 1970's technology, long
obsolete. You didn't run with just one, but with multiple strings of up
to 8 logical drives per string.  By 1984 when we started to phase out
3350's in place of 3380 drives, we had 8 strings of 8 drives, or around
20 GB on the floor.  PC's were certainly not generally available when
the 3350 was new, and if there were any in lab environments at that time
their hard drives would have been miniscule.  It wasn't until late 80's
that a 20 MB PC hard drive became "small".

Comparing present day PC's with mainframes of about 20 years ago is
about right.  In terms of gross throughput, hardware reliability, and
Operating System reliability PC's are at least 20 years behind
mainframes, and in 2020 people will be comparing their successors to the
mainframes of today, but mainframe technology will have moved beyond as
well.
...
> 
> > Are the newer drives much bigger I noticed that there are now 3390's
> > installed.  How much acutal RAM is in the machine?

Capacity of a logical 3390 drive is around 2.8 GB (10**9 Bytes) for a
3390-3 (triple density).  Real 3390 DASD was packaged with 4 logical
drives in a unit the size of a large refrigerator and could be combined
into "lonnnng" strings of up to 32 logical drives (imagine 8 large
refrigerators bolted side by side), but the only real 3390's available
today are in the used market.  They have been replaced by newer DASD
subsystems which still look like 3390 drives to the operating system,
but which emulate the 3390 architecture (track size, tracks per
cylinder, and cylinder count) using 3.5" drives and which take less than
a 30th of the floor space.  Today we have three refrigerator-sized boxes
with somewhere in the neighborhood of 900+ GB of emulated 3390's
attached to a single mainframe (values are approximate because hardware
data compression is involved and the hardware is physically capable of
supporting much more if we pay more for the priviledge of using it).  

The processing power (and RAM) in just the controller of one mainframe
disk subsystem today is almost 3 orders of magnitude more powerful than
our mainframe of 20 years ago.

-------------------------------
Mainframe

A large computer.  In the "ancient" mid 1960s, all computers were called
mainframes, since the term referred to the main CPU cabinet.  Today, it
refers to a large computer system.
  There are small, medium and large-scale mainframes, handling from a
handful to tens of thousands of online terminals.  Large-scale mainframes
support multiple gigabytes of main memory and terabytes of disk storage. 
Large mainframes use smaller computers as front end processors that connect
to the communications networks.
  The original mainframe vendors were Burroughs, Control Data, GE,
Honeywell, IBM, NCR, RCA and Univac, otherwise known as "IBM and the Seven
Dwarfs."  After GE and RCA's computer divisions were absorbed by Honeywell
and Univac respectively, the mainframers were known as "IBM and the BUNCH."
  IBM has the lion's share of the mainframe business, and Hitachi Data
Systems and Amdahl are its major competitors, making System/390-compatible
computers (see IBM-compatible mainframe).  Unisys, Sun, Digital and others
make mainframes or mainframe-class machines, but run under proprietary or
UNIX-based operating systems, not IBM's OS/390.

There Is a Difference!

One might wonder why mainframes cost up to several millions of dollars when
their raw megahertz (MHz) or MIPS rates are no higher than a PC costing
1,000 times less.  There are reasons.  Firstly, in a small computer such as
a PC, the CPU does almost all the processing.  Unless direct memory transfer
(DMA) is used, the CPU is also involved with getting data to and from the
peripherals, the most time-consuming part of the whole operation.
  A mainframe provides enormous amounts of throughput by offloading its
input/output processing to a peripheral channel, which is a computer in
itself.  Mainframes can support hundreds of channels, up to 512 in some
models. Mainframes also have multiple ports into memory and especially into
high-speed caches, which can be 10 times faster than main memory. 
Additional computers may act as I/O traffic cops between the CPU and the
channels and handle the processing of exceptions (what happens if the
channel is busy, if it fails, etc.).  All these subsystems handle the
transaction overhead, freeing the CPU to do real "data processing" such as
computing balances in customer records and subtracting amounts from
inventories, the purpose of the computer in the first place.
  Secondly, the internal bus transfer rates of mainframes are also higher
than small computers.  A 200MHz Pentium has a data bus that runs at 66MHz,
but a 200MHz mainframe may have a data bus that also runs at 200MHz, three
times as fast.  The multipliers add up.  Three times the bus speed, 10 times
the cache speed, perhaps 32 or 64 overlapped data transfers.  Multiply one
times the other, and the combination of fast buses, fast caches, multiple
memory ports and independent channels and subsystems makes a processing
machine unlike anything else.
  Thirdly, much of the hardware circuitry in a mainframe is designed to
detect and correct errors. Every subsystem is continuously monitored for
potential failure, in some cases even triggering a list of parts to be
replaced at the next scheduled downtime.  As a result, mainframes are
incredibly reliable.  The mean time between failure (MTBF) is generally 20
years!
  In addition, mainframes are highly scalable. Based on symmetric
multiprocessing (SMP), mainframes can be expanded by adding CPUs to a system
or by adding systems in clusters.

They're Here to Stay

Once upon a time, mainframes meant "complicated" and required the most
programming and operations expertise.  That is no longer.  Networks of
client/server-based PCs make mainframes look easy. Nothing is more
complicated than the DOS/Windows environment.  Add NetWare, throw in a
little UNIX for good measure, and you have enterprise computing at its most
complex ever.  With more than two trillion dollars worth of mainframe
applications in place, mainframes are here to stay, and their centralized
architecture, which is easiest to manage, may just be the wave of the
future!
-----------------------------

The only thing I can add is that compared to the modern G5/G6 Enterprise
Servers, the figures above seem way too conservative.

Mike:

Here's an analogy I like to use.....

If it is fair to compare a mainframe to a PC, then presumably, it
would also be fair to compare a Boeing 747 to a Boeing F/A-18 Super
Hornet.

Like the mainframe, Boeing 747's have been around a long time.  The
first Boeing 747 flew in 1969.  Wow!  That's 30 years ago....but they
are indeed still very much used in their industry.  So are mainframe
computers.  Just because the technology was introduced many years ago
doesn't automatically make it useless.  In fact, the avionics and
other systems on Boeing 747's have been enhanced and updated over the
years....just as the components on mainframe computers have been
enhanced and updated over the years.

Boeing 747's can carry between 416 to 524 passengers about 8,400
miles.  Mainframe computers were also designed to efficiently handle
large scale tasks as well for a large number of concurrent users.  The
Boeing 747-400 series offers the lowest operating cost per seat of any
commercial jetliner.  Hey!  Mainframe computers also offer the lowest
operating costs per transaction for large scale processing.

Now a Boeing 747-400 costs between $167 million US$ and $187 million
US$ and that is indeed a lot of money.  Mainframe computers also cost
a lot of money too!

Now let's focus on one of the newest fighter jets available, the
F/A-18 Super Hornet.  Like as PC's are fairly new, this is a
relatively new aircraft.  Just like your PC with 128 MB of RAM, these
babies are really fast.  These fighter jets can reach speeds of up to
mach 1.5 (almost a thousand miles per hour!) but the Boeing 747 is
rated to cruise at a mere mach .85, or approximately 550 miles per
hour.

The F/A-18 Super Hornet are designed to hold either 2 or 3 seats.
PC's are also designed for only a small number of concurrent users.

Now....

If you were a pilot in an aerial war, would you prefer to use the 747
or Super Hornet in a dogfight?  I would prefer the Super Hornet.

...BUT...if you are now the owner of a commercial airlines and want to
establish a New York to Los Angeles route and sold 400 tickets, would
you deploy a single 747 or would you hire 400 pilots to fly 400 Super
Hornets each holding a single customer?

The moral of the story is that just like aircraft, computers cannot be
classified as either being bad or good.  They can only be classified
as being appropriate or inappropriate **FOR THE SPECIFIC TASK AT
HAND.**

Hope this helps.
