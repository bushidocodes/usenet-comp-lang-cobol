It is NOT a question of COBOl being 'viable',
nor does new automatically equate to better.

No new programming language or database access methodology
is yet capable of replacing IBM's duo of COBOL and IMS DB/DC.
Even if something were to be developed, why SHOULD we convert?
Because it is new?  Don't insult the intelligence of IS managers.
There is  the issue of the mission critical nature of the existing databases
and the existing COBOL software and the existing systems software
and database backup and reorg programs, etc etc etc.

Companies are reluctant to upgrade desktops from Win 3.1 to Win 95,
or from Win 95 to Win 98.

What makes anyone think they will cavalierly undertake a humungous
conversion
from IMS to...to...what? Conversion to what?  There is no 'viable'
alternative.
Any suggestion that COBOL is not viable is vaporware-level thinking.
I haven't even mentioned the Return On Investment pros and cons.

There are two camps here.  Those of us who deal with mission-critical,
enterprise-level systems 7-by-24, and those who do not.

The former are acutely aware of the desirability of improving systems and
procedures
in a very careful, methodical, risk-limiting manner.

The latter seem to be focussed on change for the sake of change.  That is
wrong.
Change is to be explored, researched and embraced when it makes sense.

Just don't propose a feeble hypothesis such as 'COBOL is not viable' as a
solution
to anything.  Or even as an idea, if you have no documented 'viable'
alternative.

Good luck with your paper.  Just don't go charging at the wrong windmill.

Check out the following two front page articles:

  Wall Street Journal, April 30, 1998 "Pulling the Plug".

  Info World, June 29, 1998 "Return of the Mainframe".

In brief, recent failure rates for new systems has been about 45%,
from cost and time overruns and failure to meet design goals.  Much of
this is because of the cost and complexity of using client server and
GUI technology.  Many companies are beginning to react to the impact
of this on their bottom line.  Personally, I am convinced that we will
see a growing groundswell of such reaction from companies as these
facts become more widely known and appreciated.  Part of this reaction
is a return to more reliable and cost effective technologies like COBOL.

Another trend: use of COBOL for C/S development.  In the late 80's and
early 90's, there was a huge rush to the new technologies, and away
from COBOL.  But by 1996, surveys showed that, even in C/S development
where COBOL has not been considered strong, COBOL had taken a very close
second place to VB is language of choice, with C a more distant third.
One of the reasons for this, besides cost and reliability, is that
companies have found that mainframe based servers leave any PC based
server in the dust, both in performance and reliability.  Mainframe
operating systems, instead of being uprooted and rewritten every couple
of years for the API du jour, have been steadily evolved and improved
over decades.  The difference that makes in reliability is truly
impressive.
