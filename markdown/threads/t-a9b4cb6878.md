On Sat, 20 Apr 2002 17:53:27 -0400, Paul Raulerson wrote
> Huh? Why not? COMP is LOTS faster, at least on a mainframe, and far more

Maybe on your mainframe, but definitely not on all. The representation 
of COMP is totally implementor-dependent.

On Unisys MCP systems, COMP is stored as packed decimal. This is for 
historical reasons -- compatibility with the old Burroughs B2500. But 
MCP systems don't do decimal arithmetic (the B2500 did), so such fields 
must be converted to and from binary for most uses.

On these systems, the most efficient USAGEs are BINARY EXTENDED and 
REAL -- neither of them ANSI COBOL of course. Just BINARY is better 
than COMP but obeys the truncation rules of ANSI COBOL, which renders 
it less efficient, since of course extra code is required for the 
truncation.

Edward Reid

Glossary:

MCP systems are also known as ClearPath NX/LX, A-Series, Burroughs 
Large Systems, and Burroughs 6xxx/7xxx. This line began in about 1970 
and is very much alive today.

The Burroughs B2500 was part of a family also known as V-Series and 
Burroughs Meduim Systems, and Burroughs 2xxx/3xxx/4xxx. This line began 
in about 1965 and was discontinued about ten years ago. Naturally 
Burroughs/Unisys had a vested interest in making conversion to the 
A-Series easy for their existing customers.

Unisys had (and actually still has) exactly such a convention in both
COBOL74 and COBOL85 in the form of its "BINARYCOMP" Compiler Control Option.
When this compiler option is TRUE, the compiler treats all COMPUTATIONAL
data items as if they had been declared BINARY for efficiency; when it is
FALSE, it treats them as declared for compatibility with the existing files.

(Note also that "compiler parameters" such as this, while allowed in the
2002 draft (where they are called "directives"), are not provided for in the
prior standards to start with. )

And it's not just *predecessor* systems; in fact, the origins of this had to
do with compatibility among products from the same company being offered at
the same time in different performance ranges.

BINARYCOMP was provided not so much for efficiency as it was to provide
assistance in migration of COBOL(68) programs to the more recent dialects.
In MCP/AS (ex-Burroughs B6700) COBOL(68), COMPUTATIONAL (and
COMPUTATIONAL-1) were indeed word-oriented integers; COMPUTATIONAL-2 was
packed-decimal.  This was contrary to, and incompatible with, the rest of
the Burroughs product line at the time.

And it's not just *predecessor* systems as Ed points out.   When the 1974
standard came out, Burroughs management insisted on a single approach to the
language and its extensions across the entire product line, and a Burroughs
Corporate Standard for ANSI-74 COBOL was developed.  This corporate standard
mandated the use of packed-decimal format for COMPUTATIONAL to assist in
migration and portability, from the smallest B1710 through the Medium System
line to the largest B7800, with a minimum of changes to the source program
anywhere in the process.

Note that of these systems only the Burroughs Large System at the time had
an architecture in which anything approaching a base-2 representation of
numerics was appropriate for COBOL in the first place.

Moreover, while manipulating a BINARY item in MCP/AS COBOL74 or COBOL85
today is undeniably faster in terms of processing time than manipulating a
COMPUTATIONAL one, for any item other than one declared with exactly 11
digits or exactly 23 digits, COMPUTATIONAL is more efficient in terms of
storage.  And I would characterize the performance difference as substantial
but not dramatic; the MCP/AS architects have gone to great lengths to
improve the performance of decimal-to-binary and binary-to-decimal
conversion operators since the days of the B6700 (where users and compiler
developers alike tried to avoid ICVD and SCRS/SCRF like the plague).    So,
yes, doing arithmetic on BINARY items (particularly 77-level BINARY items in
COBOL74) is faster, but it's not clear that that performance difference
automatically, categorically and unconditionally outweighs the advantages of
portability and compatibility for each and every user.

Given that in the '74 standard the only USAGEs are COMPUTATIONAL and
DISPLAY, I believe the Burroughs decision to make both of them portable
across several very different product offerings was a wise one.  The point
is moot for the '85 standard, because a COBOL85 compiler was never offerred
for the other products in the ex-Burroughs line, and compatibility with and
portability from MCP/AS COBOL74 to MCP/AS COBOL85 is far more important than
what Unisys chooses to call the USAGE for which arithmetic cn be said to
operate the fastest.

Ummm ... Paul, I'm afraid your information is rather out of date.

It is true that in the MCP/AS COBOL(68) compiler COMP (and for that matter
COMP-1) was a word-oriented integer data type analogous to INTEGER in ALGOL.
COMP-4 and COMP-5 corresponded to ALGOL REAL and DOUBLE data types.  COMP-2
in that dialect was a packed-decimal format.

But support for MCP/AS COBOL(68) ended, as I recall, on 12/31/1999, and with
it died the appropriateness of the supposition that COMP represented a
word-oriented format on MCP/AS systems (and their predecessors).

Accoding to the standards, "COMPUTATIONAL" means nothing more nor less than
what the compiler implementor decides it means.

Burroughs management decided sometime in the 1970's that while an equivalent
of ALGOL's "INTEGER" data type could be used to get the absolute last shred
of performance on the Burroughs Large System, expecting everybody who was
being encouraged to migrate from systems like the B1700 and the Medium
System (both of which had packed decimal arithmetic, but neither of which
had a word-oriented format suitable for COBOL) to reformat all their records
was not reasonable.  Accordingly, for all Burroughs plants developing COBOL
compilers compilant with any ANSI standard from ANSI-74 onward, COMP items
would be in a packed-decimal format.  And that has been true since the very
introduction of COBOL74 and COBOL85 on MCP/AS systems, as well as the
COBOL74 compilers for both B1000 and B2/3/4000 systems.

For the current COBOL dialects on MCP/AS systems, if you want an item to be
USAGE BINARY (which is basically the same format as an ALGOL integer on that
system), the appropriate thing to do is to tell the compiler that in the
USAGE clause.

The supposition that packed-decimal items were expensive to manipulate was a
great deal more tenable on the likes of the B6700 system.  But our hardware
engineers worked long and hard to improve the hardware associated with
conversion to and from both DISPLAY and packed-decimal data types, and while
it's true that retrieving a packed-decimal field for arithmetic purposes
from a record isn't quite as rapid as retrieving a word-aligned integer for
the same purposes from the same record, it's not demonstrable that in
real-world programs the difference is generally significant.   That's been
pretty much true since the introduction of the larger Burroughs A Series
systems in the 1980's.

Moreover, there's now over two decades of precedent for the internal format
for COMP items on MCP/AS systems.  For that reason, if you decided to
"seriously take this up with the compiler writers", I suspect strongly that
you would not make a lot of headway unless you *first* convinced pretty much
the entire MCP/AS user base to support your position and agree to swallow
the costs to reformat all their files and revise all their
currently-operating programs.  I have serious doubts that that level of
support for your position from the MCP/AS COBOL user base is likely to
materialize.

    -Chuck Stevens
