When I worked for Micro Focus, it always struck me as "odd" (and something
of a statement on the LESSENING dominance of COBOL) that no vendor came
along and created an "optimizer" for the various PC COBOL compilers - as
they had originally for the IBM OS/VS COBOL compiler.  I know that Fujitsu
(now) claims to optimize much better than Micro Focus and I know that Micro
Focus *ALWAYS* tries to put additional optimization in each of its releases
and that IBM VisualAge COBOL uses much (but not all) the same optimization
as available on the mainframe. HOWEVER, I also now from working for a PC
vendor, that if someone actually spent the time/effort to create a separate
"optimization" product that read the source code, object code, and
INT/p-code (where available) the same way that Capex (then CA) did with
OS/VS COBOL, that they could come up with REAL optimized COBOL run-time
code.  (Just think what it could do for AcuCOBOL's "portable" code.)  My
assumption of why this never happened are:
  - there are so many DIFFERENT COBOL compilers for the PC (unlike the "olde
days" when OS/VS COBOL really dominated the business market)
  - the assumption is that there is a continually shrinking COBOL market and
that no one thinks such a product (products?) would be profitable
 - OS/VS COBOL was before "object code only" and the information wouldn't be
there for a vendor to "optimize" the proprietary COBOL object code.
 - The people who have the "computer science" background to understand
optimization techniques really can't stand COBOL so they don't even look at
this as a possible product.

Bottom-Line: I think it would be GREAT if the COBOL workstation vendors put
more effort into optimizing their executables, I think it might be even
better if some 3rd party got into this business, but UNFORTUNATELY, I won't
hold my breath until anyone takes this seriously.

P.S. Just try suggesting to Micro Focus that they optimize their code for
applications running in IBM mainframe compatibility mode (CHARSET(EBCDIC)
ARITHMETIC(OSVS) PERFORM-TYPE(OSVS) STICKY-LINKAGE IBMCOMP etc).  I know
that I never could get them to even think about this - and surprisingly few
of our (their) customers actually ever asked for it.  Almost all programs
running in this environment were run as "INT" code and walking was a better
description than "running"!

The question is not "can you do X?", but "can you make money doing X?".

IBM's OS/VS CoBOL inadvertently created the evolutionary niche which was
soon occupied by third-party optimizers.

The first factor is that IBM makes money from hardware.  The purpose of
IBM software is to consume as much memory, machine cycles, and EXCPs as
possible so that you will have to buy more hardware.  But it can't just
throw away cycles; people won't stand for that.  IBM's single greatest
achievement over the years is the _plausible_ waste of machine time:
waste that the customer not only doesn't notice, but actually thinks he
wants.

The second factor is that in a large corporation every single run is
charged back to some department in fully-burdened dollars.

The third factor is the peculiar deficiencies of OS/VS CoBOL.  I don't
want to get too deep into this because even on my own compilers I'm never
given enough time to do the code generator right, unless it's ForTran. 
Let's just say there were opportunities there, opportunities that are not
present in today's compilers.

Given unnecessary waste that IBM won't fix for strategic reasons and that
the customer sees in monthly chargeback, if you can show a real reduction
in chargeback, there's room for your fee.

I'm not sure the shrinking (or expanding) CoBOL market enters into it. 
The question is, how can you charge?  If you can't charge, you can't make
a profit.  I can get a thousand dollars a month on a mainframe because a)
the savings are demonstrable, and b) they are in real dollar chargebacks. 

PC users have no idea what they are spending.  Published studies range
from $5k to $20k per seat per year.  Since they don't know what they are
spending, they can't see increases or reductions.  The readers of this
newsgroup balk at paying a thousand dollars for a whole compiler, never
mind a thousand dollars a month!  What, then, would they pay for an
optimizer?  Not enough for me to write one, that's for sure.

I have to say that the people who have the "computer science" background

wants to make such a product can hire the expertise.
