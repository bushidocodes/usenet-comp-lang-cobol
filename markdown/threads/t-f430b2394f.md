No it is not. In fact, they changed the requirement to Ada a few
years ago. However, as far as I know it never worked and government
agencies are still using COBOL. The government does run a testing
service to make sure that a vendor's COBOL conforms to the standard
(actually, to the FIPS (Federal Information Processing Standard, which
points to the ANSI/ISO standard). If it does not conform, the agency
is not supposed to purchase it without a complex waiver system.
Almost all COBOL compilers do conform, however.

› Also, I'm wondering when COBOL-9X is scheduled to be released.

The schedule now says it should get final approval in mid-1998. There
are many things that can throw this off, but that is the current
schedule.

I beleive that the olde rule about "must have COBOL" was the "Brooks
Bill". It is mostly still in force, at least as far as "general purpose"
machines go. This rule helped to force the timely availability of the
1985 standard COBOL - at least on IBM mainframes.

In the case of the 85 standard, once it passed the ANSI review process, it
was then made into a FIPS (Federal Information Processing Stanrd), which
started a 12 month clock. This clock set the time in which a vendor must
deliver an '85 compliant compiler.

The 9x standard will likely not be in a position to start the clock till
at earliest sometime in the middle or late 97. Once the standard is
approved - if it is approved - vendors could deliver a product.

Biggest issue is likely to be the shear size of the standard. A question
remains - will COBOL 9x become the same as FORTRAN 90 - nice standard, too
big, too many questions, no commercial acceptance....
