put the question out there again.

Using the example:

01  CUSTOMER.
    05  FIRST-NAME  PIC  X(10).
    05  LAST-NAME   PIC  X(10).

MOVE "TOM" TO FIRST-NAME OF CUSTOMER
MOVE "JONES" TO LAST-NAME OF CUSTOMER

I'd like something a bit more friendly on the eyes:

MOVE "TOM" TO CUSTOMER.FIRST-NAME
MOVE "JONES" TO CUSTOMER.LAST-NAME

I know that the compiler assigns special meaning to the period, so
this is most likely not going to work, at least not without some sort
of special compiler option or directive set, so does anyone know of

There have been some good responses, but I'll add my  2c anyway...

1. The dot notation is a shorthand form of referencing properties and
methods within an object.

The COBOL we are dealing with here is NOT Object Oriented and what you are
looking for is an alternative to "qualification". There are a number of
reasons why you would have duplicated datanames in your COBOL program. A
good one could be so that you can use MOVE...CORRESPONDING.

If you find qualification tiresome, reconsider your need for duplicated
datanames. If the names are unique, you never need to qualify them.

I have never seen a use of MOVE...CORRESPONDING that could not be coded in
an alternative way.

2. If your suggestion was picked up it would cause further confusion between
the referencing of Objects and the referencing of duplicated definitions.
(It is likely that future COBOL WILL use dot notation, but it will be for OO
reference rather than qualified datanames.)

3. Don't be confused between Object usage and qualification, just because
they look similar.

Pete.

Maybe a more specific reason for why I was looking for "dot notation"
would be more helpful:

  01  ACCOUNT      IS TYPEDEF.
      05  OWNER    PIC  X(50).
      05  BALANCE  PIC S9(05)V9(02).

  01  CHECKING     ACCOUNT.
  01  SAVINGS      ACCOUNT.

Now suppose I want to accompliosh the following:

  MOVE "Chris T" TO OWNER OF CHECKING OWNER OF SAVINGS
  MOVE 100 TO BALANCE OF CHECKING BALANCE OF SAVINGS

IMHO - it looks much better to have these statements as:

  MOVE "Chris T" TO CHECKING.OWNER SAVINGS.OWNER
  MOVE 100 TO CHECKING.BALANCE SAVINGS.BALANCE

I realize that the TYPEDEF clause may be specific to Micro Focus, but
I find it particularly useful. This is a very basic example that I
know can be accomplished a number of other ways, but that's really not
the point of the post/question.

I have no desire to turn COBOL into C, but can't we all agree that the
second form here is much easier to read? I don't care if its "dot
notation" or the -> construct or whatever. All I was looking for was a
