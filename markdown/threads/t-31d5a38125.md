If you have ever written any kind of interactive program in COBOL or CICS or C++
or Visual Basic, you should stress that.  Especially if you are good at object
oriented concepts like one might learn in JAVA.  Often an interview will ask you
how many programs you have written, or how long the programs were. If you worked
in the computer lab you should stress that a little.

They will probably want to know what kind of operating sytems you have
experience on and what kind of hardware.  Windows, OS/390, VM, VSE/ESA, IBM PC,
Compilers, Office Suites, LINUX, AS400, etc.  

They may also want to know about your experience with Server Data and Network
Systems/topologies.  Windows NT, Windows XP, Windows 2000, Apache, Novell
Netware, CISCO Routers, TCP/IP, HTML, CGI, Pearl, etc. 

The requirements for the job did say Zero to four years experience, so you
should stress your ultra High GPA "if that is the case".  Be careful, because
they may ask for transcripts later, or run a credit check on you also.

If you had courses in software design, System Analysis, or Database Design, GUI,
etc. you want to make sure they know that you have completed them and are eager
to learn new skills, and can work well in a team environment.  Just tell them
once and dont stress it too much, uless they want you to tell them something at
the end, like a recap of your experience.

Some companies prefer to hire someone that is fresh out of college and hasnt
learned any bad habbits yet.  If you worked for some place for the last 5 years
part time and stuck with it that could make a difference.  Often they pass over
people who have worked for 10 different companies for the last 20 years like the
Contract System Developers that just happen to be out of work, who will move on
the first time they have a good offer.

Don't sell yourself short and dont be surprised if they ask for qualifications
you don't have.  It is hard to find a good qualified employee.  You have to
sound confident, and intelligent.  Research the company on the internet if they
have a website and try to learn their jargon, what they do and what kind of an
image they have, what they sell, ect.  

Dont use Acronyms too much or abbreviations; the interviewer may be a human
resources individual that doesnt work in IT.  These types are more interested in
how intelligent you sound and how well you can carry on a conversation.  They
will ask you something like "Is there anything you want to ask us?", and you
should have a question or two on the tip of your tongue.  Maybe like work hours,
parking, or availability of housing like you expect to work for them, whether it
is a new position or a temporary one, etc.  Don't tell them you are the first
employer to offer you an interview or sound desperate.

If you have hobbies or do public service work or have outside interests
sometimes that is a plus if they ask about it.  Companies are always looking for
people to do volunteer work to make them look good.

They may be willing to offer you some rediculous starting wage as a trainee.
Experience is worth just as much as a big paycheck.

I think a telephone interview is often used to screen out prospective
applicants.  They may not ask too many questions, and they may ask a lot.  Out
of 50 applicants for a recent CICS job we scheduled 6 people for an in-person
interview.

Since you have zero years of experience they will be expecting to train
you in their systems and system protocols.  You can talk up how much you
enjoyed the classes you had (how well you did); your enthusiasm will
mean more (right now) then your experience.  They will want to find out

it and added to it, at least in a class environment); they will asses
your listening / communication skills and your ability to work with
others.

Relax and sell them on what a great contribution you can make to their
