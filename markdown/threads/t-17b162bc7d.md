"COBOL II" and "COBOL 370"  are somewhat abbreviated names for IBM products. 
It is best to know and discuss the COBOL language standard that your exprience
deals with.

Terminology for that usually includes a reference to the year of final approval
for the standard.

Although it is a fact that some recruiters and middle persons may not know that
way of looking at it, if they are generalist resource hunters. Anyone active in
personnel searches relating to COBOL particularly, should know terms like COBOL
68, COBOL 74 and COBOL 85.

The COBOL II product actually had a history that stretches across standards,
with latter versions of the compiler executable having parametric means to
retrogress to COBOL 74 if you insisted on ignoring its COBOL 85 capabiblities.

Experts can discourse not only on compiler product names and language standards
but on the vendor extensions that are or are not available in earlier or later
versions by default or by parametric enablement. But it is rare that you can
get an audiance that can understand traffic in such ideation.

In modern context it is especially important to have experience with and be
able to discuss FUNCTIONs.  And I invite other, especially the standards gurus,
to clarify where this comes in per the standards year.

For me it is a little hard to discern.  In IBM shops
the products underwent rapid name changes and only gradual standards upgrades
in recent years. There is a COBOL 89, as some folks mark it.  I definitely
don't want to mislead here.  I don't know if it is a standard at the approval
level that occured with COBOL 68, COBOL 74 and COBOL 85.  But as far as helping
with vocabulary, I think the FUNCTIONs came in with COBOL 89 (and I definitely
ask for clarity from those who know).

FUNCTIONs are relevant to Y2K solutions, and therefore experienced with them is
valuable in the market place.  A bunch of recruters can not distinguish
68/74/85. But Y2K is a wake-up call that coerces modernization. Many recruiters
want to hear that you have experience 'converting' or 'upgrading', whether it
is from 68 to 85 or from 74 to 85.

((Some recrutiers do not actually understand that this 'conversion' deals only
with language syntax, technical interviewers do.  But my main point about
'conversion' is that experience with upgrading to COBOL 85 is a distinct
salable skill)).

The basic fact in modern work is COBOL 85 syntax. That's what sells along with
anything that sound like Y2K.

COBOL II actually was a major bridge tool, that allowed IBM to keep old code
from '74 and '68 to make it into '85.  Most recruiters will not understand
something that subtle.  But the reason I mention this it that participants in
really technical interviews will know this.  And if you say you have 'COBOL II'
skills, they will not receive the message that you have COBOL 85 skills until
you drive the point home. (because it is definitely possible to do COBOL 74
work with the COBOL II product tool).

Sell COBOL 85 syntax skills.  Add FUNCTIONs. Display an open mind towards
irritatingly inefficient entities like humanoids with their incessant need to
vary formats for even the simplest of data, and presto, you are Y2K compliant.

There have been several threads recently on IBM COBOL compiler names.  Since
some may have grown a bit weary of that, you may get a more complete list of
product names by going to dejanews. Search on 'COBOL for MVS and VM', An search
on 'COBOL II' will work too but you have to do a lot of manual filtering.

Anyway, perhaps sell syntax skills,  namable through standards levels, as
opposed to experience with vendor's named products. Try to keep it simple: sell
COBOL 85 skills. Discuss product names to the extent that the interviewer is
competent, but get back to standards level as the basic point. Most people are
buying COBOL 85 dressed as Y2K.

Best Wishes,

Robert Rayhawk
RKRayhawk@aol.com
