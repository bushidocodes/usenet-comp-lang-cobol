ClearPath *which*?  And which flavor of COBOL?   Makes a big difference ...

In ClearPath LX/NX series, a.k.a. Unisys MCP/AS, flavors of the ClearPath
systems, the only one that appears to match your description of the behavior
of COMP is COBOL(68).  In that environment, COMP items are contained in one
or two 48-bit words, and for example for the integer value -1 there are a
total of thirty-nine valid formats though only six of those formats are
likely.

For COBOL74 and COBOL85 on the same system, COMP is a form of packed
decimal, which doesn't match your description.

Can't speak offhand to the ClearPath IX / ex-Univac-1100/2200, though.

    -Chuck Stevens

If they came from an A-series machine, I'm out of my element.  However, 
for 2200, Comp is as follows...

9(02) - 1/4 word (9 bits)
9(05) - 1/2 word (18 bits)
9(07) - 3/4 word (27 bits)
9(10) - 1 word (36 bits)

Keep in mind that the 2200 uses 9-bit characters, and has 4 per word... 
So, following would be how that would translate...

9(02) Comp - Pic X(01)
9(05) Comp - Pic X(02)
9(07) Comp - Pic X(03)
9(10) Comp - Pic X(04)

To determine the max size of a particular field, the computation is

(2 ** [number of bits]) - 1

meaning that the largest number in a half word, for example, will be 
262,143.  This is where it can be a bit deceptive, since the number 
262143 fits in a Pic 9(05) field.  Personally, I've stopped using Comp 
(and Binary, it's UCOB equivalent) and define Pic 1's instead.  This 
removes ambiguity, and also allows for initial values that exceed the 
Pic 9 Comp picture clauses.

Hope that helps...
