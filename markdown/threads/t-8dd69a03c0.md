Pure guess - part of the hierarchy (caste) system in England.
Lawyers/solicitors are the minions allowed to advise you on divorce,
property exchange, notaries public etc., but oh never allowed to
represent you in criminal cases. They take your case 'upstairs' to
barristers, who represent you in the courts - at a handsome fee of
course.

Have to do your own homework on this one. When it started no idea, but I
think it is associated with the Inns of Court in London's Gray's Inn
area close to the Old Bailey. You do your grunt work as a lawyer, become
a partner in a prestigious law firm, don your stupid wig and gown and
get to perform in front of a guy up on the bench also bewigged and
begowned (in red).

Think of dear Old Rumpole of the Bailey. Even more hilarious - do you
recall from years ago an episode of LA Law. A Brit barrister shows up in
an LA court bewigged and begowned <G>.

From a small fact index, may be slightly out of date with what occurs
now as this is from about 1945/46  :-

Qualified member of that branch of the legal profession which has the
exclusive right to plead in the superior courts of law in England and
Ireland. In Scotland he is termed an advocate.

The above then cross-referenced me to 'Law' :-

.....Barristers and solicitors, who plead in the courts as advocate or
counsel, or otherwise act as a client's legal agent, form the two great
branches of the profession. To qualify for a call to the English Bar
takes at least three years, and the candidate must become a student of
one of the four Inns of Court in London - the Inner Temple, Middle
Temple, Lincoln's Inn and Gray's Inn. A large proportion of law students
who now enter the Inns have taken a university degree. .... a certain
number of dinners must be attended in the hall of an Inn to "keep
terms". The higher grades in the profession - King's(Queen's) counsel
(K.C/Q.C) or judge - are open only to those who have established a good
practice.

To become a solicitor (once called an "attorney"), the usual method is
for a youth to be articled to a member of the profession for five years;
or he may serve in a solicitor's office as an ordinary clerk for 10
years. There are three examinations,
(controlled by the Incorporated Law Society). Passing the intermediate
and final examinations, and the payment of certain fees, admit the
candidate to practice. (See also Courts of Justice)       - Hey ! I
ain't typing that as well !

Not deadly accurate, but looks like my guess was pretty close. Step by
step, the whole biz., in its current form, probably dates from Tudor
times. Think on 'Man for All Seasons'. Thomas More as the Lord
Chancellor (of the Exchequer),  was responsible for the Courts Chancery,
and prior to the Reformation there were the Ecclesiastical Courts..
