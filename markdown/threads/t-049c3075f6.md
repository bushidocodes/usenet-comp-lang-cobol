- **Message-ID:** `<35fac493.0@news1.ibm.net>`

```
Hi all,

I just saw that the next standard has increased the length of a Cobol
word from 30 to 31 characters. Does anybody know the rationale for
this silly thing (except that it makes DEC-Cobol compliant with the
standard)? If the length should be increased, make it substantial
e.g. to 100 characters.
```

From the proposed C standard:

Implementation limits

6   The implementation shall treat at least the first 63 characters
      of an internal name (a macro name or an identifier that does
      not have external linkage) as significant. The implementation
      may further restrict the significance of an external name (an
      identifier that has external linkage) to 31 characters. In both
      external and internal names, lower-case and upper-case
      letters are different. The number of significant characters in
      an identifier is implementation-defined.

Wait until someone invents and make a success of a computer with 3-state bits.
That will put an end to those silly power-of-two-minus-one limits that are
choosen. And that even in the standard for languages, looks like if the standard
itself is already platform-dependent!

A serious complaint: why such strange limited limits. Take for example that
figure of 64 for datanames (nothing against C, other examples in other languages
possible). That needs a 6 bit binary variable in the compiler-program. Why not
limit it at 32767 so nobody would ever feel short-handed. Now-a-days the limit
you sometimes need to know/remember as a programmer are those weird powers of 2
which just don't stay in my brains.

Sure there are some limits that will count now and then, but why things like
length of datanames ??? Not in modern compilers/languages/standards. This is the
world of 32+ Megabytes, doubling every 18 months ... why do we still think so
small where it is not needed...

The Frog
