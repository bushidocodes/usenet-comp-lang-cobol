I'm pretty sure you are wrong on this one. According to the
HR department of a former employer, there was a lawsuit over
the fact that some company DID NOT disclose that an employee
was fired (let go for cause). The company that hired this
person would not have hired them had they known about the
situation. It turns out that the employee that had been
fired from their prior position had the same set of problems
after being hired at the new company. Seems that the problem
is that this time they did more than threaten to do
something illegal...

So, yes, a company has the obligation to answer this
question. If they do not, then they open themselves to an
action should their failure to disclose derogatory
information result in problems. However, to protect
themselves, they can (and probably should) require that
reference requests must be done in writing with a hold
harmless clause.

Let me give a for instance: You terminate an employee for
threatening another with violence. You fail to disclose this
to a prospective employer. They then hire this former
employee, and a few months later they "go postal" and kill
someone.

Another for instance: You terminate an employee for
embezzlement. You don't disclose this and they are hired by
another company that is involved in the handling of large
sums of money. The person here figures out how to embezzle
money with the new employer... Guess who may be on the hook
for trebled damages?!?

You can't run into that kind of trouble if you confine your answer to position title and 
dates of employment and state that you will give no further information - you then owe 
no "duty" to another potential employer.  You certainly CAN run into that sort of
