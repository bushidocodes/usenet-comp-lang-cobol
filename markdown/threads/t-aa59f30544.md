The official OCaml site[1] has a link to various OCaml tutorials.[2]

I went through the first several parts of the online English
translation of "Developing Applications with Objective Caml".[3] I
thought it was a decent introduction to the language, though I had
experience with other functional languages (including OCaml's ancestor
Standard ML) before I tried it. It's pretty technical and more
focussed on OCaml syntax and features than on the theory behind them.

A quick glance suggests that Didier Rï¿½my's "Using, Understanding, and
Unraveling The OCaml Language"[4], also linked to from [2], might be a
better introduction for someone new to the ML family. It seems to go
into more detail about why OCaml works the way it does, and how best
to use it.

The OCaml documentation page[2] also links to a list of published
books. I've heard good things about Jon Harrop's _OCaml for
Scientists_, but it's expensive.

I've never looked into Haskell resources (just haven't had the time),
but I expect there are online tutorials and the like. Probably lecture
notes too, since it's popular with Computer Science programs.

[2] http://caml.inria.fr/resources/doc/index.en.html
[3] http://caml.inria.fr/pub/docs/oreilly-book/index.html
[4] http://caml.inria.fr/pub/docs/u3-ocaml/index.html

Pete Dashwood wrote:
> 
> Readers of this thread may find the following also interesting:
…[4 more quoted lines elided]…
> someone who is an ardent OO programmer resisting the advance of paradigms...

I don't think so. Mark Lee Smith's comment was, in essence, that there
have been OO languages with lambda operators and dynamic closures
since Smalltalk (so nearly as long as there have been OO languages at
all), so there is no "advance of paradigms" in this case.

I don't entirely agree - for one thing, I think his claim that
"Smalltalk isn't functional" isn't meaningful in the context of his
larger argument - but the basic point is a good one.
Programing-language "paradigms" like OO and functional are genres,
which is to say they're categories of convenience imposed by
commentators on the gamut of existing languages. They're not
attributes of the languages themselves.

Things like lambda operator, dynamic closures, and higher-order
functions are attributes of a programming language. You can claim that
a language which possess those is "functional", but that's really a
gloss for "you can write functional programs in this language". (And
of course per Kolmogorov you can write functional programs in any
Turing-complete language, so all you're really saying is that it's
convenient to write functional programs in that language.) Saying a
language uses the "functional paradigm" is even more tenuous.

I like functional features in programming languages, and where they're
available I often use them. I like OO features, and I often use them.
But I'm not subscribing to some "paradigm" in either case, and I don't
believe one is advancing toward me brandishing a burning lambda,
either. Yes, there's a trend for languages to pick up functional
features, and that's good. But it doesn't mean they require us to
abandon OO or anything else.

And conversely, it's possible to use at least some functional and OO
patterns in most procedural languages cleanly. I have C programs that
use higher-order functions, polymorphism, and inheritance. No paradigm
shift required; as Lee says, you use tools because they're useful. You
don't have to change teams.
