As a follow up on another thread.

Yes, it is true that you ONLY need to handle "divide-by-4" to get the
correct leap year for 1901 thru 2099.  (You do not need to "worry about"
divide by 100 or divide by 400.)

HOWEVER, if you are dealing with people who might have been born before 1901
or might die after 2099, then you DO need to remember AT LEAST the 100 year
rule - and once you add that, you need the 400 year rule to get 2000
correct.

Certainly, we are not YET into the world of "30 year mortgages" - or other
COMMON "forward looking" business rules that need to deal with 2100 - but I
(personally) think that anyone who has ANY "business rules" that need to
know about leap years - SHOULD get it right - at least up to the "4000 year"
problem.

NOTE WELL: The question of dates being stored in items that will fail in
2034 (or whatever that year is) - is something that I would (again
personally) start "documenting" and finding out how your SYSTEM (OS) handles
sooner rather than latter.  30-year mortgages (and similar "look-ahead"
dates) are going to start running into that pretty SOON. If you are storing
"dates" as an integer from some "arbitrary" start date - and your field that
stores it will fail in 2004 (for 30-year in advance dates) then NOW seems
like the time to start fixing such problems - not 2003.

It is not possible to accurately predict the application of leap days
and seconds in the long term future, because of periodic and long term
variations in the earth's rotation and orbit.  Leap seconds cannot
even be accurately predicted for more than a few months in advance.

I think that the reason that SQL allows for two leap seconds per
minute is because the designers considered that they might be
necessary.  I also think that the reason that DB2 stores timestamps in
YYYYMMDD.HHMMSS.SSSSSS format, rather than as number of seconds or
days from a fixed reference point, is because the designers consider
the vagaries of the application of leap seconds and days cause too
many long term problems for long lived computer systems developed for
serious applications dealing with long term record keeping as could be
necessary for life assurance, mortgages, government records, etc. 
Provided that timestamps are held in a form that correctly identifies
the current date and time, routines to perform the calculations to
determine the durations between them can be relatively easily adapted
to accommodate any changes in the relationships between SI (UTC)
seconds, mean solar days and tropical years.

It is highly probable that the leap year rule would be sufficient to
get us to the year 4000 A.D., but thereafter the application of rules
have to accommodate the gradual increase in day length and decreases
in year length arising from long term one way trends caused by tidal
friction.  Even short term climatic variations affect the rate of
rotation, for example, more water in the atmosphere means that more
weight is distributed further from the earth's centre and the laws of
inertia result in a slower rate of rotation to conserve momentum.  A
warmer climate allows the atmosphere to hold more moisture.  There are
also the effects of glaciation and the Milankovitch cycles to
consider. plus the possible effects of events such as even relatively
minor asteroid impacts and very large volcanic eruptions such as that
of Krakatoa and its global climatic impact.

My own view is that we can not reasonably do better than use four
digit year numbers and eschew date recording mechanisms based upon
counts of seconds or days from a fixed reference point.  Scientists
will still have valid uses for such recording mechanisms, but they are
not suitable for the administrative calendar.  Then there would have
to be another massive reworking some time in advance of 10,000 A.D.,
when we will be long since gone and it will probably be done
automatically in carefully planned stages with no obvious impact on
ordinary day to day activities.

I have written a lot more on the subject, much of which can be seen at
www.jones0086.freeserve.co.uk under "calendars and computers", however
readers should be warned that it is incomplete and no warranty is
given that it is all correct.

Robert

The question of Leap SECONDS (rather than leap DAYS) is truly an interesting
(and to me FRUSTRATING) one.

The draft of the next Standard introduces a new feature that it is HOPED
will allow for implementations (and operating systems) that do and do not
"know about" specific leap seconds (that may be either ADDED or SUBTRACTED -
from 60 seconds per minute).

While J4 (and WG4) were doing research into this, I think we found the
"international group" that is responsible for
 - declaring a specific leap second (or two - plus or minus)
 - "forecasting" when leap seconds will occur (in the next few years ONLY)

However, unlike leap days, there is DEFINITELY no way to "predict" when a
leap second (plus or minus) will occur at a "random" time in the future.  It
is based (as I understand it) on the ACTUAL calculated orbit of the Earth
around the sun - and how much "slippage" has and has not occurred.

Bottom-Line (IMHO)
 - You can/should be able to "calculate" leap days - in the past and future
using the divide by 4, 100, and 400 rules
 - You  can (but probably don't need to) have a "table" of leap seconds in
the past and immediate (1-3 years) future
 - There is no (currently available) way to handle leap seconds at a random
date in the future
 - If you have a "time" validation routine, then you PROBABLY could allow
for 58 to 62 seconds per minute - but would need to allow for non-60 values
for specific ("hard-coded") values for when the leap second occurs.
(Actually, there are specific days of the year when such seconds may be
declared - what varies is which of those days are "selected")

Not settled at this point.
A Gregorian date routine that allows for a "4000" leap year correction
is in error.  No such standard has been established.  Should the
rotation and orbit of the earth remain sufficiently constant, the
multiple of 400 at which the 4-100-400 rule needs another nudge to keep
it within 0.5 days is closest to a 3200 year cycle, so at this point the
most arithmetically correct proposal would actually be a 4-100-400-3200
year rule, not a 4-100-400-4000 rule.  But, since date systems are
created by political bodies and nothing has been officially adopted, any
comments on leap years after about 3200 are pure speculation (save for a
natural disaster, I think the built-in inertia of the existing systems
should protect us from any changes for leap years prior to 3200). 
Perhaps by 3200 the earth's rotation will have been changed by an
asteroid hit and the old leap year rule completely invalidated; or
perhaps the growing error will just be tolerated until it becomes too
great and several days have to be cut out of one year to get things back
in sync (perhaps in the year 10,000 as part of the massive Y10K 5-digit
year problem solution).  Hey, at the earliest we still have at least
1189 years left before 3190 A.D., when we have to get serious and make a
decision and get started on changing leap year code. NO PROBLEM!! :)
