>> amark@luckynet.co.il wrote:
>> >

This is a truly amazing statement, and was the cause of my first thoughts
that your post was a troll.  DBMS (DataBase Managament Systems) were first
invented years after COBOL was born.  COBOL is an acronym for COmmon Business
Oriented Language.  That's a real big clue what COBOL was created for.  When
COBOL was created, the punched card was king.  There were no 'DBMS' systems
until many years later.

>> > From cobol steams most of our troubles with 2000 year problemm.

Another truly amazing statement, founded securely in ignorance.  Every single
medical problem involving birth is associated with women.  Does that mean
that women CAUSE them?  Geesh!  There is one, and only one, reason why COBOL
is associated so closely with Y2k problems.  Listen closely now.  It is because
there are no 30 year old C programs, no 30 year old VB programs, no 30 year old
Java programs.  Are you beginning to get the picture?  Tell me which features
in any of these other languages would have prevented a programmer using YYMMDD
for a date in a database, hum?  Now, tell me what feature in COBOL would force
a programmer to use PIC 99 for year, rather than PIC 9999, hum?  Just trot
them out right here and prove me wrong.  The fact is that the Y2k problem has
*NOTHING* do do with the language used.  Unlike proprietary database systems
such as Access, which actually have specific internal date formats, all general
purpose programming languages, such as COBOL, C, Pascal, FORTRAN, you-name-it, allow you to set up a date in any form
you like.  This is fact, friend.  Any
programmer beyond a complete neophyte knows this.  Now, if we took your idea
and converted our 200GB absolutely mission critical databases into Access (an
amusing proposition at best), we would still be hostage to Microsoft's
proprietary date format.

>> >Some 15 years ago they notised, all of a sudden, that in commercial
>> >paccages, writen in cobol there are only two digits in the "year" field.

Another statement from ignorance.  Do you know what percentage of COBOL programs
are in 'commercial packages'?  Probably less than 5%, likely less than 2%. The
*vast* majority of COBOL programs are written as proprietary, custom programs by
and for individual companies or organizations.

And so we 'suddenly noticed' the problem 15 years ago, hum?  What were you doing
15 years ago?  How about 20 years ago?  How about 25 years ago?  How about 30
years ago?  Well, I was writing COBOL code 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 and 30 years ago
(as well as today).  I have written several million lines of COBOL code.  And
I can tell you as an absolute fact that I never wrote a program using a 2 digit
year in my life when I was not aware that there would be a problem with it at
the turn of the century.

I have written many, many programs which used two digit year, but none of them
within the last 15 years.  There are two basic reasons why two digit years were
used in the beginning.  One reason is that resources were extremely limited in
those days.  Memory and storage are so abundant now that no one who wasn't
programming before 1980 can have the foggiest sense of how limited memory and
storage were.  The freedom to simply decide to use 4 digit year was not there.
At every point you had to be concerned with effeciency, efficiency, efficiency.
This attitude younger programmers have now that it doesn't really matter, would
have been ludicrous in those days, and anyone who programmed like that would
have been out of a job in very short order.  The other reason two digit years
were used in the beginning is because nobody, and I mean nobody, really thought,
or had reason to think, that those programs would still be in production 30 or
more years later.  Anyone who says they knew this in the 60's has either
forgotten or is lying.  In 1980, the modern computer industry was only about 20
years old, and had seen enormous change.  Who knew that COBOL would be such a
stable environment that COBOL programs written in 1968 would still compile and
run in 1998?  Can you compile a 25 year old C program on todays compilers?  You
can't even compile a VB 3 program on VB5 without changes, and VB is a baby
compared to COBOL!   You think *unstability* is an advantage?  How much money
do you think has been spent recoding VB1, VB2, VB3 and VB4 code to work with
later versions of VB?  What if all that COBOL code had been written in VB?
You would have a Y2k level effort every two years, just to keep up!  Why do you
think businesses still use COBOL so much?  You think all business programmers
are idiots, and you have the only vision?  It is because COBOL offers features
business find valuable.  But ignorant clucks who have not written hundreds or
thousands of business programs don't have a clue what COBOL is all about.  I
have written production code in over computer 40 languages, and am certainly
not limited to COBOL.  I would not write a text editor or a compiler in COBOL
(though I have), unless I was constrained by other reasons (I was).  But if I
write a core business app like payroll, G/L, etc., you can bet I will write it
in COBOL, unless constrined by other reasons.

By 1985, we knew we were writing COBOL systems which would still be in use by
the year 2000, because, unlike 1975, we already had 20 year old COBOL programs.
Any programs written after 1985, COBOL or otherwise, should have been written
Y2k compliant.  I was certainly preaching that to my clients.  But the decision
not to remediate older system for Y2k was a, listen carefully now, *management*
decision.  Programmers, even COBOL programmers, can recommend a course of
action, but no COBOL programmer I know has authority to allocate thousands or
millions of dollars of company resources to do Y2k remediation.  And you know
what?  Most of those managers who made the decisions to delay or forgo Y2k
remediation probably never saw a line of COBOL in their lives.  So when you say
that the decision had to do with COBOL, you are again speaking from obvious
ignorance.  It had to do with *money*, not programming language.  You think
management would have been more willing to to spend $100 million rewriting
applications in C, than they would have been to spend $20 million for Y2k
remediation of existing COBOL applications?  Do you know how long some of these
systems have been under development and improvement?  You think you could just
whip in there and rewrite a hundred billion lines of COBOL code in C overnight?
Geesh!

As to COBOL being replaced simply because it is old, what kind of eating
instrument do you use?  Chances are, it was designed hundreds, perhaps even
thousands, of years ago.  How many significant improvements on the hammer have
there been in the last century?  If you live long enough, you will hopefully
gain the wisdom that just because something is newer, does not mean it is
better.  Newer might be better, but newer might be worse, too.  Time and
experience reveal this.  Right now, it is more expensive, takes longer, costs
more, and you get a less reliable result, using any language other than COBOL,
for core business programming.  This we learn, if we look at the actual numbers
from real-world projects, rather than blather about in ignorance.

Judson, I am in total agreement with you. COBOL was developed for commercial
data processing, in the days when computers, and their ancilliary resources
(dasd, programmers, etc) were difficult to acquire.

COBOL is still the language of choice for commercial data processing - I am
working on a project implementing credit/debit card processing for over 5
million cards (with normal growth, we'll probably have over 10 million in the
portfolio in 5 year's time, and who knows where we'll be in 15 years time -

the real work gets done. We can afford to use OO languages on the front-end
PCs, which can afford dedicated processors for each user, but when we get
around to large integrated centralized databases, we need efficient,
reliable, maintable and stable software suites.

As an aside, Ihave seen many complaints about C/C++ memory management - there
seems to be a fundamental flaw in the language design which COBOL does not
suffer from.
