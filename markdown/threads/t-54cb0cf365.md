Wearing as I do both "programmer" and "manager" hats and doing my best to 
reconcile the two, I was interested to read this:

http://morendil.github.com/folklore.html

The article makes a very good point about how the scientific method gets 
subsumed by Folklore on occasion, and I have seen this on various sites 
where programmers held certain beliefs, based on no scientific fact 
whatsoever, just rumour and "Folklore", having evolved out of a 
misunderstanding regarding a standard or something that was not clearly 
explained in a Language Manual. Sometimes, it can have a devastating effect 
on productivity.

"You can't put anything other than GO TO in an AT END statement. It's a 
compiler bug..." (It wasn't...)
"You can't use OLE for RDB accesss, it has to be ODBC." (No, it didn't...)
" IMS databases can't have more than 3 segment levels."  (IMS supports 
16...)

Those are just some I encountered; I have quite a few more...

The particular quotes in the article which caught my attention are as 
follows:

<quote>
"programmer productivity varies by a factor of 10 (or 5, or 20) between the 
best and worst individuals". This is a remarkable statement, not least 
because of its implications: for instance, programmer compensation does not 
vary accordingly.
</quote>

I didn't realize that anybody had published a credible paper with such an 
insupportable and unquantifiable conclusion in it. Certainly, some 
programmers are "better" than others (it usually comes down to a mix of 
experience, aptitude, and attitude) but even if you COULD show that someone 
was 10 times worse than their peers, would there be any point in doing so? 
Retrain or remove, I reckon.

<quote>
For instance, should we assume (as seems reasonable in industrial contexts) 
that productivity could only be zero or greater? This is to ignore the 
(folkloric, but remarkable and provocative) notion of "net negative 
productivity programmers":, team members who not only do not contribute to a 
team's productivity but in fact destroy others'.

</quote>

You may be smiling but I have encountered a couple of these and they are no 
joke... :-)

<quote>

It is a sad fact that critical examination of ill-supported assertions takes 
a lot more time than making the assertions in the first place; this 
asymmetry accounts for a lot of what is wrong with public perceptions of 
science in general, and possibly for the current state of our profession.

</quote>

I don't think he's advocating that we SHOULDN'T critically examine 
ill-supported assertions, but it certainly does take time and effort to 
build a counter case.

Overall, I thought this was a well-written article which certainly made me 
think.

Hope you enjoy it.

Pete.

My own experience has taught me that when managing programmers the following 
should be kept in mind:

1.Let them assess how long it will take them to do something; don't impose 
deadlines for political or any other reason. If there ARE deadlines, manage 
that with the people concerned. The fact that something is required quickly 
is not a programming problem.
2. If they try sandbagging you, tell them how long it would take you to do 
it yourself and suggest they revise their estimate. If they won't, allocate 
the work to someone else, or do it yourself. Generally, people are 
responsive if you treat them fairly and are supportive of them.
3. Make sure they get support in the form of tools, resources, and 
encouragement.
4. Once they have accepted a job and agreed the time to do it, make them 
responsible for it. Expect them to tell you if it is going off the rails and 
seek help. Foster a team approach with yourself as a facilitator to see that 
the team succeeds.
5. Reward success. (Pizza, time off, movies, whatever...)
6. Acknowledge effort.
7. Never take credit for anything yourself. Make sure that ideas coming from 
the team are recognised and acknowledged as such.
8. If there are disagreements over approaches, hear all parties, then rule. 
Don't allow simmering resentment. Resolve issues and move on as quickly as 
possible.
9. Respect your people, treat them fairly and be glad when they win.

The above are just the fundamentals, there is much more (like dealing with 
people and events when things go wrong) but if the above are in place, there 
is no need to worry about Function Points or LOC. You'll have a team that 
are happy to come to work and people will be queuing up to get on it. :-)

Productivity is a direct function of morale, and morale is the 
responsibility of management.

I certainly didn't say 'write using built in functions', I use
templating and don't care whether the output is XML or any other
format. The layout of the output is in a text file with tags marking
where the data goes a program module (or object) merges the data.

I have written several templating modules: COBOL, C, Python, Java;
they are quite easy to do and I can enhance as required to cover
special needs. I also have a PostScript merger so that I can design
forms graphically (I use tgif for this) putting tag fields in the form
and then merge the data in for invoices, Jobcards, labels etc.

Reading XML can be done using DOM or SAX.

I usually parameterize the processing with a configuration file that,
for example,  links an internal name with the XML tag name and some
processing info.

[xmltags]
basetag=CONSIGNMENT
jobno=CONSIGNMENT_NUMBER,,key
jobdesc=DESCRIPTION
order=SALES_ORDER_NUMBER
...
quantity=QTY1,i
weight=WEIGHT,n2
....

There is a lump of code that processes down the XML picking up stuff
according to the parameters accumulating the data. Each time there is
a change of 'key' it pushes out the accumulated data (in a Python
dictionary) back to the controlling program and this get sent to the
templating module to output it in some other form (in this case a CSV
that is the input format for the Transport System).

So, like templating, the reading program neither knows nor cares what
the XML tag names are beyond that they are matched to the parameter
file to be stored for passing to something else.

The processing program calls the XML reading module because the
parameter file tells it to, but there are also modules for
processing .xls, .csv, flat files and the like also driven by similar
sets of parameters in the configuration file.
