Conditional statements require their scope to be terminated, either by
a full stop or by an appropriate scope terminator.

Imperative statements are self contained and do not have scope
terminators.

Out of line PERFORMs are imperative and do not have scope.

In line PERFORMs are conditional in that they do have scope and thus
need terminating.

I have been away and missed some of this thread.  However, I think that Richard 
has given the correct answer.

The '85 Standard introduced both "scope terminators" and "in-line performs".

With the exception of END-PERFORM, all other scope terminators do one of two 
things:

1) turn a conditional statement into an imperative statement, e.g.
    Read xyz
       At End
          Display  "This is conditional"

versus
    Read xyz
       At End
          Display  "This is imperative"
    End-Read

(a period will also terminate a conditional statement, but it will NOT make it 
imperative)

2) "documentary only" e.g.
      Read XYZ
      End-Read

****

As far as END-PERFORM goes, it indicates the "termination" of a block of code 
which begins with a PERFORM (without a procedure name) and makes that "block of 
code" available for use where any imperative statement may be used.

Therefore, END-PERFORM makes no "required" sense for an out-of-line PERFORM 
statement which is (by definition) always an imperative statement.  It could be 
allowed as documentary (and I think some implementations may do this as an 
extension).  However, it would never serve any syntactic sense for an 
out-of-line perform.  It may also be noted that at least one implementation 
(Micro Focus) allows it to be omitted for in-line PERFORMs (especially when 
followed by a period/full-stop).

  ***

P.S.  For those who have replied indicating that the "scope" of an out-of-line 
perform is somewhere at the end of the PERFORMed code, this is NOT how the COBOL 
Standard (ANSI, ISO, or any other that I know of) uses the term.  "Scope" is 
used as a lexical term and not (usually?) in the sense of "under the control 
of".
