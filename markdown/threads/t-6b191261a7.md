```
I would like to obtain input from the gang regarding a COBOL version problem
that I (our company) have (has).

It's a MainFrame question, but my dealings with MF-COBOL indicate that a
similiar issue (rc=165) also exists...

READ ON for particulars!

***********************************************************

We have a product that we distribute to our client base that is written
in COBOL. The product was originally developed in the 80's, so the
COBOL we compiled with at the time was OS/VS COBOL.

As we all know, COBOL is transforming at a rapid clip - VS COBOL II
was a great new addition and, more recently, COBOL/370 is being
marketed (with a name change to COBOL for MVS or VSE or OS2...).

My problem, is that I cannot decide what to compile our new release
with - scheduled for this year.

OS/VS COBOL?
- not supported by IBM using OS/VS COBOL only
- supported today by runtimes in VS/COBOL2 and COBOL/370
- support not committed after 1996?
VS/COBOL2?
- supported today (fully) by IBM as VS/COBOL2
- supported today by runtime in COBOL/370
- support to be dropped this year???
COBOL/370?
- not well distributed yet
- not stabilized


The biggest problem that I have - over and above the list above -
is that ***> some of our client base is NOT leaving OS/VS COBOL!!! <***.

At first I thought this was a VSE peculiarity, but it appears to be
endemic to some MVS clients as well! Of course, the opposite is also
true, some clients have already said goodbye to OS/VS COBOL and are
waiting for us to get into the swing.

I've rationalized this mess by assuming the clients are confused by
the rapidity of COBOL change - and are waiting for the dust to settle
so they can have a clear understanding of their alternatives and of
IBMs direction.

Question: What COBOL should I compile with?

My initial plans were for COBOL2 (1.4.0) but I'm quickly headed for a
reversal to good old OS/VS COBOL.

Any insights would be greatly appreciated.

Donald Berry (dbe··.@d··.com)
```

```
If you have to support VSE go with COBOL II compliant code and thus
be portable across the IBM mainframe environments. You lose the
ability to incorporate the 1989 addendum to the standard (primarily
the functions) but gain everything else. With anything earlier, you
stand a good chance of not being able to work with CICS and other IBM
products. Of course you could restrict yourself to code that compiles
the same on all 3 COBOLs but I doubt that anyone would like that.

If you are only going to be MVS and VM then I would go with the
latest COBOL and work with your clients who are still VS COBOL to get
them to understand the benefits of the new compiler and see if you can
find a way that it isn't financially painful for them. VS COBOL is
starting to be dangerous. The newest COBOL integrates with a
number of things much better and also generates more efficient
code based on postings I have seen. The shop I am working at is using
COBOL II, and will have to until they are fully on CICS 3.x for ESA.
I am writing as a technically oriented programming
consultantwho started using COBOL in 1963 and has been both an
applicationsprogrammer-analyst and a systems programmer ever
since.
```

```
As you say OS/VS COBOL is not really supported for much longer, so you need
to move off of it. So the question is really COBOL2 or COBOL/370.

Cobol 2 is at more places, but more from the time its been out. Some people
moved off of VS COBOL some time ago. For them to move to COBOL/370 I see
little to be gained.

Now you on the other hand can choose between the two. You should know that
COBOL/370 is going to replace COBOL2 sometime. I would pick COBOL/370 and
save the shop a conversion in the future! I do not know what you mean that
COBOL/370 is not stabilized? VS COBOL still had APARS right before IBM said
its a dead release. Today most of the errors that I see are in the LE/370
(cobol's runtime library) and they deal more with running C in CICS or PL/1
in CICS ... not Cobol/370. Remember that LE/370 supports many lanaguages.

So I would (and DID) pick COBOL/370 about 2 yeary ago.

Have fun, what ever you pick (but don't stay on VS Cobol).
```
