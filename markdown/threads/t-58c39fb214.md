I can't really add a whole lot of insight into the St. Louis job market,
because I am basically happy where I am and haven't been looking.

I have never found a job through the St. Louis Post-Dispatch, and it's been
years since I've even glanced at their job ads. As far as I am concerned,
the best thing in the Post-Dispatch (apart from the movie listings) is Scott
Adams' Dilbert comic strip. Now that has some relevance to my job!

I do have some anecdotal evidence. My former boss (his new title is
technology consultant, and he is glad to be rid of the administrative
paperwork) told me that we paid $45 to $50 an hour for COBOL contract
programmers in 1996, and we could occasionally negotiate the rates a little
bit lower. Since about January, 1997, the rates have crept up to about $50
to $55 an hour, and there is no negotating down. This is generally a low
cost-of-living area.

About two years ago, we had a very sharp contractor working on a couple of
our projects. Unfortunately, I don't think we gave her very challenging
assignments, and she wanted a contract closer to home (she has school-age
kids). She told me in 1995 that her agency had more mainframe COBOL demand
than they could supply. I suspect this was due to the shortage of new COBOL
talent from the schools. I don't think Y2K was on the radar screen of very
many St. Louis businesses in 1995. The shortage of COBOL programmers can
only get worse.

We have several excellent contract programmers working in my department right
now. One of them left the county government to go into contracting. He says
the county lost most of their programmers to better jobs, and hasn't really
been able to replace any of them.

I know of a couple of MasterCard programmers who left a few months ago for
First Data Resources. They have a big shop in the area. I'm sure they got
more money, but the rumor I hear is that they are less happy now.

MasterCard has for a long time offered a $1000 cash bonus for bringing in a
resume that results in hiring an FTE. A couple of months ago, in a meeting
of all Systems Development personnel, they announced a $3000 cash bonus if a
COBOL programmer is hired. This year they also began paying cash bonuses to
retain existing COBOL programmers on critical systems. These bonuses are 3
to 5 times higher than annual increases.

We've been working on Y2K since April of 1996. If any large business hasn't
started on Y2K yet, it's probably too late for them. I suspect rates for
COBOL contract programmers will continue to creep up. At this point there
don't seem to be problems getting enough good contractors, but who knows?

Arnold Trembley
Software Engineer I (just a job title, still a programmer)
MasterCard International
St. Louis, Missouri
