### Cobol for new development

- **From:** "robin gates" <ua-author-2025432@usenetarchives.gap>
- **Date:** 1997-12-05T19:00:01+00:00
- **Newsgroups:** comp.lang.cobol
- **Message-ID:** `<66bqhq$b91@bgtnsc01.worldnet.att.net>`

```

I am a manager (not a programmer) in a large state government agency that
will be re-writing its entire computer system over the next several years.
This is a high volume transaction processing system that handles taxes.
Nearly everything on the legacy systems is cobol/CICS. My question is
whether cobol has a legitimate place in the development of a new system.
The cobol advocates say that there is nothing better for programming the
business logic of the system than cobol. They suggest using all the latest
GUI toos to develop the front end (including Internet interfaces), but
build the guts with cobol and a DB2 database. Any guidance for a manager
who will need to be making some key decisions in this area. Thanks for
your help.
```

I'm currently part of a team rewriting a commercial tax system. We're
rewriting from Cobol/CICS/VSAM to Cobol/CICS/DB2. The volume is way
to heavy to run on anything other than a S/390 machine and over the
past 10 years the way the system is used has forced a rewrite. I
don't know about the internet/intranet interfaces since this would be
no benefit to us, CICS transactions are used to tweak some tax data
and the majority of the tax info (tax jurisdiction data) comes monthly
via a tape from Vertex.

My past employer had bought a tax solution some years back. It was
all mainframe and required little modifications for our use (I can't
recall the vendor). If you're maintaining tax info yourself, you may
find using someone such as Vertex a better decision. It's shifts the
liability of incorrect data to someone else.
