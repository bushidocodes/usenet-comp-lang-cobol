```
Once upon a time there was a piano-player. He was employed at a large
company where he played the piano most skillfully. It was a special,
probrietary piano, with many special keys in special places for many
special functions. Because of this skill he had, he was paid a fine
salary.

One day the company decided that all employed piano-players had to
change to another instrument, a violin. A violin, they said, is much
cheaper because it is essentially only a box of wood, and it should be
more easy to play a violin because there are only four strings attached
to it. So, in the context of global competition, it would be cheaper,
they said, to hire cheaper violin-players. The existing staff of
piano-players just had to aquire the new skills, they said, as we all
have to learn continuously.

The piano-player now learned to play the violin. Although he had been a
brilliant piano-player in his days, as a violin-player, he was only
mediocre. In fact, listening to him was rather horrible, far
exceeding, for instance, the secret joys of listening to someone
playing the bagpipe.

In principle, he could have changed to playing an open-source-piano
which
he would have mastered easily - the idea of aranging the keys in
ascending order had crossed his own mind in his old piano-days - but the
company said that he would not be allowed to do so. The
open-source-piano, they said, has not been manufactured by Microsoft,
and its architecture was too simple to impress customers and competitors
alike. No - a violin is the instrument of choice, and what is good for
everybody is good also for the old piano-player.

Because of his mediocre violin-playing, the piano-player was sacked. As
a violin-player, you will never amount to very much, he was told, and
also, he was told, in a booming economy, he would easily find a new
job, such as ice-cream selling or re-tuning old pianos in such
companies which did not make the transition from the age of the piano
to the age of the violin.

What is the morale of this story?

-- Never build up skills related to probrietary tools only.

-- If you use open-source-tools, never tell your boss: He will be
   more impressed if you get results with clumsy probrietary tools.

-- Bagpipe-playing is still worse than everything else combined.

(c) Herwig Huener
```
