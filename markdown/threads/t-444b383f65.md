Hello Everybody.

First off, I'm not a newbie (over 25 years expirience 
programming in over a dozen languages).

Second, I've been quote as saying "Cobol/CICS" is my mother tongue"! 
(Since my first major application (1979) was writen in it).
So I'm not some PC convert flaming Cobol.  

I've been lurking in comp.lang.cobol and alt.cobol for a couple of 
weeks now.  In anticipation of a contract to convert a banking 
application from SCO, MicroFocus cobol, to either Linux MF Cobol 
or Solaris MF Cobol (meriant).  Meriant don't seem to support the
newer versions of Linux, so my client is tending toward Solaris.

Having said all that, here is my 2 bits:

1) Cobol can be written in such a way as to be extreemly readable, 
   and thereby extreemly maintainable.  When done so it is (in my
   opinion) THE most maintainable language I've ever used.

2) Cobol can be written in such a way as to be the extreemly unreadable,
   and thereby extreemly unmaintainable.  When done so it is close
   to the least maintainable language I've ever used.

Sound like a contradiction? Well that's my expirience.

Of course my expirience is with the older dialects of the language.
The problem, I find, in Cobol is that the Cobol language is flat,
and not hierarchical.  By this I mean that Cobol all the data and code
is global, and thereby globaly accessable.

I remember my first Cobol program, in 1977.  It brought the IBM COBOL 
compiler
crashing down (with an ABEND).  After multiple reviews of the code I was 
unable
to find the problem.  I went to the local COBOL expert (Daddy) for help.
His comment was "I wish this would compile, but ...".  Here is what I was 
trying to do:

Data Division.
  01 Rec01.
     05  Rec01-Fld01     pic ...
     05  Rec01-Fld02     pic ...
     05  Rec01-Fld03     pic ...

Procedure Division.
  Init-Rec01.
     Move ... to Rec01-Fld01.
     Move ... to Rec01-Fld02.
     Move ... to Rec01-Fld03.

Data Division.
  01 Rec02.
     05  Rec02-Fld01     pic ...
     05  Rec02-Fld02     pic ...
     05  Rec02-Fld03     pic ...

Procedure Division.
  Init-Rec02.
     Move ... to Rec02-Fld01.
     Move ... to Rec02-Fld02.
     Move ... to Rec02-Fld03.

As a new COBOL programmer I did not see any reason that the data 
definitions 
could not be defined near the code that used them!  And all the examples 
had
short little Data Divisions and short little Procedure Devisions.

Cobol programs which exceed a certain size grow into monstrous 
maintenance 
nightmares.  This is forced onto the programmer by the language itself, 
since 
it does not allow for any type of local variables, or local procedures.  
To
get around this, I started using the language in a style which was soon 
dubbed
"CALLBOL".  Spreading the Code into different compilable units.  Most of 
you
know all about the problems this creates.

Taking examples of good things from other languages:
In "C" (and many other languages), I can define variables local to a 
procedure.
Please don't misunderstand me. Yes, I can "C", but I'd rather be blind!  
But I
could really use variables defined local to a paragraph in Cobol.  

Additionally, the field names in the above examples composed of record 
name 
dash field name ie Rec01-Fld03.  This naming convention is widely used to 
offer
the programmer an idea of what is being done as shown below.

    Move ... to Rec01-Fld03.

Is read as "Move something to Fld03 of Rec01".  Hmmmm, when COBOL is 
supposed to 
be read like english the why don't we just say

    Move ... to Fld03 of Rec01.

Of course, this would get quite windy

    Move 20 to day                  of birth-date of Employee-Rec.
    Move 03 to month                of birth-date of Employee-Rec.
    Move 19 to century of full-year of birth-date of Employee-Rec.
    Move 59 to year    of full-year of birth-date of Employee-Rec.
    ....

But that could be simplified with a trick out of pascal to:

    with birth-date of Employee-Rec
    begin
      Move 20 to day                    
      Move 03 to month                  
      Move 19 to century of full-year
      Move 59 to year    of full-year
    end.

Although I think the first version is easier to read and therefore more
maintainable, the second form would surely come in handy sometimes.

In my opinion only 01 level definitions should be directly accessable
and all fields accessable only in this way!


My Question:

What do the rest of the COBOL users out there think?  What do the newer
dialects offer in this hierarchical-structuring aspect?  Is anybody 
working
on something in this direction?



Jerry Westrick

      MOVE Day     TO Day

This kind of question came up for Ada during the design of the 1995 ISO standard.

The decision was to develop a set of annexes to the language that could
accomodate
a wide variety of special needs.  The annexes add no new syntax or semantics but
simply use the existing language definition to describe them.   One consequence
was
that the original Ada Language Reference Manual (1983)  went from 14 chapters to
thirteen.

Object COBOL could probably benefit from this approach.   In my humble opinion,
some of what we in COBOL could be deferred to similar kinds of annexes,
especially
the Report Writer.   In this way, the annexes could be defined as special needs
libraries
that can be augmented and updated independently of changes to the language.

I wasn't there for the votes on this subject, but I have discussed it at
length with some of my fellow J4 members on more than one occasion.

One of the complaints that has been leveled at the earlier standards
(specifically '85) is that the modularity of the compiler (originally eight,
subsequently nine "standard" modules and four "optional" ones), multiple
levels of implementation in all modules but one (RPW),  and three categories
of COBOL implementation subsets independent of the presence or absence of
optional modules, led to a WIDE variety of *conformant* implementations that
were incompatible with each other.

Stated another way, the only constructs that could be guaranteed compilable
by any ANSI-85-compliant compiler were the minimum subsets of the NUC, SEQ
and IPC modules; it was the implementors choice what else to provide, at
which level, and in which combinations.  It was perfectly legitimate
according to the standard to have a compiler that didn't support Indexed
I/O, but DID support Report Writer and every "widget" of Sort-Merge.   It
was perfectly OK to compile RD's but syntax READ...NEXT RECORD.

One of the main goals of COBOL is portability, and the optionality of
features, levels and components within the language is counter to that goal
because it *supports* the development of incompatible implementations and
limits severely the choices of a user who wishes to produce an application
that's truly portable across implementations.  That is the overriding sense
that J4 seems to have received from the *user* community.   This was
discussed again and again and again until it became a greasy spot on the
pavement that used to be a horse quite a long time ago.    And, from all I
can determine, the issue of application and program portability won out.

There are still some who feel some of the features of the FCD should be
optional or even absent altogether (the most common facility I've heard this
about is the screen facility, but I have heard grumblings about others).
But I think all the arguments in favor of having a multiplicity of dialects
of "standard" COBOL have been carefully considered by J4, and rejected in
favor of a *single* definition of what "standard" COBOL contains, precisely
to foster inter-implementation portability.

In any case, the decision as to what features of the new standard COBOL the
user will include in his program remain that user's decision.     But I
believe the intent of J4 is that for all implementations conforming to the
new standard, the *user* will make that choice, not the *compiler
implementor* claiming that his compiler conforms to that standard.     To
the application designer who thinks, for example, that ACCEPT <screen> is
ten years behind the times, he is free to direct that it be eschewed, just
as another application designer who thinks it's the bee's knees is free to
direct that it be embraced.  Making the construct optional has the effect of
limiting the second application designer's choices, at the expense of the
first.

The point here is to maximize the *users'* choices, and that has the
side-effect of limiting the *implementor's* choices as to which features he
will include in his standards-compliant COBOL compilers.  And I think that's
the right objective, and the right choice.

    -Chuck Stevens

way or another, before it was adopted.  That they have not yet done so is
not a surprise; indeed, if they had done so, I would view that as an action
entirely out of order.   If ISO/IEC and ANSI adopt the standard, it does not

<<As there are no current plans for any validation suite for the new

It's true that the *US Government* discontinued sponsorship of compiler
validation some years back.    While I'm not *personally aware* of any plans
to produce a validation suite for the new Standard, I find the flat
assertion that out of six billion people on this planet *not one* is
currently planning to produce such a suite at any point in the future smacks
a bit more of omniscience than makes me comfortable!      ;-)

Without a *mandated* validation suite, conformance to the standard becomes
something of a "gentleman's agreement" among the implementors, I agree, but
I don't necessarily think that's a bad thing.  I would personally like to
see a validation suite made available and a formal process of validation
reinstated in a more public way than is currently the case.

Anyway, I suspect strongly the Corrections Amendment isn't reflected by
tests in the last mandated version of the validation suite, and I'm not even
sure the Intrinsic Functions amendment was.    The process of validation was
moved from "mandatory compliance with government mandate" to "voluntary
submission to verification by a private enterprise" quite a while ago.
This is hardly a new issue with the FCD.

VALIDATE) or hard to implement (Standard arithmetic and exception
handling)>>

Specifically as to RPW and Validate:  I've already noted elsewhere that at
least one company has seen a market for these features and provides software
to handle that feature.  The combination of a compiler lacking (only) such a
feature and a software product providing it comprise an implementation of
COBOL that conforms to the standard.  The absence of RPW and Validate in an
otherwise-conforming *compiler* is irrelevant if a combination of that
compiler and another software product form a conforming *implementation* of
COBOL, whether the compiler vendor markets the third-party product or not!

I happen to agree that both standard arithmetic and exception handling are
not easy on the compiler implementor.  But I also would contend that these
are BIG improvements to COBOL, in the first case for portability, in the
second case for program reliability.   And it was pointed out to me by a J4
member *many* years ago that whether something is difficult for the
implementor is fundamentally irrelevant to what is best for the COBOL
community.

<<I just question that when/if there will ever be ANY "fully conforming"
implementations>>

As far as I'm concerned,  I firmly believe there *will* be "fully
conforming" implementations once the standard is approved, though I agree it
won't be the day after it's published.  There may not be as many such
implementations as there are for '85 COBOL, or '74 COBOL, or '68 COBOL for
that matter, but I do believe firmly there will be more than one such
implementation.

<<HOWEVER, only time will tell>>

ONLY once the standard has been approved, and only IF the standard has been
approved.   That's one of my big concerns about a "fatalistic attitude"
toward the FCD and its complexities.

UNTIL such time as it is adopted, the question as to how well it will be
accepted and whether users will embrace its features simply can't be
answered.

To paraphrase and expand on "Field of Dreams":

If we don't "build" the new standard, we KNOW noone will flock to it; there
won't be anything solid for implementors to conform to, and the users will
have no single design to depend on.   A rejected standard is oxymoronic.

If we do "build" the new standard, I see four fundamental, and real,
possibilities:  implementors will ignore the standard; implementors will
build conformant compilers that nobody will use; implementors will build
conformant compilers whose new features nobody (or few) will use;
implementors will build conformant compilers whose new features will be
embraced.

Why prevent implementors from even exploring the possibility of the latter
two eventualities by short-circuiting the standards process?    Why
discourage or even prevent an implementor from marketing a
COBOL2002-compliant compiler to a (say) COBOL85 user who has no intention of
using *any* of the new features, but simply wants to make his code *more
portable*?   If that implementor sees the possibility of making a profit at
it, why not let him?

Time will indeed tell, unless we decide first what it is that time will tell
us and act upon that prediction.  I'd rather see the standard adopted first
and see from there how widely its features are accepted.

Is it possible no implementors at all will build 100%-conformant compilers?
Yes, I think it is, although I think that eventuality unlikely.   Does the
failure of any implementor to produce such a compiler mark the  demise of
COBOL?  No, I don't think so; for example, if experience shows that certain
features really should have been "optional" instead of mandatory, future
amendments and revisions can reflect that, and implementors could well
simply "skip a standard".

Do I believe the FCD is a perfect document?  No; I do not believe *any*
human endeavor is perfect.    To paraphrase one wag, "the number of errors
in a document is inversely proportional to its triviality".  I do not
believe the standard is trivial, and despite whatever flaws,
inconsistencies, discrepancies, omissions or tardiness of which it might be
rightly or wrongly accused, I strongly believe continued progress toward its
adoption benefits the COBOL community far more than further delays to ensure
its absolute perfection, and I strongly believe abandoning the adoption of
this standard on the grounds that nobody will care would be a disaster for
the future of COBOL.

    -Chuck Stevens

(1)  implementors will ignore the standard altogether;
(2)  implementors will build conformant compilers that nobody will use;
(3)  implementors will build conformant compilers whose new features nobody
(or few) will use;
(4)  implementors will build conformant compilers whose new features will be

(new):  (5)  implementors will build compilers that include features from,

To take a couple of fairly trivial examples, I believe a COBOL compiler that
includes the in-line comment and standard compiler directive features of the
FCD is arguably more attractive to the user than a COBOL compiler without
these features.   The features, in and of themselves, are an enhancement to
the *language*, regardless of which dialect of that language is being
discussed.

The same applies to most of the major and minor facilities of the FCD; the
features (insofar as they do not directly interact) are *independently*
valuable, and *any* compiler that contains them has a higher feature content
than an otherwise-identical compiler that does not.

To me, the specification of the rules for and semantics of the *individual
features* in and of itself is sufficient justification for the adoption of
the standard whether or not anyone actually implements all the features.
The COBOL standards have always been used as a "rule book" by implementors
to guide them in building enhancements to their compilers (I've already
mentioned a specific example of this elsewhere), and I think that the
presence of that document recording consensus as to how things *ought to be*
implemented is a Good Thing.

While I do believe that there will be more than one COBOL compiler
implemented in total conformance with the final standard, my point is that
if SOMEBODY produces a fully-featured OO facility, or all the syntax and
semantics of VALIDATE, or of RPW, or of Standard Arithmetic, or even in-line
comments and standard compiler directives, in a way that is syntactically
and semantically conformant with the new standard,  in combination or in
isolation, the user of that feature in such a compiler, and thus the COBOL
commununity,  has benefited from that standard.

I have been following this thread with interest.

Everybody seems to be arguing about whether the COBOL standard should be 
ratified or not, or whether the standard is good or not, or wether the 
standard was produced in a timely fashion or not.

THOSE are NOT the problem.

The problem is (OO enhancements not withstanding) that the language is 
not close enough to those who use it. One of the advantages of C is that 
there are no language elements to implement IO. So a LIBRARY was created 
to accomplish this task. Any new type of IO is added quickly and the C 
community moves onto the task of implementing the problem at hand.

I am not advocating this approach for COBOL, but it seems to me that the 
COBOL community would be better served with compilers developed on the 
GPL Open Source lines. New language elements could be added by request 
to the code managers - who would decide if and how to implement the 
request.

In todays environment, is there ANY reason why COBOL should not have 
native statements to interface to ALL of the operating system features? 
(NOT merely IO (DISK predominately) as in the past).

Why can't we EXECUTE a unix or MVS command.
Why can't we DELETE or RENAME files or datasets.
Why can't we COMMNICATE with other processes.
Why can't we WAIT or LOCK on semaphores or ECB's.
I could go on....

As I see it, only an open source compiler will allow us the freedom to 
pursue these issues and create compilers that are better in sync with 
their operating environments.

By the way - WHO SAYS portablility is a desirable thing?
JAVA is supposed to be portable, I don,t see it doing anything other 
than enhancing the user interface. It's interpretation model is too slow 
for server purposes - It's ok to use 90% of a 400 Mhz pentium to do 
something for a single user, quite another thing to use 90% of a 
mainframe to support the same single user.

But I digress. Portability has an overated pedigree.

manufacturers make to improve their machines performance over the other 
manufacturers. Why as a user, would you invest millions in a platform

There is lots of disagreement about what is structured and what is not.   From my perspective,
each construct has an entry point and a well-defined scope.    Prior to ANSI-85, few constructs
had a well-defined scope.   Consider the selection constructs.  There were two:  alternation
in the form of an IF...ELSE, and multiway, in the form of a computed GO TO.    The IF statement
was horrible because there was no scope terminator.   All kinds of strange code passed for
"structured" but an open-ended IF caused no end of trouble.   One of the issues of structured
programming is that, when programming with elementary structures,  the scope terminator
allows one structure to be neatly enclosed within another.

As to the in-line Perform, I wrote a piece for the Communications of the ACM many years ago
with the title, "Perform Considered Harmful."    The idea behind that piece was that the
COBOL Perform was too dependent on global data and violated fundamental notions of
cohesion and coupling, another notion from structure programming.   ANSI-85 introduced
a modularity structure that allowed call by reference as well as call by value.   Few
programmers adopted this, mostly because they were not getting good guidance from
their mentors.  Those mentors often had no training in computer science and were quite
satisfied with the good old-fashioned Perform their great-grandfather had used when
he was programming in COBOL.

So, I reaffirm my statement that, prior to ANSI-85, it was impossible to write correctly
structured code in COBOL.    That was not possible until scope terminators were
included in the language.  It was not possible as long as programs were dependent on
global data.   It was not possible without a good model for parameterized subroutines.

be a good compromise that avoids by reference while not losing efficiency.

COBOL could benefit from some of the above features.  It still suffers from the need for by-reference
protocol if one wants to get data returned.  There are certainly safe ways to do this, but the
programmer must exercise care.

I understand your concern about "don't see every place it impacts or is impacted."  This concern
has often been expressed by those making the transition to a more modular form of software
development.   It is one of the most widely-expressed concerns of those making the transition
to the object model.   The reality is, that as programmers become use to the model, their fears
diminish and the the concern proves to be unfounded.   This is especially true of large-scale
programs.

You mention "copy books."  This feature of COBOL was originally intended to improve modularity,
but it failed because of its lack of support for localization.   Modularity, to be truly effective, must
be complemented by localization.   I am sure you recall how, for many copybooks, we needed to
copy both a data division fragment and a procedure division fragment to make this feature
effective.   COPY ... REPLACING fell far short of good code reuse, but it was a good
step in the right direction.

I agree with your assertion that object technology is not going to help with the maintenance
of the huge inventory of existing COBOL code.   This will be an evolutionary process.  However,
improved structured techniques that support cohesive, loosely-coupled modules is an incremental
step that can be accomplished with existing tools, existing versions of compilers, and a little
bit of additional programmer education.

I am not a big fan of revolution.    However, I believe Peter Dashwood is correct in his vision
of the future of programming.   While the Object COBOL model has its flaws, it is a workable
design that can be useful in making the transition to the next stage of software practice with
COBOL.   I do not agree that COBOL is invulnerable to being replaced by other languages. That
is already happening.   The worst of these replacements is with the C++, an increasingly complicated
language design totally inappropriate for business data processing.   Other languages, though, are
pretty good candidates for replacing a lot of COBOL code.   Java is already doing so.   Several
shops I know of have converted completed from COBOL to Python.   In my mind, Ada is probably
the language that has the most properties needed to directly convert COBOL to an object-oriented
model.  One of the best designed languages in the OOP group is Eiffel, but it does not yet have
as large a following as it deserves.

Students graduating from our universities have been told by their professors about the horrors of
COBOL.   They joke about it in the classroom.    Most of these professors would not know a
COBOL program if one crawled into bed with them.  Almost none realize that COBOL is not
the language they once thought they knew.  The new students have their preferences.  The best
of them can make the choice.   We can attract them to the world of COBOL when we make it
clear that they are not making a giant step backward but are on the forefront of the new
technological innovations, even with contemporary COBOL.

For this to happen, those of us still stuck back in the 1970's need to take a few steps forward
too.    I have been as guilty as anyone in relying on old skills to create new kinds of solutions.
I do some teaching in community college and at some local universities.   My students help
keep me aware of the need to continually update my knowledge and skills even as I do my
day-to-day work.   Without the goading of those students, I would probably be doing exactly
the same thing today I was doing in 1978.

Richard Riehle
