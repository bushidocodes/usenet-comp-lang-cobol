the degree of cognition, not the mental state's cause. The four
classic mental states (pre-meditated, willful, reckless, and

existence. That a Pre-meditated mental state exists is sufficient for
the criminal law irrespective of what emotion (if any) gave rise to
that state.

prosecuted under the same law, no matter whether the emotion (if any)
leading up to the decision was hate, lust, envy, anger, fear, greed,
or lack of toilet paper.

So, in my view, adding an emotional trigger to the definition of a
crime is an unnecessary (legal) complication.

Your conclusion (hatred = state of mind = intent) is wrong. The
distinction and definition is subtle, but I bet you can puzzle it out.
Further, you seem to have a propensity to challenge someone to bolster
their assertions by appealing to outside sources. Well, here goes:

Witters v U.S. 106 F.2nd 837, 840, 70 App.D.C. 316 (et al)
(Intent equals design, resolve, or determination with which a person
acts).

Intent and motive are not in law one and the same thing. State v Logan
344 Mo 351

'Intent' in the legal sense is purpose to use particular means to
effect a certain result; whereas 'motive' is reason which leads minds
to desire that result. United Fidelity v Adair, Tex.Civ.App 29 SW2nd
940

'Intent' expresses mental action at its most advanced point, or as it
actually accompanies an outward, corporal act, which has been
determined on. Intent shows the presence of will in the act which
consummates a crime. Burrill, Circ.Ev. 284

Hatred can engender intent, but it is the INTENT that is the necessary
element of the crime. Many other considerations can create the intent,
and hatred by itself may NOT give rise to intent. Whether hate (or
anger, lust, envy, greed, compassion, etc.) gives rise to the
requisite intent is (until recently) irrelevant. You can have hate
without intent and intent without hate. The two are not the same.
