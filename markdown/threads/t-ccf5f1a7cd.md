```
Those nice people at Microsoft have recently released a complete series of 
videos (24 of them) aimed at absolute beginners (although I went through a 
number of them myself and it did me no harm...), that explain step by step 
the concepts and details of programming in C# and using the Visual Studio 
IDE.

It's a new year. Maybe time to invest in some learning?

You couldn't get a better tutor than Bob Tabor and most of what I know about 
C# I learned from him. (I also took his advice and used other resources to 
expand and consolidate the fundamental concepts he teaches.) So far, in 
cash, I have spent around $35 for some second hand books on C#. The code I 
have written with it has generated considerably more than that :-) so my 
time (and money) was well spent.

People interested in developing for .Net (and other things in the future) 
using FREE software, no runtime licensing, and now with FREE tuition, should 
invest just under 5 minutes of their busy lives and check out:

http://channel9.msdn.com/Series/C-Sharp-Fundamentals-Development-for-Absolute-Beginners

Although learning stuff is not JUST about money, you are unlikley to get a 
better return on your investment than this.

The skills you can acquire here don't just look good on a CV, they are a 
positive asset to your personal growth experience. For myself, I found that 
setting aside what I already knew and approaching the subject as if I knew 
nothing, provided some very interesting insights into things I DID already 
know. (Although you can set what you know aside, you certainly don't forget 
it completely :-) Just don't evaluate the new information through a filter 
of the old.)

Instead of trying to measure new information against old information, 
measure it against nothing. Just spend some time and get your head around 
it. When you do this, you will see the old information in a series of new 
lights and the total experience is now broader and better. (In effect, 
learning new stuff can actually improve your perception of the old stuff, 
even if you are quite sure you have nothing to learn about it. (Learning 
Java and C# made me a better COBOL programmer...It also extended my 
"programming power" so I could tackle much more complex problems than I had 
previously...)

I know that most of you will let this opportunity slip away :-) but my 
conscience would have bothered me if I didn't at least give you an option 
:-)

Pete.

PS If anyone does actually do this course (and you can do it at your own 
pace in your own time) please let me know what you thought about it. I'm 
curious to see whether my own very positive experience is a general one.
```

```
Yes, I had trouble with that when I first started (and I couldn't understand 
what the Hell a "namespace" was or why you would need one :-))

The best thing with the braces (at least it works for me...) is to interpret 
"{" mentally as "start block", and "}" as "end block", then it kind of falls 
into place.

Also, I found fairly soon in the piece that I liked to have the braces NOT 
indented... so:

if (some condition)
{ <= "start block"
    do lots of stuff
} <= "end block

... rather than:

if (some condition)
    {
        do lots of stuff
}

...or...

if (some condition)
    {
        do lots of stuff
    }

Fortunately VS is clever enough to pick up your preferences and after I 
adjusted the braces a few times, it started placing them where I wanted 
them.

The use of braces and namespaces does become apparent after a little while 
and these days I don't think twice about them.

Just one tip...

C# allows you to make a single statement without it being in a block, but 
you CAN put it in a block if you want. This is a bit confusing because you 
may see things like:

if (some condition)
    do something...
else
{
    do lots of stuff
}

Although that is perfectly valid, so is this:

if (some condition)
{
    do something...
}
else
{
    do lots of stuff
}


The thing to remember is that if you have more than one statement in an 
action, it MUST be a block, otherwise it MAY be a block.

Like everything, once you're used to it, it seems perfectly natural.

Also, If you select a brace with the cursor, VS will grey the corresponding 
brace so you can check they match and the block(s) are consistent.
```

```
This is actually a Good Thing. Method signatures need parentheses and you 
will have seen how Intellisense gives you clues as soon as you open them 
when you are referencing an already defined method.

The brackets make sure there is no confusion between array elements and 
method signatures. (if they weren't there and you encountered:

accountBalance(11)

... you wouldn't know whether it was a method being passed a numeric 
parameter, or a bunch of balances referencing the 11th one.)

It might just be me, but I tend not to use arrays a lot, preferring various 
types of collection (which come with ready made Methods I don't have to 
write) instead. It is different from COBOL where the collections available 
are a bit limited and you still have to do quite a bit of work to use them.
I especially like the "stack" collection which automatically provides POP 
and PUSH methods. I thought I would have to write it myself but Intellisense 
told me I didn't. And it worked correctly straight out of the box... always 
surprising with things IT :-)

The way collections are implemented you usually get the best of both worlds; 
you can reference your collection using a key or an index in parentheses as 
a collection method, but you can also reference it as an array (by using a 
bracket).

Don't worry. Like most things as you practice it, it becomes more familiar 
and clearer.
```

```
defragmentation service.)

de-registering them and now I want a clean slate for the "final copy".
```

```
Are you saying a "database" cannot become fragmented?

Every RDBMS I have ever worked with goes to great pains to deal with 
fragmentation under the covers. Even balanced trees like Btrieve and VSAM 
which split storage areas to create free space automatically and avoid 
second level overflow on indexed datasets, still can become fragmented and 
need reloading occasionally. Access, SQL Server, Oracle, DB2, SQL Lite, 
MySQL and PostgreSQL ALL can experience "fragmentation", inasmuch as DB 
pages can become split and "misplaced". Most RDBMS implement "clustering" to 
overcome this but usually it is up to the DBA to decide what needs 
clustering and what doesn't. To be fair, is it usually well handled 
transparently but eventually a DB will need to be reloaded in order to 
improve performance.Talk to anyone who has worked as a DBA for a few years.

If you think the Windows registry is "a highly-optimized relational database 
" you have either been severely misinformed or you have never worked with 
physical Registry hives (I have...)

The Registry hives are five physical binary files with a particular 
structure (Page>Block>Cell/Bin) which are manipulated in memory almost as if 
they were system pages. Most of what you see in the Registry is created in 
memory at run time by the Configuration Manager element of the Windows 
kernel stitiching together various base keys held in the hives. It is not 
physically anything like the structure you see and it is not physically like 
a relational database either. It is a hierarchic key system and there are no 
relational constraints OTHER than Hierarchy implemented by it.

> It CAN have unused space or
> entries, but whether it has 1000 entries or 100,000 has no detectable
> effect on the efficiency of the overall system.

"Nope.", Huh? :-)

Having had a bit of experience with "highly optimized relational databases" 
over the last 30 years, since they were invented, (I have worked with all of 
the ones mentioned above plus a couple of others) I can assure you Registry 
hives and page files CAN become fragmented. Frequent insertion will cause it 
even though free space is automatically created as insertions occur. There 
are also times when the Registry needs to be searched. If Registry hive 
blocks get split across physical 4K pages or linked keys are in different 
blocks as a result of bin (free space) allocation, it can and does seriously 
degrade overall system performance.

But don't take my word for it...

Mark Russinovich is an acknowledged authority on Microsoft system internals 
and Technical Fellow at Microsoft (I use a number of his usually free system 
utilities).

Here's what HE said  on 10th July, 2005 on being asked if a bloated Registry 
could seriously degrade performance::

"No, even if the registry was massively bloated there would be little impact 
on the performance of anything other than exhaustive searches.

On Win2K Terminal Server systems, however, there is a limit on the total 
amount of Registry data that can be loaded and so large profile hives can 
limit the number of users that can be logged on simultaneously.

I haven't and never will implement a Registry cleaner since it's of little 
practical use on anything other than Win2K terminal servers and developing 
one that's both safe and effective requires a huge amount of 
application-specific knowledge."

He then spent some time investigating, thinking about it, and writing a 
registry defrag tool. In November 2006 he said:

"One of the limitations of the Windows NT/2000 defragmentation interface is 
that it is not possible to defragment files that are open for exclusive 
access. Thus, standard defragmentation programs can neither show you how 
fragmented your paging files or Registry hives are, nor defragment them.

Paging and Registry file fragmentation can be one of the leading causes of 
performance degradation related to file fragmentation in a system."

Please note the last sentence.

from those nice people at Microsoft...

"Processes that use the registry to repeatedly modify the same values in 
small increments can cause excessive memory fragmentation in the 
corresponding registry hive. The sum of the fragmented cells can become so 
large that the hive size winds up many times larger than the sum of actual 
data in the hive. Eventually, the sum of the hive will exceed the Registry 
Size Limit (RSL), causing subsequent modifications to fail until the RSL is 
increased. As an example consider the SAM hive used to store Windows NT 
accounts. The SAM size frequently exceeds projected sizes due to the fact 
that it fragments the SAM hive when adding members to large groups. "
```
