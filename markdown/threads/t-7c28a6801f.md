```
I am assisting a colleague of mine in trying to design a way of sorting a
whole bunch of records. We are talking over 400 billion... yes, you read
that correctly.... billion. This is part of a conversion effort from IMS to
DB2, so this only has to run one time within a three-hour window. A caveat
here is that most the records are different lengths, but are not variable as
COBOL defines variable (in other words, without a length descriptor at the
front of the record).

This is running on a MVS mainframe system, so we also have the benefit of
external sort power like DFSORT. Our initial thoughts here is to take the
sort completely out of COBOL and using DFSORT with the exits to handle the
different sizes and imbedding a standard sort key.

Sorry, I don't have a lot more details at the moment. We're designing this
right now over the next week, so really I'm just trying to get some ideas
here right now. Just what is the best way to sort a large number of records
of different sizes in a short amount of time?

Thanks in advance for your thoughts and ideas.
```

```
Yes, I would agree with this response, but probably using disk in this case.
Breaking up the task into smaller pieces is a conceivable option that we're
looking at. Over the last couple of hours, however, I've learned that this
job is a MERGE, not a sort, as we originally thought. (Sorry, folks, for the
misinformation.) That makes the job a bit easier, but we still have the
mechanics of running the job within the window. I believe we'll have the
option of splitting the task over several LPARs.

> Now space is cheap.  But that process can still be used - and you can make
the
> different record types match by adding sort headers.   How, exactly does
your
> variable record format work?

What we think we're going to do for variable handling is to use one of the
exits in DFSORT to compute the length of the logical record then append a
length attribute to the front so that SORT will handle this as just a
regular variable-length record. That exit right now will be doing a bunch
more than that (i.e. header/trailer summaries, defining a static key, etc.).
At the end of the merge processing we'll have another exit to trim off the
added excess and go from there.

You're probably wondering why 400 billion records? That is actually eight
months of historical data that this company generates. They generate two
billion transactions per day. It wouldn't be hard to figure out what kind of
client we're working with here with that kind of transaction volume. It's an
everyday company that you see everywhere.

Yes, IBM could do this job, and in fact we do a whole lot of work for them.
Like this one.
```

```
COBOL does the I/O and the sort product does the "sorting".  (This is a
```
