In case you haven't heard: http://www.sun.com/third-party/global/oracle/

I'd be interested to hear any comments or thoughts you may have about this.

Here are mine...

1. This is a really significant event for IT. The President of ORACLE thinks 
it is a better deal than Oracle acquiring Siebel, PeopleSoft and BEA. I 
think it will be good for Java, although it may not remain a free language 
much longer.

2. In the same way that MicroSoft pushes SQL Server with C# and .NET, ORACLE 
will be pushing ORACLE (DB) and Java (when they are not pushing their ERP 
offerings...). It is a powerful combination.

3. Most of the SAP systems I have worked with have used ORACLE (DB) as their 
RDB. Yet the two companies are bitter rivals when it comes to ERP systems. 
Could SAP be the next acquisition on ORACLE's list? Between the two of them, 
they have pretty much carved up the ERP package solutions market. It is very 
arguable who is winning or is likely to...

Assuming a clear leader emerges, what does that mean for the rest of us? Are 
corporates likely to continue software development when they can outsource 
the whole IT deal to an ERP package that does everything? (It is fair to 
assume that the resource backing of whichever company wins will be so huge 
that the end ERP product will be pretty close to definitive. Both companies 
are looking to the Web for the future: ORACLE with Fusion and SAP with 
NetWeaver. Early days yet, but both solutions look promising...)

4. What about COBOL? If history is anything to go by, ORACLE is not a "COBOL 
friendly" company. When they acquired PeopleSoft, which was originally 
written completely in COBOL, they moved to replace it as quickly (and 
economically) as possible. While there are still COBOL functions in 
PeopleSoft to this day, the preferred solution is to use "PeopleCode" for 
application development. This is more C-like and (of course) is object 
oriented.  It is unlikely that any of the offerings are going to use COBOL, 
especially as they now "own" Java...

The single most important thing about this, in my opinion, is that, within a 
given IT market, it is a bit like Highlander ("There can be only one..."). 
Anything that poses a market threat to the leader gets acquired, or forced 
out of business. (It is easier than competing, in the long run requires less 
resource, and provides better long term ROI... (ORACLE say that Sun will 
provide  a $1.5 billion revenue stream in the first year... more than that 
in the second year...ORACLE is currently worth around $23 billion (per 
year... largest ERP software company in the world), SAP around $14 billion 
per year...)... it would seem to be a no-brainer to take over SAP...

One fly in the ERP ointment is the effect of the yet-to-be-understood 
"Cloud". If major corporations go for SAAS and decide not to buy software 
solutions, just to rent services which can then be spooned onto their own 
corporate repositories (so there are no concerns about physical corporate 
data security) and distributed to wherever needed, by the local intranet or 
VPN, this could have a serious effect on both the ERP contenders.

One company that is an emerging player in this game is called NetSuite. They 
are currently looking to get into the big league of cloud computing and 
could present a tangible threat to both ORACLE and SAP if the SAAS model 
really catches on.

Guess who owns it? (52%) [OK, I'll save you looking it up...Larry Ellison 
(and family), the CEO of ORACLE.] :-)

It makes me wonder what is likely to happen in the COBOL arena... How long 
will vendors continue scratching to sell expensive compilers and 
maintenance, and who will be the ONE? As the user base evaporates, 
competition becomes fiercer. Open COBOL is free and is looking like a very 
viable alternative. (There are good reports from a company in Texas that is 
now running their production systems in Open COBOL, having migrated their 
previous COBOL without too much difficulty.)

It seems to me that Micro Focus and IBM have little choice really; neither 
of them can drop their existing COBOL user base, so they have to provide an 
alternative pathway to the future. (Although MF are talking up continuing 
COBOL viability, the words ring hollow, and the world has largely voted with 
its feet. Cancelling the show in Dallas hasn't helped credibility, even 
though the online idea is a good one. Like IBM, they are trapped in a 
situation where they realise that customers want to be net based, but they 
can't quite bring themselves to tell customers that once they ARE net based, 
COBOL is no longer a good option. Many customers will use the available 
tools to "migrate" to the network and then find that the old approaches are 
just not workable. Yes, their systems are now running in networked COBOL, 
but they fart like dogs and require new technology to stop doing so... 
Objects and layers are foreign concepts to most procedural COBOL 
programmers. No attempt that I can see is being made to educate or retrain 
anybody. It's OK, we just recompile for the new platform and everything will 
be fine...

(I see a glimmer of hope as new, younger, programmers who have some formal 
training and exposure to other languages arrive; they are unlikely to settle 
for procedural code on the network.)

Fujitsu (Japan) seem to have sold off their COBOL interest (or maybe they 
just franchised it), at least as far as outside-Japan is concerned... (Not 
sure what is happening with Fujitsu-owned Siemens... procedural COBOL seems 
to be alive and well in Germany :-))

On the basis of what I see and hear, it seems to me that the next few years 
will generally not be a good time to be dependent on COBOL or locked in to a 
COBOL vendor.

The uncertainty surrounding it poses a level of risk that many will find too 
high.

Pete.
