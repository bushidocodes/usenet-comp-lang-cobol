(The following is controversial and arguable but is based on informed
opinion and a large amount of knowledge and experience. I'm not going to
preface everything with IMHO because what I say is OBVIOUSLY my opinion, and
I see no reason to apologise for it or be humble about it. I can support
every one of the statements below with reasoned argument and will gladly do
so if you want more information...)

Advice to the young....(remembering what happened to Polonius...)

COBOL has as bright a future as any other procedural language, although the
"glory days" (70s and early 80s until PCs became established...) are gone.

It will take you about 2 weeks to learn COBOL (although Thane, another
regular contributor to this NG, has written a book which lets you "fast
Track" it in 24 hours) and about 3 months before you become proficient. The
language is simple and elegant and ideally suited for the purpose for which
it was intended. (Regrettably it is being "enhanced" to the point where much
of the original simplicity is being lost, but don't let that put you off.)

For an investment of 3 months, you can then use it to make a living for the
next 10 to 15 years. After that, procedural languages (not just COBOL) will
be as relevant as Latin is today.

I would strongly recommend that you learn COBOL, enjoy using it, get a
background in computer programming as a basis for the skill set of tomorrow,
and then learn JAVA and HTML/XML as Web Enablement is the direction for the
future and OO (object orientation) is the methodology which will take us
there. (Some people would argue that knowing COBOL is a hindrance to
understanding OO, but I don't ascribe to that. Learning COBOL is a great
introduction to computers in general and besides, its real good fun...)

Fortunately, by the time Commerce stops using COBOL, you will have obtained
the necessary background and skills to continue to make a living whether it
involves actual programming or not.

There are still many companies in the UK who need mainframe COBOL
programmers. If you are prepared to work in London you need never go hungry.
I would recommend getting a couple of years under your belt then going
freelance.

 (I have worked as a freelance  (if you count running a small software
house/consultancy as also being "freelance"...??) in the UK and Europe for
the last 25 years and was unemployed (when I didn't want to be...) for a
total of 6 weeks in that time. This kind of life requires mobility and a
high tolerance on unexpectedness, but it is great fun and, looking back, I
wouldn't change it.)

I notice that elsewhere in this thread you were advised to learn DB2 (SQL,
actually, or "Structured Query Language" for Relational Databases. This is
excellent and VERY SOUND advice. Take it.

Good luck, and remember you can always call on the COBOL community if you
need help.

Pete.

the journeymen who could set the jigs, and the apprentices who could run
the lathes.   You didn't want to reset things very often, making as many
identical parts as possible.

Nowadays, the "jigs" are electronic.  They program the final shape to
tighter tolerance, maybe make one run on a wax dummy, and create the
part.  They make money with much smaller runs and can make parts which
couldn't have been made in the past.

kind product, everything.  But he uses CAD-CAM software and encyclopedic
knowledge (on line) of many more materials than he used before.  He
probably has interesting ways to test the strength of his products
before and after they have been made.  At any rate, what he learned 30
years ago is considerably different than what he does now.  The big shop
used to have lots of belts on the ceiling, while the home shop just had
one lathe.  Now the professional lathe is more expensive (including
cad-cam), but one can make a living with just one, out of his garage...
