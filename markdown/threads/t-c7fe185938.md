Two big urban myths I have heard :-

Large chain store some years ago. Tins of corned beef with sell by date of
00 (ie 2000). Warehouse rejected the entire batch thinking it 90+ years
old.

The salient point for Year 2000 is that each individual case seems trivial

There has already been one instance reported in the Washington Post about
how Y2K will affect the food industry. There was a US firm who used their
computer system to track the length of time certain stocks of food had
been kept in their warehouse. Written in COBOL, the program projected
forward the number of years of "shelf life" for products entered into the
database, then used that projection to determine if the product was to be
kept or discarded as too old. The projection figure for one lucrative
product of theirs was a 4-year shelf life. Early this year the program
began spitting out instructions to destroy entire blocks of this product
as having become too old when, in fact, it had only been put into the
warehouse a few weeks earlier! The problem was that projection figure
came up with "00", indicating to the program that this item had been on
the shelf since the year 1900, thus it was FAR beyond its shelf life and
must be thrown away. The firm lost more than 2 million dollars worth of
good product before the error was discovered and corrected. Don't know
the name of the firm, but the WashPost should still have the data.

At the moment I'm rewriting a field system in Visual BASIC, from the original
S/36 RPG II. The way the application works, on a weekly basis, data is shipped
to the mainframe, in several files, for further processing and consolidation.
One of the main problems identified was the fact that the mainframe expects to
received numeric data in packed format (COMP-3). Since VB is unable to pack
numbers easily, a decission was made to ship data as pure ASCII entries in .txt
files, with numbers in zone decimal format. This means that the record lengths
sent will be longer (e.g., 280 bytes as opposed to 256 bytes). At the receiving
end, a COBOL program will reprocess the files sent, producing sequential files
with records containing packed numerics. The dates being sent are in format
YYMMDD, so I suggested that, since we're going to have to reprocess the files
anyway, maybe we should ship dates in format YYYYMMDD. The receiving program
can snip off the leading YY, until someone finally gets around to solving the
Year2000 thing at the mainframe end. This way, at the PC end at any rate, we
won't have to worry about that problem. Sounds like a pretty simple solution,
right? Guess again. Suggestion was nixed, because, the mainframe programmers
are too busy right now maintaining the existing programs, and really couldn't
be bothered to go changing the receiving program. Final decission? I'm gonna be
shipping dates from the PC in format YYMMDD... and it's now almost 1997!!
Whenever the mainframe people get around to solving the problem at their end, I
can go back and take care of it at the PC end.

Point of this little dissertation? The Year2000 thing isn't really a COBOL (or
RPG) problem at all. It's a management-has-its-head-up-its-ass problem. Unless
you'd like to call the situation I'm creating at the moment a Visual BASIC
problem.

There has already been one instance reported in the Washington Post about
how Y2K will affect the food industry. There was a US firm who used their
computer system to track the length of time certain stocks of food had
been kept in their warehouse. Written in COBOL, the program projected
forward the number of years of "shelf life" for products entered into the
database, then used that projection to determine if the product was to be
kept or discarded as too old. The projection figure for one lucrative
product of theirs was a 4-year shelf life. Early this year the program
began spitting out instructions to destroy entire blocks of this product
as having become too old when, in fact, it had only been put into the
warehouse a few weeks earlier! The problem was that projection figure
came up with "00", indicating to the program that this item had been on
the shelf since the year 1900, thus it was FAR beyond its shelf life and
must be thrown away. The firm lost more than 2 million dollars worth of
good product before the error was discovered and corrected. Don't know
the name of the firm, but the WashPost should still have the data.
