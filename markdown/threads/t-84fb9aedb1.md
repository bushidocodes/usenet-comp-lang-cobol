```
The newsgroup comp.risks carried a story saying 
"Over half of software bugs are currently not found until downstream
in the development process leading to significant economic costs." 

I don't understand this.  I would have thought that "a software bug" 
is a problem that is left undiscovered, by definition.  If "upstream"
includes normal coding and testing, then thousands of problems
are encountered and solved during development.  Only a tiny 
handful of problems are unwittingly turned over into production; 
when they reveal themselves in the field, they are properly
labelled "bugs."  
The quotation sounds a little like saying "over half of an island
is surrounded by water." 

Ross
http://community.webshots.com/user/ross_klatte
```

```
My point, trivial as it was, is that defects in general are for 
the most part encountered / detected and corrected during the 
very earliest stages of development, not after the software has
been released to the public.   
On my first compile, I might very easily hit 500 syntax errors.  
Each of these constitutes a "defect in general."  By the time I
submit a program for integration testing, it is very likely that 
several thousand "defects in general" have been identified
and corrected.  The article and your endorsement thereof
do not pay the slightest attention to that fact.  

I heartily agree that, after a program has passed primivitive
testing and unit testing and integration (system) testing, and 
no error has been detected, and if it then goes into production
and promptly blows up, then the cost of correcting that particular
production error can be very high.  So what?  Neither analysts 
nor programmers make mistakes on purpose.  If they did, it
would be sabotage, not a mistake.  Programmers and 
designers tend to place their confidence in their own infallibility.  
Managers tend to place their confidence in forms that have been 
properly filled out.  Nevertheless, a program that goes into 
production is usually bug-free, as far as it goes.  Some very
useful programs, such as Windows or the Internet, go into 
production with known defects.  Should the Internet have 
been withheld because of its obvious built-in security weaknesses? 
Of course not.  

So some bozos have published an erudite paper which concludes
that "errors are more cheaply corrected sooner rather than later"?  
And another bozo has affirmed the validity of their conclusions?  
These authors, and you, Rick Smith, are just demonstrating
```
