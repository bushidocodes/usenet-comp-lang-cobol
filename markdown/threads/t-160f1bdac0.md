Working on Mainframe COBOL environments for as long as I have, I expect
jobs to last (with maintenance) for some time.

But in our new integrated environment, I keep finding people using
applications on MS-DOS, Windows 3.1, dBASE III, etc, which are necessary

of being fixed.

towards an age of disposable IS?  We throw away TVs instead of repairing

them.

There are times when it is best to throw away applications
without replacement, times to replace, and times to repair.
This has always been true; but it is becoming easier to
replace the applications with new technology than to repair
the old technology. Furthermore, the new technology may
provide additional features that may be used to enhance
the application.

There are three broad classes of PC-based business
applications: specialized, customized, and personalized.

A specialized application may have been new and very
cost effective for the business; but the original supplier
may have left the business leaving the application area
to others. Replacing the application makes sense.
Alternatively, the application may no longer be cost
effective. Disposal might be more appropriate.

A customized application may have been innovative for
its time; but now a few suppliers may have created
specialized applications that satisfy the general
requirements for the system. Replacement with the
specialized application should be considered.

The technology needed to create a system with
commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) software may not have
been available; so a customized system was built. The
technology is now available in COTS. Replacement
should be considered.

A personalized application may have been developed
by a department mangager so that she might better
manage the department. After she is promoted, the
new manager may be unable to use the application
because his view of the business is different from hers.
Thowing away the old system should be considered.
The new manager may then decide what, if any, new
systems should be put into place.

What makes replacement better than repair for some
PC-based applications is cost. Where appropriate, it is
far less expensive to use COTS technology on PCs than
to write and maintain custom software on any computer.
This also means that, in some cases, it is more cost
effective to do complete replacement with new technology
than to repair old technology, on PCs. Furthermore, the
relatively small investment in an existing PC-based system
has less effect on the decision to dispose of a system.
It is easier to throw out a $2000 system than a $2,000,000
system; even though disposal may be the best choice for
both.

Perhaps you could clarify your statement about OO, I don't
see the connection to disposable IS.
------------------
Rick Smith
