Facebook, MySpace, and, indeed, most of the "social networks" are perfect 
examples of a good idea being subverted by a small minority. By far the 
majority of people on Facebook are there for social reasons and they enjoy 
the service. But they simply don't understand the risks involved. (You won't 
find me on Facebook,although I get around a dozen invitations a week from 
people who want to be my "friend"... Fortunately, I know who my friends are 
and don't need reinforcement from a computer network :-))

There is a cynical abuse of these networks for marketing  (relatively 
harmless, but annoying) and other, more sinister activities. Best avoided.

(I should add that I DO subscribe to ONE social network: LinkedIn.  This is 
a professional one and I only joined it because various people who had 
worked with me wanted me to recommend them. I keep a very low profile on it, 
have been on it for over 6 years now and have not experienced any unwanted 
side effects from it. It is extremely unlikely you will ever see me in 
MySpace or Facebook, however...)

I suspect that, in your case, Jimmy, because you are generally chatty in 
your mails, the man has wanted to get to know you better and thought 
Facebook would be the way to do that.

Probably completely innocent with no other motive than being social. Most 
people who are on Facebook would react that way.

It shows the subtle shift that has occurred in our attitudes and that has 
been catalyzed by the age of instant communication.

How many people 40 years ago would think it perfectly usual and normal to 
show their holiday snaps to a total stranger? (Or imagine for one moment 
that a stranger would be interested?)

Whether this is "good" or "bad" is a subjective opinion and, for me at 
least, the jury is still out...:-) However, if it was possible to return to 
the days before the first communications satellite, I'd definitely vote 
against it.

It always amazes me that people think their corporate emails are private and 
confidential. They think their personal messages are secure and that posts 
(such as this one) will only be perused by the people they are intended for. 
They think that once something is deleted it is gone (it isn't, 
necessarily...). They think that SPAM filtration protects their systems, 
that AntiVirus programs are impenetrable, that when they browse the Internet 
no-one notices or tracks where they go, that forwarding funny pictures, that 
have been virus checked, to friends is safe... the list goes on.

To be fair, you can't really blame them. People who don't understand how the 
technology works (or can be made to work by interested parties), just want 
to use it, they wouldn't perceive the risks.

There is an argument that says some of these "intrusions" by Government 
Agencies help to keep us safe and none of us can know how many potential 
Terrorist activities have been thwarted by them. Then the democrats 
(deliberate use of small "d") will question why they can't be "up front" 
about it, and the response is that if they were "up front" about it that 
would defeat the purpose and would render their efforts useless.

There are some people who just like to snoop; there are others who don't. 
(There are some people who are paranoid and see conspiracy around every 
corner (that's why Doc has his tinfoil hat...) and there are others who are 
naive and trusting to the point of foolishness.)

If my neighbour goes away from home they leave a key at my place (and I do 
the same with them). I don't feel compelled to go and look around their 
house., and I'm pretty certain they don't want to rummage around mine. I had 
occasion to enter their house recently while they were away because a window 
had blown wide open and there was torrential rain coming down. I entered the 
house, fixed the window, and left within 30 seconds. I told them I had been 
in when they returned, and saw they were not in the least bit concerned.

When it comes to social networks we should be mindful of  the sage advice of 
Eubulus, the secretary to King Gorboduc:

"Good is, I grant, of all to hope the best,
  But not to live still dreadless of the worst,"

via DHCP or statically it is still possible to identify where the machine 
is.

"illegal network programming". If you can persuade a machine to divulge its 
MAC address you can uniquely identify it. (I use this for my wireless home 
network and it means that ONLY machines known and authorized can use it.

instantly connected to the internet as soon as they power up, without 
needing to login. I remind them that they have visited before with the same 
machine and my network remembers them.)

Given you now have a unique machine, connected to a node from an ISP who 
also has that machine's MAC address on his server, it is certainly possible 
to locate roughly where the machine is. (In fact, the ISP almost certainly 
has the house address...). If it is connected over a cell phone network it 
can be located to the nearest cell phone node tower with or without the 
ISP's co-operation... If it is a mobile device with a GPS card you could 
theoretically locate it to within a metre...

For those of us innocently going about our business, none of this is a
