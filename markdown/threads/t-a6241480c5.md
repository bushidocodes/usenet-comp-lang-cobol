shops may be forced to abandon COBOL because of a lack of programmers, to be 
ludicrous. It simply reinforces my belief that some of the IT management 
around just doesn't get it.

They will be forced to abandon COBOL because the time for COBOL is nearly 
over, the paradigm has changed, and because they didn't have the brains or 
balls to plan for it years ago when it would have been much less traumatic.

I already responded in this thread with my thoughts about this and, by a 
strange coincidence, I have been commisioned to write an article on the 
subject for a very lively developer website (mainly populated by Java guys).

Some of the phrases in my responses here will probably find their way into 
the finished article. :-)

When you are being paid to write something (no matter how reasonable the 
sponsor may be) it is always at the back of your mind that you don't want to 
be too hard on them... :-) I will say what I believe (I always do...)  but 
I'll try to keep it fair and reasonable. (Instead of my usual unfair and 
unreasonable as I can do here with impunity :-))

The important thing here is not about what language you move to (some people 
simply buy a package), it is about ensuring that FUNCTIONALITY  (and that 
includes functionality buried deep in legacy COBOL systems) is made 
available to the business. Modern languages encapsulate functionality (with 
reusable Classes and object instances of those Classes) better than COBOL 
does; that is really the main reason for its decline. Modern COBOL can do it 
too, but it is harder to write and most COBOL people take time to make the 
necessary mental adjustments. The ones that do move to OO COBOL use it for a 
while, then realise the other languages are much easier to write and can do 
more, so they just move on.

It was interesting to see the comments in the article about why people 
stopped using COBOL.

Give it three years... :-) (2015...)

members to have hand guns, provided certain very stringent requirements are 
met. Cops don't carry guns and have only recently been issued tasers. Due to 
an alarming increase in armed offending cops now have access to guns but 
don't carry sidearms. At the time I'm writing about (the late 1960s) 
American television had not worked its magic on the fabric of our society 
and cops didn't actually need guns...)

So people weren't exactly flocking to get sent off to a hot steamy jungle 
where your life was likely to be in danger.

Conscription had to be in place but it was only tolerated because we had 
commitments. The system worked as follows:

1. 19 year old males registered for military service.
2.  When they turned 20, their birthday was put into a barrel
3. There was a Lotto style draw of a set number of brthdays. If yours was 
drawn you would become a soldier. (At least for a while; your employer was 
required by Law to keep your job for you. Mine didn't and I could have sued, 
but it was easier to just get anoither job...)

As I didn't want to go to Viet Nam (on the grounds of having nothing against 
the Vietnamese, allied with a genetic predisposition to not looking for 
trouble) I didn't think it was fair I should have two chances in the raffle, 
when everybody else only had one.

I spoke out about this and the Registration Staff Sergeant (who I later 
found to be a very fair-minded man...) said: "OK, pick one."

I picked the 20th March (on the simple grounds that it was first in the 
series).

The 21st of March was not drawn; the 20th of March was.

The Company I was working for had it deferred on the grounds that I was "key 
personnel" but it could not be deferred indefinitely and I eventually had to 
do it.

Once I got into it, I actually quite enjoyed it even though I never got to 
shoot an any people in black pyjamas.

NZ, being a meritocracy, they tested all 700 odd of us for personality and 
IQ (in some cases they found little of either) and sent the top 5% for 
Officer training.  I was in the Signal Corp (they reckoned a computer 
programmer should be able to lay wire on a battlefield under intense fire) 
and, as an officer, I was allowed to use a sten gun. (A very primitive, 
inaccurate, weapon by today's standards; it cost two shillings and sixpence 
to make, was an unrifled tube with a magazine and trigger attached and 
worked on the optimistic principle that accuracy was unimportant as long as 
you fired lots of rounds... BUT, it was an automatic weapon and that was SO 
COOL!)

They told me that if I was prepared to join up permanently, they would 
ratify my acting commission and I could be a career soldier. I told them I 
wasn't into acting, couldn't swear an Oath of Allegiance to Queen Elizabeth 
the Second and her heirs and successors (sorry Ma'am, it isn't personal...), 
and received all the aggravation I needed in my life from my wife, who was 
very upset about me being in the Army, and probably wouldn't go for it as a 
career choice.

I still have a faded photograph of myself in uniform, along with the boys of 
7 platoon... Hey! wasn't that Custer's outfit?

Ah, happy times... :-)

However, there maybe some validity to the story I told.  In May 1964,
Major General Norman B. Edwards, Chief, US Joint Military Advisory
Group, Korea, began preliminary planning to send a Korean Mobile Army
Surgical Hospital to Vietnam. The Korean Ministry of Defense (MOD) and
the United Nations Command (UNC) agreed. A survey team of six Korean
and five US officers left the ROK in August 1964 for Vietnam. In
September an agreement was reached for the Koreans to send and operate
a Mobile Army Surgical Hospital (MASH) unit in the RVN. The deployment
of the ROK Army (ROKA) 1st MASH began in September 1964, first
activated at Vung Tau.

The ROK Military Assistance Group, Vietnam (ROK-MAG-V), and what came
to be known as the ï¿½Peace Dove Unitï¿½ were set up between February and
June 1965. It would consist of 2,416 men and women from a Korean
construction support group ("Pigeon Unit"), a Korean Marine Corps
engineer company, Korean Navy Tank Landing Ships (LSTs) and Landing
Ships Mechanized (LSMs), and a Korean Army security company. This
would grow in short order to an Army engineer battalion; an Army
transport company; a Marine engineer company; one LST with crew; a
security battalion; a service unit; a liaison group, and a mobile
hospital (already in Vietnam). An aerial support group was set up
later.

ROK-MAG(V) was headquartered at Bien Hoa, near Saigon. The "Peace
Doves" built many school buildings, freeways, roads, and bridges for
the Vietnamese in the RVN.

The ROK then sent three combat divisions to the RVN over a 12 month
period starting in September 1965. 

The ROK sent the Capital "Tiger Division" to Qui Nhon, and the 2nd
Marine "Blue Dragon" Brigade to Cam Ranh Bay and then to Tuy Hoa to
get things started in 1965. Then, starting in April 1966, they sent in
the 9th ROK "White Horse" Division. Once done, the Marine Brigade
could move into the northernmost I Corps to join with the US III
Marine Amphibious Force (III MAF). In turn, the Koreans created a
Korean Army Corps that extended from Binh Dinh Province south to Ninh
Thuan Province along the RVN's coastline.

The Blue Dragon Marine Brigade was positioned at Cam Ran Bay, in
southeastern Khan Hoa Province, then moved to Tuy Hoa, then moved to I
Corps.  

Cam Ran Bay was in III Corps along with Vung Tau.
