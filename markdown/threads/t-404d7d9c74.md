Bill Klein wrote:
> Doc,
>  What updard/downward compatibility issue do you think there would be
> if IBM created a *NEW* Usage - that conformed to the new ISO COBOL
> Standard (i.e. USAGE BINARY-FLOAT-xxx) and that conformed to the IEEE

The trouble is that the "new ISO COBOL standard" has no more credibility 
than the old one did. It is all very well for some interested parties to sit 
down and create new useful extensions, but it is like designing a hip 
replacement for someone in the last stages of  lung cancer.

The reality is that the "new ISO COBOL standard" is unlikely to be 
implemented by anybody, let alone IBM. (Don't get me wrong; I'd like to be 
wrong about this, but, on the observable evidence, I don't think I am.)

We discussed in this very forum, more than 12 years ago, that whoever was 
responsible for the COBOL standard needed to get their act together and find 
a fast track through the interminable reviews, red tape and committee 
meetings. And engagement was needed with the user community. What happened? 
Nothing. People who DID take the trouble to make submissions were simply 
ignored. (Ask Jimmy Gavan about that...) The committee was largely composed 
of people who could get their current company to sponsor them for the 
boondoggles that passed off as standards committee meetings, or could 
persuade their current employers that it might be good to have a finger in 
the COBOL pie.

That argument no longer washes with their employers and most of the major 
players withdrew.

The very few people who worked tirelessly to improve COBOL, not because they 
wanted to improve their own CVs, but because they really cared (and I 
include you in that category, Bill) were villified for pointing out problems 
with the processes and procedures, in the hopes of improving them.

It is no surprise to anyone reviewing the history of the COBOL standard that 
nothing significant has happened since 1985, quarter of a century ago.

It took 17 years to produce the 2002 standard and nobody has fully 
implemented it. (I don't blame them, personally...)

The people I deal with every day who are looking to move off COBOL are using 
mainly 1985 standard, but occasionally 1974. They are NOT looking or 
pressing for COBOL 2002. (In fact on the few occasions when I have mentioned 
this, the response has been bewilderment...didn't even know there WAS a 2002 
standard. Shows how engaged the standards committees have been with the user 
base...)

We live in an age when communication is global and instant. Did we have a 
COBOL standards web site (with FAQs) or blog where people could contribute 
or discuss and argue finer points? No. Why not?

Technology too "cutting edge"? For people holding the fate of a computer 
programming language in their hands?

No money to build such a site? Funny, there was no problem with organizing 
meetings in Las Vegas and Florida...

No. The fact is that the people on the committee didn't WANT to have to 
answer to anybody and decided they could do this without outside 
"interference".

It has been a fiasco throughout.

I'm still heartsick over it, but there is no point in dwelling on it.


>
> I certainly wouldn't want IBM to change the meaning of COMP-1 and
> COMP-2, but I don't see why they cant/shouldn't create a new USAGE
> allowing easier communication with other IBM (z/OS) languages.  OTOH,
> they have indicated that they never will.

Has anybody stopped and thought WHY they never will?

Intransigence? Lack of resources?

No, I don't think so.

Could it be that they have dispassionately evaluated the future role of 
COBOL in the modern world  and decided that it isn't worth pursuing?

Their future is in Java and other OO languages. They are not stupid. COBOL 
is provided only as a service to existing legacy and the ever-diminishing 
base of old timers. It will be phased out when they can safely do that, so 
why waste further investment in it?

The fact is that they don't WANT COBOL interacting with modern languages 
because they are two different paradigms and the result simply degrades both 
sides.

People who DO want to reuse existing legacy in a modern environment need to 
refactor it into objects.

(http://primacomputing.co.nz)

Recompiling it for platforms like .NET is a very poor solution long term (in 
my opinion; I agree that is arguable...)

But many are content to simply rebuild systems using the new languages in 
the new environments, or replace them with a package.

As more and more people are findng the increased productivity they get when 
they move away from COBOL, allied with the fact that encapsulated Object 
Oriented systems cost much less to maintain, the world is voting with its 
feet.

Vendors like IBM are not ignorant of this.

Pete.
