Well, HeyBub, where's I'm from it's Pretty Women and _Basketball_

Which reminds me of a story which illustrates what we are talking
about...

Forty years ago I found myself attending Rose Polytechnic (now Rose
Hulman Institute of Technology, or RHIT) in the backwaters of Terre
Haute, IN. Was then a very, very good, even _excellent_, undergraduate
science and engineering school, and still is, right up there just a
tad below MIT. (Remember here we are talking undergraduate program
only.) Really, all in all, a somewhat surprising institution to find
in our modest state of Indiana.

It was the first quarter of my college experience. I studied hard, was
doing well (in fact, I finished the quarter _very_ well), and my
fellow students were certainly plenty smart, interesting, and the
appropriate measure of rowdy for a rather nerdy bunch. I had
significant scholarship assistance, so the money issue was OK. And
yet...

Thing is, back then it was exclusively male. (They later dropped that,
it is now coed, but still only nominally so, with probably 80-90% of
the students still male.) And it was small (only about 800-1000
students total), and the only majors were the sciences, math, and
engineering majors.

I can't remember the exact date, it must have been some 1967 mid-
autumn afternoon, but I can remember the experience vividly. I was
taking a walk around the rather pretty campus, after lunch, and I had
a few saltine crackers, and I crunched them up, threw them into one of
the two ponds which graced the campus, and watched the fish come up
and gobble them. "This is _fun_!" I thought to myself.

Then I took a step back, mentally, and probably physically, and
realized with a small inner voice "You are at a place where your idea
of fun is to feed crackers to the fishes."

I was in an excellent place, but it was a very, very _narrow_ place.

The very next semester I was enrolled at Indiana University -
Bloomington (IU). Just in time for IU to play (and lose) in the Rose
Bowl (they have not returned since, and they offer us little hope of
returning again in my lifetime.) But that is not the end of the story.
For this is, after all, Indiana, and not Texas.

By 1972 I had met there the woman who I was to marry. About the same
time Robert Montgomery Knight had arrived. And by 1975, by the time I
got my Masters degree, IU had won the NCAA. There was much dancing in
the street that night, and back then, not as much mischief as we find
today at championship schools on win night.

Transferring from an _excellent_ school to a _good_ school with
basketball and women was, in my mind to this day, one of the best
decisions I have ever made. :-)

And to return to the nominal CoBOL thread, lest we have to change the
subject line prematurely, I believe, by analogy, that same kind of
attitude has served me well in my career. Rate/salary is a component,
but by no means the only component, in career satisfaction.

yours,
