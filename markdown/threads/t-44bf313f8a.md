In the evening of the 14-th of December 1900
Max Planck held a lecture before the
"Deutsche Physikalische Gesellschaft" in
which he explained his mathematical treatment
of black-body-radiation. In order to fit
the physical reality to his formulas, he was
forced to assume that light comes in quantas.

He himself was very reluctant to accept this.
Today we know that this was the first outbreak
of the framework of classical physics, which
was, at that time, believed to be a complete
description of nature.

Without quantum mechanics, it is hard to see
how one would ever develope semiconductor
devices. Computers, if there were any, would be
built with vacuum tubes. So there might be a
slim chance that COBOL would exist in a
society which does not know about QM - but the
internet certainly would not be there. No Java,
no Microsoft, no XML, no what have you.

Also, without QM, there were no nuclear weapons,
so history would have taken an entirely different
course since WWII.

Of course, as Howard Brazee has mentioned in the
other posting correctly, the time was due for this
discovery, and probably within short time,
somebody else would have started it all.

I believe you are referring to Max Planck's discovery of the Quantum,
encapsulated in his formula: E = hv

Where E is the energy being radiated, v is the frequency of the radiation,
and h is Planck's constant (6.6 x 10 -34 joule seconds)

(Basically, what this means is that the higher the frequency, the more
energy is contained in the Radiation. However, it is not infinite because
the "oscillators" in the atom which produce the radiation are limited to
specific intervals. (No one knows why, even today...) This led to the
discovery of the structure of electron shells and the release of packets of
energy ("quanta" ) which became the basis of Quantum Physics.

Planck arrived at his conclusions by a happy accident (he got the maths
"wrong" (more correctly, he did not complete the integration but stopped at
the answer he wanted)), but obtained the right answer. Einstein later went
on to use this work in the paper for which he received his Nobel prize,
concerning the effect of light knocking electrons out of metal. (NOT
Relativity...as many people think).

Planck's work solved the mystery of "Blackbody radiation" and the
"Ultraviolet catastrophe" and completely revolutionised classical physics,
opening the door to Quantum Physics, Quantum chromodynamics, and the
Uncertainty Principle, without which none of the electronic devices
(including computers) which we take for granted today, could function.

Some people (myself included) rate this discovery as one of the most
important scientific breakthroughs in the history of the Human Race,
comparable to the work of Galileo and Newton, and providing the building
blocks for Einstein, Heisenberg and the Nuclear age.

Pete.
