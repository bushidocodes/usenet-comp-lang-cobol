I may have spoken too soon... In the last few days, The Bay of Plenty has 
seen a number of earthquakes. As they were mainly sited off-shore and in the 
morning, I slept through them :-) however there was one today (Wednesday 
29th December) that was in the afternoon. I was away visiting friends up in 
the hills around Paeroa (about 50 miles away) so I never felt it, but some 
friends had felt it and mentioned it when I got home.

Here is a local newsclip:

<quote>
A second earthquake with a Richter magnitude of 3.3 was felt in parts of the 
Bay of Plenty Wednesday afternoon.

The quake, recorded by GNS Science, struck 10km north of Matata at a depth 
of 5km at 2.14pm, and could be felt in Whakatane.

"It is usual to have small swarms like this, it doesn't happen all the time, 
but its part of living in active New Zealand," says GNS Science duty 
seismologist Caroline Ashenden.

"There's no real trend to them. The earthquakes this week have been small 
and they could go on for a while or they could be over now, you can't pick 
it," says Caroline.

More than 2000 earthquakes have been reported by GNS Science in 2010 in the 
Bay of Plenty region, which includes Tauranga to the west, Opotiki to the 
east, south to Rotorua and north to the sea, including White Island.

A 4.4 magnitude earthquake was recorded at 8.57am Wednesday 10km north of 
Matata, at a depth of 7km, and a 5.4 magnitude earthquake was recorded 30km 
north-east of Tauranga on Monday, December 27 at 3am.
</quote>

As you can see, we are faily inured to them and, for the most part, there is 
no damage or harm to people or property. However,  the fact that there was a 
5.4 less than 20 miles from where I live is a bit disconcerting.

Still, nothing we can do about it, and I don't plan on emigrating :-)

It is part of the price we pay for living on a volcanic plateau. White 
island, which is mentioned in the news item above, is one of the weirdest 
places I have ever been.  I went out by helicopter and it affords a unique 
opportunity for photography of the crater of the active volcano. We landed 
in the crater, with breathing apparatus (the sulphur is pungent) and it was 
just astounding. Boiling mud, a red hot stream of fizzing water, beautiful 
configurations of discoloured clays and pure sulphur crystals sparkling in 
the sun, and clouds of ash puffing skywards from rock fumaroles that would 
take the skin off your hands if you touched them. Only about a quarter of 
the crater floor is "active" at present so there's a fairly large area to 
walk around on, but there are carefully marked paths and nobody would be 
inclined to ignore them. They tried a commercial sulphur mining operation in 
the 1930s and set up a plant and huts for the workmen. In the middle of the 
night there was an eruption of boiling mud and a lahar swept 11 men to a 
pretty horrible death. (Some of the bodies were never recovered being 
literally dissolved in the boiling flood). No attempt has been made to mine 
it since and today it is strictly for tourists, who spend about 90 minutes 
and visits are subject to a resident geologist in Whakatane giving the OK. 
There is a place where the molten sulphur runs as a stream into the Pacific 
Ocean and it is really spectacular. From the air you can see the yellow 
turning to green amidst huge of steam as the hot sulphur hits the sea.. The 
15 minute helicopter flight is about two and a half hours by boat, but the 
trip is good. We were fortunate enough to see a pod of dolphin frolicing in 
the Bay and apparently Orcas are fairly frequent.

http://www.vulcanheli.co.nz/

If you look at the "News" tab on this web site you will see that alerts 
regarding the island, quite often refer to associated earthquakes nearby. 
Some people believe that White island is like a pressure cooker valve and 
releases pressure from the plateau; if it wasn't there, a large part of the 
North Island would probably blow itself off the map. Lake Taupo, in the 
middle of the North Island was formed by a single volcanic explosion around 
25,000 years ago (not SO long in Geological terms...). The lake has an area 
of  238 square miles (616 sq. KM). There were subsequent sporadic eruptions 
until "the BIG one" in around 100 BC. This was one of the most violent 
eruptions on Planet Earth in the last 5,000 years.  The eruption column was 
twice as high as the eruption column from Mount St. Helens in 1980, and the 
ash turned the sky red over Rome and China. The eruption devastated much of 
the North Island and further expanded the lake.

It may be shaky, but it's "home" to me :-)

Pete.
