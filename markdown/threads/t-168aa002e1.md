I've greatly enjoyed reading this thread.  As a neophyte "bloodsucking pimp" I'm
glad to have the eons of experience this group has to offer in the "what not to
do" category.

I've seen a few of my own snafus surface, and appreciate the help dejanews gave
me in finding this highly-amusing thread.  For example, there is such a love/hate
relationship between the contractors, the clients, and the recruiters.  I'm not
sure I've seen one civil word about anybody except the poster (the curse of
usenet perhaps?).  Makes me wonder how most of these posters survive a standard
interview cycle.....

Some insight....

In attempting to achieve a fine balance between contractor happiness (that's how
I get referrals) and client happiness (let's face it, that's how I get paid), I
need to protect both sides. Quite frankly, if you're not working with someone who
is willing to do what needs to be done, you should find a different
agency/headhunter.

I respect the contractors by ensuring that I know the details of the job:  pay
range, skill set, location.  I don't dick around with their time because I read a
resume (if available) before calling.  I try to actually listen.  My company pays
weekly, (but we only do W-2).  If a resume or posting says "No recruiters" that's
fine with me.  I don't call because, hey, it's their loss :)  I also attempt to
ensure that they get the best rate possible.  This is how I get paid.  My
commissions (and this is industry standard) are based on the margins involved
(Typically 50% margin, 20% of which goes to taxes and overhead /i.e. pay rate
=$50, bill rate ($50 x 1.5 = $75), gross margin ($25 x .8 = $20)/  I'm
commissioned off of that $20.  Honestly, I don't see why the $50/hr employee
should complain about that, if it's a fair rate.  This is what protects the
contractor, the more they make, the more I make.

I respect my client by ensuring that I meet face-to-face with any local
contractor I place.  I check at least two references and verify pertinent
information before setting up an interview.  I determine their needs in detail
(and not by talking to a personnel jockey, but by discussing things with the
hiring manager).  I make sure I understand the terminology so I can properly
assess the candidates.  I make sure that the best people get in the door.  And,
yes, they are my bread and butter.  I've never been paid by a
candidate/contractor for a placement/assignment.

Yeah, I occasionally screw up and miss the mark, but at least I try. BTW, this
nonsense about potential litigation against an individual within a company is
pure nonsense.  I'd consult a lawyer before expecting any great result from a
suit against one (usually indemnified) individual.  I suspect the poster was just
pissed ( or perhaps he's one of the liberal arts majors that failed law school).

To tell the truth, in 25 years of freelancing, I have never yet
run into this.  Perhaps it is a local thing (I am in Canada). The
body shops around here all work on a percentage basis.

The norm seems to be either a 70/30 split or a 85/15 split,
depending upon who does the invoicing and financing.
IE- at 70/30, I turn in my timesheets (signed), and get paid.

arrives.  I always considered that reasonable, as the
sales cycle is expensive.  One salesman can make a decent
living by keeping half a dozen programmers working ... a full
time job.
