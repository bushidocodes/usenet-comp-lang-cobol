2000-08-08 18:10:00 MEST

I just happen to read in http://www.ucc.ie/xml/ :

   C.4   Which parts of an XML document are case-sensitive?

   All of it, both markup and text. This is significantly
   different from HTML and most other SGML document types.
   It was introduced to allow markup in non-Latin-alphabet
   languages and to obviate problems with case-folding in
   scripts which are caseless.

Now the analoguous to OO-Cobol. "Problems", the text says,
and problems we discussed recently - think of the
round-trip conversion of 'ï¿½' to 'SS' in Cobol Names.

Whatif OO-Cobol-names were to become case-sensitive? What
would that do to existing applications?

We could have that with a directive like

   >> CASE SENSITIVITY IS ON

etc, - then, existing applications would be unaffected
entirely.

Compilers on machines such as the Telefunken TR4
(which have 6 bits characters) could ignore this
directive or reject it. (But then again, I do not
think that a TR 4 could host a living cobol-compiler.)

Just a thought. If it is a stupid one, tell me why.

Herwig

This is even more complicated than it may seem.  There is no collating
sequence that can properly put mixed case English into sequence let
alone most of the foreign languages.  The designers of Unicode - the 16
bit character encoding of all of the world's living alphabets plus most
symbols used (the plus sign, the have a nice day or happy face - hex
263A, etc.) have specifically stated that no collating sequence is to be
assumed for the character set.  Indeed reading the Unicode Standard is a
mind boggling exercise.  For historical reasons many of the characters
in European alphabets can be represented either by 1 character or a
character plus a non-spacing modifying character.  As this becomes more
widespread the semantics of comparison become more complicated to say
the least. Ordering rules differ between languages and even within
American English major encyclopedias differ in the application of the
rules.  We are leaving behind the days of all upper case letters.  Those
who have had to deal with other than the English language and English
alphabet have probably known these complications for years.  I can see
promises by the major sort vendors that their nationally sensitive sorts
using double byte character sets (or even an upper lower case standard
European 8 bit character set such as ISO 8859-1) will take only 20 times
the CPU cycles more than 1 that just uses either the standard ASCII or
EBCDIC.
