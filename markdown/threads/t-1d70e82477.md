Having worked in the banking industry for 19 years I can unequivocally
say that is untrue.  They do not clear the check right away.  While
your current balance may reflect the check you've just deposited, your
available balance does not.  Your available balance is what you can
withdraw funds against, not your current balance.  Depending on your
account type and bank policy, the general rule is:  Available Balance
= Current Balance - Uncollected Funds.  Some banks use total float
instead of uncollected funds, but the result is the same except for
the number of days the funds are unavailable.  Float can be up to 7
days, uncollected funds can be up to 10 days.  However, the Fed
regulates how long a bank can "float" check deposits.  For interstate
checks I believe it is no longer than 7 days.

Definately not true.  There are two types of holds.  There is an "exception
hold," a hold that is put on for a specific reason (usually because we tried
to verify funds on the item and was told the funds were not available, or
the check was already returned once for NSF and has been redeposited).
These holds can be up to a certain amount of days, the number which I forget
(I haven't worked at a banking location for over three years).  I believe
it's 4 business days for a local check (within the say Federal Reserve
region) and 7 business days for a non-local.  The other is a "case-by-case"
hold, which can be put on for most any reason.  These holds can only last, I
believe, 2 / 3 (local/non-local) days.  There is certainly no such thing as
a permenant hold.

The length of these holds, by the way, is governed by Federal regulations.
