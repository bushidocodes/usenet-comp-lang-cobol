Hear, hear! Efficiency and stability are not mutually
exclusive. I pride myself on writing programs that run
as quickly as possible & that are stable and reliable, such
as being able to handle empty input files (abending when
appropriate), checking for table overflows (abending etc.), avoiding division
by zero & making numeric checks 
a part of the read routine. A lot of efficiency can be obtained with simple
common sense. Example: some years
back, the insurance co. I work for modified its IMS drafts
data base by adding two segments to it. The monthly
download pgm was modified to retrieve these new segments. The new segments were
only required to be
downloaded for the December run, but the programmer
retrieved them first, and THEN looked at the date parm
to see whether they should be written to the output file.
With a data base numbering in the millions of segments,
this was not a trivial concern. I modified the pgm to look
at the date parm first, and skip the retrieval calls for months other than
December. Result: an annual savings
of $25,000 in execution costs. This one simple change
has literally saved the company a quarter of a million
dollars in the ten years that it's been in place.
To sum it up, design the pgm to do only what it has to
do, when it has to do it.
