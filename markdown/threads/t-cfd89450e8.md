work is decreasing. Even if you are busy, you should try just one day to

day will be a better day, and don't you forget to reward yourself. Do not
work harder - work smarter, and reward yourself for every little success.

I worked for a company for seven years. I found that if I arrived at
7:30 and was the first person in the front door I was also the last
person out of the door (at 20:00). Eventually I realised that it did not
matter how much I did there was always more to be done. If you feel that
you are doing a good worthwhile job and if the management show their
appreciation regularly then you would be surprised just how much stress
you can tolerate. Unfortunately, all you need is a worthless
unworthwhile slog or an unappreciative management and the fun goes out
of the job. That happened to me. When I realised what was happening I
started delegating all the work that I didn't fancy doing and would not
find interesting onto my juniors shoulders. I then concentrated on the
more interesting work (pre-emptive problem management, work on systems
that I knew well and liked..) and slowed down. Rather than do work in a
hurry (and make a mistake) then having to apologise/grovel to the
customer before doing a rushed fix (recursive fixs here) I checked and
double checked my work before releasing it to the live environment.
Result: less mistakes, less stress, happier me. If you can not delegate
work then slow down and make sure that what you do is correct. I saw one
programmer work every possible hour over six months on a new project
developing a new type of reporting suite (previously untried by our
company), he wanted the overtime to buy a new car. After the delivery he
went off sick for the next six months. The decline in his health had
been apparent during the project but the managers only cared about
delivery of the project and not their worker. Don't let it happen to
you.

Finally, I suspect it is not wise to tell your management that you are
suffering from stress. They won't see it as being an indictment of the
workplace but rather that you are incapable of being the young dynamic
systems professional that they had previously thought you were. I
watched one Business Analyst suffer a nervous breakdown. When his
managers spotted that he was missing meetings and failing to meet
deadlines they asked him what was going on. When they found out he was
ill he was told to resign.

> Anon wrote in message ...
> >How do you handle the stress that eventually comes in a programming

Just as a mental exercise:
I've classified the 'stress' that I most notice into 4 technical
categories. There surely are more on the 'personality conflict and
office culture' side...

1. There's a transaction/job/system problem and it needs to be fixed
immediately. I.e., correctly, with little testing, no resultant new
bugs, and damage repaired.

2. There's a nighttime job failure and other jobs/systems are waiting
for it to be fixed/rerun.

3. There's a project with a management-declared deadline, that must be
met or managers will not get their bonuses.

4. The program you are writing must absolutely work correctly the first
time, and forever.

While all have nuances in their stress-causation, one common thread is
TIME.

Typically, management wants you work on #1 and #2 until you get it
fixed. You aren't expected to sleep, eat, rest, etc. until you get it
done.
Similarly, on #3, when you find yourself 'behind schedule', you're
expected to allocate (often for no compensation) weekend and evening
time to the project.

On #4 you need time to completely test the functionality.

Based on the above one-faceted view, it would seem that one could
alleviate some stress by demanding/begging for/simply taking/etc. more
time for the chores at hand.

But easier said than done - often management has other goals than
employee mental health. Time pressure is very common.
No need to detail (to this forum) how target dates are picked, how
specs change, how other things steal the time from the grunts.

So maybe it's true in this case - TIME is of the essence. Acquire the
time to do the job and maybe it's smoother sailing.
