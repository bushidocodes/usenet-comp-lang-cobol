If it is their business to Make Money, and knowing your present salary

› It seems when we discuss rates they are always comparing the offered rate to

Ummmm... just a *wee* bit, aye. I usually attempt to start asking
questions as *soon* in the call as possible, establishing a kind of
rhythm...

'Oh, you have a slot for me? How interesting! Where is it located?
...
'I see, it is in (name of city)... very good, and how long is the contract
for?'
...
'Of *course* renewals are possible but... Life is Uncertain, isn't it?
Initial term of contract is (n)... alright, then, what kind of work is
it?'
...
'Yes, so they want me for (n) months in (name of city) doing (skillset)
work... that sounds like something I can do; how much are they offering
for this?'
...
'Oh, *I'm* sorry but I Asked You First... what are they offering?'
...
'What I am making now has *nothing* to do with what they have budgeted for
this position; please answer my question... what are they offering?'

... and you take it from there.

affect whether or not his/her company extends an offer to you. If you
currently make more than they are able to pay, then why waste their

anyway. Why offend you by offering a salary that you wouldn't take
anyway?

right to answer it or not.........and, as with any question a
recruiter asks, your willingness to answer may or may not affect
whether you get a job offer.

-
When I was able to contract, and even when it came to perm jobs,
I made a point of *never* providing "salary history."
-
Why? Because it is an unrealistic and artificial measure of
what I am worth on a new job.
-
I made $25.50 an hour as a teacher with LA Unified SD; but I
only got paid for actual *classroom* hours! The time I spent
after class with grading, lesson prep, record keeping, etc. was
*unpaid* time. I was working about 80 hours a week, plus
attending mandatory classes 3 nights per week (also unpaid).
So, was I earning $25.50 an hour? NO WAY!
-
Before I lost my home and two businesses in Texas [oil disaster
& Peso devaluation] I was working as CFO for a company and
making $425 per week. That would be *crappy* pay in SoCal. So
when an interviewer would ask for salary on my last full time
job, I'd say: "I don't expect your company to be able to match
it; my net take-home for one week made the mortgage payment, plus
the escrow payments for taxes and insurance, on a well built
three-bedroom home with a large yard, including nut and fruit
trees, and two out-buildings."
-
Yup -- that's the truth. In that neck of the woods my
mortgage/escrow payments on a 3-bedroom home were a little over
$300 a month. I couldn't expect a new employer to give me a
starting salary of $2,000 a week. Oh, for the right company
I was/am worth far *more* than that, but it takes a few days
to prove that to them.
-
Past salary cannot have any influence on current salary, because
each job and each individual is unique. As a ditch digger I
wouldn't be worth 50 cents an hour, but as a trouble shooter and
analyst I could be worth $1million an hour to the right company.
-
[OK, it seems rather far fetched, but its true. As a ditch
digger I'd not last 10 minutes. As an analyst & "questioner" it
took me less than an hour with a top programmer to have him find
out that the "impossible" not only could be done, but could be
done easily with the information and ability he already had but
didn't *realize* he had. The firm which needed the program doing
that makes cars in Japan and lots of other places... but I can't
name them.]
