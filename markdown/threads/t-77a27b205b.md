```
As someone made a few days ago the ELSE-IF proposal - which I find
very interesting - I would also propose what I find an amelioration of
the language.
In Cobol, we use levels for the data in file, working-storage and
linkage sections. The 01 level might be considered as the record
structure name, compared to other languages.
Example:
01 date1.
	02 year  pic 9999.
	02 month pic 99.
	02 day pic.
01 date2.
	02 year pic 9999.
	02 month pic 99.
	02 day pic.
If you want to move month of date1 to month of date2, you have to
write:
	move month of date1 to month of date2.
I, personnaly, would prefer:
	move date1.month to date2.month.

I find it more concise (less verbose than 'move ... of...') and easyer
to read and manipulate with an editor. And for a succession of levels,
it would be easy to write:
01 date1.
	02 year.	
		03 part1 pic 99.
		03 part2 pic 99.
	02 month pic 99.
	02 day pic.
01 date2.
	02 year.	
		03 part1 pic 99.
		03 part2 pic 99.
	02 month pic 99.
	02 day pic.
You can write:
	move date1.year.part1 to date2.year.part1
	
Easy, isn't it?

Some reactions?

Alain Reymond
```

```
Sounds like a manageable problem.  If the period is preceded by a valid
group name and followed by either a valid group or field name then the
period could be inferred as being used to join a qualifier(s) to a field
name.  And, if the syntax were goofed up somehow, the result would be
very traditional COBOL, 50 or so compiler error messages, one of which
actually means something.

As far as some compilers not requiring spaces following periods,
considering the period is usually a terminator, not having at least one
or two spaces doesn't lend itself to readable code.  So enforcing the
rule that a terminating period must be followed by white space wouldn't
be a bad standard to enforce.  Generally speaking, for the code to be
easily read and debugged the period should be followed by a new line. 
Having two statements on one line isn't a good practice.  

> Obviosly, it is not at all COBOL-like.  The next idea will probably be to remove the
> MOVE statement and use = instead and change the = for relational
> conditions to ==.

This sounds at least equally unCOBOL-like.  

The only reasonable argument I see here for not accepting the proposal
is that it came late in the game.  I don't accept the "disaster"
argument or "not at all COBOL-like" contention.
```

```
  if and only if
```

```
It is interesting that this discussion prompts some to comment on the value of
getting more on the screen.

Actually I think that neither the OF clause nor the dot syntax addresses the
real issue. I feel that the problem is not that we are taking up screen space
with a qualifier, but taking up space again and again with the same qualifiers.

It would be better to implement some kind of lexically scoped context setter,
as in ...

MANAGE 
   FROM
       from-data-level-2 of 
       from-data-level-1
   TO
      to-data-level-2 of 
      to-data-level-1
 END-MANAGE.

All ambiguous MOVEs would be disambiguated with the currently active data
movement MANAGEment directives.

We could ease the lexical scope complications by implementing this construct as
paragraph domain by default. To get it to apply to a section or an entire
program, you might need something like ...

MANAGE  [IN] SECTION
   FROM
       from-data-level-1
   TO
      to-data-level-1
END-MANAGE.

or ...

MANAGE  [IN] PROGRAM
   FROM
       from-data-level-1
   TO
      to-data-level-1
 END-MANAGE.


We could dodge the fact that some practitioners will not understand lexical
scope extent by requring MANAGEment at PROGRAM level to be the first thing in
the PROCEDURE DIVISION, MANAGEment at SECTION LEVEL to be the first thing in a
SECTION.

Paragraph level data movement MANAGEment statements would be trickier. For some
shops it would be best that industry tools permit a compilation parameter to
force paragraph level data movement MANAGEment statements at the begining of a
paragraph only.

That is the lexical scoping of paragraph level MANAGEment directives could be
very sophisticated, but some shops could suppress the fancy stuff (you want
simplicity in MOVE ... OF ... syntax, we have simplicity, but don't do anything
slick!)

The problem with wasted vertical dimmension, is not the isolated occurence, but
repeated occurence of waste; creating a program 'big picture' that is hard to
see, because almost none of it is on screen at any given time.

The proposed dot notation, does conserve horizontal realestate, a bit.  And
what has not been discussed, it also permits top-down expression of the data
structures, where as classical syntax with OF (or IN) phrases is inverted.

Compared to OF clauses, the unique name with qaulifiers built-in (usually) on
the front, does save space.  But in programs where you are not dealing with
movement back and forth between data structures, but are instead just operating
on a data occurence, there is a penalty in horizontal space and typing out the
front-qualifier over and over again.  So this excellent alternative is by no
means free.

In many shops the generation of the unique data name is a by-product of the use
of data dictionary tools, and shop standards for the DBAs work eliminates any
discussion of this. The names with a uniquely qualifying identifier on front
are there, please enjoy them. 

And although these do help you avoid the OF phrases, the generation of the
names by automata frequently introduces a revisitation of the problem. This is
because in large shops there are many projects and many components in each
project.  Inevetably you get names that look the same all over again.  As in,

   PROJDB01-CURRENT-BAL
   PROJBD01-CURRENT-BAL

or within a project

   PRJDB101-CURRENT-BAL
   PRJDB201-CURRENT-BAL

The unique qualifiers frequently look very similar because the relation between
support staff like DBA and projects will only tolerate relatively small
qualifiers.  The name space gets congested. The desire of DBAs to  keep similar
things together, leads to look-alike (at least look-similar) long before a name
space is exhausted.

The story goes on, though.  Initially in some shops there is a stong purist
policy, that tries to keep the same data element named exactly the same in all
projects, such as 

   PROJDB01-CURRENT-BAL
   PROJBD01-CURRENT-BAL

This usually derives from a very naive concept of data 'control'.  Eventually,
DBA policy may relent and allow names like

   PROJDB01-CURRENT-BAL-INPUT
   PROJBD01-CURRENT-BAL-GL
   PROJRP01-CURRENT-BAL-REPORT

Not always, but sometimes this is in response to complaints about coding errors
on look-similar data names.  The net result is dataname that will look unique,
even if you drop the front qualifier. A similar result flows from the
inexerable change in staff in DBA, where CURREN-BAL may later be generated as
CURR-BAL or CUR-BL.

In the end, the change of policy, inconsitent enforcement, and personnel change
leads to many data layouts that have unique names anyway and the front end
qualifier just becomes all burden no reward.


But even in the less complex environments it is a fact that small qualifiers on
the front of data names leads to similar names and therfore errors. But the
technique is still a very good solution.

OTOH, it would be nice to have a dot syntax, and anything else that will allow
top-down data structure explication.  And I think lexically scoped syntax
directives, such as something similar to a MANAGE statement could safe a lot of
human-machine interface resources in porjects where datanames are identical.
```
