```
Starting a "new thread" for those who want to argue "might is right" versus
"right is might" versus "cultural relativism".
Let me preface this by my personal ethical "background".

I am of an age that I might have been subject to the US draft for Vietnam.
I applied for "conscientious objector" status with supporting documentation
from both my home rector and my bishop (Episcopal - not a common position -
but one supported for specific individuals in that denomination).  This
"claim" was denied by my draft board, but "luckily" I was declared 1F (not
as unfit as 4F - but close).  I had determined that I would go to jail
(probably NOT to Canada) if I were required to serve in a combatant role.

At that time, I considered what I would have done in WWII.  I come from a
"long line" (both sides of the family) of "Middle European" - often German -
Jews.  Given what we know now, how would I have felt about serving in WWII?
I didn't ever come to a final decision then, and I don't really know much
better now.

If WWII had ended some "other way" how would the winning side have
documented the American internment camps for Americans of Japanese descent -
as opposed to the Nazi concentration camps?  As is often pointed out, the
winning side DOES tend to write the histories.  (Ever read some of the
historical analysis of how Shakespeare might of written Richard III, if he
was NOT writing under a Tudor Queen?  And this doesn't even get into whether
his Henry VIII is even good reading/performing).

From my historical studies (not as great as others in this list), there is
no country/government that I would call "always perfect".  I don't even
think I know of any (or certainly not any of any length of time) that is
"always awful".  (I spent a summer in Leningrad - when it was Leningrad -
during its celebration of the 50th anniversary of the Communist revolution.
I wouldn't personally put either the Russian Communists, nor the Czarist
Russians; much less the current Russian government in either the "always
right" or "always wrong" category.) I know of many cultures that "thought
they were doing RIGHT" when history eventually judged them to have made
"unethical" decisions.  This might describe the European colonial powers in
"civilizing" both South America and Africa *OR* the American government in
"bringing the TRUTH" to either American Indians and former Spanish colonies
after the Spanish-American war.  The view that "our culture" is better than
yours and that we are "helping you" by FORCING our values and system on you
has happened time and time again.  I am personally too close to such cases
in the 20th (and 21st) centuries to give any "absolute" answer to when in
the past - or currently - this is still going on.

My PERSONAL ethics says that it is "wrong" to kill or wage war because you
think that "your group is GOOD" and "their group is EVIL".  This applies to
BOTH sides in Northern Ireland; BOTH sides (or all sides) in the Middle
East;  All sides in Sri Lanka, Philippines, Basque, and several regions in
Africa (to name just a few).  Furthermore,  I tend to lean more on Gandhi's
side than on either of the Bush's sides when it comes to "*IF* you attack us
or our friends, then we are JUSTIFIED in attacking you, your families, your
friends, and anyone who ever speaks up for you."  This is NOT a particularly
common view in the US (and I doubt that it is particularly common in many
other places in the world).  It is, nevertheless, where I stand on these
issues.
```

```
republic -- 1 b (1) : a government in which supreme power

democracy -- b : a government in which the supreme power

> > In a republic, the supreme power, always, remains with
> > the people. In a representative democracy, the supreme
> > power is in the hands of the representatives.

In a republic, power resides in the people and is exercised
according to law. This means that irresponsible government
may be addressed in the courts.

In a representative democracy, the power 'of the people' is
exercised by representatives. This means that irresponsible
government must be addressed by removing the
representatives and by undoing their acts.

I see little evidence that most politicians or, for that matter,
voters understand the difference between a republic and
a democracy. When a politician runs for a U.S. State or
Federal office and claims that "If elected, I will < do this,
that, and the other >", they are stating that they do not know
what their duties are. If a politician states they will follow the
law (constitution) as set forth by the people, they understand
their duty; but have no chance at being elected.
```
