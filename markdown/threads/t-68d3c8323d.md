Matt, I've cranked out several million lines of COBOL code over some 30
years. There are two issues in the Y2k scene that are commonly
misunderstood. For fairness and accuracy, they should be articulated
correctly to the public.

First, it is not accurate to attribute the programmers themselves as
having little vision to have coded programs in such a way as to cause
this Y2k problem. It is inaccurate for two reasons. One reason is
that programmers, being the very logical creatures we must be, have been
well aware of the pending Y2k problem all along. It was simply the
demands of efficiency being placed on us and our systems which dictated
using dates with a two digit year. For people new on the scene, it is
very difficult to appreciate how limited memory and storage space was 20
or 30 years ago when these programs were originally written. In most
situations, had a programmer proposed using a four digit year in those
days, he would have immediately been told to go with two digits, because
people did not want to key those extra two digits on input, and because
it would consume too much storage resource. The other reason is that
programmers have been crying "Wolf!" again and again to management for
many years that the problem needed fixing. However, programmers do not
make management decisions to allocate funds. It is management, not
programmers, who has been putting off addressing this problem. Good
programmers get their job satisfaction out of writing good software.
No competent programmer wants a problem with his code. It is a strong
point of pride among programmers about the quality of code they write.
But management is cost conscious, not willing to spend money or cause
extra work to avoid a problem years in the future. One small point in
management's defense, at least until the late 1980's, is that no one
really knew COBOL programs would have such longevity. Computers were
relatively new, and the rate of change had been dramatic. It was not
certain back in the 60's and 70's that programs written then would
still be running at the turn of the century. The historic baseline was
too short. But by the late 80's, management did know. There were 20
year old COBOL systems, and programmers were warning them.

Second, the COBOL programming language itself had absolutely nothing to
do with creating the Y2k problem. There is not one single attribute of
the COBOL programming language which in any way contributed toward
creating the Y2k problem, or in making it worse, or in delaying its
solution, or in making its solution more time consuming or expensive.
There are two reasons COBOL is associated so much with Y2k. One reason
is that the vast majority of programs written during this period were
written in COBOL. Other systems written in other languages have just
as many Y2k problems, perhaps more. But there just aren't nearly as
many of them. The other reason is that COBOL has had a far greater
longevity and stability than any other programming language. This is,
in fact, an advantage of COBOL. Many COBOL programs written 30 years
ago can be compiled today with little or no changes. Because of this,
a greater percentage of systems written years ago in COBOL are still
around, being productive. Why would I bother to make this point?
COBOL has been a real money saving workhorse for business. But COBOL
already receives much undeserved criticism by people who do not know
what COBOL is about and why it is so valuable in business programming.
It would be very unfortunate for business to turn away, even a little,
from such a useful tool because COBOL's image became tarnished with
undeserved blame.

I'll add another here: no one had any thought that the formats
of data files are by nature in the business world, very self-
perpetuating. Given that some programmes are written to use
or create files with dates with the 2-digit year, any following
programs written to process those dates, even if these latter
programs were written years later, now for instance, will either
have to use to use the 2-digit dates in the old files, or use
copies of the files rewritten with 4-digit years, or depend on
being able to slip some interpretive layer of code between the
file and the program (assistance from the I/O API of various
operating systems that programs run under would be useful
here !)

The problem comes to the fore whenever a date calculation
crosses the 2000 01 01 barrier, such as adding a time interval to
a date and time in the 1900's and getting a date and time in the
2000's, or subtracting a date in the 1900's from a date in the
2000's, and getting a negative time interval because of the 2-
digit year.

The 2-digit year is by no means the only problem in this area;
some operating systems have used dates such as 99360 or 99999
(the 360th. day of 1999, or the 999th. day of 1999) to indicate
that a data file is never to expire (the data file space to be
freed for other use or overwritten), but what happens to such
files on 2000 01 01 - will the files be expired ? On some old
systems they would ! There are a few other similar horrors
related to year or other date or time numbers stored in machine
binary.

As a COBOL programmer for 10+ years, here are some of my observations on Y2K
and COBOL.

First, until recently, the only way to get the current date from the system
(speaking of mainframe machines here ...) gave it to the program in YYMMDD
format. So even if one alotted for the CC field, a '19' would still be hard
coded in the field. And as many others have pointed out, those extra two
bytes cost made storing the century unwise.

Second, in the 80's, with all of the "reengineering" and "downsizing", DP
staffs were slashed and consultants were used to supplant the reduction in
force (although the head counts still lagged behind pre-late 80's levels).
Consequently, continuous process improvement fixes (like Y2K) took a
backseat to "sexy" new business initiatives that required code
development/enhancements.

Finally, a lot of management shortsightedness has prevailed. When I first
started, your manager would be someone like you, a code-cranker with much
more experience but with the requisite business knowledge. Now, your
manager is likely to be a pointy hair Dilbert type, reassigned from customer
service with no real "systems" knowledge. So old archaic code still runs,
all the while management tows the line, keeping costs down, hoping that the
system won't crumble ... at least until they transfer over to more "cushy"
assignment. Meanwhile old program routines, unsupported because of the
reduction in staff and outsourcing, are ticking time bombs, headaches for
the code support staffs that maintain the software.
