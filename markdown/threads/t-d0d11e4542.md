Both Microsoft and IBM had their own compilers in the EARLY '80s.  Microsoft
then "re-logoed" the Micro Focus compiler as their own.  Then Microsoft used
Micro Focus as their COBOL compiler vendor - and then they got out of
"COBOL" entirely - but kept strategic relationships with several vendors
(including both Micro Focus and Fujitsu - that I know of).

Assuming your Microsoft compiler is anything "after" Version 1, then it is
probably a Micro Focus "re-packaged" product - and they do provide the best
"compatibility".  Whether or not it is the best strategic product for your
site, depends on what you plan on doing with your existing COBOL
applications - and how much you plan on spending on them.

4.5 was the last MS Cobol to be able to target 16 bit OS/2
5.0 can target DOS and Win 3.x but NOT OS/2.

Both come with conversion utilities for converting from MS Cobol 2.2,
but I don't have specifics off the top of my head.  I always inferred from
that
that MS 2.2 was the last of MS's native Cobol compilers.  Of course,
making INFERENCES about MS products is always dangerous. :-)

I have both installed and can dig out more details if someone
needs the information.  

While there are many criticisms of MS products (some valid, some not)
and 16 bit programming is a backwater by today's standards, I found
one thing about MS language tools of that vintage VERY appealing,
they went out of the way to make mixed language programming well 
documented and doable.  Under Windows the model seems to have moved
to don't do mixed language programming, but keep each language into it's
own DLLs.  Then let the DLLs call each other.  Documentation on actually
using languages together, when by direct linkage or by DLL/dynamic linkage
is harder to find, and often sketchy.

My experience with 360/370 etc. architecture was that IBM did an excellent
job at documenting mixed language issues for OS/360 in the late '60's and
early '70's.  But I've had no experience with that architecture since then.
Does anyone here know, are they still documenting mixed lang. as well as 
they were?
