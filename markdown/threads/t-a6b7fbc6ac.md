Problem: 

1. Face-to-face meetings have some drawbacks: 
a. Travel costs. 
b. Productive time lost to travel. 

c. Host must commit significant time and expense to logistics.

2. NCITS decided to explore the potential of electronic meetings as an
occasional substitute for face-to-face meetings.
3. A electronic chat mock meeting is the appropriate way to do this.
Holding a real meeting as a test would put at risk timely conduct of
NCITS business if this initial electronic forum experiment is not
successful.
 

Exploring Solutions: 
1. The meeting will employ electronic chat technology already 
successfully used by a TC. 
2. The mock meeting will be short (2 hours), but in order for it to be
realistic it will follow an agenda and feature a single real subject
for discussion and deliberation.

3. The subject will be: "Electronic Meetings: Is This Meeting 
Working?" In other words, the meeting will discuss the meeting itself.

4. The mock meeting will be considered a realistic experiment if at
least 50 percent of the regular quarterly NCITS meeting attendees
participate. A good representative attendance is important because the
experiment is an NCITS Ad Hoc Committee assignment.  These membersï¿½
experience with the mock meeting will be used by NCITS as a basis for
possible revision to NCITS procedures, and their personal experiences
with this experiment will guide their own votes.
 

Assumptions: 
1. There will be people who believe that electronic meetings can be
used effectively;
2. There will be others who believe that electronic meetings cannot be
used effectively;

3. Therefore, active debate with opposing positions can be
anticipated, just like issues at a regular NCITS meeting.

4. All regular NCITS members and TC officers/members are invited to
participate.
 

Potential Questions to be discussed or explored at the meeting: 
1. How to adhere to an agenda in an environment where it is natural to
have several people "talking" (typing) at once? Is it still necessary
to maintain a speaking queue?
2. How would votes be conducted? 

a. Authentication by logon and password? 
b. Closing discussion and validating results with a decision record? 

c. Secret ballot by sending chair an email with confirmation of vote
acknowledged by chair online (not disclosing actual vote)?

3. Without experience in electronic meetings, how do we schedule an
appropriate amount of time for a topic?
a. Will topics typically take longer? 
b. Will topics typically take a shorter period of time? 
c. It would appear that we would need to conduct several meetings to
gain enough experience to answer these questions.
4. Does the chair need a special communication channel, or will
attendees naturally give the chair appropriate attention and
deference? Will the chair need enforcement mechanisms to control the
floor or maintain a speaking queue?
5. How to apply Roberts Rules of Order to such a meeting? 

6. An electronic chat meeting unavoidably allows capture of a
permanent record of all discussion. Is this a deterrent to open debate
and expression?


Logistics: 

1. Attendees will need only a connection to the Internet (28K is
adequate) and a standard Web browser. There is no need to make special
firewall provisions.
2. Two days before the meeting, as the host of this mock meeting,
Steve Haflich <smh@franz.com> will distribute instructions and the URL
that will be used.  The message will be sent to the NCITS distribution
list.

I believe the waste of resources we have seen over the past 10 years is
mainly due to mis-management and outmoded procedures. There would be no
reason why people who are currently involved in the various committees
couldn't contribute if they wanted to, but the Management of it would be
different than currently:

1. NO closed door committees.
2. NO boondoggles from Corporate Sponsorship unless something really good
has been achieved.
3. ANSWERABILITY to ALL.
4. NO Membership or eligibility requirements. Equal rights for all.
5. A Timeboxed and iterated, interactive, approach with the document being
publicly visible as it proceeds.
6. Rigid adherence to SIMPLE ENGLISH, with examples given to replace
convoluted legalese.
7. Any revenue generated by the said document would be distributed equitably
to the people who worked on producing it.

Given all the above, why would such a Standard have any less credibility

To clarify (hopefully) some of these terms (players)

J4 is a TC (Technical Committee) that reports to NCITS

NCITS *used* to be known as CBEMA

NCITS is the "authorized" Secretariat for ANSI for certain "groups" of
things - including programming languages (the old "X3" group of projects).

ANSI is the American (US) Standards body.

ANSI, NCITS, and J4 *all* have explicit rules REQUIRING their work to be
"open" - for input, review etc.  They do, however, retain control over their
own rules and each body *must* follow the rules of its parent (i.e. J4 must
follow NCITS rules and NCITS must follow ANSI rules).

   ***

ISO is the International Standards Body.

Its rules are COMPLETELY different than the ANSI rules.

ANSI is the US member of ISO.

WG4 is the COBOL "Working Group".  Working Groups have almost no rules but
also no authority.  On the other hand, the "secretariat" almost ALWAYS
follows the "recommendations" of the WG convenor.  The convenor almost
always follows the recommendation of the working group.

WG4 "reports" to SC22 which is "comparable" to NCITS in that it has a bunch
of projects - including the programming languages (but NOT SQL anymore - as
I recall).

SC22 is a project of ISO/IEC - I think (but am not positive).  When it comes
to the difference between ISO and IEC, I don't really understand it.  I just
know that all the COBOL "stuff" is under ISO/IEC/JTC1 - the "J" in JTC1
sort-of combines ISO and IEC - I think.

Working Group meetings are EXPLICITLY closed (to previously designated
delegations).

Working Group documents are explicitly "private" (not to be distributed
outside the WG)

Working Groups do *not* have official "voting" rules as they are required to
indicate "consensus" not votes.

    ***

When talking about "procedures" any attempt to lump J4 with WG4 or NCITS
with SC22 is or ANSI with ISO is liable to get you into trouble.  On the
other hand, saying that all of them are "bureaucratic" is reasonably
accurate.  Even the "attached" note is indicating a "pilot" project and it
will be interesting to see how it progresses (and to consider how "late" it
is in occurring - when compared to comparable procedures in many of its own
members')
