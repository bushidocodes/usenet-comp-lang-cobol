As far as I know,
You can compile if you have Acucobol for System V, and If you have
the iBCS2 insertable module.  Only problem is, when you link, you
are stuck because the linking is different in linux than SCO Unix.
 
Once someone figures how to provide SCO or unix libraries without
violating unix license laws, and further, if you use the unix
ar and ld versions, it will work...
 
I wish I could help these system programmer types.  I tried
using lpicobol under linux.  The compile worked, but the link
didn't.  But then again, the same is true about unix C
and linux C, the linker is different...
 
In summary, the compile step will work, but the linker
won't.  So why bother, until the linking step is in place...

The trouble with such suggestions is that the economic calculations
don't look very favorable.

Let's assume that we need two engineers plus to support such a product,
with support people etc and overhead, assume this comes to $250,000 a
year. Suppose that we can make $25 off each compiler sale (that's a bit
optimistic for a $50 selling price, with manuals etc, but OK).

Now, the question is, can you sell 10,000 copies a year of the Linux
compiler, given that this is a market that is used to free software,
and largely C oriented?

I wouldn't get *my* money on it.

Really the only economically successful cheap compiler was Borland
Pascal. Borland lost a ton of money trying to emulate this  with
other languages (remember TUrbo Prolog???).
