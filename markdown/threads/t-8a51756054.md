I suppose it inevitable that, every year or so, someone must
clarify the difference between "structured programming" and
"modular programming." So Richard Riehle, Pete, Jimmy,
and others, here is the clarification.

Structured programming refers to the arrangement of
statements in a procedure and uses words like: sequence,
choice, and iteration, to describe the structure of the procedure.

Modular programming refers to the decomposition of systems
and uses words like: cohesion, coupling, encapsulation, and
interface, to describe the relationship between separate
procedures.

Procedural decomposition (PD) and object-orientation (OO)
are types of modular programming. Both PD modules and
OO methods may consist of "structured" code (or they may be
spaghetti).

There is no comparison or contrast between "structured
programming" and "object-oriented programming". They
occur at different levels.

Pete, OOP will never replace "structured"; though it may
replace PD. ;-)

Jimmy, you have made "my skin crawl" at least two dozen
times since my last post on MP vs SP. ;-)

Richard, what consitutes structured programming is
well-defined and your description is so broad as to be
incorrect. See, Youdon, "Techniques of Program Structure
and Design," Prentice-Hall, 1973, for more complete
descriptions of both modular programming and structured
programming. You might choose to re-read the chapters
on "Modularity" in "Structured Design" with the idea that it
is a discussion of modular programming; not structured
programming. ;-)
