I am graduating from a good Community College IT Management course in
Atlantic Canada and I also hear of this shortage of COBOL programmers.

Could it be that (for some illogical reason) the shortage is so great
that nobody wants to talk to entry-level programmers?

I was looking for a 4-week *free* work-term in COBOL, preferrably in
Atlantic Canada. I had to take a Visual dBase project. Although, this
project is giving me great experience (I'm building it from scratch on
my own), I really wanted to work with COBOL because of its
opportunities. But it seems that without 2 years experience, I'm either
useless, or too expensive.

Frankly, taking the risk of sounding arrogant, I honestly can't see why
an employer would not want me to work for him/her even though I would
have to be trained some more!

Anyway, I'll keep looking and reading what everybody here has to say.

Jean Richard.

I agree with you about it being difficult to find work if you are
entry-level. I started programming in COBOL some 12 years ago as a
sophomore in high school. At that point, I knew I wanted to be a COBOL
programmer. When I was almost finished with my college degree, I began to
look at job openings, but 90-98% were for COBOL programmers with 2-5 years
minimum experience (this was when I lived in Dalton, Ga. - the carpet
capital of the world). Nobody wanted to give the starters a chance. I
finally found a night operator job at a local carpet manufacturer that had
some COBOL programming involved with it. After a year of being operator, I
was promoted to programmer when the programmer I was under left for another
job. If it weren't for that company willing to give me a chance, I would
have had a difficult time finding a starting COBOL position.
The company I am with now, Intelligent Information Systems, has an
aggressive college recruiting program whereby they go on campus and recruit
newly- or nearly-graduated programmers. In fact, the recruiting person was
telling me that more and more companies in the Research Triangle Park are
recruiting college graduates heavily for programming and other technical
positions. It may be due to the fact it is cheaper to hire a graduate and
train them the way you want them instead of having to "deprogram" them from
their previous habits. Or, it could be the companies are looking at it
from a salary standpoint. Who knows? In any case, I would like to see
more companies follow this recruiting strategy and give new programmers a
chance to get some experience under their belt. A lot of good, raw talent
is out there waiting to be given a chance to prove themselves as
programmers.
