Some years ago our company was requested to assist a client with converting
COBOL to VB6.

It really wasn't hard and because there was not a lot of it we did it
manually.

I wouldn't personally recommend anyone doing this as a way into the future,
but we were paid for the job so we did it.

I believe C# would have been a better choice but they wanted VB6.

There are COBOL compilers that can emit C# or Java, but I don't know of any
that emit VB6.

However, there ARE converters that can do it.

Here's one (which I haven't tried - I don't think it was around when we did
the exercise);

http://cobol.software.informer.com/download-cobol-code-converter-to-visual-basic/

Alternatively, you might be able to achieve it by doing 2 passes:

Compile COBOL to C#, then convert C# to VB6, for example.

Not a path I'd want to walk, but most things can be done if you have the
will and tenacity to do them.

I have been programming for over 40 years. Over the years, some of my fondest memories are writing source code analyzers, converters, or generators. I think you will enjoy this task!

If I were going to do any of it again, I would use awk or perl -- that eliminates your having to work on the more mundane aspects of parsing. Here are some other suggestions that come to mind:

1. Use the original source compiler as much as possible. After all, it knows what it is doing. You can use, for example, cross reference listing options.

2. Start with simple examples, insuring that you can go from the original to the target language. Expand functionality as you go.

3. Think in terms of the 80-20 rule. You don't necessarily need target code that doesn't need a human to finish it up. What you want is for your converter to do the majority of the work. In that effort, your converter will get better!

4. Keep in mind that the interim data you accumulate as you do the conversion will be very useful for other inquiries later on -- both in analyzing the old code as well as analyzing the new; e.g., what data is touched where and how.

5. Remember it can be an iterative process. You are not obligated to keep the target code. As your converter improves, you can reapply it.

6. Your converter doesn't need to have optimal performance -- the end result is more important than how you get there.

7. Have fun! If you are a true programmer at heart, the entire effort will be a joy.

I am not nearly as brilliant as many of the other participants here in the forum. Especially as age is taking the edge off of my thinking. Nevertheless, I figured a few of these thoughts off the top of my head might prove useful to you.
