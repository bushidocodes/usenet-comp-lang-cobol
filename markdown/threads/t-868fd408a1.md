LAST word about Zeller:


Zeller's Congruence

----------------------------------------------------------------------------
----

Many computer programs need to be able to indicate the day of the
week that a particular date falls on. When that situation occurs,
programmers use a date conversion algorythm such as "Zeller's Congruence".
Using this algorythm, write a program that will allow a user to enter a
month, day and year and will then tell what day of the week that date
falls on. Months are numbers from 1 to 12 with January being one and
December being twelve. Days will be 1 to 31. No day will be entered
that exceeds the number of days in that month. Years will range from
1800 to 2100.
Zeller's Congruence:
If the Month is greater than 2 then the Adjusted Month is the
Month - 2 and the Adjusted Year is the Year entered, otherwise
the Adjusted Month is the Month + 10 and the Adjusted Year is
the Year entered-1.
The Century is the Adjusted Year / 100. (No Remainder)
The Year In the Century is the Adjusted Year - 100*Century.
(No Remainder)
Add all of the following:
(13 * Adjusted Month - 1) / 5 (No Remainder)
Day Of Month
Year In Century
Year In Century / 4 (No Remainder)
Century / 4 (No Remainder)
77
>From that total subtract:
2*Century
The remaining total is Answer Number One
Divide Answer Number One by 7. (No Remainder)
Multiply that by 7
Subtract that from Answer Number One.
If you did it correctly the result will be a number from 0 to 6
representing the day of the week as follows:
0 = Sunday 4 = Thursday
1 = Monday 5 = Friday
2 = Tuesday 6 = Saturday
3 = Wednesday

Here is a C version of Zeller's Congruence which doesn't have that problem:


The day of the week 'W' (0 = Sunday) is given by



W = ((13*M - 1) / 5 + D + Y + Y/4 + C/4 - (2*C)%7 + 7) % 7;


where

C is the century (year/100)

Y is the last two digits of the year (year%100)

D is the day of the month

M is a special month number, where Jan and Feb are taken as

month 11 and 12 of the previous year (subtract 1 from the year)

and each division is truncating division, and cannot be combined.


The properties of modulo arithmetic are such that we may apply it
individually to each term of a sum. To guarantee that our sum is positive,
we can find (2*C)%7, which is subtracted instead of 2*C, and add an
additional 7 before the final %7.

The code I just posted does actually not use the congruence. Here is some
C-code that does:


Converts the date into a number of days since a distant but unspecified
epoch. You can use this function to calculate differences between dates, and
forward dates. Use days to date() to calculate the reverse function. Author:
Robert G. Tantzen, translated from the Algol original in Collected
Algorithms of the CACM (algorithm 199). Original translation into C by Nat
Howard, posted to Usenet 5 Jul 1985.

Source Code - (sfldate.c)
{
long
year = GET_YEAR (date),
century = GET_CENTURY (date),
month = GET_MONTH (date),
day = GET_DAY (date);

if (month > 2)
month -= 3;
else
{
month += 9;
if (year)
year--;
else
{
year = 99;
century--;
}
}
return ((146097L * century) / 4L +
(1461L * year) / 4L +
(153L * month + 2L) / 5L +
day + 1721119L);
}
