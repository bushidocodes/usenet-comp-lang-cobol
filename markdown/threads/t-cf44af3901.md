Randy,
I appreciate your reply. And you made very good points, however I think
you went off onto Unix and mainframe tangents that have nothing to do with
the my original message. Please, remember the "context" of what we are
talking about. Tully mentioned PC COBOL and it's price and value as a
development tool compared to Visual Basic. So, obviously, the operating
system we're speaking of is Windows. I would never compare the price of
Visual Basic to MicroFocus on Sun Unix etc, etc.
With that in mind, the bottom line is, the enterprise edition of Visual
Basic beats out any COBOL attempt at Windows RAD.
Also, I've been programming in COBOL for 10 years and I love it. I've
written programs for DOS, several flavors of Unix and now for Windows.
Remember, in my message I am in no way bashing the COBOL language. It's my
bread and better. And that's exactly why I expect more of it's technical
advancements. 9 out of 10 COBOL projects I work on are now for Windows
95/NT. It's not my choice, it's what the business world is turning to.
Win95/NT sales just keep rising and developers must follow. My company
actually lost sales to competitors because they had a more robust Windows
product, done in less time with VB than we did. I am simply asking COBOL
vendors to catch up in price and value in the Windows arena. And they are
getting better all the time, but they are definatly not there yet.
As far as Fujitsu goes, they've been extending their free offer for
several months now, and the enterprise price is $500 for 1 year support and
updates. I got the professional edition for a $250 subscription just a week
ago.
Now that the Windows features are getting richer in COBOL, it's time to
bring prices down to compete in the VB, Powerbuilder, and Delphi market.
If that means unbundling features like CICS compatability etc, etc, then
do it. Be more flexible with products. Don't make the folks who aren't
porting legacy systems to Windows pay fat prices for features they don't
use.
MicroFocus made a great attempt in Visual Object Cobol and then they
dropped it. Why? Spent too much on advertising to quickly? What are your
thoughts?
Bottom line is, I'd love to be using MicroFocus and animator or
NetExpress at home, but it just cost tooooo much.
As far as pounding nails with screwdrivers, well that was cute. "10
times better" was a figure of speech. I wasn't being literal. I was
stressing a point. Ok, here's one - you've got to see the forest beyond the
trees. 8~)
Hey, good talking with you Randy. 8~)

Siber Systems has a product called *mf2fsc* that does what you want
-- it converts MicroFocus Cobol to Fujitsu Cobol. The free version of
product is available for download from http://www.siber.com/sct/.

It serves as a sample application that showcases CobolTransformer.
CobolTransformer is a general framework/library/tool written in BtYacc and
C++ that includes: Cobol parser (12 better-known dialects),
tree manipulation library, and generator of readable Cobol code
(PrettyPrinter). CobolTransformer is already licensed by a number of
companies and they were able to cut down Cobol converter development
time dramatically.

The mf2fsc free version does approximately 50% of the necessary
conversions and it is a good starting point for your conversion
process.

A commercial version of mf2fsc that does most of the conversions
automatically will be available in 1-2 months.

Tully and Daniel,
I have not looked at the prices for these products so I am
saddened to learn they are so expensive. I agree that these compilers
are over priced. The reason they are overpriced is that, despite what
Daniel says, 'tales of the mainframes death have been greatly
exagerated'. The only reason they can get this exorbitant money for
their software is that the corporates are stupid enough to pay it, and
even at these prices it will still sell. But it is also a kind of
self fulfilling prophesy of low market share. They would rather 4
times the money, and 1/4 the market share, from one customer.

The other reason for the rip off, IMHO, is that because they
are kind of products that support and offload mainframe development,
they are also tied to all other version and compatibility problems
with mainframe software. By which I mean, what good is COBOL without
CICS, IMS, DB2, SQL, MQ, etc etc etc. Microfocus will give you this
in their development workbench, but when you have this you have about
$5 million worth of mainframe software and hardware. In a sense then,
PC COBOL by Microfocus and CA is overpriced, because IBM manage to
over charge so well for all the mainframe products for which these are
functional equivalents. I have a friend who works for Microfocus and
he has told me he just recompiles mainframe systems of up to 10,000
programs onto servers and they execute without problems. Instant
Client/Server conversion. Except that the clients are Pentium PC's
that function as dumb terminals. Who cares. It's a lot cheaper. I
have the Fuj compiler, and it is very good, but I never use it because
it is not 'IBM compatible' in the broader environmental sense of the
term.

Ed Yourdon can bad mouth COBOL as much as he wants. This is surprises
me, as do these langauge debates in general. From what I have seen
any new feature that appears in any language, that survives and is
genuinely worth having, will be copied and incorporated into every
other langauge on the planet. For instance the CASE statement in
structured programming, which is implemented in COBOL as the EVALUATE
statement. There will be OO extensions to the next release of COBOL.
It may take 10 years, but without doubt, eventually all the latest
toys and features will be there for you to play with. Purists may
argue, and in fact probably will argue, that the OO implementation in
COBOL is no good, but then C++ is also a very old langauge that had OO
extensions grafted onto it, and I have read many people criticise OO
C++. Purists will say that OO programming can only work properly in a
language that was designed from the ground up for Object Orientation.
Unfortunately, this statement fails to appreciate that there are
already millions of lines of code out there in languages that pre-date
OOP, and that people would like to incorporate some OO function in
this old code without reprogramming entire systems.

All this 'langauage/acronym/buzzword of the future stuff' just amuses
me these days. I have been in this industry for 20 years and there
has never been a time when there has not been some silver bullet,
'just over the horizon', just another few million in investment away.
And of course, none of them ever worked. I just spent 2 years working
on LU 6.2 mainframe interfaces to a $100 million Client/Server system
that was ultimately cancelled. Do I care? They pay me to put the
code it. They pay me to take it out. I do one or neither or both
very well.

You have to get cynical and mercenary to really enjoy it. But
everytime COBOL and mainframes get trashed in the media I see money
for me and continuing employment. Only PHM's are stupid enough to
believe what they read in the computer press. I have been reading
about PC's and all this wonderful Internet stuff for years. I keep
telling myself that as soon as I cannot get a mainframe job I will
cutover to this wonderful glamorous exciting PC stuff. But I earned
close to $120K last year contracting on mainframes. Not great but not
bad. Just keep cruising along. Ed Yourdon and I and both you guys
will die long before COBOL. The biggest threat I see to my continued
employment is not any magic bullet. It is the hordes of really
intelligent, really young, really lean and really mean programmers
from the thirld world (China, India, Philippines, etc etc) who come
here and offer to do the work I do for half to a third the price. I
would guess that this will be the factor that ultimately forces my
retirement.
