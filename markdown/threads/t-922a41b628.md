------- snip -
At the time I ordered it, it was a permanent license to use the basic
product, including *royalty free* distribution of executables you linked
from it. There were add on modules which cost extra included for
evaluation, but the base product was a permanent license for a free and
functional commercial release version. At that time they also had a deal
(which was scheduled to expire September 30) for odering the upgraded
version at a steeply discounted price (e.g., the $3,000 pacakage was $500).
The upgrade added such things as ODBC drivers, a higher speed sort module,
etc. None of the add-ons was required for basic COBOL coding, or for
getting a DOS or Windows program up and running.

Personally, I wish I had had the extra $500 lying around to order the
subscription to make the license to the ODBC drivers legal. However, what
I got was great for the price. I also think it is a smart marketing move,
as compared to a certain market-leading PC/UNIX COBOL Workbench publisher
(with the initials MF) which seems to want to squeeze every nickle out of
you, like they were in an uncontested market. When you can make even IBM
(which was giving away eval Visual Age for COBOL disks a while ago) look
more flexible than you, you deserve to lose market share big time.

One thing which is missing from the Fujitsu product (at least, so far as I
have been able to determine) is a link to a CICS, or an IMS product. I
don't think their target market is quite the same as IBM, MF or CA, in that
there is little mention of tying to mainframe legacy systems, or of how to
port. Of course on the other hand this is an interesting validation of
COBOl on the PC platform. If someone is willing to publish a COBOL for PCs
that is *not* targeted at legacy transfer, then there's hope management
will get a clue about using it for *new* development on PCs.
