Excepting the 1401, this is all NEW stuff. I wish they had pictures of the 704,
650 and especially the 7090 data-entry keyboard, which looked like a piano
keyboard with 36 keys. A proficient operator keying six bits at at time with
three fingers of each hand looked like he was playing a piano. Another nice
touch would be a 7044 console with 2-3 Selectric typewriter guts in the
background, awaiting duty when the operator terminal failed. A 407 plugboard
would be ultime de la ultime from that era. 

Moving forward (time waits for no man), the RCA 301 deserves a museum of its own
for the mickey-mouse peripherals it fielded. For instance, a disk drive made by
Wurlitzer that looked and functioned like a juke box. And a CPU that stopped and
turned on a red light when an I/O error occurred. I imagine conferences at RCA
headquarters in the David Sarnoff Building where high-level decisions were made
.. 'We don't have time for that error-recovery crap. Our ship date is next week.
Just do something to make the problem go away.' Those were heady times. 

Robert

I vividly remember the daily, sometimes hourly, battles
to keep other people from ripping down standard boards
used on a daily basis so that they could run one-shot jobs.

Some of the actual physical tug-of-war matches were legendary.
Eventually, we resorted to lockable covers to protect the payroll boards.
Amazing, but 100% true.

The ne plus ultra however, occurred when my supervisor
interspersed-master-card-gangpunched the job cost master file.

The noise from the IBM 519 reproducing card punch was thunderous
in its final few moments.  The sight of my rather portly supervisor
flying across the machine room to hit the Stop button in a red-faced panic
is etched on my mind for all time.  A priceless memory.
