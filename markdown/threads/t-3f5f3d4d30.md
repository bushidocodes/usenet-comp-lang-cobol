I just thought that I would start a new thread giving my VERY personal
opinion on a few topics in a current (unnamed) thread.

1) For me, the term "vaporware" has a specific meaning.  It means software
(or hardware?) that a vendor markets (and possibly even sells) which is NOT
available to those who follow THAT vendor's rules for acquiring and
installing it.  This means that if someone SELLS me V1.2 of product XYZ and
all they can deliver to me is V1.1, then V1.2 is vaporware.  If they CAN
deliver V1.2 to me - but I don't have the environment (money? resources?
etc) to install and use it, that does NOT make it vaporware, unless they
sold/marketed it to me (or others) without telling me what would be required
to use it. (In that case, I consider this more "false/deceptive advertising
than "vaporware".)  If they offer me a BETA or "pre-release" copy of V1.2,
then (to me) V1.2 is still vaporware.

2) No one knows when what is currently a DRAFT ANSI and ISO Standard will
become an official Standard.  Certainly the best guess (and I agree with
this) is that it will be "about" December 2002 for ISO and very soon after
that for ANSI (possibly even simultaneous).  However, until the very last
approval step actually occurs NO ONE (no vendor, committee member, or other)
can claim "conformance" to what the final Standard will be.  They can claim
to conform to much (all? part?) of the current FCD (with or without some of
the TENTATIVE changes that are being worked on by J4 - but won't even be
looked at by WG4 until November).  On the other hand, there are some (but
certainly not all) features that have been pretty "stable" for several
iterations of the draft, and my best GUESS is that "we all" know how they
will end up (if/when the standard is approved).  Letting the readers of
comp.lang.cobol know when there is such a feature in the draft Standard to
meet their "real world" problems of today (as raised in this forum), seems
like a good and useful thing (to me) to do.  I have and will continue to do
this - even as I *vocally* oppose the current ANSI/ISO J4/WG4 "procedures"
which (again in my personal opinion) have done more harm than good.

When such features are mentioned, it is often useful to mention which
vendors already support such features (in part or whole) AND (again in my
opinion) to recommend that paying customers try and get their "vendors of
choice" to implement these specific features as quickly and completely (and
conforming to the latest draft Standard) as possible.

3) Data "validity" is (has been - always will be) a major issue in all
multi-program applications.  The draft Standard provides a number of ways in
which data integrity can (programmatically) be checked and hopefully be
improved.  NOTHING will ever make it possible to create a "large real-world
application" totally fool-proof - although various design and implementation
approaches AS WELL AS program "tools" (syntax) can help with this.

The draft Standard includes a VALIDATE feature that is (in my opinion)
particularly useful for this.  To the best of my knowledge the only existing
"production" implementation of this facility is for ICL - and even that is
not all that close to the current FCD.

Strongly typed items I think (but am not certain) are available in a couple
of implementations (Fujitsu V6.1 being one - NetExpress with ISO2000
directive possibly being another).  I can GUARANTEE that neither of these
are exactly what will be in the final version of the Standard, because the
last public review found some problems that are being "fixed" even as we
speak.  There was an excellent article in CobolReport a few months ago
talking about this feature - and anyone interested in it might want to look
at this.

Common Exception Handling, Standard Arithmetic, and a number of other new
and enhanced features may well help with "data integrity" (and
validation/validity) issues.  However, none of these are implemented in
their final Standard version (as far as I know) by any current COBOL
implementation.  This doesn't mean that existing COBOL programmers shouldn't
know about them - and possibly even structure new programs to take advantage
of such features (when/if their vendor of choice provides them).

4) There are advantages and disadvantages of changing "subject lines" and
snipping portions of text when replying to newsgroup messages.  I know that
recently one person thought that I was replying to the "original" point of a
thread when I thought my "snipping" made it clear that I was responding to a
later comment.  Certainly when you read someone's reply to a thread that you
started (or recently posted to), I would recommend that you look and see if
it was YOUR note that the response is directed to - or someone else's.  This
may or may not impact how you read the reply.

   ***

I hope that this note doesn't sound like I am trying to "waffle" too much on
multiple sides of issues, but did want to share MY opinions - without in any
way trying to say that other posters aren't entitled to their own.
