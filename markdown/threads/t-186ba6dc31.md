```
Neither ASCII nor EBCDIC actually "define" how a sign is stored *unless*
"SIGN IS SEPARATE" is specified.  IBM's "conventions" for internal signs for
EBCDIC data is pretty common in even non-IBM environments that use EBCDIC
(for character data).  When it comes to ASCII, there are several different
"conventions".

Tell us which compiler and on what operating system you are working - and we
can tell you what values are used for "internal" signs (assuming you are
talking about USAGE DISPLAY numeric data - not COMP-????).

Note: Even knowing the compiler and the operating system MAY not be enough.
Some compilers (notably Micro Focus) have a VARIETY of ways of storing
internal signs - depending upon the compiler directives selected.

Bottom-Line: If you want to "know for sure" what is happening when
converting DISPLAY numeric data from one convention to another - use the
SIGN IS SEPARATE clause.
```

```
As Bill's reply says, the embedded sign can be problematic.  So, get it
out of the way.

The sensible way to convert is to first, working in either EBCDIC or
ASCII, convert to SIGN SEPARATE (either leading or trailing), or even a
floating sign.  Then, convert to the other representation,  ASCII to
EBCDIC or EBCDIC to ASCII.  Finally, convert back in that target
representation to the original picture.  1985 ANSI COBOL allows MOVE of a
Numeric Edited input to a "normal" Numeric item, so there are plenty of
choices.

This advice goes even more strongly if the input is not Numeric Character
(Packed-Decimal or BINARY)!
```
