Not sure if the author of that blog is or was or ever has been a
programmer and, if he is/was, ever worked in a software house.  I
would dispute his statements which follow::

"There are companies which create software which actually gets used by
customers, which describes almost everything that you probably think
of when you think of software.  It is unlikely that you will work at
one unless you work towards making this happen.  Even if you actually
work at one, many of the programmers there do not work on
customer-facing software, either."

My empolyer, for the most part, is one of those "create software"
companies and except for some underlying utility and online
transaction build type systems, everything we write gets used by our
customers.  We currently offer over 500 applications to 14,000
customers in over 100 countries.  So, even if you are a hired gun like
I am, you will be working on "customer facing" software.  That is true
for 98% of our programmers.  And I would venture to say that is true
for most software houses in any industry.

The author also mentions Peter Drucker and his cost center/profit
center terminology.  My company adopted such a breakdown sometime in
the early 90's.  Now maybe in some places, programmers may be in a
cost center and those in developement probably fall into that
category.  But once something is developed and goes on the market, the
programmers supporting that software are now in a profit center.  The
author says, "Engineers in particular are usually very highly paid
Cost Centers, which sets MBAï¿½s optimization antennae to twitching.
This is what brings us wonderful ideas like outsourcing, which is
ï¿½Letï¿½s replace really expensive Cost Centers who do some magic which
we kinda need but donï¿½t really care about with less expensive Cost
Centers in a lower wage country.  Nobody ever outsources Profit
Centers."

Another ridiculous idea born of probably never having worked at a
software house.  As I stated,  before, once software is developed and
goes on the market, programmers supporting it are part of the profit
center.  I have seen, for a brief time, entire applications' support
outsourced to programmers in India.  Once it was realized that the
quality of work was not as good, the support was moved back to the US
and the Indian company used for more mundane tasks like coding
conversions or installation assistance, or upgrades due to regulation
changes but this is done under the guidance of a US based person.

In short, maybe the blogger had some experiences as an Engineer and
what he writes might be true, I don't know.  I've only worked as a
programmer and am more than happy to identify myself as such.

Consider the following:

01 numeric-fields.
     12 fld-A pic 9(8) value 12345678.

*> swap the 2 elemental fields without using any other storage...
                                                         fld-A 
fld-B
                                                     12345678      87654321
      subtract  fld-A from fld-B        12345678      75308643
      add        f ld-B to     fld-A        87654321     75308643
      subtract fld-A  from  fld-B        87654321     12345678

The actual result in fld-B would be negative, so the fields need to be 
unsigned.

All very well for numbers, but what about Alpha characters?

There are several ways I can think of for going about this, but none of them 
are really elegant and they all require REDEFINES. If anybody has an elegant 
solution please post it for the sake of completeness and our edification :-)

If it were my problem I would revert to using Boolean functions (AND, OR, 
NOT, and XOR) on the two strings. But that presupposes your compiler 
supports those functions (both MicroFocus and Fujitsu COBOLs do) and it is 
really an Assembler solution dressed up clumsily in COBOL.

Fortunately, in this day and age we don't have to even think about 
conserving storage in this way and it is just an intellectual exercise.
