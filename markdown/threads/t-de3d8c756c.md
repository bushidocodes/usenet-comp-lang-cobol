Thank you Marko.

This unmoderated group is one of the last bastions of free speech on the
planet. No sponsors, no moderators/censors and no constraining rules.

That makes it precious and if nobody posts to it, we will probably lose
it...

The fact is that, in the glory days of COBOL, it was a lively place where
people with a shared interest came to talk about COBOL and then went off
topic with far more interesting posts about stuff in general. :-)

Although I have been taking a beating here for over 20 years, it has
(mostly) been fun and I have enjoyed it.

I beat the drum for OO COBOL when it was released, (and got rubbished for it
:-) - look where OO is now...), beat the drum for replacing Indexed access
with Relational databases, (and got rubbished for that - look where all the
data is now...It was the recognition that people would need to move their
indexed data to RDBMS which led to the writing of the Migration Toolset, and
the refactoring of old COBOL into a tiered architecture. I was told that it
would be impossible to automate this refactoring and generation of a Data
Access Layer because of the infinite variety of styles of COBOL it would
need to accomodate, when I first proposed it here - today it is 100%
automatic; a few mouse clicks. :-)).

I urged COBOL guys to expand their skill sets (and was met with stony
silence by most people here - sadly, most of the "Everything I want to do I
can do in COBOL" crowd are no longer working...), I argued good and bad IT
management and was saddened by some of the appalling experiences people
reported here. I argued religion (respectfully) with the deeply committed
until they realised that no minds would be changed (it was still some of the
most interesting discussion I've had in this group). I had my mind blown by
the sheer depth of knowledge about COBOL and about things in general by some
of the people here; it made the effort worthwhile and the time well spent.

However, in 2014, the use of blogs has largely replaced forums like this
one. I considered starting my own blog but to do it properly requires more
time than I have available and I don't want to do a half-assed blog... when
you can write an article and find that many thousands of people read it, it
is quite humbling and makes you want to ensure that what you say is of high
quality :-) . That takes time and thought and both of those commodities are
valuable to me at the moment.

(I still haven't ruled out a personal blog through the PRIMA site, maybe in
the new Lounge section (when we get it finished...:-)), but there are many
demands on my time and I'm engaged in some massive effort at the moment
(bringing LINQ to the Data Access Layer of the PRIMA Migration Toolset -
this means getting C# and COBOL to build and exchange data buffers so that
LINQ (using C#) becomes available to COBOL applications. The protoypes
(which contain no embedded SQL) have proven that the LINQ performance is
many times better than the equivalent ESQL in COBOL, and there are no
limitations on connections, (as there are with the Fujitsu PowerCOBOL
runtime which limits the number of SQL CONNECTs you can make at any
moment )... all of this will keep me busy for the next few months.)

With the decline of interest in COBOL and the shift of emphasis away from
Usenet, the energy of this group has slowly dissipated and interesting posts
here (at least, interesting to me...) are rare now.

Although COBOL is still a part of my daily working life (and will be for
some time) there are other things I want to move on to. Mobile development
(probably... - the development tools are getting better all the time and I
have experience now with XAML, javascript, and C#). I also want to build
part of the PRIMA site into a "COBOL friendly" place where the scattered
remnants of the 20th century COBOL programmer community can come for
exchange of ideas and amusement. :-) (I also plan to cater for 21st century
people who are required to deal with COBOL legacy... there is much to do...)

Given all of the above, don't look for frequent posts here from me in the
coming year.

However, I 'll keep an eye on this forum and respond if anything catches my
attention :-)

Pete.
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."
