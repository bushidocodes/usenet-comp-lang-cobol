"MERANT grants you a perpetual, transferable, non-exclusive, royalty-free right to reproduce and distribute the Generated Code in

adds significant and primary functionality to the Generated Code.  Your license to reproduce and distribute any Generated Code
generated by you using the Software prior to the termination of this Agreement shall survive any termination of this Agreement.
Copyright (C) 2000 Micro Focus International Ltd. All rights reserved."

Now read this definition:

"ob�ject code n

In my opinion, the situation is *very clear*.  When I compile a free standing, statically linked EXE program, then the WHOLE EXE is
"Generated Code" (object code), is it not?  What other functions are in there, except those necessary to translate my COBOL language
program into binary executable?  If Micro Focus chooses to copy some of those routines from a runtime library instead of doing
direct translation, that is merely a code generation technique, not a difference in substance.  Even assemblers can have *some*
runtime library code.  Often file I/O code, and if nothing else, initialization and program exit code.

This is the 'Bottom Line' as I see it:

a) In what other industry do you pay fees to a tool vendor in order to distribute a thing created using those tools?  Can you
imagine every user of a piece of furniture paying royalties to the manufacturers of the saws, hammers, staplers and other tools used
to create the furniture?  What about the tools used to create the fabric?  Imagine an automobile!  Such a concept is absurd on the
face of it, and should be rejected by any user of any "tool" product.  No other industry could enforce such a rediculous thing, and
just because enforcement is possible in software, why should we put up with it?  The end user of a compiler is the developer, *not*
the end user of applications written using the compiler.

b) My own Net Express compiler license was just trashed three times in 12 days, while both installing (one machine) and uninstalling
(two machines) Net Express SP 1.  In addition, uninstalling SP 1 removed the icons from the Net Express Start Menu directory from
both machines!  Now, Micro Focus is insisting that not only I, but my customers as well, suffer this outrageous, buggy, greed
motivated practice of theirs, applied to the software I create using Net Express.  Even if the runtime licenses were free, this
problem would still exist.  This directly attacks my reputation as a software developer, without *any* benefit to me, or any
recourse on my part, other than moving to a different COBOL compiler, or abandoning COBOL altogether.  Either course of action would
have a significant impact on my company and my clients.

c) I have invested something like 20 years of my career into developing applications using Micro Focus COBOL.  One of my currently
running systems is over 17 years old, another is 15 years old, both originally written under MS-DOS.  Now Micro Focus are changing
the rules on me, shortly *after* I paid them two years maintenance with the understanding that I could distribute statically linked
EXE files without run time licensing.  This is unconsionable at best.  They have changed my business model significantly without my
consent or approval, and certainly not to my advantage.  My feelings are very clear: I have been BETRAYED and SCREWED.

d) We've talked a lot in these newsgroup about the demise of COBOL.  Personally, I do not see how COBOL can survive long term,
unless some drastic changes are made.  Most of the industry as a whole doesn't have a clue *why* COBOL is a good language for
business applications, and why others aren't.  Virtually nobody is promoting COBOL, and the compiler vendors are doing their best to
eliminate small independent developers like myself.  Yes, no doubt COBOL will continue running on big iron for a long time, through
inertia.  But most people don't realize that, at least in the U.S., more than 75% of people work in companies with fewer than 20
employees.  We small companies, collectively, hire more than *three times* the number of people as companies with 21 employees or
more.  We little guys *are* the larger market.  Not many people have invested more time in COBOL than I have, or would grieve its
passing more than I.  But I am seriously considering moving to a different development platform, as a matter of self defense.
Unfortunately, there *is* no language that I can use and have confidence the programs will last 15 years without significant
modifications or even rewrites.  For example, there is no Microsoft product in existence that one could have used to write a major
application even 10 years ago, and it still be running on their latest OS today, with little more than recompiling to get here.
Looks like my clients will have to pay the price, right along with me.  I am not a happy camper.

Hello Jimmy,

Glad to be baaaaaaaaaaaack. ;-)

Micro Focus makes it plain that after installing Net Express 3.1 SP 1,
*anything* compiled using NE requires a runtime fee.  Now, consider the
ramifications.  If every object compiled with compilers X, Y and Z require
runtime licensing.  Now consider a project developed using compiler X, also
using object modules compiled with compilers Y and Z.  What does THAT do for
the OO reusability model (or non-OO for that matter)?  Uck!

I'm a very big believer in 'principles'.  At least partly because, but perhaps
entirely because, we live in a causative universe, everything, and I mean
everything, works because of 'laws' or 'principles' that are objective and
universally effective, though obviously not always universally known,
recognized and understood.  This applies not only to the base physical
universe (e.g. F=MA),  but the vastly more complex and subtle (therefore much
less obvious) laws of human affairs and endeavors (two obvious examples: you
can't spend more money than you make indefinitely, and abusive acts engender
hate).  Most of us in technical careers are more familiar with physical laws.
But those who work in politics or psychology, for example, are well aware that
there are laws governing human relations.

Naturally, there are business principles as well.  People who understand these
principles and apply them effectively will make lots of money.  Those who
don't, won't.  Sometimes these principles work rapidly, sometimes very slowly,
but always inexorably.  Let me give you an example.  Circa 1979, Apple was
arguably the most successful microcomputer vendor in the world.  They were
doing lots of things right, and were reaping the benefits, both in money and a
fanatically loyal customer base.  So successful, in fact, that IBM, the then
largest computer manufacturer in the world, broke an inviolable rule of theirs
when they emulated Apple's open architecture policy when introducing the IBM
PC in 1981.  But in 1979 Apple broke a fundamental business rule; they started
playing hardball with both dealers and customers.  When this happened, I knew
without doubt, and said so many times, that Apple would pay dearly for this.
It took 20 years, but eventually had to be bailed out by arch-enemy Bill Gates
to the tune of $350 million.  No one not familiar with the Apple community can
understand what a humiliating circumstance this must have been.  Playing
hardball with customers, therefore alienating them, will eventually kill a
business, though it may take time.

I am personally convinced that when we break any of these principles it will
*always* cost us in some way eventually.  I am also convinced that charging
for runtime fees breaks both business and ethical principles.  First, because
if you examine the costs of using tools, across all human experience, you will
find that charging the end user of a product created using a tool just isn't
done, except in this instance, and possibly a few other very, very rare
instances, though I can't think of any.  Secondly, it simply isn't fair,
especially when it is applied after a tool has been in use for some time,
which is certainly the case of Micro Focus.  In such cases, it is simply
another form of extortion.  Both the developer and the end user have made
business decisions based on certain circumstances, then the tool vendor
changes the rules of the game *after* both developer and end users are
committed.  I will agree that a runtime licensing fee *can* be "fair", but
only if it is applied in such a way that, to the end user of the product
created to use the runtime, it is a strictly one-time charge that can be
absorbed as part of the purchase price,  and when they require an upgrade, say
for running on another platform.

The interesting thing about these principles is that they are completely
objective, and they work whether we understand them or not, or even know about
them.  If a compiler vendor want to charge an exorbitant price for their
compiler, fine, free market forces will control that.  If they want to charge
for support and upgrades, fine, R&D needs to be funded, and you get benefit
for your money.

Yes, I have heard the arguments "We need the runtime revenues or we will go
out of business" until I am sick of it.  Absolute rubbish!  The true statement
would be "We need the runtime revenues or we will go out of business *using
our current business model, which we created by figuring in runtime fees*."
Does anybody think that the market would (or even could) let absolutely
*essential* items such as compilers, go away, simply because no runtime fees
are charged?  Duh!  The market will pay what is necessary, until the cost
becomes so high that other alternatives make more sense.  Does anybody think
that COBOL is any different in this respect from any other computer language?
Duh!  COBOL is the most stable computer language ever designed, and has been
so for longer than most other languages have existed.  In the last decade or
so, where has most of the cost of maintaining COBOL compilers been?  It *has*
to have been in two areas: updating runtimes for new platforms and adding new
features ala the new COBOL spec.  In the first case, I certainly don't object
to paying for the ability to now compile to a 32 bit Windows platform instead
of 16 bit DOS or 16 bit Windows platforms, for example.  In the second case,
why should end users of developed software have to pay for elaborate and
(obviously) costly features such as OO COBOL (sorry Jimmy!  ;-), that may
never be used by more than a tiny fraction of COBOL developers anyway?  Even
you real OO advocates have to admit that OO COBOL will almost certainly never
be more than a small percentage of all COBOL, regardless of what it's merits
may be (See, I'm trying to be nice :-).

Bottom line: Vendors, charge us whatever is necessary for the compiler,
support and upgrades for you to stay in business.  We can plan for that and
pass the costs along to our customers in the price of our products.  Break up
the compiler into levels (e.g. VB, Visual Studio, MS Office), costing more for
more features.  The market will then determine which COBOL vendors survive,
based on merit and value.  But please, don't make *our* customers subject to
your whims, and please stop telling us that runtime fees are necessary.  It is
at the very best an ignorant statement, or disingenuous.  If you doubt it,
there are many compiler vendors (e.g. PowerBASIC, smaller market than COBOL,
few 'deep pocket' developers) who produce excellent compilers of very high
quality, have been profitable for years, and never charge runtime fees.
Q.E.D.
