Mike,
I assume that in your reply, EWD refers to Edsger W. Dijkstra.
I had just packed for an office move, but I had to go find the Dijkstra Letter
to the Editor entitled "Go To Statement Considered Harmful" in the
Communications of the ACM, Volume II, Number 3, March 1968. He said, and I
quote:

Editor:
For a number of years I have been familiar with the observation that the
quality of programmers is a decreasing function of the density of go to
statements in the programs they produce. More recently I discovered why the use
of the go to statement has such disastrous effects, and I became convinced that
the go to statement should be abolished from all "higher level" programming
languages (i.e., everything, except, perhaps, plain machine code).

I have not added any emphasis or punctuation to this quote, and I hope I have
typed it 100% accurately. The letter goes on for quite awhile about the reasons
for this statement.

How do you square this with what your posting attributes to Dr. Dijkstra? Did
he recant at some later point in time? If so, many of us are not aware of it.

I'm not interested in restarting the "Go" debate, just making sure that people
understand that Dijkstra was adamantly opposed to the use of the construct
PERIOD. There is a remark that, because the language contains Go, it MUST be
useful in some situations. COBOL contains ALTER; do you wish to defend it as
well? My personal opinion.

Colin Campbell
