I thought that this might interest the readers of this note - from today's
(February 21, 2001) MERANT Press Release,

"... MERANT's Egility e-business software products revenue grew 18% at
constant currency exchange rates and made up 59% of MERANT's total revenues
for the quarter. Revenue from Cobol solutions, which represented 38% of our
revenue, declined 18% at constant currency exchange rates due to the
expected decline in demand for Cobol and mainframe-related products. ...

Forward-Looking Statements  ...
 Future results will be difficult to predict as MERANT transforms its
business strategy to provide e-business solutions and away from certain of
its past primary markets, including the market for Cobol and
mainframe-related products. ..."

I guess we could look at this a number of ways:
1. The market for "pure play" COBOL products is decreasing.
2. The market for Legacy Renewal products (i.e. web enablement) is
increasing.
3. Merant is getting some serious competition in the COBOL market and it's
starting to hurt.
4. Merant is refocusing their business from selling Compilers to selling
EBusiness solutions.

From what I've seen, number 4 is the most accurate although I think its
probably a mistake.  It's a strategy they started during the e-boom and now
that we are in an e-crash it may not pay off.

Yes. Decrease in 'techs' teaching COBOL. I've ascertained  that it's still going
strong in Texas, (Thane Hubbell). Against that it has been dropped in some
institutes in Australia, (Ken Foskey); will be dropped in Limerick, Eire, in two
years, (Michael Sheehan). With possible exception of one private school here in
Calgary, (which I haven't checked out yet), the last 'tech' that was teaching
COBOL dropped it three years ago, "With no gnashing of teeth from the commercial
community". Will Price of objectz.com did an 'introductory' on OO in Quebec -
now that institute no longer teaches COBOL.

If it isn't taught, how do students learn it, and where is it going to be used ?

(Meanwhile of course, the folks here in the clc group, make the occasional bitch
about what COBOL 'should be doing' and still keep pecking away like ostriches
looking for grubs in the sand. Perhaps the older ones are just waiting for
retirement and don't care).

December 2002 ????. (Please don't get me going about J4 and WG4 !)
