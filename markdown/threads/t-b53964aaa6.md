A couple of weeks ago, Tommy Wouters asked for an implementation in Cobol of
the Game of Life.

For anyone interested, I have a Life "Engine" (by which I mean a no-frills
working program!) which you could use as a starting point for you own
experimentation, or as food for thought. It compiles on Fujitsu Cobol "out
of the box", though it'll probably need a couple of modifications for other
compilers.

If you'd like a copy of the source, e-mail me. If there's more than a
flicker of interest, I'll post it here -- it's not *that* big (or celever!)
-- so watch this space. Only please be patient -- I only visit at weekends,
and my newsservers lagging a little at the moment.


Explanation
===========

For anyone who doesn't yet now what is meant by The Game of Life, here is an
excerpt from A.K.Dewdney's excellent _The Armchair Universe_ (used without
permission, but I don't think he'll mind!):

==== AKD (from "World Four: Life in Automata"): ====

There is also a discrete two-dimensional space called Life, a game invented
by John Horton Conway, the well-known University of Cambridge mathematician.
Readers may well remember this engaging exercise from "Mathematical Games"
columns in SCIENTIFIC AMERICAN by Martin Gardner. In October 1983, Brian
Hayes described in "Computer Recreations" in the same magazine how the game
could be realized in a spreadsheet program. Life is an infinite
two-dimensional lattice of square cells whose states are influenced by the
states of neighboring cells. Time is also discrete and from one tick of a
cosmic clock to the next each cell is either alive or dead depending on a
set of very simple rules:

o If a cell is dead at time (t), it comes alive at time (t+1) if, and only
if, exactly three of its eight neighbors are alive at time (t).
o If a cell is alive at time (t), it dies at time (t+1) if, and only if,
fewer than two or more than three neighbors are alive at time (t).

With this set of rules everywhere in effect on Life's lattice, an initial
configuration of live cells may grow interminably, fall into a cyclic
pattern, or eventually die off. Through more than a decade of
experimentation by Life enthusiasts it has become clear that Life is far
more complicated than anyone had thought ...

==== AKD (ends) =====================================

########################################################################
IDENTIFICATION DIVISION.
PROGRAM-ID. GAMELIFE.
******************************************************************
* *
* GAMELIFE: Game of Life. *
* *
* *
* PURPOSE *
* ------- *
* Implement John Horton Conway's Game of Life in COBOL. *
* *
* *
* LANGUAGE *
* -------- *
* Should work without modification on Fujitsu Cobol. *
* *
* *
* SUB-PROGRAMS *
* ------------ *
* Calls ZZRANDOM (limited Random number generator). *
* *
* *
* RULES *
* ----- *
* The 'classic' rules for Life are as follows: *
* *
* At t+1: *
* *
* A live cell with fewer than 2 neighbours will die from *
* loneliness. *
* A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbours will survive. *
* A cell will be born in an unoccupied space with exactly 3 *
* neighbours. *
* A live cell with 4 neighbours or more will die from *
* overcrowding. *
* *
* If you wish to vary these rules, see the condition-names *
* (level-88s) under WS-COUNT-OF-NEIGHBOURS, which is how *
* the rules are implemented. *
* *
* *
* NOTES *
* ----- *
* There is currently no way to terminate the program -- I *
* have been zapping it with the Task Manager! *
* *
* The screen-handling in this program is a bit strange. It *
* may be something to do with the fact that screen-handling *
* is not my speciality. But it works. *
* *
* I had to insert a delay loop to slow the display of the *
* screens down. Although it displays nicely on my machine *
* at around 2 screens (i.e. generations) per second, you may *
* wish to adjust the delay, and you can do so in the para *
* 21-MAIN-LOOP. *
* *
* *
* STUFF YOU MIGHT LIKE TO DO (in no particular order) *
* --------------------------------------------------- *
* Make it work in graphics mode. *
* *
* Improve the screen-handling. *
* *
* Display info about generation #. *
* *
* Allow user to quit by hitting a key. *
* *
* Allow user to set up start generation manually (with *
* cursor keys and space bar to toggle dead/alive). *
* *
* Allow user to save start-up screens. *
* *
* Allow user to specify varying screen-sizes. *
* *
* etc etc. *
* *
* *
******************************************************************

20-MAIN-PROCESS.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* The Finished flag is initialized, but as yet nothing is *
* coded to set it true. So this is an infinite loop. *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*

21-MAIN-LOOP.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* Pause for a bit so screens aren't displayed too quickly. *
* Display Current Screen. Having displayed it, it becomes *
* the Previous Screen from which we will calculate the Next *
* Generation ("t+1"). *
* *

PERFORM 90-DELAY-LOOP
1000
TIMES.

MOVE WS-CURRENT-SCREEN
TO WS-PREVIOUS-SCREEN.

VARYING WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
FROM 1
BY 1
UNTIL WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
> WS-ROWS.

*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* Display the Current Screen/Generation. *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*

FROM 1

24-GENERATE-CELL.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* Calculate new state for an individual cell. *
* *
* Neighbours are counted, then the rules are applied (see the *
* data item WS-COUNT-OF-NEIGHBOURS and its level-88 values for *
* details). *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
PERFORM 25-COUNT-NEIGHBOURS.

IF 88-PREVIOUSLY-ALIVE
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
IF 88-CELL-DIES
MOVE SPACES
TO WS-CURRENT-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
ELSE
MOVE WS-PREVIOUS-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
TO WS-CURRENT-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
END-IF
ELSE
IF 88-CELL-IS-BORN
MOVE WS-DISPLAY-CHAR
TO WS-CURRENT-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
ELSE
MOVE WS-PREVIOUS-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
TO WS-CURRENT-CELL
(WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB)
END-IF
END-IF.


25-COUNT-NEIGHBOURS.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* The 8 adjacent cells are considered to be neighbours. *
* *
* Since Life Space is cyclic, some of the neighbours of *
* marginal cells (i.e. those at edges and corners) are found *
* at opposite positions on the screen. *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
MOVE ZEROES
TO WS-COUNT-OF-NEIGHBOURS.

PERFORM 26-INSPECT-ROWS
VARYING WS-ROW-OFFSET
FROM WS-MINUS-1
BY 1
UNTIL WS-ROW-OFFSET
> 1.


26-INSPECT-ROWS.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* For each neighbouring row, inpect all cells. *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
PERFORM 27-INSPECT-CELL
VARYING WS-COLUMN-OFFSET
FROM -1
BY 1
UNTIL WS-COLUMN-OFFSET
> 1.


27-INSPECT-CELL.
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
* *
* Address a neighbour, applying correction for cell at the *
* edge of the screen. *
* *
* Note that the reference cell doesn't qualify as its own *
* neighbour, so it's not counted. *
* *
*----------------------------------------------------------------*
EVALUATE WS-ROW-OFFSET

WHEN -1

IF WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
= 1
MOVE WS-ROWS
TO WS-PREVIOUS-ROW-SUB
ELSE
MOVE WS-CURRENT-ROW-SUB
TO WS-PREVIOUS-ROW-SUB
SUBTRACT 1
FROM WS-PREVIOUS-ROW-SUB
END-IF

EVALUATE WS-COLUMN-OFFSET

WHEN -1

IF WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB
= 1
MOVE WS-COLUMNS
TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
ELSE
MOVE WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB
TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
SUBTRACT 1
FROM WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
END-IF

WHEN 0

MOVE WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB
TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB

WHEN 1

IF WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB
= WS-COLUMNS
MOVE 1
TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
ELSE
MOVE WS-CURRENT-COLUMN-SUB
TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB

TO WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
END-IF

END-EVALUATE

FROM WS-PREVIOUS-COLUMN-SUB
END-IF

IDENTIFICATION DIVISION.
PROGRAM-ID. ZZRANDOM.
******************************************************************
* *
* ZZRANDOM: *
* *
* *
* *
* PURPOSE *
* ------- *
* Return a random integer in a given range. *
* *
* IMPORTANT NOTE: A frequency plot of the distribution of *
* random numbers returned by this routine in the 0-500 range *
* showed a number of anomolous peaks in the distribution. *
* *
* Therefore, do not use this routine if an even distribution *
* is required! *
* *
* *
* INTERFACE *
* --------- *
* CALL 'ZZRANDOM' *
* USING WS-RANDOM-NUMBER PIC S9(9) COMP *
* WS-RANDOM-RANGE. PIC S9(9) COMP *
* *
* where WS-RANDOM-NUMBER gets the result in the range *
* 0..WS-RANDOM-RANGE. *
* *
* *
* NOTES *
* ----- *
* If a negative number is supplied as the range, the absolute *
* value is used, and no error is flagged. *
* *
* SYMBOLIC CONSTANTS may not be portable. If these present *
* any problems, substitute WS items with a picture of S9(9) *
* COMP and apply the appropriate value. *
* *
* *

< ZEROES

IDENTIFICATION DIVISION.
PROGRAM-ID. GAMELIFE.
******************************************************************
* *
* GAMELIFE: Game of Life. *
* *
* *
* PURPOSE *
* ------- *
* Implement John Horton Conway's Game of Life in COBOL. *
* *
* *
* LANGUAGE *
* -------- *
* Should work without modification on Fujitsu Cobol. *
* *
* *
* SUB-PROGRAMS *
* ------------ *
* Calls ZZRANDOM (limited Random number generator). *
* *
* *
* RULES *
* ----- *
* The 'classic' rules for Life are as follows: *
* *
* At t+1: *
* *
* A live cell with fewer than 2 neighbours will die from *
* loneliness. *
* A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbours will survive. *
* A cell will be born in an unoccupied space with exactly 3 *
* neighbours. *
* A live cell with 4 neighbours or more will die from *
* overcrowding. *
* *
* If you wish to vary these rules, see the condition-names *
* (level-88s) under WS-COUNT-OF-NEIGHBOURS, which is how *
* the rules are implemented. *
* *
* *
* NOTES *
* ----- *
* There is currently no way to terminate the program -- I *
* have been zapping it with the Task Manager! *
* *
* The screen-handling in this program is a bit strange. It *
* may be something to do with the fact that screen-handling *
* is not my speciality. But it works. *
* *
* I had to insert a delay loop to slow the display of the *
* screens down. Although it displays nicely on my machine *
* at around 2 screens (i.e. generations) per second, you may *
* wish to adjust the delay, and you can do so in the para *
* 21-MAIN-LOOP. *
* *
* *
* STUFF YOU MIGHT LIKE TO DO (in no particular order) *
* --------------------------------------------------- *
* Make it work in graphics mode. *
* *
* Improve the screen-handling. *
* *
* Display info about generation #. *
* *
* Allow user to quit by hitting a key. *

* *
* Allow user to save start-up screens. *
* *
* Allow user to specify varying screen-sizes. *
