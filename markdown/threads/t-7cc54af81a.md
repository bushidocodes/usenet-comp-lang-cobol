I have always been pleased that, at least in my experience, there is
less sex bias in IT than most fields.  I don't think I have ever seen
a shop large enough to be statistically significant that had less than
about 1/3 female programmers.  I agree with you, the women programmers
that I have known have been, on the average, just as competent as the
men.  But on average the women, with notable exceptions, do not drive
quite as hard as the men do.  Not that they don't do a good job.  It is
a difference in attitude, not ability, and it isn't drastic.  But one
of the hardest drivers I know is a middle aged woman, who sometimes
works such long hours that it affects her health.  And she doesn't have
to do it, no one pushes her but herself; she doesn't even get overtime.
She actually intimidates some of the men in the shop, with her drive
and ability. :-)

At any rate, anyone who has both male and female children and thinks
that the psychological/emotional differences between male and female
are all environmental is either blind or has the perception of a potato.
Though individuals vary enormously of course, as a group, females tend
not to enjoy the more 'techie' subjects, such as math or science, in
school or at work, as much as males.  There is concern right now in the
medical community, for example, that women are not entering the more
technically demanding and esoteric branches of medicine at nearly the
rate of males, and they are needed and would be welcome there.  Males
and females tend to be intelligent in different ways, and interested in
different things, from the very beginning.  There is vast common ground,
of course, and all individuals vary greatly.  Though males tend to enjoy
more and be better at math than females, some of the best mathematicians
are women.  I was amused at a study several years ago by a rabid feminist
psychologist who studied young school children to prove, once and for
all, that there was no built-in math bias between sexes; it was all
environmental.  After six years, she admitted through her clenched teeth
that yes, there really was a built-in bias, that males did generally
like math better, and were generally better at it than females, and
males generally tended to have better 'spatial perception'. :-)  Females
are just as smart, of course, but they tend to be smart in not quite the
same way as males.  There was another interesting study done on babies
from two or three weeks to two or three months old.  Part of the study
was to put the babies individually in an enclosed space where the only
thing they could see was a video screen.  A pretty, colorful picture was
displayed on the screen, and there was a string attached to the baby's
hand such that every time the baby pulled the string the picture on the
screen changed.  The babies were observed until it was clear that the
baby had learned to pull the string and change the picture.  After a
while, the string was switched off so the baby could no longer change
the picture.  The films of this are very amusing.  Almost invariably,
the female babies would start to cry, but the male babies would get
angry and start jerking at the string with all their might.  Does that
sound familiar to anyone? :-)

We're just different, folks.  Neither is 'better' or 'smarter' than the
other, but males and females are different in many more ways than the
obvious physical ones, and the learned differences are trivial compared
to the built-in ones.  Anyone who has been married for more than a week
and doesn't know this is also blind or has the perception of a potato. ;-)
