That's just silly.

"Readability" is completely subjective, and I have seen COBOL programs
written by English speakers which were pretty "unreadable".

Programmers can "hide their tracks" in ANY language and COBOL is no more
immune than any other language.

A PL/1 programmer will argue that the compact preciseness of the language
makes it very "readable", so will someone who writes in APL.

You might as well say that Swahili is "unreadable" or Mandarin Chinese is
"unreadable" when, in fact, the problem is that you have never learned to
read them.

In the early days of COBOL, "readability" was touted as a big sales point by
marketers and it impressed bean counters who had no programming knowledge.

(Showing a contrived COBOL program to an accountant helped to remove some of
the mystery and consequent fear which he had about programming and
programmers and made him more likely to approve the purchase of a COBOL
compiler...)

Over the years it became apparent that people could write crap COBOL just as
easily as they could write any other language and the much vaunted
"readability" could only be achieved with unnecessary verbosity. So the
implied benefit of making maintenance easier, in reality, just led to
thousands more lines to have to wade through. COBOL consistently requires
more LOC than modern equivalents. That makes it more expensive to develop
and maintain.

WELL WRITTEN COBOL, may be more "readable" than some other languages, but so
what? The only advantage in readability is ease of maintenance but, to a
trained programmer, ANY language they are trained in is readable, and the
less of it you are required to write, the less chance of error and the
easier it is to scan and find problems.

The tragic flaw here is in thinking that the language you natively speak can
be used for "programming". A programming language has critical advantages
over a linguistic language when it comes to programming. Certainly, you may
feel a bit more "comfortable" if the language you are programming in
resembles the language you use to communicate but that is a two-edged sword.
You start introducing the sloppy thought processes engendered by the
slackness of normal language and suddenly you are writing "NOT A OR B" and
wondering why the results are wrong.

"Readability" WAS important (but not to the degree it was promoted) when
everything revolved around source code.

These days many of us use objects and components for which we may not even
HAVE source code. (The Windows OS running the machine you are probably
reading this on has well over a 1000 components you can use in your own
programs; the .Net Framework has several HUNDRED thousand. You don't have
the source for any of them, but it doesn't matter because you are never
going to maintain them. "Readability" is moot.) Even in this environment,
wise programmers will still write readable and clear code, but it isn't
necessarily readable and clear in a linguistic sense...

"Readability", as an advantage for COBOL, is simply an old chestnut based on
a marketing myth.

Let it go.

Pete.
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."

Ah, yes. I remember it well. Intended to be all things to all people.

PL/I - "Program Language International" ... except that when it was not
seized upon by the international community it was renamed to be "PL/1" -
"Program Language 1st version".

Most people to this day, when speaking of it say:"PL/1", not "PL/I".

Later, the idiocy of this particular marketing was realized and the name
officially reverted to PL/I but by then, it was pretty much dead in the
water. That's why we never saw PL/2.

(Somewhere, in storage, I have two manuals for this language, both printed
by IBM a couple of years apart, one with "PL/1" on the cover and the other
with "PL/I"...)

The sadness of all this is that PL/1 was actually an outstanding programming
language and, when I look at it today, I realize it was ahead of its time.

Bill's statement that: "nothing that has come out since does that particular
job as well." is, of course, arguable, and PL/1 is not the only candidate
that could throw its hat in the ring.

The real strength of COBOL is in batch processing on a single centralized
processor (which is what it was designed for) and PL/1 certainly does that
every bit as well as COBOL. So does REXX, and there are arguably others
also.

But even COBOL's primary strength of processing large volumes of data
quickly in batch, has now been surpassed by new languages like HADOOP which
can process a very large variety and volume of data structures (not just
"files") across multiple processors, at speeds that leave COBOL gasping.

The world has moved on. COBOL has been surpassed in transaction processing
and batch processing but that doesn't make it any less useful for people who
want to use it.

As noted previously, it will continue as a niche commercial programming
language for some time yet, but the glory days are gone and won't be coming
back.

Pete.

"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."
