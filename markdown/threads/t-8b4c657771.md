It bugs me that you seem to support not just the reasoning needed to resolve
confusing issues on one's own (good), but the logic that creates and disseminates
confusion in the first place (bad).  I know that you don't come right out and say
this.  It is implicit to some degree thus your apparent (tacit) complicity.

A TEXTBOOK or PRIMER on a subject that is not clear, well written, proofread,
professional, thorough and complete is a disservice to all concerned.  I feel this
way about "Cobol From Micro to Mainframe"  Grauer, Villar & Buss 3rd edition, �1991.
This book has errors of fact, misplaced diagrams, is incomplete in explanations,
misleading on some topics, and not very clear on others.  There is bad grammar,
mispelling, incomplete index, missing topics, etc.  I have to credit our instructor
for helping to weed past some the problems created by the book.  If your professional
life entirely depended on RTFM and this was the manual, you'd be in crisis!  It may
be that no better book has ever been written, doubtful, but there is no doubt at all
the first directive for a textbook, especially a primary one, is to elucidate not
obfuscate. 
The referenced book cost more than US$75 and is used in our course for 3 quarters of
introductory Cobol.  The discussion of 88 levels provided has not really filled me in
on the topic to where I can use them successfully, and 88's appear not to be that
complicated.....  This is true on a number of basic techniques that are 'covered' in
the book.
TS

write the publisher, it's basically  unsupported unless I research many problems with
the book.  That's doing their work for them, for free, with no acknowledgement.
With the likely chance that they will ignore me.  I was ignored in discussing this
with the department at my school.  They have some other pretty rotten books in their
curriculum.  Take the Operating Systems book they use:  it was published in 1992.
Full of errors in fact, presentation, current use, the line taken by the department
is that it's the best available.  It's simply not that;  a few minutes online turned
up much better texts.  But the discussion was over!  Case made.  

The book for networking concepts was so out of date.  They discuss OS/2Warp as a
major innovation, and NT as a sidelight.  (Try to find an OS/2 shop.  Try not to find
an NT shop.)  Unix was presented as something that exists but is not common....  This
is so inaccurate  and unrealistic as to be laughable.  The chapter on the osi model
didn't manage to make it understandable in 40 pages.  I saw a single web page that
made it all clear in 2 paper pages worth of presentation.  Let's not even get to the
subject of subnets.  We didn't in class because nobody could follow the course by
using the book.  This gem came from 'QUE'.  I've always had so much respect for that
publisher too!  I guess there can be a rotten apple in any barrel.

I've done a lot of plumbing and carpentry.  It's true, that like in most service
fields, you have to suit the customer's expressed wishes.  But to succeed in the job
and thus the field, you have to work to code, and more than that, you have to produce
a working product in the end, on both schedule and budget, which may not include
every design element or feature orignally expressed by the client.  This may vary by
method, placement, inclusion, etc.  While some aspects may be held as constants (no
exposed pipes, buy douglas fir doors from here, use oak moldings), some must be

schedule; oak is fine, but painted moldings made of oak will not be smooth and will
