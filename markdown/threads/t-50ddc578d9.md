Given some of the recent threads about "open stuff",  I thought I would pass
along the following *OBSCURE* information that was just sent out to the J4
distribution list.  Before you try and fight your way thru it, I think it is
talking about combining a couple of "ANSI" (or ISO?) committees.  It also
talks about the status of some of the existing projects and Standards.  I

it deals with (among other things) some of the

 - Language Independent Datatypes

From: Daniel Gillman, gillman <at> census.gov

Dear L8 Members, T2 Members, and Interested Parties,

As many of you know, there is an effort underway to try to merge L8 and
T2.  The merged group will be known as L8.  L8 is willing to accept any
current T2 project for which there is active interest and support.  This
means there needs to be 2 or 3 people who have the expertise and time to
devote to developing,  maintaining, reviewing, or revising each project.

Please review the list of T2 projects attached below (Murray Freeman
supplied this information).  The projects are broken into 3 categories:
schema, syntax, and SC22/WG11.  If you can support and actively
participate in the work of one or more of these projects, please let me
know NO LATER THAN 17 SEPTEMBER 1999 via email.  Please make sure you
list each project you can support.

For your information, L8 meets informally monthly in Washington, usually
at the National Institute for Standards and Technology.  We always have
a conference telephone link to the meeting and often have video
conferencing set up.  Both have proven to be effective ways to allow
participation for people who can't travel to the meetings.  The L8
plenary sessions (3 or 4 times per year) also use these links, although
members should try to physically attend at least one plenary each year.

At the NCITS meeting last week, the status of T2 and the possible merger
with L8 came up.  NCITS is very concerned about the vialbility of T2.
So, we need to decide which projects can be supported in L8 as soon as
possible.  T3 expressed interest in taking the ASN.1 projects.  They may
be interested in others as well.

Let me know if you have any quesitons.  My phone number is
+1-301-457-3690.

Yours,
Dan Gillman
Chair, L8

>Expert/Editor: ISO editor Bancroft Scott <baos@oss.com>

All these projects are presently in maintenance mode. ASN.1 standards
are
divided between the 4-part ISO 8824 (NCITS project 520-M) which
specifies
the abstract syntax, and the 2-part ISO 8825, which specifies its
concrete
encodings. With the demise of SC21, Bancroft has indicated that he may
use
the ITU-T channel to handle any maintenance responsibilities on these
collaborative standards (ISO 8824-1, for example, refers to itself as
"this
ISO 8824-1 standard|ITU-T Recommendation X.680").

ASN.1 was used initially to specify a "meaningful" data syntax for use
at
the OSI presentation & application layers, as opposed to the treatment
of
message data at lower levels only as strings of octets. I consider T2's
efforts, to avoid its overspecialization to OSI usage, a success story;
ASN.1 is now used, for example, by the VISA/MasterCard system.

Briefly, ISO 8824, the abstract syntax, specifies how the data is
organized,
and ISO 8825 how it is encoded. ISO 8825-1 specifies its full blown
Basic
Encoding Rules (BER), in which each data item carries a tag and length
indicator in addition to the data itself, whereas ISO 8825-2's Packed
Encoding Rules (PER) carries abbreviated tags and length indicators only

where items are either optional or may vary in length. In spite of T2's
advice to the contrary, an IEEE standards group, as might be expected,
insisted on referring to BER instead of the abstract syntax, leaving
them
out in the cold as far as the more efficient PER was concerned.


Specification for a Data Descriptive File (DDF) for Information
Interchange
NCITS project 40-M, JTC1 project 21.08.01; IS 8211:1994
>This is in maintenance mode, a revision of an older version which
happened
to be the first "A/I" standard. Generally, an ISO 8211 file carries a
description of its structure in its first records, plus further item
descriptions when needed therafter.
Expert/Editor: Al Brooks <brooks@icx.net>


Representation of Numerical Values in Character Strings (NRICS)
NCITS project 104-M, JTC1 project 21.32; ANS X3.42:1990, ISO 6093:1985
>This NCITS project, whose object was to bring X3.42 into alignment with
ISO
6093 except for the latter's "decimal comma," became "stalled" when its
editor (myself) was unable to keep up with the necessary work in
addition to
his duties as T2 Secretary and Vice Chair. In addition, following the
project instructions involved "destandardizing" X3.42's treatment of
exponent signs.
Generally, both ISO 6093 and ANS X3.42 specify three numeric formats:
NR1
(Fortran Iw), "nnnnn"; NR2 (Fortran Fw.d), "nn.nnnn"; and NR3 (Fortran
Ew.dEe), "nn.nnnnEsnn". where "s" is the exponent + or - sign (only
X3.42
allows it to be blank if the exponent is zero or positive).
Expert/Editor: Murray Freeman <x3t2mf@ptd.net>
