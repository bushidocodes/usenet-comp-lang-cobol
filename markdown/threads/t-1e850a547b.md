## Well, you certainly came to the right place.

> I guess I'm really looking for some general info about Cobol.
> My current understanding (I'm sure I'll be corrected) is that it is
> largely used within the financial sector,

## "Business" sector would be more accurate. This encompasses
financial (of course), but lots of other areas too (marketing,
advertising - i.e., direct mail - inventory control, bill of
materials,
client/shareholder relations, law, medicine, insurance, etc.
If its a help, most COBOL programmers wear wingtips and
work 9 to 5.

>that a lot of old programs,
> originally written in Cobol, are still in use.
## You bet. There are more programs and lines of code in COBOL
in daily use than any other language. Much of the fuss over Y2K
issues involves COBOL programs written thirty years ago that are
still running.

>I get the impression that
> much of the work involving Cobol is related to maintenance work but
that
> 'new systems' are now being written in other langs.
## Yes and no. Yes there's a lot of maintenance, and no, not all new
systems written in other languages. And cosider a "new" application
that has to interact with 5,500 in-house COBOL programs and a staff
of 100 COBOL programmers. What language would you use?
>
> As certain langs may be better for certain types of tasks (eg Java
useful
> for web-based tasks), is Cobol better for certain types of tasks
than
> others?
## Sure. For example, while exceptions abound, COBOL is the hands-
down winner for ease of maintenance. If you intend to write a program
with an expected lifetime of more than two weeks, many times COBOL
is an excellent choice.
>
>
> Any recommendations for useful books and/or informative web sites
would be
> much appreciated.
## There are NOT a LOT of websites - a search for "COBOL" will find
most.
And what kind of books? History? Elementary programming? Advanced
programming? Language standards?
