Steven,

there is a fundamental shift required in attitude and approach when moving
from SPF type editors to PC ones.

After using ISPF on the mainframe every day for 20 years, you become
accustomed to the idea of line and block commands and working at a line
level.

PCs manipulate text in an entirely different way using cut and paste and Box
editing. Text is generally free format and is manipulated that way. (The
same applies to COBOL code...) I happen to like the Fujitsu editor because
of its colour coding and box edit capabilities, but it took me some time to
get used to it and I have seen people here saying how awful it is (and it
is, if you are used to mainframe editors).

When these facilities are included into a mainframe simulation product, the
programmers are baffled by them and can't see the point, hence the general
bewilderment over why a perfectly good product like SPF/PC moved away to
SPF/SE (which was apparently more PC-like.)

From my own experiece, I know it takes some time to make the necessary
mental adjustment. Nowadays I wouldn't go back to a green screen and line
editing, and on the rare occasions when I have to, it feels like wading
through molasses.

Obviously, Editors are dear to everyone's hearts and we all prefer what we
have come to know.

I guess it comes down to preferences, just like programming style does...

Pete.

Technically, you can't get SPF/PC anymore, because Command Technology
no longer supports earlier versions of the product.  I have several
versions, including versions for DOS & OS/2.  The ones I use most are
a DOS version with a 1989 copyright, and SPF/WIN Version 1.02
copyright 1995.  The 1989 DOS version does not support edit macros,
but otherwise works quite well and will fit (uncompressed!) on a 360K
floppy disk.  SPF/WIN occupies about 3 megabytes of disk space.  It
supports REXX and ISR edit macros, has minimal gui-fication, but has
Y2K bugs in displaying dates in the directory listings.  
 
> Don - you said that they stripped away a lot of the ISPF look and feel,
> and the REXX/CLIST support.  Do you know why they did that?  You would
> think that you don't fix what isn't broken...

If you ever visit bit.listserv.ibm-main, this topic has been discussed
in great detail.  Apparently, Command Technology Corporation got rid
of all the old developers, and the new ones were all PFCSK's who
decided that nobody knew REXX and therefore C would make a much better
macro language.  I haven't tried the current supported release of
SPF/PRO.  I assume it still has some resemblance to ISPF, but the
old-time mainframers really hate the loss of REXX macros.

There was also a DOS-based ISPF editor called Tritus/SPF, but that
company has gone out of business and the product is not available
(except as bootleg) and not supported.
