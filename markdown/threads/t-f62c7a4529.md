From: Stephen J Spiro    Subject: Re: Cobol as a structured language 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Everyone is unhappy about how long it takes to update the COBOL standard, and the lack of sensitivity by the "guardians" of COBOL to the COBOL "community".

Ok, here is the truth.
Since I joind the J4 COBOL Committee in 1995, membership has ranged from 10 to 15 members at a time. Currently, we have 12 members.
6 from compiler makers:
IBM
Merant (MicroFocus)
Fujitsu
Unisys
Compaq (Tandem)
Hewlett-Packard
1 from a foreign committee - Information Technology Standards Committee of Japan
1 from a user company - EDS
4 independent consultants:
Stephen J Spiro (ME!) from Wizard Systems
Barry Tauber (self)
William Klein (self)
Bob Karlin (Karlinskorner).

The independent consultants not only pay their own way, and pay an $800/year documentation fee to NCITS, we also LOSE billing time. Barry and I contribute to the discussions , but not many papers. Bill's and Bob's contributions (prodigious lately!) sometimes depend on other factors such as how much their clients need of their time. 

There are some other individuals/companies who SOMETIMES contribute papers or discussion. 

SOME of the other people on the committee get a lot of time from their companies to work on the standard, some do not. Or it can vary.

The COBOL Standard has been completely rewritten from the 1985 edition... the format has changed to be much more readable. It is an incredible effort, by a relatively small number of people. 
It is NOT the fault of NCITS or ANSI or ISO/IEC that it has taken so long. It is because we have added -- and argued over -- an enormous number of new features. 
SPECIFIC changes must be written up in a "Full Revision Change Proposal", which is then discussed at a J4 meeting. Remember, we are writing a STANDARD, which means the language must be complete, accurate and unambiguous. All other places in the standard which may be impacted must be referenced. Impacts (if any) on previous standards must be documented. Etc, etc, etc. After discussion, the proposal is "accepted", "accepted as modified" (which requires a rewrite, but goes directly to the project editor), "accepted, but must be reviewed after suggested changes are made", or "rejected".
THIS IS A LOT OF WORK! 

You want it to go faster? Join the committee. Get your employer to sponsor you for the committee. Write papers even if you are NOT on the committee. Joe Katnic, too late for this standard, but if YOU write up the syntax for the Operating System interfaces (we will get you a template for the format), there is a good chance the features would be in the next standard.

------------
Personally, in spite of all the really good things in the standard, I think there are enough BAD features that I voted AGAINST advancing the standard at the J4 meeting last month. Features are being picked up by compiler makers in response to client requests.... I would rather have the new standard "Excellent and later", rather than "fair and now". 
------------

If you are not contributing to the solution, I cannot take your complaints about delay seriously. Bill Klein I take seriously. I have no reason to believe that better/faster progress would be made if the work of the committee were under different sponsorship. In spite of ISO's grumblings, COBOL really IS in the Public Domain. Anyone out there want to start their own committee to develop the language? If you can convince me that it will do a better job, I'll join you --- but I don't think anyone will take me up seriously.

ALL of the members of J4 are unhappy about attempts to copyright the COBOL standard. We are actively negotiating with (i.e., objecting to) NCITS/ANSI/ISO regarding this issue.
The price of the hardcopy relects the costs of producing and distributing a relatively small press run.
If we have anything to do with it, there will always be a free download available.
If anyone actually tries to ENFORCE a copyright on the COBOL standard, I would give serious consideration to suing them for "theft of work product" for my contribution.
To the best of my knowledge, ANSI (as well as NCITS and ISO) is a non-profit organization.
