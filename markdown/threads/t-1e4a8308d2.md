COBOL - A Natural Candidate for E-Business!
===========================================

Sounds like heresy doesn't it. 
After all, in a culture that professes 'new 
is best', COBOL should have no right to make claims in 
e-business. The facts are rather different, however. 
Many large companies have a huge pool of COBOL-based 
applications that constitute their core business systems, 
and e-business means that these systems need to be 
integrated and connected to the outside world. As a 
matter of fact, this is the most pressing issue facing many
large companies. Forget about Java and XML, many IT managers 
would be happy if their applications could talk to each other 
and provide an interface to the outside world.

It just so happens that some of the highest profile e-business 
applications are based on COBOL server applications. Federal 
Express and Schwab have both utilised COBOL extensively for 
the application servers that do the actual work of processing 
data. There are in fact many other examples, and it is my 
belief that at the server end of these applications, COBOL, 
and particularly Object-Oriented COBOL, as supplied by 
vendors such as Micro Focus (now confusingly called Merant), 
will play a major role in allowing companies to migrate 
their applications into an e-business environment.

The alternative is too awful to contemplate. Rewriting 
applications in Java may give purists a feeling of 
euphoria, but in reality no one is doing this. Or if you 
feel really masochistic, you could try and rewrite them 
in C++.

Every large company has to start from where it is. This 
means taking old, arthritic applications and 'wrapping' 
them so they become a stable starting point for future 
developments. These applications also need to be integrated, 
and CORBA has proved to be particularly useful in this 
respect. The integration between COBOL and CORBA is now very 
mature, and equally importantly, there is a rich set of 
experience to draw upon.

It is a testament to the power of fashion 
in the IT industry that a language eminently suited to the 
development of business applications is ridiculed at the 
expense of one that is more suited to creating drivers 
for analogue to digital converters.

As far as I am concerned, there is no good reason not to use 
one of the contemporary versions of COBOL for developing the 
server end of e-business applications, and in reality many 
rganisations will. After all, Merant is growing at around 
40 per cent a year, so quite a few companies must be using 
its products.

As is usually the case in this industry, someone will spend 
years cobbling together a Java-based e-business, applying 
copious amounts of tape and string, and by the skin of their 
teeth get something that performs a useful business function; 
and we will all marvel at it. The fact that it could have 
been written in COBOL in a tenth of the time will be totally 
overlooked: after all, we must celebrate how rapidly we are 
advancing in this industry. We might not buy so much otherwise.

The problems associated with a wholesale adoption of COBOL are 
cultural, and largely based on prejudice. But it is simple. If 
you are a large company and you want to move forward from where 
you are without taking huge risks, COBOL is worth at least 
a look.
