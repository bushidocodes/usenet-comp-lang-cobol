You are in pretty good shape, and you probably can do all of the contemplated
combinations without maintaining separate loadlib versions. 

Since you do not have any of the really old compiler stuff around, you do not
have a syntax change issue and you do not have the ILBxxxx service module calls
to deal with.  Since you are at COBOL II or better, then you are dealling with
IGZxxxx service modules, and one way or another they should be found in your
CICS environment under OS/390 LE.

(A brief aside: considering the assertion that you have COBOL II can be tricky,
since it is possible to have migrated to COBOL II with compile options to parse
COBOL 74 syntax.  Some organizations do that to buy time to convert source code
to COBOL 85 later ... times up. If your shop has done that you DO have a
potentially hefty source code conversion issue front and center.  I think the
compile parm is COMPR2. That name reflects a compiler syntax regression to a
Release 2 of a compiler that predates COBOL II.)

So for you things should be easy to do, but sometimes hard to talk about.
You state in part
>> Sterling Software (CoolGen, formerly IEF) pretends that one has to recompile
all components ...
<<
I encourage you to not be so harsh on them.  Really what we are all dealing
with together here is a Tower of Babel effect that takes hold in the
application projects when we talk about this.

Many COBOLers do not have a thorough understanding of the linkage technology,
and the run-time environment that came in with COBOL for MVS, is essoteric for
sure.  Project management personnel typically are not current technically.
Frequently they can not understand.

What you are dealing with is a situation that may require you to relink.
Specifically, the intermediate product COBOL for MVS allowed a MIXRES option
alternative (which you may not have ever used), that is not viable in the new
OS/390 LE. You need to go RES (this is actually for COBOL service submodules).
This impacts mostly batch.

Use of RES can create compile parameter combination challenges, specifically
relating to static and dynamic calls. You then proceed to try to converse about
the difference between static/dynamic calls and static/dynamic links and the
merry-go-round never seems to end.

Eventually, you get to a simple, but sometimes expensive position. You will
perhaps conclude that you are dealing with a software regeneration issue: some
relinking, some recompiling.

In the new world, the softer the link, the better.

Vendors are now trying to stay away from the intricacies. It is safest to say
'recompile.' The general issue is to 'regenerate systems'.  Many shops,
however, do not have standalone link procs enumerated, so there could be some
work ahead to get to a postition where you can just re-link.

On the CICS side, the major consideration is AMODE and RMODE, since your CICS
stuff is probably already RES. Generally this is an optimization consideration,
you want to get everything above the line if you can. The vendor's software may
have certain requirements in this regard.

An important, and not so obvious problem here is that you may have to test many
more modules than you change! It is especially difficult to convince upper
management of the need for testing resources in this area.

COBOL for MVS allowed Language Environment. COBOL for OS/390 requires it.  One
important implication of re-linking a module (or regenerating a whole system)
is that you now have new COBOL service modules (possibly dynamically configured
by systems programming) as the foundation of your executable environment. For
critical systems it IS necessary to prove that that environment will work for
your programs as coded and linked. 

Despite the cost of testing, it is almost alway easier to find the problems in
test then in production (especially with CICS or other transaction oriented
systems that could drive thousands of damaged records onto files within a very
short period of time).

LE/390 allows system programmers to pull strings, to set parameters across all
modules. This can impact CICS programs carte blanche.  It is a good idea to do
atleast minimum system testing of systems regenerated for LE.

In short, you are in good shape because it sounds like you do not have a source
code conversion issue. You may need some relinking, and you may benefit most by
concluding early that you need to be able to regenerate entire systems, not
just a few modules.
And finally, for critical systems, you need to observe your modules in the
LE/390 environment in test mode at a low level of detail.

Our read of the manuals convinced us it was OK or we wouldn't have gone
into production mode with COBOL for MVS -- and for the most part is was
OK.  But after hitting two similar problems after one week, both having
the simple solution of recompiling a support subroutine, we came to the

subroutines be recompiled first.  The problem only surfaced in the CICS
environment and only occurred when the main program was "higher-level"
than the subroutine.  My suspicions are that this may have been more

correction was cheaper than spending much time analyzing the load
modules and then trying to explain to applications development in terms
meaningless to them when they would or would not have a problem. 

Finding nice clear rules explicitly laid out in a manual can give you a
warm fuzzy feeling, but having it in writing doesn't guarantee
correctness.  Empirical evidence in your own environment is the only
ultimate authority.

Finding it documented gives you a defensive position from which to fire
the first salvo at the vendor, but you may still end up losing the war. 
When presented with a conflict between manuals and code IBM (and I
suspect other vendors) has historically elected to either (1)insist the
code is "working as designed" (WAD response) and create a documentation
APAR to alter the documentation, or (2) admit the code has a problem and
either create a correction APAR or make a committment to rectify the
problem in some future release.  A WAD-documentation change is a much
simpler solution to implement from the vendor's standpoint; so even when
you feel your case is clearcut, forceful persistence may be required to
convince the vendor to commit to a code change (and to work your way
past the "database searchers" to get to someone who understands the
product).  This resistance is not totally unjustified:  the empirical
behavior of product code is itself a form a "documentation", and some
customers may have dependencies on that behavior, even where it
conflicts with manuals.
