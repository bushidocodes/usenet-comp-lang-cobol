Noone has actually showm that the programmers who coded 2 digit
years did the wrong thing.

How can you say such a stupid thing, you ask?

- without the saved memory some programs might not have fitted
into memory, and so could not have run, thus losing benefits
- extra costs for more memory to run bigger programs
- wasted screen real estate meaning more interactions
- you had to scan and test anyway just prior to Y2K so actually
having y2k complint code did not save very much
- most of the non-y2k compliant code *was* actually discarded
befire 2000.
- the time value of money means it was better to spend $100 in
1999 than to spend $10 in 1972.
etc

All of these considerations apply now. I advocate writing non-y2k
commpliant code from now on, unless the particular app needs to
go back to the C19.

Anyway, according to Moore's law computers will be smarter than
people by 2020-2030 or so, so it will be their problem!

It didn't save disk space even then. In the 1970s I was already using Julians for my 
dates internally.  A 32 bit binary julian (i.e. 4 bytes) can hold every date from the 
start of human history (well, 4713 BC anyway) to the next several millenia and takes 
only 2/3 the disk space of MMDDYY and half that of MMDDYYYY. At the shop where I was the 
programming supervisor we had a couple of standard functions (only a few lines of code) 
to put dates into or out of julian form.  Julians have the other advantage that date 
arithmetic works with them directly - that was as much my reason for using them as the 
disk space issue.

(A "julian" is the date expressed as the number of days since 1 Jan 4713 BC.)

That depends on what you mean by "Anyone who 'solved' the Y2K problem
by using windowing."  If you mean anyone who continues to use 2 digit
years in the database, you are correct.  But most windowing solutions
are not (should not) be used in that way.  The idea is to permit the
user to continue to enter 2 digit year, but reliably expand them into
4 digit year to be stored in the database.  Sliding windowing permits
this do be done reliably for the next 8,000 years, assuming the user's
dates meet certain criteria.  Actually, if the database is purged in
such a way that all dates in the database will always meet the criteria,
then sliding windowing could be used for database dates too, though
ordering and comparison would be cumbersome.  Since you didn't specify,
my remarks were based on that assumption.  But I will explain how
sliding windows should work anyway, in case you meant what I thought
you did. :-)

The reason you gave to show that using windowing is bad, is that if
you compare the 2 digit year to some constant, 20 or 50 in your
examples, eventually you would reach a point when that number was
passed and no longer would work.  This is true, if your code compares
the year to a constant.  A 'sliding window' is based on 'reflecting'
the 2 digit year into a century defined around the 4 digit current
date obtained from the operating system.  For example, the target
century could be the current year - 79 through the current year + 20.
Or, for dates that are always in the future, current year through
current year + 99.  Consider the following code which takes a MMDDYY
date input from a user and converts it into CCYYMMDD form.  The
century defined here is current year - 79 through current year + 20.
As long as all the dates processed will be within that range at the
time they are entered, this routine will work correctly indefinitely
(until 9999).  If the input dates do not meet that criteria, sliding
windowing is not applicable.  Note that the century could be defined
as any 100 year period relative to the current date, even dates
entirely in the past or future, by making both offsets negative or
positive, respectively.  The difference between the two offsets must
always be exactly 99. :-)
