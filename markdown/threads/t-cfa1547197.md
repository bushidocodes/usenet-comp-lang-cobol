```
The June 19th Computerworld had an article by Ed Yourdon, in which he
discussed the problems of legacy systems (nobody's around who programmed
them when they need maintenance) - and applied this to e-time.  He
suggested the same types of solutions we have been working at in our
older legacy systems to web processing.  Things such as extreme
programming, dual programming, knowledge transfer, etc.

It is interesting to think of systems which become legacy within 6
months - written in languages which are not designed to make maintenance
easy.
```

```
"Legacy" systems exist because they service some crucial need.
They aren't going to go away, even if the original programmers do,
regardless if they are written in COBOL (the first legacy language :-)
or  C# (Microsoft's "new" language, about to be become a source of
legacy code).

The only hope for managing such legacy systems is
a) better design information capture
b) better tools for handling/inspecting/updating the design information
     and the the code.

-- IDB
```
