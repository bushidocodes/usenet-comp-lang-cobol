I may have found a better way.

A couple of days ago, I got a piece of unsolicited marketing material via
e-mail (I do not use the "j" word). It had no name, no phone number, no
contact address and no "remove" link; it asked me to send a form with my
credit card info to an e-mail address at another domain, and referred to a
web site (at yet another domain)  I should click on to get rich or lose
weight or have my way with Tyra Banks or something equally unlikely.

I thought this very naughty to request money without identification; so I
wrote to abuse@domainname.com for the e-mail address, the original mail
address on the message and the web site. I got a 'bot response from all
three. Two of the three followed up with personal notes (very specific, not
form letters). One said they checked and found the sender id was not at
their domain, and sorry they couldn't help. The web site operator
(homestead.com) wrote back that looked over the web site, decided that the
user was in violation of the terms of service, and they shut it down.

So, perhaps a real good way to discourage irresponsible use of these free
ISP' (e.g., yahoo, hotmail, homestead, etc) is to write to the
"abuse@domainname" address when you get unsolicited propositions not
complying with your idea of "reasonable marketing promotions."

My $0.02.

MCM
PS: anyone got Tyra Banks' phone number?

Bad move. Here's a test.

Get a HOTMAIL (or YAHOO or EXCITE) free mail account. Do nothing with
it for a month. You will discover it has NO mail sent to it.

Then, on your next piece of SPAM, where you are given the opportunity
to 'remove' yourself, do so with the HOTMAIL account.

Your HOTMAIL cup will runneth over almost immediately as the spammer
now has a known, good, e-mail address.

A trick we use is to send e-mail to spammers with a return address of
'abuse@(whatever)' We have NEVER gotten mail addressed to
'abuse@.....'.
