Seeing as I manage customer support for a software vendor, I feel obliged to comment on this. 

My company supports versions of our products dating back about 20 years. Some of those versions have bugs that were fixed in subsequent releases. Some of those versions were written a decade or more before the operating system that some client is trying to run them on. Try juggling 8 or more different versions of your product on 10 or more different releases of a given operating system, with 5 or more releases of any particular operating system and you quickly realize that the information you're expecting the support people to keep track of grows exponentially. By the time you add in any other third-party tools like compilers, databases, and version control systems, in all their myriad releases it's miracle that you can get an answer at all. 

When the support staff knows that the problem you are reporting has been fixed, and especially when no workaround exists, why are they wrong to suggest upgrading. We fixed the problem. You chose not to install the fix. Who's the one responsible for failing to resolve the problem?

I don't work for MERANT but I can speculate how the support group feels about the licensing changes. They've got an improved product, that installs pretty much painlessly over earlier installations. There is every technical reason to upgrade, but some bonehead strategic decision sets up a huge barrier to migrating. The support staff find themselves between a rock and a hard place watching customer satisfaction plummet and support revenues fall as a consequence of a decision that's supposed to make the corporation more profitable.

Karl
