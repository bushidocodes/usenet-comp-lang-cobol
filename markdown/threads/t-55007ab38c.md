All you need keep track of are the low order digits then. For n up to
18 you can do it in COBOL. Say you are looking at the millions place.
Any digits to the left of that digit will no longer influence that
digit. So you can do your calculations MODULO 10**N which is what you
get with a PIC 9(N) - ignoring overflow (size errors). For numbers
MODULO (MOD) 10**N the maximum period for the group of digits as a
whole must be 10**N. It's actually less - my reference books are at
home - so I'll guess and say 5*(10**(N-2)). The period of the left most
digit within that group of powers taken MOD 10**N should be less, I
think. [After 35+ years my number theory is a bit fuzzy. :-) ]

I suspect that the N rightmost digits taken as a group have a shorter
period when the starting number (call it A) have factors in common with
10. But this may not apply to the individual digits in that group.

Here are results from my test data:

N \ A   2    3    4     5    6    7
1     2.5  2.5    5    10   10  2.5
2       5    5   10   100   25   25
3      10   10   20   500   40   50
4      20   20   40  2500   80  100
5      40   20   80 12500  160  200

The table entries are equal to 10**N divided by the period for the Nth
digit left of the decimal point (once cycling of that digit starts).

So 2, 4, 5 and 6 which have factors in common with 10 do less well than
3 but 7 which  has no factors in common with 10 is worse than 3.

So far (A=2..7,N=2..5) it looks like the period of the Nth digit is the
same as the period for all of the low order N digits. (I made an error
in my table as posted before. For A=6,N=2 the table entry should be 20,
not 25.).

Here is a restatement of what Knuth (TAOCP, vol 2, 2nd ed, page 20)
says:

for N at least 5, and starting values not multiples of 2 or 5, the
maximum period of the group of N low order digits is (10**N)/20 if and
only if the remainder of the starting number when divided by 200 is one
of these values:

3, 11, 13, 19, 21 .... 179, 181, 187, 189, 197.

Otherwise the period is less.

Note: This harkens back to an earlier discussion in this newsgroup
about writing random number generators in COBOL in which the initial
number is successively multiplied by a constant and the subsequent
products are taken MOD 10**N using a PIC 9(N).

See Knuth's "The Art of Computer Programming", vol. 2, section 3.2.1.2
(in particular Theorem D) for results related to number theory.
[Multi-precision arithmetic routines are covered later in volume 2.]

Note that the rightmost digits will cycle more quickly than others.
Some choices of your starting number will have longer periods in the
lower order digits than others.

I don't know about the high order (leftmost) digits.

At one time, when computers with decimal hardware were more common, one
method of generating random numbers involved taking the middle decimal
digits of a number and squaring them, then taking the middle digits
from the product, etc. It turned out not to be that good a method.

I expect you are going to see a regular pattern with a low cycle
length. Of course you want to avoid starting numbers that are multiples
of 2, 5 or 10 too!
