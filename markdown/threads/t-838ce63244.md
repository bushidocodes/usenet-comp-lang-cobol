Ouch! That reminded me of the years I spent staring at green forms and 
writing COBOL into them. I decided I could improve my COBOL (at least as far 
as syntax... logic takes years...) if I "punished" myself every time I made 
a mistake. COBOL coding sheets were normally filled out in pencil so errors 
could be easily corrected. I did mine in ink. Ball point, no Tipex... (Ah, 
the confidence of youth...). If I made an error I scrapped the whole sheet 
and rewrote it. Within about three weeks I was able to code a 700 line COBOL 
program with no syntax errors.

This was important.

In those days we sent the sheets to another location a couple of hundred 
miles away, where they were punched into cards and fed to an IBM system. The 
compile listing was returned and amendments were coded and posted. (Sequence 
numbers were fundamental and the process could not have worked without 
them.) It could take several days to get a compile result. We learned to 
flowchart logic and desk check it thoroughly BEFORE ever setting pen to 
paper... The whole business was time consuming and expensive.

Although it was highly disciplined, I'm not sure it was any better than 
today's iterative, interactive approach: write it, compile it, fix it, 
compile it, fix it compile it... all in minutes.

I have some sympathy with someone wanting print layout sheets (maybe for 
nostalgia? or maybe just because: "That's the way I learned to do it and I 
like it that way...") but for me, I hope I never see another one in this 
lifetime. Counting bloody squares and adjusting fillers... Thank God for 
Crystal and Stimulsoft. If I can design reports on a screen by dragging and 
dropping the things I want IN the report, then that works for me and is much 
less stressful on the eyesight.

Still, chaq'un' a son gout; if what you do works, then keep on doing it, 
...but don't be afraid to try and improve it... :-)

Pete.
