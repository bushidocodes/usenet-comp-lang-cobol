I can see where INITIALIZE might be inefficient in that it
generates move statements for every field. It would only be
inefficient if you were using it to repetitively initialize
a table for example. Thousands or millions of executions
might cost CPU time. INITIALIZE is highly efficient though
if you use it (as the name implies) to initialize areas in
your housekeeping routines. Especially when you need to
intialize COMP-3 or COMP fields.

A word of warning: INITIALIZE does NOT initialize FILLER
areas. ALWAYS move spaces to the group level first and then
INITIALIZE. Otherwise you'll have junk (usually low-values)
in the FILLERS.

Ron.

Have no idea why they would say that. I'm a consultant too and where
I work, they have a standard of moving spaces to record layouts and then
INITIALIZing them. They do both because INITIALIZE won't move spaces to
to FILLER which may be included in records to allow for future expansion.
Since this is a standard in batch was well as on-line, I wouldn't think
there's a SERIOUS performance drawback. If there was, I don't think it
would have been made a standard. (Unless the benefit from the standard
outweights the performance hit they take....don't know though.)

I know it's very frustrating to code conversion programs that take VSAM
and IDMS data into the DB2 world when their records weren't initialized
properly. Since DB2 forces valid numbers for all numeric columns,
every numeric VSAM/IDMS field must be evaluated for NUMERIC qualities before
it's moved to DB2 host variables. If you don't, you'll blow up with
S0C7 errors as soon as you hit a field that contains uninitialized
data or a value that didn't apply to a certain record type so the original
programmer just left garbage in it! Curse them. Curse them. And many
of them still don't understand why they just can't leave LOW-VALUES in
numeric fields that aren't used. Curse them, curse them all! They
make my life hell. So much so that I wrote a program to interpret
COBOL record layouts and code up IF field IS NUMERIC.... statements
that I could poke into conversion programs.

Cheers! Cheers! Couldn't agree more. I'm so sick and tired of
hearing, "OH, DON'T DO THAT, IT'S TERRIBLY INEFFICIENT!" To that
I reply, "At what release did you hear that?" Most of the time
it turns out to so ever long ago when it was true.

My current client has TONS of copybooks which call assembler language
programs to fix various "inefficiencies" in COBOL and CICS. Most of
which are probably from several revisions ago. Unfortunately, these
"standards" perpetuate themselves because nobody looks into whether
or not they're still problems.

Now, because of these "standards", it's very difficult to migrate
development work off of the mainframe to a Unix/MicroFocus environment.
Why? Well, for starters, they're having to duplicate all of these
cutesy little callable modules that are available on the mainframe in
the name of so-called "standards".

In my opinion, unless there's just something absolutely horrible with
a verb or command which renders it erroneous, it should never be
a standard to "avoid usage". More often than not, that organization
will find itself becoming more and more reliant on custom solutions or
missing out on the advantages of built-in solutions. Not to mention
the headaches such solutions cause when upgrading releases of CICS,
MVS and COBOL.

In the case of the INITIALIZE verb, I would think that the simplicity
and reliability of a GROUP level INITIALIZE would pay for itself
many times over every time maintenance is performed on that program.
Less chance for uninitialized data creating an ABEND down the road.
We just have to convince management that people time is far more
expensive than machine time. Especially when boxes continually
get more and more powerful.
