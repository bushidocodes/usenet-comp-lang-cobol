Among other sources, from obscure places such as the front page articles
in the Wall Street Journal of Thursday, April 30, 1998 and Info World
June 29, 1998, and from a 1996 study in the Client/Server Market Report,
from which I quote "In fact, COBOL and 4GLs will see significantly
increased use in client/server settings, and COBOL is right behind Visual
Basic as the leader in terms of penetration ... ."  What much of academia
is oblivious to is the serious concern in the business world about the
skyrocketing costs and reliability problems encountered with the newer
gee-whiz technologies.  Currently, about 45% of new projects are never
implemented because of cost and time overruns and failures to meet design
goals.  Much of the blame for this falls on attempts to use the newest,
complex technologies whether they are justified or not.  The effect on
the bottom line is becoming very noticeable.  Many very large companies
(see Wall Street Journal article above) are beginning to rethink their
strategies, and a lot of them are reverting to older, more cost effective,
reliable technologies, such as COBOL.  This has been going on for a while,
and the trend toward C/C++ and C/S for everything and everybody has begun
to shift back to cheaper more reliable methods.  The Info World article
above highlights the fact that companies are realizing that PC based
servers are practically toys in performance and reliability when compared
to mainframes.  The very fastest PC servers can't begin to match the
throughput of a mainframe.  And the same goes for reliability.  Companies
used to almost 100% uptime on their mainframes are facing far less
reliability in their PC based servers.  These facts have spurred a real
resurgence in the use of mainframe based servers for large enterprise
networks.  This is one of the reasons why, by the time of the 1996 survey,
COBOL came in as a close second only to VB as the C/S development language
of choice, with C/C++ a more distant third.  And COBOL use is growing, not
declining, as many people suppose.  The 1980's shift away from COBOL has
reversed in the 1990's, and only a part of it is due to Y2K.  Actually,
in part Y2K has helped to show companies just how much their businesses
have been profiting from COBOL all along.  COBOL was doing the job so
quietly, smoothly and effectively that many senior officers had almost
forgotten COBOL, thinking it was no longer relevant.  But as companies
struggle with the difficulties of implementing the costly, complex and
fragile newer gee-whiz technologies, they are realizing that this is far
from the case.  But academia still hasn't gotten the message about COBOL,
even after forty years.  Perhaps they never will.

As an interesting aside here, I recently saw a posted comment from the
editor or publisher (don't remember which) of the C User's Journal.
He said that even though he lived and breathed C/C++, he used a COBOL
system to process the subscriptions and circulation for the magazine.
He said (I'm quoting from memory): "I don't need an abort from an
uninitialized pointer just when I am ready to send out thousands of
magazines."  Academia has never understood that this is where business
lives, every day.  COBOL is simple, reliable, cost effective, stable,
and very efficient at what it does.  Those are COBOL's strengths.
COBOL is not good for everything, but for what it does well, nothing
else even comes close.
