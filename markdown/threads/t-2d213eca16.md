```
Some facts about the US job market:

"Computer programmers held about 585,000 jobs in 2000."
"There were 22,000 self-employed computer programmers in 2000. "

This statistic is programmers only. Total IT jobs are around 1,000,000.

http://www.bls.gov/oco/ocos110.htm

"Roughly 27,000 technology jobs moved overseas in 2000, according to a November
study by Forrester Research. It predicts that number will mushroom to 472,000 by
2015 if companies continue to farm out computer work at today's frenzied pace."

Technology jobs includes engineers, chemists, etc. I would guess 10-15K of the
27K are IT workers. How many of those are programmers? Well under half; a
disproportionate share of jobs moved overseas are call center (help desk). At
most, 5,000 programmer jobs (in 2000). 

That's only 1%.

http://www.cnn.com/2003/TECH/biztech/07/14/moves.offshore.ap/
http://tech.tbo.com/tech/MGAP1WDM3ID.html
http://www.forrester.com/ER/Press/Release/0,1769,780,FF.html

How many of the 585K programmers work on mainframes? Computerjobs' home page
shows the distribution of ads by sector. Of 12,000 IT jobs listed, 520 are in
"Legacy". That's 4%, which appears low to me. Applying 4% to 585K gives 24,000
IBM mainframe programmers. I believe the correct number is closer to 40,000.

How many IBM mainframe jobs are available in the US now? Searching the three
largest job boards for "CICS"  returned 236 on DICE and 335 on Monster and 119
on Careerbuilder (only 2-3 in Arizona). Approximately 25% of those jobs have
been filled or never existed (another topic), so about 250 IBM mainframe jobs on
public boards, which are the domain of independent recruiters filling mostly
contract positions. The number of internal company openings not posted on job
boards is at least three times that: 750.

http://computerjobs.com/homepage.aspx

Given average annual turnover of 12% (see below) and time-to-fill of two months,
we'd expect to see 1,000 IBM mainframe openings at any single point in time.

http://www.waldentesting.com/about/article6.htm

I believe statistics are distored by contractors, who appear to be unrepresented
in the 12% quoted above. If 15% of the 40,000 programmers are contractors
changing jobs once a year or more, we'd expect to see 250-400 ads on job boards
(supply normally exceeds demand). If the other 85% change jobs at a rate of 12%
per year and time-to-fill is two months, we'd expect to see 700 non-job-board
(internal company) openings. 

That's close to the number of present openings. 

FWIW, the number of openings for "C++" is 2,400 on DICE and 2,500 on Monster --
7 to 10 times the number returned for "CICS". Overlap is insignificant; a search
for both returned only 20. "Java" returned 3,600 and 3,900, respectively, of
which only 40 are in Arizona.

Based on a month of close observation, I'd say hot spots are NYC and DC; warm
spots are NC, Chicago, Atlanta, Dallas and Austin; cold spots are California,
Arizona and Florida  (no reference to climate intended). Having spent time in
all those places, I'd rank them: 1. Chicago (esp. NW suburbs), 2. NC, 3. Austin,
4. Atlanta, 5. DC (Md), 6. Dallas, 99. NYC.
```

```
Well said Robert. There's too much 'jobs to India!' banging of the drum
recently. No company would devolve their entire IT function offshore if it
meant losing quality control - unless they don't care about quality and
developing their products, systems and service that is - as Amex are finding
to their cost. A lot of the work I do at the moment - I often wonder what
would happen if half of the work I get to do was sent offshore. Yes the
system could be kept hobbling along for some time, but it would be
eventually shambolic.

It's a red herring - like the 'hot summers' we all remember when we were
kids, you remember the halcyon days. The real problem is the global economy
(soft as pig droppings), Enron, 9/11, dot.com bubbles bursting, dubyah, and
so on. If you can't find work, that's where you should be looking, not at
the percentage of jobs sent offshore. The days of A/Ps having a devine right
to a job is over - we're in the same boat as everyone else now.

Which is still a pain in the arse, mind.
```
