This post is not intended to restimulate a pointless debate.

It is being posted as a matter of record and because I said I would.

Some months ago, in the midst of a raging debate here, I suggested that we
should look at an alternative group to administer the COBOL standard.

This was prompted by extreme disillusionment with the current J4 activities
and the fact that we STILL don't have a COBOL 2000 standard.

Furthermore, it was obvious (at least it was to me...) that the current
Establishment is hopelessly out of touch with the user base, is not
achieving anything of value, and is apparently not answerable to anybody.

This would not be something to undertake lightly and I therefore decided to
get some feedback on exactly how important this issue is to the COBOL User
Base. (By "User Base" I mean in the broadest context...programmers,
Managers, Corporate IT VPs, Compiler implementors and vendors, in fact,
ANYONE who is connected with COBOL (but no specific advance was made to
Acadaemia, as they are generally not enamoured of COBOL anyway and tend to
follow whatever is interesting and fashionable.))

A period of four months was allocated to elicit responses. COBOL shops were
polled, around the World. Posts requesting feedback were made to this
Newsgroup. A large amount of time and effort was spent by myself and some
others in attempting to get a "feel" for how important the issue of a COBOL
Standard is.

I had intended to discuss this one-on-one with various people, including
some vendor representatives, at CW2001 but this was sadly cancelled due to
the economic decline, and the events of September the 11th meant that I
changed my travel plans anyway.

However, there has been enough response (or actually, lack of response) to
establish a position.

Here are the results:

1. Most people do not care about the COBOL standard and it does not affect
them in their daily lives. On-site programmers are MUCH more interested in
what their particular vendor has implemented, rather than what the standard
requires.

(This, while disappointing, is understandable.)

2. The ONLY people who are interested in the workings of J4 and the
production of the COBOL Standard are those who are actually involved in it.
Some of these are dedicated people; some of them see it as a chance to
improve their own marketability.

3. Vendors and implementors are required to keep an eye on the standard for
the sake of marketability, but they pay far more attention to what is
actually needed in the market place. Sometimes this coincides with a
proposed standard, but the vendors cannot risk losing Market share by
waiting until the mills of J4 have gound exceeding fine, so they go ahead
and implement their best guess of what will probably be in the standard
anyway.

(I honestly believe that the only reason COBOL is still around, is because
of the efforts of these people; it has nothing to do with a Standards
committee.)

CONCLUSION:

I will not personally be proceeding with an alternative Standards group
because I believe there is no requirement for it, and there are not enough
people in the user base who would be prepared to contribute and support such
a group with their time and knowledge.

This is not an endorsement of the current arrangement, which I still see as
scandalous. It is just as irrelevant as any alternative would be.

Finally, I'd like to thank all the people who DID respond. Some of these
responses were heartfelt. There ARE people there who DO care but they
represent less than half of one percent of the estimated COBOL user base.

Here are some quotes:

1.
"Now, my short answer on your questions. Although I think what you are
proposing has merit I personally don't see that it will help COBOL in the
long run. In my opinion the lack of action by J4/ISO/ANSI/whomever has
doomed the language. It doesn't even matter about capability anymore. As you
are more familiar than I, you can do just about everything in COBOL now
especially when you throw in the OO pieces.

The problem is that COBOL was unable to adapt quickly enough via the current
process to maintain its position as the premier multi-platform,
transportable language that it was."

2.
"<in response to "Are you happy with the current Standards
Organisation?">.... I could fill pages about my disappointment in
that organization. I was remotely involved in the CODAYSL 97 standard which
we know never happened and I can't help think the ignoring of that standard
and the "missing" the deadline caused COBOL untold harm as a viable
programming language for the future. Considering the 2000 standard was also
missed I have to wonder if the XJ4 or whatever they call themselves now,
isn't trying to kill COBOL altogether."

3.
"<in response to: "Are you happy with how it is produced?">

no, it seems unnecessarily complicated, but having worked on committee
produced rules (in the 70's I helped create the Students Rights and
Responsibilities document for my university and later a overhaul of the
student government structure) - I understand that more then half the
time is spent defining a common vocabulary to which we (the users) are
ignorant and ignored."

There were less than 50 responses and ONE of them expressed satisfaction
with the status quo. It was from a member of the committee.

Expressing discontent is one thing, doing something about it is quite
another.

I am satsified that this is issue is not important enough, and neither would
there be enough support forthcoming, to warrant my time and energy being
spent on it further.

Pete.
