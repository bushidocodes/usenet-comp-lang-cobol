That's a longish story.  Western Christianity had pretty much decided on the 
contents of the Bible by about 500AD or so, but the contents weren't 
actually made rigid until the Council of Trent in 1545-1563 in direct 
response to Protestant rejection (beginning with Luther who termed them 
"good to read" but not inspired) of what the Roman Catholic church terms 
"deuterocanonical".   Meanwhile, Judaism had accepted 24 books as the 
"tanach" by about 200AD, and it is this smaller set of books that 
Protestants accept as canonic Old Testament.

The New Testament canon is fixed in the West, but Luther seems to have had 
low regard for Hebrews, James, Jude and Revelation and at one point rejected 
them.

Eastern Orthodox are not as rigid.  Wikipedia comments:

Ethiopian Orthodox: Include Jubilees and Enoch to the OT and Shepherd of 
Hermas, the First Epistle of Clement, the Acts of Paul, and some other books 
in the NT.

Syriac Orthodox:  Exclude 2-3 John, 2 Peter, Jude and Revelation from the 
NT.  This group also accepts the canonicity of the Peshitta (in Aramaic) 
over the GNT.

Armenian Orthodox:  Adds the Third Epistle to the Corinthians to the NT.

In addition, there are a number of writings of the Church Fathers that were 
read as canonic in the early church but later eliminated from canon, in some 
cases for no other reason than that they weren't written by Apostles, 
despite widespread acceptance that they were genuine writings of "second 
generation" church leaders.

I've read the epistles of Clement and Barnabas and the Didache and 
personally find them of value (more as a "guide" than as a "rule", lest I be 
accused of accepting them as "canonic"!).

And from a historical basis I think there's probably "genuine" material in 
the Gospel of Thomas (rediscovered in Coptic at Nag Hammadi)  that doesn't 
appear elsewhere in the NT, although figuring out which of those non-canonic 
elements are valid and which aren't make it tough to figure out what's 
useful and what's not in the available recensions.
