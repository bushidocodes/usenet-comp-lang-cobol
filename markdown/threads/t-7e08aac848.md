In many mainframe shops that I've been working for
in the last 20 years, I have found that there exist
various ways to do testing on Cobol programs.
Different people use different theories to sort out
inconsistency that may not apply to a certain
business situation. And those who do the testing
may not be the ones who will make the move of
program changes to the final target of the production
environment. Moreover, testing methodologies are
very different from the administrative techniques of
program migration. They tend to be more iterative and
time consuming while change management is simply
straightforward and standardized.

Many Cobol testing can involve several systems in
order to make certain that a particular modification
will make a right business impact as it is being
assumed before hand. Within each system, there
may be many Cobol sources that need to be written
or modified in order to apply the new business
requirements. Therefore, implementation of new
business models would dictate different approach
to the testing strategies.

Unit testing is almost straightfoward and self-evident.
At this level of exercise, newly applied logics are being
executed to see how the new concept will produce in
terms of outputs and overall business ramifications.
Once newly coded logical routines have been validated
within a certain Cobol program, the next level of testing
should be submitted and explored.

System testing expands the testing discipline of the
involved Cobol object module beyond its own immediate
impact of the program and onto other business systems
and the overall performance of the new inserted codes.
This kind of testing may require actual production inputs
in order to verify the robustness of the change and the
inserted codes. It may also require to have those load
modules moved, copied, or re-linked to a different
environment in order to insure the completeness of the
testing methodology. This kind of creative response to
the challenge of a good testing situation will also allow
the testers to introduce various business parameters as
conditions to act as testing criteria.

A lot of system testing can also be done in conjunction
with the user. By comparing test results from different
approaches of the testing situation, one can have a
better idea on how changes to a particular group of
Cobol programs can have impact or will react and
behave under real conditions of the business realities.

Cobol semantics can closely mimic the business reasoning
of the human mind. Yet, it has the power and precision as
much as other programming languages that are currently
available. By varying the testing methodologies, from one
testing environment into another, and by comparing the
testing results from various setting of the different locale
of the testing methodologies, we can rest assured that our 
applied changes to the different Cobol programs within
a certain business system have met the requirements of
the new business conditions.


Chim Sotuon

I am a system tester for an application in a COBOL/DB2 environment.  I
do not generally get to see the actual changes that have been made to
the COBOL programs.  I usually have a requirements document to work
from, form which the developers are supposed to prepare their design
and eventually, the code.
    I normally use actual production data for regression tests and to
test actual programs.  I am not concerned with the actual program
logic, but only with the system inputs and outputs (in effect, a black
box testing method) and sometimes with the system performance.  But
sometimes when an application change may involve data that is not yet
in production.  The required data would be available in production
only when the application change goes in.  In these cases, I would
have to manufacture test data.  Not a fool-proof operation, I must
say.  Usually trial and error.
    As in post #3 above I have experienced a certain level of
animosity (including a public harangue once) from developers when
defects are found in the system functionality.  I have now learned to
accept this as part of my job.

Chim,
Sounds like you could use a regression testing tool.
One I have worked with is the Progeni product called 3Rs (www.progeni.com).

Using a fixed test database you supply, this system captures screen and
report output and moves it to a PC in a special format.
You make your changes.
You capture the output again.
3Rs compairs the two versions and lets you know what changed.  It's pretty
smart about this - for example, if the change you are making is to increase
the size of the Account number field, which moves all the lines on a report
over 2 characters, 3Rs won't tell you that every line on the report is
wrong.
However - to use an example from another post here - it would tell you if an
entire Pell check were missing or the wrong amount.

One of the neat things about 3Rs is that it works across platforms.  It was
initially designed to test platform migrations.  Those captured output files
are stored in a common format, no matter what platform they were produced on
(CICS, Unisys, Microfocus on Unix or Windows, etc.) and then they are moved
and examined on a PC.  So if you capture a system under CICS, migrate to
UNIX, then capture the new system using the same test data - 3Rs will test
the migration.
As you can imagine, the tool received lots of use during Y2K work since it
can detect and not falsely report inteneded changes like date field
expansion while catching uninteneded errors.

If you want more info on 3Rs contact rtk@nospam.progeni.com (remove the
nospam. of course).

In my experience, one of the most effective "testing" techniques is
rarely used.  This is the "Structured Walkthru".  Because it takes 3 -
5 programmers a couple of hours or more, most managers insist there is
not enough time for this "luxury".  In my experience, and the
experience of everyone I know who uses them, they save MUCH MORE
physical testing time than they use.
In one shop, I was responsible for the key control program for the
entire system.  The COBOL was pretty straightforward, but the CICS
used unusual system services.  This manager not only thought a
Structured Walkthru was a good idea, he suggested opening it to
whoever wanted to attend, so they could see how it was done.  Over 20
people attended, I got some excellent feedback, and the  program was
stable when installed.  The only problems encountered in physical
testing involved interactions with the operating system and CICS
system parameters which could not have been detected in the walkthru.

Structured Walkthrus also promote an environment of "Egoless
Programming", in which the QA people are seen as part of the team, not
"outside troublemakers".
IT IS EXTREMELY IMPORTANT that structured walkthrus be "error
detection processes".  I was very unhappily surprised on one
assignment when I was called in by the manager and told that the
structured walkthru had indicated a large number of problems -- HE
used structured walkthru's as  performance reviews!

Stephen J Spiro
President, Wizard Systems
