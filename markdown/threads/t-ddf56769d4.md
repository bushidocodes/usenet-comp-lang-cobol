I would say that it is... and it isn't.  On the one hand I recall a case 
where a local sherrif was on the take, confiscating drugs and guns that 
were seized as evidence and selling them to folks inside his circle; this 
case was cracked by diligent application of open data laws regarding 
evidence logs.

(The sherrif even staged a break-in at his own offices to cover this up... 
if one might imagine anyone taking seriously a break-in with no signs of 
forced entry.)

On the other hand... I do not believe that anyone of moderate sanity would 
demand that use of public health facilities demands that a patient 
forfeits all doctor-patient confidentiality.  (to the simple-minded who 
would pout 'Well, why *not*?' I would answer 'Let's start with the Old 
Soldiers' Home, shall we?' and then work assiduously to find something of 
greater value to occupy my time.)

>
>I saw at the mashup a few weeks ago this sort of thing in practice. People 
>can build quite staggering applications by combining APIs that give access 
>to public data. And you certainly don't have to be an IT whizkid to do it.

You do, however, need to know which data are covered under what laws lest 
you violate them.  There's also an associated tyranny that worries me... 
consider the case of a person involved in divorce proceedings whose spouse 
manages to get hold of telephone logs.  Now add to a spouse who knows 
about ping-tracing and purposefully removes the battery from their 
telephone to prevent such a thing.  Stir in a bit of 'You take the battery 
out of your telephone?  WHAT DO YOU HAVE TO HIDE???' and there is one, 
easily conceived situation where saying 'I want to be left alone' is 
readily turned into 'I am guilty of wanting my whereabouts to be unknown.'

Me, personally, I don't take the battery out of my telephone... I have a 
tin-can with a string and taking myself out of the loop requires only a 
well-tied knot.

DD

All good points, Doc. Personal freedom is a knife edge which we can only 
walk with vigilance.

As for cell phones... with the ongoing deterioration of my eyesight I found 
the one I have just too annoying.

(It rings and you scrabble to find glasses; you need to make a call and the 
poxy little buttons are too small for easy operation, voice dialling is 
notoriously error-prone, and watching the language being murdered by texting 
is something I really get no enjoyment from. Besides, I'm not good at it and 
I don't want to be. I had a girlfriend who could send me 3 more text 
messages while I was still responding to the first one; it was a doomed 
relationship... :-))

Some time ago I took to leaving it in the trunk of my car for possible 
emergency use and eventually the battery went flat and the pre-paid SIM 
expired. (That's another thing I don't think is at all fair; if you buy a 
cell service up front why should it expire...?) Now when I go on a trip 
there is an extra element of spice because I DON'T have a phone to hand. In 
the old days you went to the nearest farm if you broke down and they pulled 
you into town with a tractor; I guess I'm still in that mode... (If there is 
a real emergency I can charge the phone from the in-car charger in a few 
minutes and by law the emergency number has to be enabled whether you have 
paid for the service or not...).

Most of my friends are horrified that I cannot be reached 24/7 (they have 
the same reaction to my refusal to join Facebook; I'm on LinkedIN but I 
joined when it was a business network and before it became social)... 
Gradually, they are coming to accept that I actually enjoy having some 
control over when I am available and I don't need to be "online" 24/7. The 
younger ones see me as "eccentric"; the older ones wish that they could get 
away with it... (when you have family, you really can't...).

I remember with fondness making tin can telephones as a kid, but it all got 
replaced by walkie talkie kits which were sold as toys and had a range of a 
few hundred yards.

The world (and technologies) moves on...

Pete.
