```
>I'm taking a cobol course and in order to practice writing programs and
>complete projects I need to go to the colleges'  lab...It would be much more
practical to be able to edit, compile and debug the projects
on my home pc and then go to the lab with the working code.

That could be a problem, depending on what kind of environment you are working
in.  I wanted to do the same thing you are describing, but it didn't really
work.  At my school, we work in the MVS operating system on an IBM mainframe. 
The compiler on my PC is different in several respects.

1. Assign clause: on the mainframe I must assign file-names to a ddname used in
my JCL submission.  On the PC, I must assign to specific files.
2. Status codes: the PC environment likes to have status codes (and an
evaluation of them) for all file operations.  At school, we never did this for
sequential files.  We didn't learn about that until we worked with VSAM.
3. File organization.  I don't use the PC much, and I still need to focus on
Thane Hubbell's chapter about this.  I'm not very hip to LINE SEQUENTIAL, etc. 
That's another consideration when you want to code school projects at home. 
Org will likely be different at school.
4. COBOL version. If you are learning from Thane's book, you are looking at
COBOL II.  My course didn't get into that until the very end.  You can code
COBOL I on the Fujitsu compiler, but if you are trying to use ideas from the
book on your school examples, they might not work if you're using a COBOL I
compiler at school.  COBOL I doesn't have features like scope-terminators and
the EVALUATE verb, etc.

I found the Fujitsu useful for my school projects mostly as a syntax check.  I
looked up just as much as I needed to make my school project compile on my PC,
even though everything before the WORKING-STORAGE SECTION was always very
different and the reports, DISPLAYs and input test data didn't work at all the
way they would on the mainframe.  But I did eliminate a bunch of syntax and
logic errors, and then I took my laptop to school and just re-typed what I had
written.  Kludgey, but it reduced the time I had to listen to the goofballs
horsing around in our school access room.

RoadRaat
```
