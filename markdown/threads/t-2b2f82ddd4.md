Alistair Maclean wrote:
>Judson McClendon writes
>>A free society tolerates being exposed
…[8 more quoted lines elided]…
>ntly set free (lack of evidence, despite witnesses).

Denying them the 'right' to physically assault or molest others, no.
Denying them the right to express their abhorrent views, yes.  The basis
of law is that it protect the innocent from harm and punish those guilty
of causing harm.  Assault and murder should be, and probably is, illegal
in every culture.  Free expression is only denied in oppressive cultures,
and in cultures rapidly moving that way.  The way to combat destructive
ideas is to oppose them with truth.  What if it is the truth that is
being suppressed?  The real problem here is that laws or customs that
can be used to suppress 'bad' speech can be as easily used to suppress
'good' speech.  All that has to happen is that those who have the power
to determine 'bad' and 'good' are replaced by those with a different
agenda, or their masters replaced by those with a different agenda.

>>To avoid tyranny, a free
>>society must allow dissenting, even unpopular, opinions to be freely
…[3 more quoted lines elided]…
>bring about the destruction of the same society.

Only if the population is foolish enough to believe it.  How else could
a bad government be taken down, other than by internal descent or external
force?  The way bad governments fall is through knowledgeable descent of
the oppressed.  The way good governments stand is by the knowledgeable
consent of the governed.  In both cases knowledge is the key.  How can
citizens become knowledgeable, if they only hear part of the story?  Who
is the 'guardian' to decide what they are to hear?  What if they become
corrupt?  Who do you trust to control what you are permitted hear, and
what you are permitted to say?  Whith free expression, Citizens can listen
and believe or reject any opinion they choose.  This is the right of free
people.  If they blindly follow fools, there is no way to justly govern a
population who has abandoned wisdom for folly.  They bring about their own
destruction.  Free speech, and its subset, free press, are essential to a
free society.  This principle is not an opinion or a guess, it is hard,
cold fact, known for thousands of years.  It is used and abused every day
to defend against, and to support, oppression.  Any conquering army, any
new despot, and the even dumbest of dictators, moves immediately to
control all forms of communication.  Why?  Because, if they do not, they
will not survive for long, and they know it.  There is not one tyrannical
government on the planet, nor has there ever been or ever will be one,
whose citizens enjoy completely free speech.  Why?  Just trot over to
North Korea or China and start talking about how bad the government is.
You will soon learn that those governments understand this principle quite
well. ;-)  In any such government you care to name, people are imprisioned
or killed simply for speaking against the government.  Why?  It is very
simple.  If all people are free to express themselves, then public descent
will eventually bring down any tyrannical government.  If people cannot
speak freely, then it is difficult or impossible for descent to be
articulated, coordinated or spread.  Individuals may hate the government,
but they are virtually powerless to do anything about it.  They are afraid
to even tell their friends, because they might be turned in for violating
the law.  There is no way to expose false propaganda, because the people
who know can't tell anyone about it.  If free expression is abridged, even
a little, the precedent is set in the people's minds to provide a vehicle
for any would be tyrant to use for this purpose.  The same applies, even
in a free society.  When a group's free speech is suppressed, those people
have lost their voice, their ability to influence society, or even express
how they feel or what their problems are.  How or when would discrimination
against minorities ever be addressed, if they are denied a voice?

>>  This is the very foundation stone upon which freedom rests.
>>Otherwise, many more opinions, and eventually truth itself, will be
…[9 more quoted lines elided]…
>shore up the regime as the prime triggers for the soft revolution.

You are looking at the effects, not the underlying cause.  Why do you
think they were moved to withdraw the troops, or any of the other moves
in that direction?  The Soviets were hard, cold realists.  They never
did anything unless they were convinced it was to their advantage.
Joseph Stalin had millions of Soviet citizens murdered and starved
because he found them inconvenient.  One of the reasons they suffered
many early defeats in WWII was because Stalin had recently murdered
most of the commanders in the army, devastating their whole command
structure and morale.  Stalin left quite a lagacy that his successors
did not fail to notice.  Not since Kruchev was one man in the Soviet
Union ever permitted to wield such arbitrary power.  Gorbachev would
never have been permitted such sweeping reforms unless those who
supported him were also convinced.  What convinced them?  Did they,
after almost a century of lies, simply decide to 'be nice'? :-)

>> and the Soviet Union.
>
>I thought the USSR was brought down by Gorbachev refusing to continue to

Thanks, you support my point.  Why was lying effective before?  Why was
it no longer effective?  Was it because human nature had changed?  Or
was it because information, and thereby influence, from the West had
shown the people how totally false those claims were?  Why do you think
Gorbachev, and especially the many others who supported him, knew that
the lying was not useless?  It was because those long oppressed people
now *knew*.  Had their free speech not been suppressed, they would have
known all along.  Which is why it was suppressed in the first place, of
course. :-)
