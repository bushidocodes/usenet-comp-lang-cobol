One thing you might consider is seeing what CLEP tests relating to
programming in general might be available; check with your local college.
These are exams used by colleges and universities to award credits based
upon what you have learned outside of formal classes.

Over the years, I've seen entry-level applicants fall into one of four
categories: (1) new graduates with CS or IS degrees, (2) graduates with
non-technical degrees in disciplines that were of particular interest to
the employer (ie: Big 8 accounting firms might hire MBAs or accounting
majors and train them in COBOL.), (3) people with a programming certificate
from a tech school, and (4) current employees moving up from other jobs.
With the advent of the desktop and relatively inexpensive tools, it's now
possible for a self-taught programmer to become an recognized expert by
publishing his/her own creations as shareware/freeware.

When I used to interview prospective COBOL programmers, I never had an
applicant bring in any sample code. If they had, I would have examined it
for readability -- how easy it was to understand their logic. Like most
interviewers, I would ask some questions about the language itself to
insure that the applicant had a sufficiently broad knowledge of it. I
would also throw in at least one "trick" question, looking for responses
such as "I would look that up in the manual under ", or "I don't
know. HOW DO YOU DO THAT?". In short, for a good technical candidate, I
would look for a combination of experience and resourcefulness.

Hope this helps. Good luck, Susan.

Cory wrote a bunch of stuff, that, although 100% true, isn't *REALLY* 100%
true. The question you have to ask yourself is: "Do I really, really, REALLY,
want to become a computer programmer?" If the answer is an immediate, and
emphatic YES!!!!!, then, believe me, regardless of how gloomy and hard and
boring and tedious anyone makes programming out to be (whether we're talking
COBOL, Assembly, C/C++, SmallTalk, or whatever), to you, it won't be difficult
at all. Instead, it will be an adventure that you'll enjoy. Something you'd be
doing anyway, only now, someone will be PAYING you to have fun. If, on the
other hand, you hesitate, and have to THINK about whether you want to become a
computer programmer, my best advice is: stick to setting up W4W macros, and
find yourself a different career path.

Like anything else, computer programming isn't for everyone. It's got to be
something you WANT to do, otherwise, it'll always be a boring chore, and you'll
never really understand what's going on (and, THAT, can be frustrating as
hell).

As for a COBOL proficiency exam... I've been cranking code professionally for
16+ years now: starting with 370 Assembly (called it BAL back in them days),
then COBOL and CICS under VSE (called it DOS back in them days), VAX/COBOL,
RPG/400 and COBOL/400, plus, Visual BASIC in between (VB has been extremely
good to me: enabled me to buy this here fancy tower PC in my bedroom, and my
brand new car outside)... and I've never, ever, taken any kind of proficiency
exam/test/evaluation, except for answering questions on job interviews.

I've heard rumors that there's some kind of AS/400 association or other that
awards people some kind of initials that they can put on their resumes once
they pass some kind of tests or other, but, I don't have any first hand
knowledge. I believe Microsoft has a similar setup for their products and
development tools, but, again, I'm not 100% sure. Not sure I'd really I'd put
much credence in them pieces of expensive paper myself either: usually, just
means you had the stamina & patience to sit on you rear end for eight hours a
day, for 14 days, while someone lectured on politically correct coding
techniques (I just *KNOW* that I'm gonna get flamed for this!).
