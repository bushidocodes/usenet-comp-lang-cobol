Weeeeellllll.. when it was we'd do things like define contiguous tables
and refer to elements by 'out of range' subscripts.  Consider:

01  BIG-HURKIN-TBL01.
    05  BHT01-ITEM OCCURS 1000.
        10  BHT01-DEPTNO PIC 9(05).
        10  BHT01-STUFF  PIC X(25).
01  BIG-HURKIN-TBL02.
    05  BHT02-ITEM OCCURS 1000.
        10  BHT02-DEPTNO PIC 9(05).
        10  BHT02-STUFF  PIC X(25).

... and y'load *that* sucker up during 0100-HOUSEKEEPING from your
lookup-table file.  Then when you refer to BHT01-DEPTNO (SUB1) and SUB1 =
1005 you are actually looking at the fifth entry in BIG-HURKIN-TBL02 (or
BHT02-DEPTNO (5).

*Classic* trade-off of resources... decreases EXCPs, increases core use.

Pretty PLEASE, do NOT assume that two "consecutive" 01-levels in
Working-Storage section will actually be stored in "order" in memory and that
"over-subscripting" will work.

IN FACT, this is a major "migration inhibitor" for some sites going from
OS/VS COBOL to VS COBOL II or IBM COBOL for this-and-that.  The reason that
this becomes a problem is that the "newer" IBM optimizer is able to detect
"unreferenced" working-storage items - and not bother to allocate storage for
them. Therefore, (for example) if you have

  01   Tabl1.
         05 Each-Elem Occurs 32000 times.
             10   Elem  Pic X.
   01 Tabl2.
        05  filler     Pic  X(32000).

If you then ONLY reference "Elem" including the items over 32000 - the newer
IBM compiler will "assume" that you are telling it the truth and you never
use Tabl2.  Hence it gets no storage.  Therefore, any reference to ELEM
(32001) will get WHATEVER happens to appear in storage after Tabl1.

Also (fyi) the use of the EXTERNAL attribute makes WS-items non-consecutive.

For those who think this is an IBM-mainframe only issue, look at the Micro
Focus 01-Shuffle directive (and I suspect several other vendors' options).
