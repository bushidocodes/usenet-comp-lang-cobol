### Mainframers: Take back the light (spotlight, that is)

Hi, 

I've been thinking about the perennial cry of "the mainframe is dead" and what
it means to us. Although we may laugh or pooh-pooh it, as insiders, I am afraid
there is a very real danger of us mainframe oriented folks getting so insular
we don't see the reality until it is too late.

The reality is, simply, we are allowing ourselves to be marginalized. In a
company, everyone knows about the PCs around; when people talk about "the
computer" they mean the PC. When management talks about spending money for IT,
it's for PCs, JAVA, and the Web. When kids go to college / university to learn
computer science, it's UNIX or Windows products.

Meanwhile, mainframes are quietly running the bread and butter applications of
the business. Telephone companies are not going to email 10,000,000 customer
bills each month, nor print them on 1,000 PCs lashed together. 

What we have learned (hoepfully) over the years is to use the right tool for
the work at hand. UNIX, PCs, and the Web are important: make no mistake. But we
in the mainframe world have lost the edge we once had, and we find few young
people interested in going into our part of the business (you can't blame them
if they don't know we exist or they consider us just dinosaurs in dead-end
work). With no attention from the up and coming youth, we have little future. 

What happens is often disasterous. I had one client where a manager made the
fiat: all new work will be UNIX and C, and we will be off mainframes in five
years. This for a shop with over 40 MVS images with 80% of the code written in
COBOL (and virtually no C skills inhouse). The result is he was let go after
two or three years into the program (with hefty a severance package, grrrrr),
the company lost ground, and morale was decimated.

Today, when we call to see about training applications programmers on the
mainframe, few of the people we initially reach even know their company has a
mainframe. If they know that much, they don't know who to put is in touch with.
We (mainframers) don't exist in the mind share of many folks.

But we can change that. We can take back the [spot]light.

When I joined IBM back in 1968 (!), they told us "Everybody sells". Those of us
who joined for reasons of learning the technology were at first put off by
this: sales people, after all, are slimeballs, right? But we gradually came to
understand that we sell to our colleagues, spouse, friends, family: we try to
convince people to do some activity we want to do, or to act in some way we
want them to act, and so on. So in this larger sense, it is true: everybody
sells (or if you don't you lose the sale).

We techies are very good talking to each other about arcane, or practical,
issues of code and options and how things work. And we are being blindsided by
a growing disinterest. We need to take some difficult [for most technical
folks] steps and reach out and promote our industry, as a matter of self
preservation. Or we will have no relevance in the larger world.

What can we do? Here's some ideas:

* have some events to involve a wider audience
  + open house / data center tour
  + brown bag luncheons to talk about applications
* reach out to internal publications, let them know there is a mainframe
in-house and the role it plays
* build web pages on the corporate website that explain what the mainframe
does, and who the relevant people are

I've put together a few skeleton web pages for such an activity, free for the
taking, if anyone is interested. Go to our home page at
http://www.trainersfriend.com and click on the link to "Free internal
marketing" which will give you a list of some skeleton pages ready for you to
tailor and upload to your corporate intranet.

I am personally very concerned, careerwise, and I hope many of you are, too.
I'm happy to add other skeleton pages at this location if anyone wants to
contribute to this project. And I'm happy to publicize other things we can do
to regain some awareness in the corproate and public mindset for mainframe
computing.

Of course, there are exceptions to everything I've said above: there are
schools that still teach mainframe topics; there are companies where the
mainframe is well known and well respected; there are managers who have some
balance and perspective; there are some young people going into the mainframe
part of IT. But they are in the minority. And we have to ask ourselves if that
is the right proportion.

Kind regards,

-Steve Comstock

You are absolutely correct.

Time and again I find mainframe people resisting "new technology" with a
condescending attitude that PCs are toys and the REAL work is done on the
mainframe. In the last decade this attitude has changed into one of fear as
Client/Server has made large inroads into what was traditionally mainframe
territory.

Of course the mainframe isn't dead. It is an essential part of the Corporate
IT arsenal, but it no longer has the place as "top of the heap" which it
once held when it was "the only game in town" (prior to 1983). Neither
should it have, if we judge on merit and not self interest.

The fact is that true IT professionals will not owe special allegiance to
either the mainframe environment or the Workstation one. They should be
capable across IT platforms irrespective of what those platforms are. They
should be seeking to serve the rest of the enterprise with solutions that
utilise the best tools for the particular job, and that means hardware AND
software. The question of which platform should be favoured, or which should
have the "spotlight" is about as relevant as saying which photocopier or
mail sorter should make the cover of this month's internal magazine.

Today's IT Professional should be an all-rounder. Capable in (at least)
COBOL and Java; able to use the Workstation GUI interface just as easily as
the mainframe CLI; aware of the strengths and weaknesses of IT technology
and aware of emerging technology which could be of value to the corporation;
but, above all, instilled with a sincere desire to assist user departments
in achieving meaningful and useful solutions, in a spirit of goodwill and
comradeship. Using IT technology to make life easier for the rest of the
enterprise should be the primary goal. User unfriendly systems should not be
tolerated, whether mainframe based or based on the Network.The IT
professional should be proud to be a servant of the rest of the enterprise.
That means providing support not just at a technical level, but also able to
listen and understand the problems with departmental procedures and
policies; an understanding of the way the business works, not just the bits
and bytes.

When this comes about (and I've seen it in a few places) the IT department
is no longer sidelined. Their services are in demand and they are valued by
the rest of the organization. They don't need to claim the spotlight, it is
placed on them by the rest of their colleagues... And they usually turn the
accolades back to the User departments.

Any mainframers who develop this kind of attitude will NOT find themselves
sidelined. Not only that, but their careers can be expected to take off...

There are a number of reasons for this. The arrogant historical attitude of
IT departments towards the rest of the Organization is not without weight
here. User departments were made to go cap in had to IT and ask for
solutions. They were very often made to feel like  idiots because they
didn't understand the technology, and the solutions they required were long
in coming and often inadequate when they arrived. The "technocrats" were
often a law unto themselves and were almost "unmanageable" even by their own
Managers. If anyone upset them they moved down the road and took their
expertise wth them. (To be fair, there were a lot of bad IT Managers around
too, which didn't improve the situation).

Suddenly people found they had a computer at home that their kids were
using, and it could do things that the multi-million dollar mainframe at
work couldn't. And, instead of reaching for this technology and building it
into the corporate IT solution, the IT people closed ranks, sniffed at the
"toy computers" being purchased by departments out of their own budgets and
tried to enforce restrictive rules to control the corporate data asset.

And so the traditional mainframe IT department became sidelined.

It was sidelined because (with few exceptions) it consistently failed to
deliver, and not only did it fail to deliver but it made people feel bad.

Small wonder then that user departments preferred to work with their own
systems, learning spreadsheets and word processing and databases by trial
and error, rather than be humiliated by IT programmers. At least if they got
it wrong it was private... As time has progressed there are more and more
"computer literate" people arriving in the workplace. These people have
higher expectations. They won't accept waiting months for the mainframe
Waterfall to trickle towards system delivery.

Both of these factors just require a little time... give it a few years. My

2. Expand your horizons beyond the mainframe into IT in general. (My

Never been sorry I did and now find working with TSO/ISPF as I did for

3. Encourage youngsters to do more with mainframes.
(We should certainly be doing this in the simple interest of ensuring that

4. Several of the above...(I guess the point I am trying to make is that it

maketability. However, a knowledge of all of them makes you a  VERY

IT professionalism is about IT solutions. Why limit yourself?

it's not the mainframe that's dying, it's the traditional low-level
file based data model that has to be shed.  it can only work in a
one tier application.  true client/server (two tier) won't even
tolerate it.

from the point of view of a unix/database/etc. type who has ended up in 
a "dinosaur pit", the problem is less about the language war or the 
gui/chui war, but the data war.  while it was appropriate 20 years ago,
the COBOL/VSAM mindset is truly bad today.  this is especially true
where organizations (mine, in particular) transliterates VSAM file defs
into database schema, and think they've "converted" to <name DB of your
choice>.  the application COBOL I/O routines are kept intact, the 
metadata is still in the tables, SQL overhead plus procedural processing, 
and so on.  guaranteed to fail, but penny wise.  

data modeling, and accepting the word of Codd, are what will bring
COBOL-ers into the next millennium.  it's the other kind of reversing
the mindset:  procedure vs. object, procedural vs. relational.  the
three (or more) tier approach is as much about sets (the relational
model) as about objects.  and, by the bye, object databases are nonsense.

Staggered by your post, Robert!

So much good sense in so little space...<G>

I believe you have put your finger on exactly what is "wrong" with the
"fortress mainframe" mindset.

However, I think you may be being a little too hard on the people who are
converting from VSAM/ISAM. Everybody has to start somewhere and it would not
be permitted to throw away everything and start over (even though, as you
correctly point out, this might well be the proper course to follow).

Personally, I'd rather see them converted to an open Relational Database
(even an imperfect one) than see COBOL locked into the file system and
slowly isolated until it dies...

Opening up the organisation's file-system-based data is a good first step.
As their facility with SQL improves and their understanding of the
Relational Model (even though much of this "understanding" is at a
subconscious level at first, and may not necessarily be verbalized into a
discourse on Normalization; they just do it because they can "see" that it
is more efficient (and more flexible) to remove repeating groups and combine
key dependencies...) improves, then the full power of the multi-tier model
becomes apparent and people start smiling again...<G>.

I have been advocating the use of multi-tier databases and the conversion of
COBOL systems to an open RDB for some years in this and older COBOL forums.
The resistance has been incredible. But it is understandable. When people
spend a lifetime doing something a certain way, and it "works", it is very
hard to persuade them that things have changed and a different approach is
now required.

Your post sums it up beautifully.

Pete.

P.S. I share your reservations about Object Databases and I think it's time
it was pointed out that Object Oriented programming doesn't NEED a special
database technology; Relational is fine....
