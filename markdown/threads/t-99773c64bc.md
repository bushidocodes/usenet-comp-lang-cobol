FAQ stands for "Frequently-Asked [or -Answered] Questions". The idea is
to collect questions that have been, er, frequently asked, and the
answers to them that have appeared in the newsgroup.

With any luck, a newcomer would then be likely to find that the question
he was about to ask has already been answered, and the answer has been
recorded in the FAQ list, which saves time and bandwidth for the
regulars, as well as serving as a collective memory for the newsgroup as
the cast of regulars inevitably evolves.

Note that the FAQ list is not created to save time for the newcomer,
although it does. It is created to save time for the regulars.

Now, this may a little subtle, but please bear with me. There is no such
thing as a consensus on what questions should go into the FAQ list. It
happens indirectly and automatically through the questions and answers
that are posted in the newsgroup. It defeats the purpose if the FAQ
maintainer puts something into the FAQ list because it seems "desirable"
rather than because it has been "frequently asked".

If the FAQ maintainer must ask a question, then that question is ipso
facto not a frequently-asked question.

Nor is there such a thing as a consensus on the answer that should go
into the FAQ list. If there happen to be two (or more) camps in a
newsgroup, two (or more) representative answers are included in the FAQ
list -- or the question is discreetly omitted.

Last but not least, just as the questions and answers posted in a news
group "should" be on-topic, though many are not, the questions and
answers chosen for inclusion in the FAQ list should be on-topic. That is
to say, in this newsgroup, they "should" be about the computer language
CoBOL. (All components of a newsgroup name are significant.)

Although heated discussions about netiquette can and do take place on
individual newsgroups, these matters can not be decided by one newsgroup
any more than individual towns can decide whether to drive on the right
or the left. Usenet as a whole works better if everyone drives on the
same side, and it doesn't even matter too much which side it is.

The things that have worked best in practice so far in the history of
Usenet are codified in "Rules for Posting to Usenet", RFC 1036, and a few
other periodic postings in news.announce.newusers. However, far from
viewing these rules as the distilled wisdom of experience, many newcomers
view them as the coprolites of old farts, and feel themselves honor-bound
to violate the rules simply because they exist.

Fortunately, no one will die if you insist on expressing your
individuality by putting your response above the quoted text instead of
below, as someone might if you expressed your individuality by driving on
the wrong side of the road. There is, however, a small increase in
entropy, roughly the equivalent of expressing your individuality by
throwing a candy wrapper out of your car window. IOW, when even your
newsreader tells you to trim your quotes, trim the damned quotes, don't
turn off the warning.
