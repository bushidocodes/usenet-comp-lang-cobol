Bullshit. I've been around the block more than a few times, and this is 
typical of the reasons why projects overrun. 

Let's re-state the problem. A programmers (not a user) wants a replacement 
for his code. I have provided an equivalent replacement. Not more precise, 
nor less. IF he had a problem with precision, I'd expect a programmer to 
state that in his statement of requirements.

In the case of users, you MUST know the users and you must know his 
application. And yes, if he says six months I'd have made it accurate to 
within the run cycle of the system (if it runs monthly, then we're arguing 
about nothing). An online system needs timestamping to within the 
resolution of the hardware clock (and even CICS hit a problem with that as 
machines got faster). That's part of the fundamental design of the system. 
In this case, it's not required and not dealt with.

OK. I did explain my position, but you read right past it. One last try.

I know who you are. You've got around three to five years' experience in 
the industry, and you're pretty good at your job. And you're good because 
you care enough to research such arcane stuff as date routines and care 
about them being right. Would I hire you? Yes I would (but you'd probably 
refuse through injured pride).

I'm approaching forty years in the job, and I can say that, in the next 
three to five years, you're going to go through a Revelation that forces 
you to discover that, for all your knowledge and enthusiasm, the IT world 
is bigger than you know. When that happens, you'll be onto the web, 
surfing all sorts of sites trying to learn all you need to know about 
your new environment (and you'll do it because you're good and you care) 
and if you're good enough, you'll establish a new reputation as a 
designer/project manager (whatever opportunity presents itself).

And if you're good enough, that will continue for another eight or so 
years. You'll be an experienced project manager/ lead designer or 
whatever, but you'll be bothered by people not doing what you think is 
essential to see your project/your design go in easily - because of what 
you think is their own stupidity. But you can't do everything, so you 
have to live with it.

After eight years of that, you may find yourself as Programme Manager. 
This is tough, because you've got layers of people underneath you who 
won't do what you want. But with a sense of humility (which you'll have 
developed by now) you might be able to persuade them to follow your 
vision of how a system should evolve.

And sometime during that period, you'll be screwed by some programmer 
deciding to take your specs and expand it beyond all recognition, 
creating his own masterpiece. It takes longer, and is difficult to debug, 
difficult to commission, and costly to maintain. And these are your 
problems, not his - he thought he was doing the Right Thing.

As his manager, you need to be able to tell him in the fullness of time 
that he screwed your project because that little piece of code has caused 
overruns because everyone else was waiting for it, despite the hours of 
overtime he did and the nervous breakdown he suffered.

And then you'll decide that it's better engineering to build something 
tailored to the requirement, rather than complicate things beyond what 
was needed.

Has this happened to me? Not in such terms - my problem has always been 
finding people good enough to build the designs I've done. But I've been 
there when I was a junior - I've built routines that you wouldn't 
believe, then been faced with maintaining them long after I should have 
moved off the job onto other work. And in the end, following some 
Revelation, I rewrote them to be more mundane and merely meet the 
requirement established by the system.

So. Build away. It's not my project, and there's nothing wrong with 
knowing more than you code. But do no more than is necessary, because 
added complication will come back to bite you.
