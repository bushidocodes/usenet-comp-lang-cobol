All of the instances I see corresponding to KJV "for ever" in the TR are 
translations of "eis tous aionas ton aionon" or its variations.  I think 
your translation is fair, although I would contend that it implies more 
emphasis in English on the definite articles than is supportable from the 
Greek.

The phrase has all the earmarks of a conventional idiom, just as "per omnia 
saecula saeculorum" or "in saecula saeculorum" do in Latin, and there are 
three "degrees" of this found in the GNT -- "eis ton aiona" corresponding, 
say, to "forever"; "eis tous aionas" to something like "for evermore"; and 
"eis tous aionas ton aionon", "for ever and ever".  I would contend this 
last could be reasonably rendered "from eternities [each consisting] of 
eternities" and that's literally closer than the traditional rendering, I 
think, to conveying the idea from the Greek (though perhaps a bit over the 
top!).

The only reference I see to any of these that can be considered to show 
eternal hellfire and damnation for the "general population" seems to be 
Revelation 14:9-11, and that's for a very specific transgression that, 
arguably, nobody's *yet* been placed in a position to commit.  And applying 
citations from that particular book to any era other than that which it 
describes are particularly troublesome, given its apocalyptic bent.

Which specific passages are you referring to in the New Testament that show 
indisputably that "the eternal abode of those who reject Jesus is a 'lake of 
fire' ... that lasts forever", in so many words?   I know you believe 
they're there in black and white; all I'm asking for is chapter and verse 
for them, because my searches of the KJV don't ferret them out.
