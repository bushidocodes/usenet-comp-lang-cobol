```
Here, again, a little history is helpful.
Prior to computers as we know them NOW,
there were unit record machines that processed
information one 80 or 90 column card. Perhaps
even 5 chanel paper tape.

As a consequence, such processing required lots
of matching, collating, and sorting. Please do not
drop the box!

Early computers interfaced THAT world. DASD
or Mass storage, etc. were yet rare. Tape drives
were limited in capacity AS WELL.

INTERNAL first and last pass SORTING fit that
world well. A Univac I "tape time" was six minutes.
Most applications ran in that time as programs were
and still may be I/O bound.

Doing a colation, or selection, etc. at the same time
the data was handed to the sort program saved as
least on tape time. Tapes on that system held 1000
blocks of 60 characters each. An input file could
easily be many boxes of cards converted to tape
requiring multi-reel files with their control problems.

The guy who upgraded Flowmatic to integrate
the early handling with the sort, was as still is
in my book a hero.

These days, your camera can have more storage
and it is the size of a stick of gum or perhaps
a small CD.

Such a technique is in my opinion applicable
in todays world. Are we storing pictures? or
other forms of data or NOT.

That guy I mentioned worked for Univac at
Ontario Hydro at the time, and he fixed
Flowmatic to allow this.

Certainly, in todays world there are needs
to accomidate different conditions and the
pico seconds saved are Not that significant.

Oh, perhaps they are if you are trying to
hit Mars.

Warren Simmons
wsimmons5@optonline.net

P.S. If I don't get to use a spell checker on
this there may be several laughs for some of
you, and I do not mind at all.
```

```
This reminds me of an old early 1970s Varian machine relabeled and sold
as the DC1100 by Burroughs. As one of the few Burroughs programmers
in the Southeast 'smart enough' (read 'dumb enough' :) to tackle assembly
language on the thing, it was much like what we would label microcode
today. At the end of the two week course, the final goal was to ring the
bell on the TTY console. That's it, no message, just ring the bell. I had
several years heavy assembly background, so my group was the only one
of four to complete the project. It took a routine roughly 1,000 (that's right,
'one thousand') lines long just to read a card. You had to read each column
in two I/Os, first to read the uppermost 6 punches (yes, punches = bits)
and the second to read the lowermost 6 punches. Then you had to translate
the 2 bytes of punches using the encoding scheme of the card. One small
advantage was that the sucker could read or punch ANYTHING, even 80
columns with all 12 punches out in each column. It made an interesting
sound when you punched that, reminiscent of a dump truck filled with
gravel striking a wall. Though the computer was much larger (spinet
piano size) the front panel was similar to the first 8-bit microcomputers,
like the Altair or IMSAI, with lights and switches to read and set
addresses and data in binary. There was no OS, and debugging was fun,
fun, fun, especially at 2AM. :-)
```

```
Sorry!! I was wrong about the # of rows, columns, etc.    :-(      My error
..... I trusted faded memory instead of checking references.  Here's a brief
description copy and pasted from the webpage at URL
http://homepages.cwi.nl/~dik/english/codes/punched.html  -----

Finally by the end of the sixties IBM came with the much smaller 96 column
card. The definition has 26 rows of 32 columns with punch positions for
small roundish holes. The 26 rows were divided in 6 upper rows, 2 rows that
were always blank and 18 lower rows. The first 32 columns were defined by
the 2 topmost rows of the upper part plus the 6 topmost rows of the lower
part, giving 8 punch positions for each symbol. The remainder was defined in
a similar fashion as shown in the table below. Within such a group the rows
were designated from top to bottom as D, C, B, A, 8, 4, 2 and 1. The reason
for this curious arrangement was the possible interpretation of the symbols
on the top of the card. A symbol was only interpreted when the D and C
punches were both absent, in that case on that place the symbol was printed
(this allowed interpretation for the most commonly used 64 symbols). In rows
7 and 8 32 further symbols could be printed, but they would not have
associated punches.
```

```
There aren't many situations where I pick which sort is the most efficient to
run.   I usually pick which is the most efficient to maintain.

With this in mind (and with knowledge of run-time efficiency in the back of my
mind) - I default to external sorts - using internal sorts where the data
indicate.

Sometimes the internal sort can winnow the amount of data down more effectively
than an external sort does.

Sometimes the internal business logic necessary to create the sort key works
well in CoBOL - and there is no good reason to write out the file with the new
key simply to make the sort external.

Sometimes the sort order is determined by business rules.   Sure, I can write
out a SORTIN file, but why?
```
