The following is my solution to the original challenging post
by Thane, to develop a program using Cobol, to determine if
there are any common parents in a pedigree's family tree and
calculate the coefficients.
Having just seen Bill Murray's solution, ALOHA Bill, I have
to say his is a lot more sophisticated and beyond my limited
Cobol II knowledge.
My solution is more simplistic in the approach and could be
done in any release of Cobol.

The way to solve this is to view it this way.
The INQUIRY-ID has 2 parents, therefore there are 2 paths,
one up from it and the other back down to it.
You could use 2 separate arrays for this but i'll use 1.
The INQUIRY-ID 'A' starts the read(randomly) on a MASTER file
and if it has 2 parents loads the PUP, SIRE and DAM into the
array FAMILY-HISTORY. The SIRE and DAM that is stored in
the array will become your future PUP keys.
I read the MASTER in the order of up the left side 'B' first,
then up the right side 'C' last. You do this for the number
of GENERATIONS-REQUESTED(maximum 10) or until you have no
more parents to put in the key. As you read, you store the
results in the array, unless it has no parents, then you get
the next one to be read from the array. The Row number you
got the parent from is put into the FROM-ROW column.
This points you along the same path back down the trees
after you establish the common parents.

The master file format is:
01 MASTER.
10 PUP PIC X(8).
10 SIRE PIC X(8).
10 DAM PIC X(8).
* PUP is the key

* The array is:
01 FAMILY-HISTORY.
10 HISTORY OCCURS 1024 TIMES.
10 FROM-ROW PIC 9(4).
10 FAMILY OCCURS 3 TIMES PIC X(8).
* Same layout as Master, Pup-Sire-Dam.
10 SIRE-FLAG PIC X.
10 DAM-FLAG PIC X.


Keep track of the END-UP Row number you'll need it later.
Now using the above samples the array would hold.
* From-row Pup Sire Dam Sire-flag Dam-flag Row(not stored)
0 A B C 1
1 B E I 2
2 E H L 3
2 I H K * 4 END-UP
------------------------------------------------------------
1 C D M 5 begin-down
5 D E G 6
6 E H L 7
6 G H N 8 END-DOWN
------------------------------------------------------------
0 - - - etc upto 1024

As I mentioned, those with no parents don't need to be stored,
in this example these are H, L, K, N and M.
Now we search the array from 1 to END-DOWN (8) looking only at
the SIRE's column and find and flag the SIRE's that have any
duplicates further up the tree. You don't need to flag the lowest
row your comparing to (i'll call it LOW-ROW), the other HI-ROW.
You don't let the LOW-ROW go past END-UP and you won't need
to flag anything if HI-ROW is greater than END-UP. What am I
saying? You only need to flag the duplicates in the up tree.
If your LOW-ROW hits a row with a flag on it, for that column,
you bypass it and go to the next row.
So when your LOW-ROW is 1 HI-ROW can go from row 2 to 8.
So when your LOW-ROW is 2 HI-ROW can go from row 3 to 8.
So when your LOW-ROW is 3 HI-ROW can go from row 4 to 8
and flags row 4 for H.
So when your LOW-ROW is 4 it finds a flag and skips that row.
Now LOW-ROW is greater than END-UP so it's done.
You do the same procedure later with the DAMs.
While doing this, to make this a little faster, I'm going to
store the ID of any parent that is a duplicate and which row #'s
they reside on, into another array called MATCHED-UP if HI-ROW
is <= END-UP, along with LOW-ROWs ID and row number.
Otherwise the info goes into MATCHED-DOWN.

01 MATCHED-UP.
10 DUPE-UP OCCURS 20 TIMES.
20 UP-ID PIC X(8).
20 UP-ROW PIC 9(4) OCCURS 10 TIMES.

01 MATCHED-DOWN.
10 DUPE-DOWN OCCURS 20 TIMES.
20 DOWN-ID PIC X(8).
20 DOWN-ROW PIC 9(4) OCCURS 10 TIMES.
* These could store any value from 2 to 1024 for 10 duplicates
* of 1 parent, and holds upto 20 different duplicate parents.

* In this case we store:
* MATCHED-UP. MATCHED-DOWN.
* UP-ID UP-ROW UP-ROW to 10. DOWN-ID DOWN-ROW DOWN-ROW to 10.
E 2 E 6
H 3 4 H 7 8
L 3 L 7
* These are the row numbers from the HISTORY array,
* where duplicate(common) parents are located.
Note: The flag on row 4 is to stop you from repeating the 7 and 8
which was done by row 3, but you still store the 4.
You use these arrays to get your starting points for common parents.
>From the above you will match up the following:
E 2 with E 6
H 3 with H 7 and H 8
H 4 with H 7 and H 8
L 3 with L 7

>From this we can build the common ancestor trees by having 2 more
arrays to store the IDs.
The first ancestor tree:
01 UP-TREE.
10 UP-ID PIC X(8) OCCURS 10 TIMES.
The second ancestor tree:
01 DOWN-TREE.
10 DOWN-ID PIC X(8) OCCURS 10 TIMES.

So using E from UP-ID and the 1st value (2) we go bacwards down
the HISTORY array using the PUP and it's FROM-ROW to return to A.
You store the common parent and each PUP into the UP-TREE.

* This will produce:
* UP-ID(1) UP-ID(2) UP-ID(3) UP-ID(4) UP-ID(5) etc...
E B A
* (Es PUP) (Bs PUP)
* You'll have to unload this from RIGHT to LEFT to end up
* with A-B-E.
* You keep the UP-TREE until you've finished all common
* down-tree parents.

Now for the 1st DOWN-TREE.
And using E from DOWN-ID and the 1st value (6) we go backwards down
the HISTORY array using the PUP and it's FROM-ROW to return to A.
You store the common parent and each PUP into the DOWN-TREE.

* This will produce:
* DOWN-ID(1) DOWN-ID(2) DOWN-ID(3) DOWN-ID(4) etc...
E D C A
(Es PUP) (Ds PUP) (Cs PUP)
* You'll have to unload this array from LEFT to RIGHT
* to end up with E-D-C-A.
That should give you A-B-E and E-D-C-A.
* (UP-TREE) (DOWN-TREE)
You'll have to drop one of the common parents for the next routine,
along with the INQUIRY-IDs 'A'.

When you unload the UP-TREE and DOWN-TREE, place the IDs into
another array VERIFY-TREE.
If you find a duplicate the tree is invalid and you ignore it,
otherwise you compute the coefficient.
Note: I just found out the formula for the coefficient is:
SUM-COEFF = SUM-COEFF + ((0.5 ** PARENT-COUNT) * 0.5)
I think, that's it.
Seems like it would need SUM-COEFF PIC S9V9(17) BINARY.
Where PARENT-COUNT is the number of parents left in the
valid tree.

01 VERIFY-TREE.
10 UP-DOWN-ID PIC X(8) OCCURS 20 TIMES.
* This could hold upto 10 generations.

Check the VERIFY-TREE.
B-E-D-C is valid, no other duplicates.
