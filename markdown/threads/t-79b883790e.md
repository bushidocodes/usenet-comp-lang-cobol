I'm not sure it meets the definition of "game", but in a two dimensional
array, elements are either alive or dead (or "X" or space  OR 1 or 0
depending on how you want to represent it).  In going from one generation to
the next, a live cell (i.e. array element) stays alive if surrounded (one
"square" in any of 8 possible directons) by 2 or 3 live cells, a dead cell
becomes alive if surrounded by exactly 3 live cells, and all other
combinations will kill a cell.  "Out of bounds" cells are treated as dead.
In a very simple example (called traffic lights) if on a chessboard only the
cells b1, b2, and b3 are alive, the next generation will see a2 and c2 come
alive while b1 and b3 (only one live neighbor each) will die.  The cell b2
stays alive.  Successive generations will cause the live cells to go from a
row of three vertical to three horizontal or vice versa.  I recall seeing
this in a Scientific American article sometimes in the late 1960's.
