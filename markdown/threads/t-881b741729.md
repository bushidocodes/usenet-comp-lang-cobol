```
Everybody is talking up the demand for COBOL programming skills
prior to Y2K, but nobody discusses demand after. IMHO there must be
a chance of considerably reduced demand. The point being that...

... many organizations will staff up for the problem, and
quite reasonably decide they can 'down size' 'right size' as in
fire every one when they are no longer needed.

... if every man and his dog (and woman and her dog) decides
that COBOL programming is a great way to make money for a few years
a lot of new people will be attracted to the profession. Most of whom
will be clods who know nothing and produce less than they consume,
however after 2-3 years of real experience some will actually learn
something (too late to make any real contribution of course).

So I am sure we can all look forward to the chance of getting
the sack on Jan, 1, 2000, or shortly thereafter. Salaries might
also have increased considerably and everybody, including the press,
will be horrified that 100,000's
of mere 'geeks' might be earning $100K to $200K per year. Management
will also be horrified, but will console themselves that by firing all
this over priced talent, in the interests of remaining competitive,
they can justify their next round of $10 million dollar bonuses.

A few survival strategies suggest themselves...

... don't get duped into mentoring these idiots, so turn down
the 'team lead', 'tech lead' or 'mentoring' opportunity unless it
comes with a huge pay rise. We all know that you will end up doing all
the work. Especially avoid having to provide on the job training for
all these high priced teen-aged consultants who will no doubt be paid
more than you.

... if you are a contractor, charge what that market will bear
(that is to say, be a good capitalist). Don't sign a long term
contract and be prepared to bounce around at short notice if rates
rise. The end of the millennium only occurs once every thousand years.
Might as well take advantage of it while it is here.

... prepare yourself for a potentially long period of
unemployment. Don't lock into any long term financial commitments,
like a 30 year mortgage on a big new house, on the basis of your new
income. Save your money and get ready for maybe several years of
unemployment, or maybe get another degree or something.

Live long and prosper... MJL.
```

```
The firm I work for: years ago they decentralized by creating level II
datacenters around the world hooked up to the level I mainframes.
Then we centralized to a "megacenter" due to cost savings thus
removing the level II machines. Now we are chasing the client/server
bullet but now they've realized the cost (peoplesoft/sap/etc) and
aren't moving everything.

Yes, PCs are getting faster but mainframes are getting faster and
cheaper too. Check out IBM, they've seen an increase in mainframe
orders. Client/server can be a mess. I've just been through upgrades
to naming services and time services to support an internal security
upgrade against hackers. Can you imagine the time/expense to keep all
your worldwide systems in sync when your security application
invalidates your encryption key every few seconds (kerberos)?

Client/server helps but it's not a silver bullet. I believe it was
last year that FedEx, a Malcom Baldridge Quality aware winner, frankly
admitted it would be too expensive and without a proper payback to
convert to client/server.

Just a thought,
```
