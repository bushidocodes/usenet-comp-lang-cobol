Generalizations are USUALLY logically invalid, or indeterminate, (not 
necessarily "wrong") because they imply the use of ALL. This makes them 
"sweeping statements" and it is often impossible to determine the logical 
validity of a sweeping statement.

"Islands are surrounded by water." (implicitly: "ALL islands are surrounded 
by water") is a sweeping statement  that happens to be true. (It is true 
because the DEFINITION of an island is "a body of land surrounded by 
water").

"Donkeys eat carrots." (implicitly: "ALL donkeys eat carrots") is a sweeping 
statement whose truth we simply cannot establish. If there is ONE single 
donkey somewhere on Earth that DOESN'T eat carrots then it cannot be true. 
As we are not going to check every single donkey on the planet, the logical 
truth value of the statement simply cannot be established. (Importantly, any 
further statements derived from it or based on it are also unable to be 
resolved so the whole argument can never "succeed"). However, as MOST 
donkeys eat carrots it is not WRONG. In strict logical arenas it is 
important to qualify sweeping statements with SOME, MOST, ALL, A FEW... and 
so on.

For these reasons, generalizations are (usually) best avoided. :-)
