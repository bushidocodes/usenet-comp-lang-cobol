Greetings,

I'm probably opening a can of worms here that I really don't want, but I'll
do it anyway.

I'd like to get some feedback from everyone on what criteria you use to
judge how effective, efficient and work oriented a programmer is.  What do
you judge productivity on?  Is there some qualitative method you use to
compare one programmer to another?

Old straws like number of lines of code don't seem to be effective.
Differences in coding techniques often render that comparison ineffective.
Bug rates also seem to be somewhat arbitrary.  For instance, when code is
being developed, you tend to expect bugs.  Gross targets, like number of
programs written, fail to take into account complexity of the programs
themselves.

I've done some scanning in the trades for possible metrics to use and I
haven't made much progress.  Any input from others "in the trenches" would
be greatly appreciated.

For background, I'm a Systems Manager in a COBOL/CICS/VSAM shop.  We do both
production and maintenance work.  I have 3 teams under me, with a total of
15 programmers at present.

Thanks,

Paul,

I wouldn't normally respond to a post like this  because, as you said, it is
a can of worms <G>.

However, you were frank and stated your position and I believe you are
sincere.

Here's what works for me....obviously, there are no hard and fast rules and
individuals are the most important thing, but the following generalisations
have been arrived at over a 37 year period, of which at least 15 years have
been managing (and, more importantly, as a manager, working with...) other
people...

1. Forget metrics. Management is more Art than Science.

You are already finding that indicators like lines of code mean diddley...
(I also have severe reservations about Function Points (when used to measure
performance; OK for assessing predicted workload, but even then, don't take
it too seriously. There is NO substitute for experience and
understanding...). Most of the supposed metrics that I have encountered have
a more negative effect on morale and performance than they are worth. There
is little to be gained by measuring the performance of a team which is
already de-motivated by knowing that they are not trusted and will be
subject to scrutiny and bean counting. Besides, they will probably spend
more of their time filling in activity logs than you can really afford...<G>

The ONLY criterion on which I measure performance (my own and other
people's) is: "Was the desired result achieved?"

If it was, was it achieved in the desired timeframe and cost constraints?
Did it "hurt"? (We have all worked on "painful" projects). How could the
"hurt" be alleviated in the future?

2. So, if we are not going to measure anything, how DO we assess
performance?

1. When allocating tasks to people sit down with them 1-on-1 and go through
the job in hand. Ask THEM to tell you how long it will take to deliver. If
their answer is unacceptable, don't erupt and start screaming "incompetence"
<G>, simply ask for a justification. Be open, honest, and fair. If they are
"sandbagging" tell them you will get someone else to do it, (or do it
yourself) and find another task for them.

(This works extremely well... I had a case once where a prima donna
estimated a certain job at 10 days because he wanted to "coast" it and I was
a new Manager who he hadn't met before, so he made the mistake of assuming I
was just like all the rest... I expressed surprise at his estimate. He then
asked me how long I thought it would take. I said: "around 6 days for a
reasonably experienced programmer; a man of your calibre could do it in
4...." He then challenged me to find ANYONE who could do it in 4 days, so I
did it myself. Never had a further problem with that particular individual.
And the others looked at me in a different way also...<G>.  (Of course I
worked 12 hours a day on it, but they didn't know that <G>, and it was worth
it for a few days to establish credibility...)

If they continually "pad" estimates, consider firing them. It is a betrayal
of trust.

If they genuinely will take longer than you think is fair, factor in their
experience and training and cut them some slack. Tell them you are doing
this, but don't do it for more than three months. If they need training, see
that they get it. (Provide it yourself if you have to...a major
responsibility for you as a Manager is to help your team to develop.)

Once estimates are agreed with individuals, do everything you can to ensure
they succeed. Support them and help them. Make sure they get the resources
and tools required to do the job.

Now it is possible to measure performance. If they continually achieve the
targets they have set, and you have agreed with them, EVERYBODY is happy,
and you can argue that your team are performing well.

If they don't, (and, if there are any, it will always be one or two
individuals that don't...), have a little private chat with them expressing
your concern, find out if there are other factors affecting their
performance and again, cut them some slack, but only on the understanding
that their performance improves.

If it doesn't, fire them or move them. If you can't do either, make it clear
to your own Management that there is a problem and you are now carrying dead
wood which will debilitate the whole project and put it at risk.

(In all the years I've been doing this stuff, I had ONE occasion where this
was necessary and, fortunately, I was empowered to fire the persons
concerned. I didn't want to do it and I took far too long over it. My Boss
advised me to do it (but wouldn't force me) and I believed I could "manage"
the situation. He was right and I should've done it much sooner.

The lesson learned is: If you find people with incorrigible attitudes for
their own twisted reasons, get rid of them at the start or after one
straightforward and blunt discussion. It isn't personal. It just debilitates
a team to know that one or more members are covertly hostile and "swinging
the lead", while everybody else is motivated and trying to achieve their own
and the project's goals.)

3. PLAN your projects, but avoid TASK oriented planning. GOAL oriented
planning works much better. So your MS Project plan should state GOALS and
their expected achievement dates, not TASKS and how long they should take.
(Individual programmers will be assigned tasks, as described above, but
these should always be in the context of achieving a certain goal. Stress
the goal, NOT the task...) When planning, assume 6 hours actual work from
each individual per day... If you feel comfortable assessing tasks in hours.

4.Try and avoid using the Waterfall. If your Company implements it as a
Management Policy, do EVERYTHING you can to get that policy changed.  RAD
type Management, based on User involvement, Timeboxing, and iteration
towards agreed prioritised goals, is INFINITELY more successful.

I am presenting on this at CW2001. Try and attend if you can. I am currently
preparing a lot of "give-away" material on this subject that is designed to
help Project Managers. Sorry if this sounds like Evangelism, but I have
tried it both ways...RAD and the Waterfall. There is no comparison. And
COBOL projects CAN be RAD managed with or without so-called "RAD Tools".

The whole idea of metrics is connected back to the Waterfall. You can see
from the discussion above how the overall GOALS are MUCH more important than
the individual tasks. Instead of wasting time filling in metrics forms, get
your teams motivated to achieve the project goals. Then turn them loose and
they will spend their time working towards the goals, rather than worrying
about the performance statistics.

4. Make a point of chatting each day to individuals for a few minutes about
what they are doing, and listen to them. Ask them if they feel they will
achieve the goal they are working to. If not, find out what went wrong and
help them fix it. It is incredible how the whole atmosphere of working
changes on a team that is succeeding. Work actually becomes fun, problems
are a temporary diversion like a crossword puzzle that provides satisfaction
when you solve it.

Promote an atmosphere where it is OK not to know something, provided you ask
about it. Positively reinforce "right" action and attitude.

And take them to lunch when they achieve milestones. <G> (Seriously, it is
very important to show them that you really do value what they are doing. I
have personally paid for team lunches in Companies where it wasn't the
"policy" to do so. The amount spent is well worth it.)

SUMMARISING:

Back to your original request...

> I'd like to get some feedback from everyone on what criteria you use to
> judge how effective, efficient and work oriented a programmer is.

First, "effective and efficient"...
Do they deliver on time and on budget? Are your time constraints realistic?

Remember that overstressed teams are a false economy. Pressure can be
applied for brief periods only, and when it is, make sure that you are the
last to leave at night...If you have to pull an "all nighter" (and I don't
recommend it) do NOT coerce them into it, encourage them. Provide pizza in
the office, or a meal break. Don't let ANYONE work after 1:00am. The fatigue
induced errors cost more than sleeping on it and working a few extra hours
for the next few days. Besides, if you plan it properly and monitor progress
in an informal way, you shouldn't need to pull all nighters...

"work oriented"
(Personally, I don't care whether they are "work oriented" or not...I've
worked with people who were fanatical surfers or skiers and saw "work" as
simply a means to this end... No problem. They deliver in the Workplace,
they get to indulge their passion. And, as a manager, I now have something I
can motivate them with...."I'll pick up your ski pass next weekend, if you
get that thing running by Friday". (Beats paying overtime...<G>)

>What do
> you judge productivity on?

Results.

And how much of an asset to the Team as a whole they are.

If the "best" programmer is an insufferable asshole who treats his/her
colleagues with disdain, work on some attitude adjustment. Until you can
achieve that, keep them fairly isolated. If it looks like a lost cause, get
rid...Good work that causes other people's work to suffer is NOT good
work...

On the other hand, a junior colleague who is eager to learn and enthusiastic
and listens to the more senior people, can be a huge asset, even if they are
not technically as productive (yet) as their peers. I don't like "levels"
within a team and I abhor the "us and them" mentality of "management" and
"workers". Everyone in the team is valuable or else they wouldn't be there.
In brainstorming sessions and walkthroughs, EVERYONE has an equal right to
be heard and their opinions and ideas considered and weighed. It is WHAT's
right, NOT WHO's right...

>Is there some qualitative method you use to
> compare one programmer to another?

Why would you want to? They all (including yourself) have strengths and
weaknesses. That's why we build teams.

If there are budget cuts and someone HAS "to go" (and I am assuming that
this is inevitable and you have fought it tooth and nail <G>), your
judgement call should be based on the saving that must be made,and not on
the individuals in the team.

Obviously, most of the above is simply opinion. But I have tried to address
the issues you raised, within the scope of a very limited post.

It is refreshing to see Managers who are prepared to actually ask these
questions. I wish you every success.

Pete.

Understand totally what you are saying and we all go through this. It is
very tempting to think "Well, I can do this in 2 days, why is he taking 4?".
The fact is that people with less experience than yourself WILL take longer.
That's part of why you are there. To help, encourage, and transfer some of
your own skills to them so they can grow. (the nice thing is that when you
do this, it is good for your own growth also...<G>)

I also understand  (and share) your concern about imposing timeframes (even
realistic ones) on others. That was why I came up with the strategy of
letting them set the timeframe. (Within reason, of course...and subject to
negotiation. I covered this in previous post.) Think about it from your own
point of view. What is the initial reaction when a manager says:"I want this
by tomorrow. Do it now." ?

There is a reflected difference in attitude between the shop where the
Manager says: "I have allocated you 5 days to do this."

And the one where the Manager says: "Let's have a look at this task and see
if we can come up with a realistic estimate, together."

The first relects "Us and Them". The second reflects a Team, where the
Manager is there to co-ordinate the effort and help and support the people
on the shop floor.

Guess which one achieves the best results and has the lesser staff turnover?

I remember  a Manager doing something like this to me once. As the Senior
programmer at the time, he figured it was OK to drop something on me and
demand a result within a timeframe that was simply stupid. I grinned at him
and said: "Well, I hope you find someone who can do it. I can't." He was
outraged, but eventually settled down and we ended up with a tight but
achievable timeframe.

The point is, Paul, that if you create the right team spirit and attitude,
people WON'T sandbag their estimates. They will give you the time it will
honestly take.  In fact, as things improve, you will find individuals trying
to better their own last estimate and, in effect, competing with themselves.
(I know this is true because I've worked this way in the real world and
found it to be so. There is no Academic theory behind it; it just works.
PROVIDED you treat them fairly and honestly and reward extra effort.) As
they succeed, their confidence grows and this leads to more success...and so
on.
That's why, as a Manager, you have a vested interest in making sure they
succeed...<G>

You trust them, but use your own judgement and experience to see how close
they are to your own estimate. I think this is what you mean when you say
"comparing " them to yourself. Nothing is really achieved by comparing
people against each other or yourself. All that happens is that some have
their confidence shaken and others have their ego boosted. Neither of these
results really help your project.

Glad it is of help. I have been fortunate to work with some very enlightened
Managers when I was a Programmer, and some fantastic teams since I have been
a "Manager". I have seen what does and doesn't work.

My experience has been that metrics are, at the very least, of dubious value
and managers who put faith in them are asking for disaster. At the worst,
they are a thoroughly de-motivating and counter-productive imposition on a
project team. There are enough problems in developing and implementing
commercial computer systems without having Big Brother breathing down your
neck at the same time. The only Metric that is worth anything is the Result.

I'm sure you've seen at least one system where programmers fill in the
"status" of various tasks, "Coded" "debugging" "in test" ...all tied back to
the Waterfall. In my naivete I even tried automating this once when I was a
Senior Programmer, tying a status update into Job Submission, counting the
number of compiles and links and tests...honestly thinking that if people
didn't have to do the paperwork they wouldn't object to being measured...(I
was much younger at the time and trying, as always, to do my best...<G> We
dropped it after a few months...)

Got loads of information, but none of it actually helped us achieve the goal
of completing a system. All we could say was that we knew what was going on,
but that  knowledge didn't help us in any way to achieve the end result any
quicker or better.

If one guy takes 10 compiles on average to get something running and another
takes 4, but the one who takes 4 spends days drawing flowcharts and desk
debugging, so they both come out with 4 days at the end, where is the value
in the metric? The only meaningful information is that it takes them 4 days
to get a program clean...the RESULT. (This kind of measurement is even less
use if you are using PCs for development; it doesn't cost any more no matter
how many compiles, links, tests, the guys use...yet we still see mainframe
sites obsessed with this 1970s bullshit...) You could compare the code
produced and decide if one is more maintainable than the other, but that is
a subjective judgement, not something you can measure. Still, as the
Manager, you have the right to make this call and take action if seriously
unmaintainable code is being produced.

I think the reason metrics don't work is because we are dealing with people.
People are notoriously random variables...<G> But when you get them
motivated they can achieve fantastic things...

They aren't mutually exclusive, but, from my perspective, it seems that one
is simpler than the other, at least as we've been doing it.  The Waterfall
process seems to be the way to do it if you want to tightly track where
you're at in a particular project.  But along with that, it requires fairly
constant montoring which takes a great deal of time.

The Goals oriented method, on the other hand, states "This is my
destination".  However the programmer can best get there (within reason) is
the preferred way.  This cuts out a lot of montoring and seems to improve
the lot of management, at least to that degree.  Or it could be seen that it
allows management to be somewhat cavalier in their duties.  Just because
every step in the process isn't being followed closely, doesn't relieve
management's responsibility to stay on top of things.  At best it cuts down
on the administrivia that seems to occupy a larger portion of the day.

One thing I would add that most places ignore that the military
stresses:

a regular scheduled (quarterly is minimum) review of how that person is
performing. Even if (or especially if) everything is good, Say it (
preferrably in writing) to reinforce it.  This also allows you to bring up
something small (or maybe several smalls) that really should not wait til
annual review, correct them now with the individual's INPUT.
If you put it in writing, then the second time you can refer to the first
(and ask him/her to bring his/her copy of previous memo to meeting).
Also ask how the employee feels in the position, (overwhelmed,
underworked, just about right, ready for more / harder work, etc)

Done right, this 'nips it in the bud' for most problems, lets the employee
know that his work and work quality and job satisfaction is important to
YOU.
