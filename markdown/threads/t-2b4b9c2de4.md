Yes, I see your problem.

Here's my take on it. (Totally opinion and many here will disagree. But,
like you, I have also had to argue COBOL's case with senior and middle
management, and you are absolutely correct that making wild,
unsubstantiable, claims for zillions of lines of code simply destroys your
credibility and makes them less likely to listen to what you are really
trying to say.)

1. COBOL IS declining in importance. The reasons for this are manifold.
Newer, quicker, cheaper, techniques, methodologies, and tools, and a failure
by the COBOL User base to keep COBOL in the forefront by embracing the
latest facilities in it. (The IT development World is moving away from
procedural code into components and Objects and there is only a limited
place for standard COBOL in this arena. It isn't just COBOL; PL/1 is also
affected similarly...)

So, accepting that COBOL is no longer "the only game in town", is there
still a place for it?

The answer is an unqualified "YES" and it has nothing to do with how many
COBOL programmers there are worldwide (Thane reckons more than 6,000,000; I
would say nearer 3,000,000 (I am excluding China), but neither of us can
support our claims, and it is irrelevant if you are looking at a specific
case in a specific organization), or how many lines of COBOL code are in
existence (which is also irrelevant, even if you COULD substantiate the
claims, UNLESS your organisation is planning World domination and will take
over the COBOL maintenance and development for the entire planet <G>...I see
the Chinese are getting into COBOL through the ISO ...you're not connected
with them I take it <G>?)

The FACT is that in your organisation COBOL  is currently being used. (If it
isn't, then you are on a hiding to nothing. To suggest starting COBOL
development NOW would be an extremely difficult position to defend, despite
the passionate arguments you will hear from people in this group, UNLESS
there is a clearly defined IT strategy for the organisation where COBOL can
be shown to play a role...see below).

Given that you HAVE COBOL code and it isn't "broken" then there is certainly
a good case for continuing to ensure some COBOL expertise remains on your
site. The cost of replacing the existing code before it has returned on the
investment made to develop it, should not be acceptable, given that the only
drive to do this is "fashion"...

2. There are much broader issues that need to be considered when you look at
it from the point of view of management of the IT resource, rather than the
narrow view of a COBOL programmer on the shop floor. Irrespective of the
number of lines of code currently being maintained, is your company likely
to continue to develop bespoke systems in-house for the foreseeable future?
Has NOBODY suggested outsourcing or a package solution? Will the current
level of investment in IT development be maintained for, say, the next 5
years? Is the company likely to increase its exposure on the Web and
Intranet? In other words, is there an IT strategy that is clearly defined so
that everyone knows where the company is going "ITwise"? If there is, it is
much easier to argue where COBOL fits into said strategy; if there isn't,
then it is like the tail wagging the dog..."We'll build a strategy around
COBOL or Java/Unix..." (As if the two are mutually exclusive...they're
not.)No. Decide where you are going, THEN decide what you need to get there.

3. I have worked with COBOL for 38 years. I have seen the language and its
role in IT change. The way of the future is NOT the way of the past, but,
during the transition, there is a very valid case for maintaining some level
of investment in COBOL. How long will the transition take? I said "15 years"
in 2000. I have seen nothing to make me change that estimate.

All of the above are reasoned and (hopefully) valid arguments for using
COBOL in 2004. You don't need exorbitant claims for a Worldwide COBOL user
base. which has doubtful bearing on your specific case, anyway.

As an aside, let's consider that there are several trillion lines of COBOL
in existence, and around 3 billion are currently being produced annually.
(Nobody knows what the real figures are, but few would contest that the
number of lines per year is declining...).

So, it is fair to say that the World has a pretty solid investment in COBOL
legacy systems.

BUT, even if this is so, the "new" techniques of data mining, warehousing,
and code refurbishment have automated tools that can rip through source code
at more than a million lines a minute...

I have seen demonstrations of tools that can isolate COBOL code functions
and turn them into objects. We all know there  are tools out there can can
convert COBOL to Java (to quibble about whether it is GOOD Java or not is
simply churlish; it is a start. The original COBOL may not have been that
"good" either...).

The point is that if all the COBOL code in the World currently, needed to be
replaced, it could be. (And in a realistic time scale, say, less than a
year...remember it is NOT a serial process; it is occurring on many sites
all over the World, even as you read this.). As systems reach their "sell
by" dates, the pressure to replace them becomes more urgent.

The Gartner estimate of $25 a line is based on it being done MANUALLY. It
won't be. The real cost is more likely to be fractions of a penny per line.

That is why I said that the number of lines of code and the number of COBOL
programmers, doesn't matter when considering whether to use COBOL or not.

COBOL must stand or fall on its own merits, measured against the needs
(current and future) of the organisation, and the organisation's IT
strategy.

Currently, it can do this. Whether it will still be able to do so in 15 to
20 years, only time will tell.

Pete.
