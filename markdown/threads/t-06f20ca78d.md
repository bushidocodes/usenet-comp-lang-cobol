```
I lecture a course on Files and COBOL at UEA, Norwich. I've been using the
course partly to get students thinking about file and database optimisation,
partly to introduce them to the language, and partly to get them used to a
more mainframe development environment: all the poor lambs have seen is
point-and-click interfaces, and it's good for their souls (and their job
prospects) to realise that there are other ways of approaching development.

Until this year, I've been using Vax COBOL (and let's not argue about how
closely the Vax approximates a mainframe environment). Over the summer the
University Computing Centre (CPC) is retiring all the Vaxes, and I'll
therefore have to teach the course on DEC Alphas, PCs (with Linux as an
option), or Macs. CPC are proposing DEC COBOL on the Alphas, with Informix
C-ISAM as the indexing add-on. I'm not enormously keen: this doesn't offer
the sort of mainframe experience (TM?) that I want the kids to get, and
doesn't give much variety in terms of access methods.

I'm looking at two other approaches. One is ACUCOBOL's product, which seems
to give a good implementation of the '85 standard. The other is to turn the
course on its head and take an object-oriented approach to the whole thing:
the attraction of this is that I teach an OO Analysis and Design course in the
same semester, with considerable cross-membership.

I'd be grateful for any suggestions and reactions.

Luke Fitzgerald
```

```
I use Acucobol-85 and have for about 8 years. The company I work with
currently retails a software product for Large Scale Government and
commercial contractors, some of which employ over 8000 employees in a
single division. These companies mostly select UNIX derivatives because
of their price vs. performance although we also have VAX computers as well.

1. Performance: Our real world performance numbers that come in from our
clients show Acucobol approximately 5+ percent slower than a compiled and
linked implementation specifically Vax COBOL. All other variables being
equal. Its slower because it is interpretive runtime. Although it is
slightly penalized in performance, to our organization it maintainability
and portability far outweigh this drawback.

2. Support: In my humble, yet subjective opinion, support from Acucobol
for the product is above the industry standard.

3. Extensibility: The runtime can be extended by writing your own C
routines which can be called by any Acucobol program.

4. GUI: Acucobol also offers a new version called Acucobol-GT with
built in Graphical syntax.

I'm currently working on a client/server project where I'm using Acucobol
and ANSI Standard Embedded-SQL to manipulate financial data stored in
Informix, Oracle, Sybase and other DBMS may come later. We use a GUI
builder for the user interface and database connectivity and Acucobol for
the server side processing since most GUI tools do not have the syntax
necessary to handle sophisticated financial manipulation and reporting and
client side processing is just to slow for the size of companies we work
with.

Therefore I would recommend using Acucobol for UNIX(whatever flavor) and a
database so that the students can learn to think in long vs. short database
transactions, rollback and recovery, locking concurrency and database
design. Although, I have used ISAM files for some time I believe the
database engine will make ISAM obsolete in the future. I say that because
of my experience in the retail software development market. If you ran a
PC-UNIX and Acucobol the relative investment could be small.

Hope this is of some help

P.S. No I don't work for Acucobol Marketing.
```
