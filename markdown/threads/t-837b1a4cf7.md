If you want to change something on certain lines and certain columns
using ISPF, you have several ways:

1) you can exclude the lines you want to change with the exclude
primary command or with the x line command
2) you can mark lines between labels by putting labels on the first and
the last line of the block you want to change. Say you use .f for the
first line and .l for the last line.

Suppose you want to change any character on column 50 to 55 into spaces
you can give the command:

1) c p'=' ' ' all x 50 55
2) c p'=' ' ' .f .l all 50 55

both ways will work. If you don't like it, you can undo things with the
undo command. I worked also with Roscoe. I prefer ISPF a lot!

The subject of mainframe editors raises my blood presure.
Because mainframe working environment is one of
the largest cause of reduction of quality and quantity of
work one can do.

As I often say, we work *for* a mainframe environment,
not *with* it.

As for editors, this type of software is the most individual
interface to the user. Type of education, biography,
frequency of use and availability determine which editor
is best.

In most cases, for larger edition work, an editor on
a desktop computer is by far the best choice. Also, of
course, some sort of network for transporting files to
and from the mainframe. However, although I have my
preferences, I do not recommend a specific editor.

Forcing employees to use a editing environment which
they do not like not only reduces quality and speed of
work. It even has consequences for health. The motivated
programmer defies every impediment to his work.
Unnecessary obstacles have dire effect on motivation
and wellness. Also, using an unfamiliar editor may
cause a programmer to look more frequently from
keyboard to screen and back. This may have ill
effects for the eyes, expecially for older users.
(Standard medical textbook tell details, but reading
that is no fun, I warn you.)

So, if there is a company policy "one editor for
everybody" and "what is good for everybody is good
for you", I deduce that there is no specific interest
in quality of work and no interest in health of employees.
Alternatively, there may be considerable ignorance
in management about how to archieve results.

IMHO the best choice is: For small editing jobs on
the mainframe, use any editor which is there. If that
happens to be your favourite editor, you can of course
use that editor for everything. For all others, a
desktop solution is best.

And, whether mainframe editing or desktop-editing:
Speed is important - I dont see any use in keeping
programmers waiting.

Herwig

address space creation failure messages due to page space
shortages).

each TSU (Time Share User) takes up one address space.  As a
result, IBM makes many of the TSO & ISPF modules RENT for loading
into LPA/ELPA.  Each TSU has to allocate the same data sets
(initial logon/proc), and then allocate all of the data sets
needed for the user (ISPF libraries and the like).

requirements.

moving from them to TSO in the area of resource requirements

and that you are going to have to change ROSCOE "execs" to REXX

ROSCOE just like you can in WYLBUR, so that you don't have to use
CICS (and you can make direct IDMS calls!).

TSO/ISPF, you can expect to need to define 2-4 more PAGE data
sets AND add a new CPU AND add C/E-store to the system to hold
down paging.

TSO/ISPF or to CICS.

I'd quietly check to see if all this has been taken into

I always liked VM/CMS/Xedit on the mainframe. Especially when they added
Rexx (years before it got to TSO/ISPF). That environment was almost
infinitely configurable, and Rexx skills came easily in a PL/1 development
shop (many similarities of syntax).

When we migrated from VM to MVS in the mid 80s I recommended ROSCOE over
TSO/ISPF for developers because:
- (a) there was the significant performance hit of multiple address spaces
(TSO) over a single shared address space (ROSCOE) - our developers were used
to being logged on all day and we didn't want to end up with them having to
queue for machine time;
- (b) there were [very] superficial similarities between the look-and-feel
of ROSCOE and CMS/Xedit - in particular, emphasis on the command line - no
vast collection of ISPF panels to navigate to do simple tasks (and we didn't
like the ISPF "=" command-line shortcuts for aesthetic and learning-curve
reasons);
- (c) ROSCOE had a macro language (RPF) that didn't quite live up to Rexx
but was still a whole lot preferable to Clists.

Of course, all the Sysprogs and Ops had TSO/ISPF but the developers got (and
still have) ROSCOE. Our Sysprogs and Ops still prefer ROSCOE for tasks that
don't absolutely require ISPF. They use another non-IBM product for the
machine consoles.

Over the years we've built up a fairly large library of macros providing a
range of day-to-day functions. We have LIBRARIAN as well.

A number of TSO products can be run under ROSCOE (ROSETSO) though not all
have appropriate threading models for running in a shared environment. We
run ISPF products such as SDF in a slave ROSETSO region. Not having full
FILE-AID support has always been a limitation, though.

In those days ROSCOE was developed and sold by ADR. When CA took it on there
were the usual promises of much more development investment, which went
largely unfulfilled and subsequently dried up entirely. No surprises
there.... I certainly recognise the management inclination to ditch CA.

Anyone who has a PC or Unix workstation but is nostalgic for Xedit - or just
wants to get back to coding via a keyboard rather than a mouse - might like
to look at Mark Hessling's excellent GNU freeware packages at:

http://www.lightlink.com/hessling/

Mark has a very good simulation of Xedit called THE (The Hessling Editor) to
run on various platforms (including Win 95/NT) - it incorporates Rexx
support. He also maintains the equally excellent Regina Rexx interpreter.
Add some file transfer services and you've got an interesting proposition
for a cheap offline coding environment. No interactive PL/1 or COBOL
compilers, though. (You can always pay IBM for the Visual Age products if
you really need it.)
