On  2-Feb-2004, Habitant <berlutte@sympatico.ca> wrote:

> >There is no federal election in the U.S. (except by the Electoral College).
> >ALL elections are done under the authority of each state.
>
> How inconvenient!

Maybe so.    It was done so for good reasons, but as a business problem it did
have the following huge advantage that I hadn't thought of before the last
presidential election:

Consider that the popular vote for the country was about as close as the popular
vote for Florida was.    But nobody wanted to recount the votes in most states,
as those states weren't close.   Heck, some states had hundreds of thousands of
absentee ballots uncounted - that couldn't have made a difference even if they
were 100% for one candidate.

If we had had a single national popular vote, they would have been counting
chads all over the country.

This is something to think of when anybody designs a national ballot system.  
There are business advantages to isolating components of the election.    Heck,
this is an issue that is important with lots of business decisions that
programmers and analysts need to account for.
