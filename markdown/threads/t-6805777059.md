The answer to this question is: you wouldn't want to even if there was.

What am I talking about? Ok, you already said that your program needs
some time to 'massage' (my term) the data, which is why you can't lock
it (w/UPDATE) on your initial READ. So, you read the record and you go
off and do your thing. Finally, when you're ready to update it, you read
the record again with UPDATE and, whoa, guess what? While you were off
processing the data, somebody else has updated the record and your data
is no longer valid.

Probably the best way to control this is to include a last update
date/time stamp *within* the record, but this needs to be designed into
the record and all programs that update the file need to 'follow the
rules'. If you want to really be sure everything is kosher, you would
need *three* copies of the record: (1) the record before you updated it,
(2) your updated version of the record, and (3) the record as it is
after your READ with UPDATE before your REWRITE it. Then, compare (1) to
(3) before writing (2).

I'm not sure this is that big a deal, though, because I think that if
your share options are specified correctly, you should be able to do a
record-level update lock without locking the entire file, but this may
still lock the CI and may be different depending on your CICS version,
platform, etc. I'm sure somebody else will have the answer to this one.

CICS locks records at the key level ... however, VSAM itself locks records
at the sequence set level, i.e. all control intervals within a control area
are locked until such time as the update takes place ... CICS lock is far
more granular than the inter-region lock by VSAM ... In lieu of saving many
copies of a record image during non-exclusive reads I create a doubleword
"bit-key" by adding each successive word in a record ... upon re-reading
the record for update I regenerate the bit-key to see if anything has
changed since the non-exclusive read ... if the keys are different I
backout the transaction and start over again ... I've used this technique
for many years very successfully, the technique basically errs on the side
of conservativism and has the advantage of requiring very little data be
passed between pseudo-conversational invocations of your CICS program.

The method you describe below is inadequate as any other program could
update the record you are working with between the time you read it the
first time and the time that you rewite it.

Here is one solution that I have used. Write a temp storage queue when the
program executes for the first time. Read the record you are after and
rewrite it to the temp storage queue. When you are ready to update, read
the same record for update and compare it to the record you read in from
the temp storage queue. If they are the same, do your rewite. If they are
not the same, you have a situation where some other program changed the
record before your program could and you need to start over. Delete the
temp storage queue when the program terminates so that it does not stay in
cics memory.

This seems even more akward than your method, but your method does not
provide for the possibility of another program changing the record.

If someone has a better way. I would certainly appreciate hearing it.
