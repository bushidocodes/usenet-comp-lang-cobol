thus freeing the programmer from keeping up with all his states.  It

advantages.  And Cobol is starting to stick its toes into the
object-oriented waters.

  While I don't recommend "mixing old & new" coding features, you are

With Micro Focus COBOL (for example), it is ENTIRELY possible to mix

   EXAMINE & TRANSFORM & OTHERWISE & OO & Intrinsic-Functions

all in the same source code.

Each compiler-vendor decides when (if ever) to drop support for "older"
coding techniques and syntax.  IBM (when they introduced VS COBOL II, R1 -
in the mid '80s) decided to DROP syntax support for some - but not all -  of
their older extensions. (Even then, they weren't part of the "current"
standard language definition.)  This is why VS COBOL II, R1.0 - although
*basically* only a '74 Standard compiler didn't support some of the old
language such as EXAMINE, TRANSFORM, READY TRACE, etc.

Micro Focus (on the other extreme) *never* drops support for previously
supported language constructs - even "bugs" in earlier compilers (cf. their
"OLD-NEXT-SENTENCE" directive).

Most compiler vendors don't go to the extreme that Micro Focus does - but
almost all continue support for "older" language - if they think their
customers still want/need it.  (See IBM's support for the CMPR2 compiler
option - supporting the '74 Standard all the way thru IBM COBOL for OS/390 &
VM - but finally dropped in Enterprise COBOL).
