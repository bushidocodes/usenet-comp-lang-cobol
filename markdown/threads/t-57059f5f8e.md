The reason every one is "making such a big deal" is because A) It is
estimated that there is a 500,000 programmer shortage to actual make the
changes necessary by Year 2000 (USA Today, Mar 21, 1997) and the code
changes are not the difficult OR the labor intensive, part. The TESTING
of the changes is estimated to be 60 to 70% of the work involved. This
is not just single-program testing in a vacuum...most Year 2000-impacted
programs also feed data to other programs; these will have to be tested
whether they have Year 2000 "problems" or not. Also, the time factor
alone is a major problem: most companies do not have to Dec. 31 , 1999
to make this change; most companies have targeted Dec 31, 1998 as the
date, so that they can test for a year. This narrows the window to
roughly 18 months. Further, companies that are vendors for other
companies may find that those companies are mandating that they be Year
2000 compliant by a deadline that is FAR sooner than Dec 31, 1999. The
best example of this is MasterCard, which told all of its bank card
machine providers that they had to be YR2K compliant by THIS September
!!!!

It is a far greater problem than simply changing a date....

I'm a systems programmer and the year 2000 problem really isn't an
issue for me, but i do talk to a lot of I. S. programmers. None of
them ever have told me that the changes are hard, or even non-trivial
for that matter. The problem lies in the volume. If you don't have
much experience programming outside of hobby or a couple of classes,
most people just don't realize how much code exists at even a small
shop. The I. S. department of a typical small company with just four
or five full time programmers using a development platform like COBOL
may have 300 different data file definitions and 3000 programs with a
total of over 250,000 lines of code. The inter-dependence of these
programs makes even trivial changes to them just not like anything
you'll see writing small class projects that are usually completed in
one all-night programming session just before they are due and then
thrown out like a paper cup at the end of the semester.

I'm afraid you are part of the problem.

The actual code changes may be FAIRLY minor, but then again, they may be
MAJOR. Things like hard coded data literals (If year < 80 move etc...),
will need finding and changing. Date fields may or may not be
identifiable. File conversion will ne necessary. All those archived tapes
in the salt mine vault will need to be considered, (How can I read them if
I change the programs to read all the new format records?). Testing will
be a nightmare. I may (and have) run into a proram in a job stream without
current source or no source at all. Date fields in some screens MAY need
to be changed. In one file we (stupidly) keep the actual MAP as the data
record, and read and write directly from it. Conversions to this file are
always time consuming and fraught with poetential for error.

Just going in and chaning dates in not all that has to happen. There are
some tools to aid in the analysis, but ultimately EACH program must be
examined for problems and there are THOUSANDS of them!

This is not as trivial as you make it.

I heard a MIS professioinal quoted on NBC television news. He stated, "I
cannot fathom the conecpt that there is enough time remaining to fix the
year 2000 problem". They were discussing the fact that many companies
would fail and that there were not enough people to attack the task at this
point.

This is a BAD thing. Even if your shop has fixed all it's problems, it is
a bad thing. What if your business suppliers are not ready? Can you
survive?
