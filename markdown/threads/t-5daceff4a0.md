```
A frequent contributor here has asked me to post an attached photograph.

As CLC is a non-binary group, none of the news servers I use will accept the
photograph, so I'll describe it
briefly:

It is a  photograph from a 1954 edition of Popular Mechanics, showing how
they expected a "Home Computer" might look in the year 2004.

The caption with it reads:

""Scientists from the RAND corporation have created this model to illustrate
how a "Home Computer" could look like in the year 2004. However, the needed
technology will not be economically feasible for the average home. Also the
scientists readily admit that the computer will require not yet invented
technology to actually work, but 50 years from now scientific progrtess is
expected to solve these problems. With teletype interface and the FORTRAN
language, the computer will be easy to use."

(If anyone would like to see the photograph, I have posted it to the
following server:

http://enternet.co.nz/users/dashwood/popmechPC1954.jpg )

The first thing that strikes me about this is the optimism and confidence
with which it is expressed.  It was written before the first satellite was
launched, before men (or even monkeys and dogs) went into space, when an
international phone call travelled thousands of miles along copper wire (and
required a small mortgage to pay for it...),  before Hi Fidelity audio was
invented (never mind stereo and sound surround), and a 12 inch black and
white TV was considered "luxury". Just 15 years later, men walked on the
moon.

As an aside, there are still some "old timer" techies around and they have
recognised the technology depicted in the photo. It is a composite, but much
of it is taken from the control room of a submarine.

One of them writes:

""Amazingly enough, you've found a control room for a ship or submarine,
probably nuclear powered.  I recognize many individual items and whole
sections of that control panel.  Ignoring the keyboard and television, the
room is manned by three men at the panel.  The double wheel at the left is
the start of the linkage to the ahead and astern steam throttles, the center
is the boiler or reactor control and indication section and the right is the
electric plant control.  The engineering control room of a Trident submarine
is still virtually unchanged from this photo...."

Pete
```
