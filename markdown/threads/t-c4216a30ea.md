COBOL needs your help.     A US public review is underway on the
reaffirmation of the current ANSI COBOL standard.   If not reaffirmed,
the standard will be withdrawn.  You might think it does not matter if
the current standard is withdrawn.   But IT DOES MATTER.  Withdrawal
of the ANSI standard will create a perception that nobody cares about
COBOL and the media will use it for more articles saying COBOL is
dead.  It is a *black eye* for COBOL.

Can you do anything to ensure the standard is reaffirmed.  Maybe not.
Due to an administrative oversight the public review was not started
in time to complete reaffirmation by the required date IF THERE ARE
negative comments. I'm sure you know that Bill Klein has submitted a
comment opposing reaffirmation.

Does that mean your support of reaffirmation would be wasted.
ABSOLUTELY NOT.

Even if not reaffirmed, do you want it said that COBOL died and nobody
cared?   Positive support for reaffimation would do a lot to blunt the
harm that will be done by withdrawal of the standard.  PERCEPTION IS
EVERYTHING.

What can you do?   Send your comment in support of reaffirmation BY
APRIL 4, 2001.     You do not need to be a US citizen.    The
following gives the information you need to submit a comment (send to
both NCITS and ANSI, reference the COBOL standard and its two
amendments):

Perhaps two standards are better than one.

1. The official standard from either ANSI or ISO, or both.
2. An industry standard from a consortium of COBOL
    vendors, users, and other interested parties.

At all times, the industry standard will be a proper superset
of the official standard.

The official standard provides a high degree of stability
and portability by having no options and being revised
or amended no more frequently than every five years.

The industry standard provides optional features where
the rules apply only to those choosing to implement the
features. This standard may be amended much more
frequently, perhaps annually.

When an optional feature from the industry standard has
been widely accepted across a range of processors, it is
migrated to the official standard.

While there are an amazing number of details required
to set up a consortium, it is possible and was done with
OMG < www.omg.org >.

Re Screen Section, Report Writer, OO or anything else being "required"
versus "optional'.

At the beginning of the development of this revision, their was STRONG
consensus to get rid of "optional" things in the COBOL Standard.  (This was
almost demanded by WG4 and was strongly supported at J4.)

The EXAMPLE that was used for the reasoning on this was Report Writer which
was optional - but would have been useful to many COBOL programs and
programmers in the '70s and '80s - but was NOT implemented *because* it was
optional. Therefore, programs that did use it (because it was available or
their primary platform) found that their applications were NOT portable.
Furthermore, because some vendors didn't implement it, many programmers
didn't use it and therefore MORE vendors dropped support for it in
subsequent releases.  This situation was viewed as BAD for any feature in
the next COBOL Standard.

This is a position that I *still* support.  As much as I *love* Report
Writer, I would prefer seeing it removed completely from the Standard than
having it be "optional".  The same for Screen Section, OO, etc.

UNFORTUNATELY, somewhere along the line, the draft standard keep the
"removal" of OPTIONAL as a term - but ended up with the EXACT same
"concept" - now called "processor dependent'.  This ORIGINALLY included only
those items that "used to be" in the "hardware dependent" list (i.e. things
that don't make sense if your compiler is targeted at a hardware/operating
system that doesn't support it, e.g REEL for systems without tape drives or
WRITE AFTER ADVANCING for systems without printers).

For various historical, political, and/or country-specific reasons,
Processor Dependent is now defined so that the compiler vendor has COMPLETE
choice in whether or not to implement those features - and need not document
any reason for NOT supporting them.  (This sounds like "optional" to me -
but at the last WG4 meeting, one attendee - who works for a compiler
vendor - said words to the effect of "Let's not call it optional - so our
developers may not notice that they aren't required to support everything in
this list".)

Currently Screen Section, READ PREVIOUS,  and File-Sharing/Record-Locking
*are* in this list; report writer, ISO 10646/Unicode codesets (in some but
not all uses), and OO are not in this list.

I do *not* think that there will be much support at either J4 or WG4 to
change the contents (add or subtract) to this list at this late date.
However, I would suggest that the readers of this note "look at" the current
list - and the list of "enhancement" in the Substantive Change NOT effecting
list - and decide if you think some things are in the processor dependent
list that shouldn't be or aren't and should be.  This is a totally
REASONABLE public review comment - I just don't know how "successful" such a
comment would be.

Don't know if it helps Howard, but I'll try to answer your question. (In the
good old days (before OO) I used multiple Entry points frequently...)

1. Multiple Entry points allow you to reflect different "facets" of the same
code. The "advantage" was not so much a programming one (you could certainly
achieve the same result with multiple programs, as you pointed out) as an
efficiency one.

As a general rule, the less code you have, the better. There is a very high
risk of error when the same code must be maintained in multiple places.

For example:

Let's say I have a  called program that gets passed a buffer of characters
and converts them to Hex in another buffer.

Sometimes I want to convert Character (ASCII or EBCDIC) to Hexadecimal, and
sometimes I want to convert Hexadecimal back to character . The lookup table
would be duplicated if I used 2 modules. By using 2 different Entry points
to the same module I get ONE piece of code which deals with "conversion" and
I don't have to duplicate any reference data. (It is also self documenting
and easy to remember; call 'Char2Hex' using char-buffer, hex-buffer or, call
'Hex2Char' using char-buffer, hex-buffer...simple, efficient and
consistent.)
(This is a very simple example but it is taken from real life and I actually
have such a subprogram which runs across platforms. I wrote it 30 years ago;
today I would use different Methods in a component, exactly as Jimmy
described for OO.)

2. I have had much more complex examples with up to six different entry
points to manipulate the SAME data in different ways (hence, different
"facets" of the same code or data.)

Sometimes different Entry points can be used to pass different formats of
parameter to the same underlying code "engine".
(This is a variation on "function overloading" in modern parlance.  A
"variation" because function overloading technically requires different
parameters to be passed to the SAME object; although we are using the same
engine, it will have different named entry points...but it is a pedantic
argument. The reality is that the same code can accept various different
parameters. There are times when this can be very useful...)

It would be fair to say that there is no advantage in using entry points in
today's World. I can't think of a single example which could not be
(better?) handled using OO Methods.

Both entry points and Method coding are MUCH better solutions than
duplicating code into separate modules.

Pete.

Exactly.  And an AS/400 workload would crush a Windows box.  A dozen
pick-ups could move the contents of a container, a dozen Windows servers

> Making analogies like that doesn't answer the question. 

> Can someone please
> be specific about what the advantages of an AS/400 over a Wintel network

You probably need to be more specific about what you mean by 'Wintel
network'.  Do you mean NT Fileserver + local ethernet fat clients, or
TSE plus thin clients, or multi-tiered central clustered servers with
fail-over plus remote client-server nodes ?

While a 'NT fileserver plus fat clients', or even TSE or other thin
client may seem cheap and cheerful it doesn't scale well.  You can't
just add more CPU power and memory and hope to get more throughput (just
as getting the 7 litre V8 pickup doesn't help it shift containers). 
What works with a handfull, or even a couple of dozen users on Windows
can't be made to work with hundreds of users.  With Windows that means
you have to build clusters of multi-CPU servers and then add fail-over
and load-balancing.  This makes it _very_ expensive.

With multi-tier on multiple sites there are also severe support and
distribution problems.  The more units in the system the more likely
that some will fail.  With Windows the user is often tied to a machine
by his configuration and his private data.  Users can't just switch to a
different screen/keyboard and carry on working (without added software
to cope with this that then requires even more server capacity).

By having everything on a central box with reliable built-in fail-over
the system keeps running regardless of any failure.  It doesn't matter
which screen a user is at they can continue even if 'theirs' failed, the
business still runs.

It is not so much the compelling features of an AS/400 (or AIX or
UnixWare or HP/UX or Sun or ..) but what Windows lacks.  Most of what it
lacks can be added by clustering multi-CPU servers, having sufficient
redundancy, and adding additional software, but then the costs add up to
more than the AS/400, and certainly lots more in support costs.

Back to the Standards.

I offer the following:

1. COBOL does not need anybody's help. It is alive and kicking and probably
in better shape now than it has been for its entire life. The Language is
fine; the public perception of it is wrong. This perception has arisen
historically because COBOLlers have allowed it to. It needs to be changed
and I believe it will be. (I am certainly doing everything I can at every
level possible to get it changed...<working my little fingers to the bone
writing stuff that will hopefully stimulate some thought>)

2. The ANSI standard made very little contribution to this. Bill has made
the point eloquently in his article on COBOLReport that years have gone by
with nothing to show for it except more meetings, more documents, and very
little achieved.

3. In another private forum I made these same points a couple of months ago;
you can put in all the effort you like, until it MOVES no WORK is done.
Personally, I believe the members of the committee are dedicated people.
They are just not being managed properly. Maybe they are constrained by laid
down procedures. If so, these procedures should be changed.  If they CAN'T
be changed, then Bill is 100% right to question the value of the whole
process.

4. The best point Thane made was that Open Source won't happen without an
approved standard. It certainly won't be helped by not having a standard.
Thane says they would be sued by vendors but I'm not so sure. If the Open
Source movement were the most responsive to User needs, we might see them
implementing stuff that is needed and wanted before the established vendors.
A competitive marketplace would certainly keep everyone on their toes...

5. I think we need to consider what has been achieved by the current
situation. COBOL has not died because there was no new standard. There is
still (and will be for some years yet) an established marketplace. History
has shown that the Companies who respond to this marketplace do well.
(This is not aimed at Merant ,and I realise they are a different company
than Micro Focus, but I'll never forget how extremely angry I felt when they
shafted the User base on VISOC. I actually enjoyed and supported the Micro
Focus products and then they just pulled the rug...What happened? A free
offer of a COBOL compiler from Fujitsu. An alternative. Frankly, I would've
been glad of any alternative. ANYTHING to get out of Micro Focus...! Nearly
four years later we see Fujitsu establishing themselves as a Market leader.
I have never regretted switching. I have bought and will continue to buy
their products, as long as they continue doing business the way they are.
The point? LISTEN to the Marketplace if you want to do business there....)

6. How many of the end users you develop applications for, care (or even
know) whether you are using standard COBOL?

Would you stop developing applications if they did? No. Because as long as
there is a Market for what you can produce, you will produce it...

7. I see a future for COBOL. But it will be different to the past. Very
different. Some forthcoming articles on COBOLReport and About Legacy Coding
will postulate where I see it going. I can tell you certain things right
away...

7.1 The days when everything revolved around Source code and the Language we
used was paramount, are almost over. Modern Networks require different
Languages to support them and the solution is to use the BEST Language for
the job, NOT any particular ONE language. COBOL is still the BEST language
for programming Business solutions. But these solutions will be developed in
an altogether different way in the near future.

7.2 The second nail in the coffin of Source code being King, is the rapidly
increasing availablility of Components. These are Objects with a standard
interface that can run on any platform and are consistent. They do what they
are specified to do and can be "plugged" into various "containers" (Windows
forms, existing source code (OO), and  Web Pages.) Corporations will buy
them off the shelf for less than the cost of a COBOL programmer/Day. It's a
bit like "outsourcing" the corporate IT development in the same way the
motor pool is outsourced, you buy or lease the vehicles but you don't
maintain them... As these Objects are supplied (appropriately enough) as
OBJECT code, there is no longer any need to maintain a skilled Source
Language Department. If the component doesn't do its job you bin it and get
another one...exactly analogous to the assembling of modular stereos or
other electronic equipment. (You wouldn't manufacture your own PCBs, neither
do you care what factory produced the bricks you built your house from, just
as long as they perform to the specification required of them.)

Against the background described above, it really seems a bit pointless for
those of us on the shop floor to worry about the Standard.  The Vendors will
respond to the needs of the Market place (if they don't, they won't be
vendors for very long...) and the essential functionality that is required
for component development and use will be part of COBOL as it  is part of
every other Modern Language (Java, C++, even VB...) namely, Object
Orientation.

The Standards committees are currently arguing about OO COBOL but that
hasn't stopped both Merant and Fujitsu producing useful implementations of
it.

I am sorry to see Bill standing as a "Voice in the Wilderness" (I know how
that feels...<G>) and I am sorry to see Thane feeling that the efforts of
the Committee are being undervalued and undermined.

The truth is that, in the long run, it really doesn't matter.

Pete.
