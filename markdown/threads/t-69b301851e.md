```
Back in the days when I was using Micro Focus COBOL, I had to do something
similar.  Please keep in mind that there may have been a better way to do
this. But being a novice in the language, not doing exactly how to do it,
and having to meet a deadline,  I did the following:

1.  Dug out a HP Laserjet PCL book to get the printer control codes that I
would need for my report.
2.  Imported my graphic into MS Word.
3.  From Word, saved the graphic as mypict.prn, making sure that the printer
was set to the printer I needed for my report.  In my case, the graphic was
at the beginning of the report.
4.  Used the MF COBOL call (x'91'?)
        MOVE PRT-ID TO COPY-LJDSK1-NAME.   (PRT-ID would be myreport.prn or
whatever)
        DISPLAY COPY-LJHDG1-DSK UPON COMMAND-LINE.
        CALL X"91" USING RESULT, FUNC, COMMAND-LIN.
    WS looked like:
        01  COPY-LJHDG1-DSK.
              03  FILLER           PIC X(19)
                    VALUE "COPY /B MYPICT.PRN ".
              03  COPY-LJDSK-NAME  PIC X(12).
           03  FILLER           PIC X(06) VALUE " > NUL".
5.  Opened PRT-ID Extend and continued with the report output.

Not exactly pretty code, but it worked quite well.  Makes me really thankful
for Fujitsu Powerform now, though.

Denny
```

```
This kind of only applies if you have a postscript printer.  One of
the ways I do lasered reports is to write out the postscript from the
cobol program.  As pertaining to the logo, you could use a graphics

it, and combining it with the data you wrote in in postscript
previously and send the file to the printer - and it prints.

The whole process gets very old fast, but postscript does allow for
some really nice forms and reports, logo's become a little bit of
pain, but once you get it down, its pretty good.  The major
disadvantage of that, is that you can only use postscript printers,
but I have found away around that.

To make a really long story short, if you use v5.1 of Acucobol, they

logos on your forms.
```
