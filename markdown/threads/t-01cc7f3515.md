```
In the specific case of EXIT PERFORM, what you've got in the '02 standard is
close to what you want.

EXIT PERFORM is currently permitted ONLY in the context of an inline
perform, and upon execution of it control passes to an implicit CONTINUE
statement immediately FOLLOWING the (mandatory) END-PERFORM matching the
most closely preceding, and as yet unterminated, PERFORM.  (ISO/IEC
1989:2002 Page 449, 14.8.13.3, EXIT statement, General Rule 9).

EXIT PERFORM CYCLE is currently permitted ONLY in the context of an inline
PERFORM, and upon execution of it control passes to an implicit CONTINUE
immediately PRECEDING the (mandatory) END-PERFORM phrase that matches the
most closely preceding, and as yet unterminated, PERFORM.  (Ibid., General
Rule 10).

Expanding the permitted contexts for EXIT PERFORM and EXIT PERFORM CYCLE
beyond the scope of INLINE performs has been proposed for discussion and
consideration for a post-2008 standard.
```

```
…[34 more quoted lines elided]…
> finish the current cycle?

Neither. It bails immediately. No other statements within the perform loop 
are executed.

In fact, you may code the above as:

sos000.
   PERFORM WITH NO LIMIT
      ...
      If {terminating condition}
      EXIT PERFORM
      end-if
   ...
   END-PERFORM.

The "NO LIMIT" business removes the necessity for 1) test terminal 
condition, 2)set flag, 3)test flag.


Where you put the "IF" statement can determine whether you want the loop 
fully executed or skipped entirely when the terminating condition occurs: 
Put it at the end to fully execute, at the beginning to fully skip. There's 
also no reason you can't have multiple EXIT PERFORM statements within a 
single loop.

Example of NO LIMIT thingy:

MOVE 0 TO NUM-IN.
READ IN-FILE.
PERFORM WITH NO LIMIT
   IF IN-FILE-FS NOT = '00'
        EXIT PERFORM
        END-IF
   ADD 1 TO NUM-IN
   READ IN-FILE
   END-PERFORM.
```
