Your 'leading edge techies', as, it appears, most programmers, know almost
nothing about Rexx, even though it is right under their noses. Anything that
makes your work easier or faster, or extends your skilld, is worth getting.
If your present environment doesn't support you, and can't be persuaded to
do so, its time to start figuring out your exit strategy.

Rexx will always be a great tool to have in your toolbox, but you might
consider leveraging this knowledge to try NetRexx, as an easy entree into
Java. Many rexx once-offs will translate easily, so your main start-up
challenge will be setting up the java jdk environment (your systems people
might even be willing to set up a java environment on your mainframe). Your
'leading edge' people might look again at your skills when you can show
rapid generation of java classes that work! (And you can see the java code
generated by the NetRexx compiler, so if you keep the programs simple, the
java should make sense too.)

my 2 cents...

It's been a few years since I had to undergo the review process, but 
this sounds like something that used to happen to me on a regular basis.
Part of the problem is that the reviewer doesn't know what it is that 
you do, how you do it, or what's involved in the process. Another part 
of the problem has been mentioned in another message in this thread- 
they have to find something to ding you on in order to justify a smaller
raise.

My solution (not optimal for quality of life, but great for job 
security) is to maintain/enhance my qualifications independently of work
(IE night classes and seminars), and bring a copy of your current resume
to your evaluation.  Explain that you're using all of your work time for
production and doing education on your own time.  Show the reviewer your
resume (which should also include your continuing education info).

This serves a number or purposes:  First, the fact that you have 
continued your education is displayed, as is your motivation in doing it
on your own time.  The subtle thing this shows is that you have an 
up-to-date resume...  With the job market as tight as it is for IT 
people, that will make most executives pause for thought.

Over the course of a few years I went from second shift mainframe 
operator to D.I.T. using this approach.  It IS a pain, make no mistake, 
but it depends on your priorities.  It also helps if you're an insomniac
with no life.  After you're a few rungs further up the ladder you can 
see about developing a life.

Another alternative is to look around for another job with a smaller 
company.  Yes, I can hear the arguments to that statement already, but 
hear me out.  A smaller company will have a smaller budget, but you'll 
be getting a larger part of it, and with fewer middle managers, you'll 
be in a better position for raises and promotion opportunities.  My last
review was conducted by the owner of the company and ended in my being 
made D.I.T.  Another advantage of a smaller company is that you'll wind 
up getting to do all sorts of interesting projects that will, in turn, 
enhance the resume...  When I started here, I was (mostly) qualified as 
a programmer, PC hardware tech, and network designer / installer / 
administrator. Now I can also design / build / program automated process
control systems. Now, here's the interesting thing from the long-term 
point of view - my only formal training was in programming.  The other 
stuff came from occasional night classes and informal 'opportunities' 
that came up during one thing or another.  Remember, a lateral career 
move is one way of avoiding the 'legacy' label.
