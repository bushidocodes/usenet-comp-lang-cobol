```
There are a number of reasons why people may be currently seeking to limit 
their further investment in COBOL.

I find myself needing COBOL compilation ability to provide components and 
executables for generated source code, but I stopped paying for COBOL 
maintenance and support some years back, and do not intend to invest further 
into it.

Many people just go with what their vendor tells them and keep upgrading 
even though the upgrades seldom deliver anything exciting or new, and they 
keep support paid up because it means a cheaper upgrade, even though the 
"support" (if they even need it) can leave much to be desired.

Mainly for my own convenience I developed scripts that allow me to run COBOL 
on ONE machine  but have any number of machines access it on the LAN. The 
vendor says this is not possible but it seems to work OK... My applications 
(if they need a COBOL compiler) can run one locally or remotely. I have been 
looking at legal ways to distribute a compiler with my application software 
but it is too hard and too expensive for the clients and for me.

The Cloud looks like an obvious solution.

ONE copy of a licensed compiler runnning on ONE core in the cloud with 
access on demand when compiles are needed.  COBOL on tap... it is just a 
logical extension of what I'm currently doing on the LAN. It is a bit like 
having a chair in front of a computer that runs COBOL. Different people can 
sit in the chair without violating the ONE Seat/ONE Machine licence.

You may remember that a little while back Gary Crook posted here announcing 
elastic COBOL. COBOL runnning in the Cloud.

I've taken another look at this service and I think it is excellent. The 
elastic compiler generates Java, which is not useful to me as a Microsoft 
developer, but I'm sure many people would find this handy. The concept is 
imaginative and simple. From a user point of view, you always have the 
latest version of COBOL, no upgrades required, can access your development 
machine from any location, and have all the housekeeping done for you 
(backups, hardware expansion, etc.) And you only pay when you use it.

I had a chat with my hosting ISP (who is amazing), to see if he has any 
plans for Cloud support. He hasn't currently but he gave me some excellent 
advice about setting up a private cloud and exposing a machine on my own 
premises, so clients can access it. I've had enough practice now at writing 
Web Services to be able to provide the necessary one very quickly, and I'll 
use a VM running on a dedicated server. The point about VMs is that you can 
snapshot them and restore them if problems arise... You can be fixing what 
went wrong, without the service coming down.

With Public Cloud availability getting over 98%, the old chestnut about not 
having COBOL available without Internet access sounds a bit thin. I'll start 
off with a Private Cloud so I can test the water, but once it is all ticking 
over I could make it Public.

I'll more than likely go ahead with this as soon as I have the time, but I'd 
be interested to hear what anyone thinks in terms of pros and cons.

Would you consider COBOL on tap for your own purposes?

Do you really need the trouble and expense of an on site COBOL compiler in 
2012? Why continue paying for upgrades and support that are really 
neither...?

If you can run one-off and batch compilation remotely, for a fraction of the 
cost of doing it on-site, why wouldn't you?

I believe Elastic COBOL is defintiely a step in the right direction.

Pete.
```
