Another mainframe user wondering why mainframe hardrives cost 20 times
as much per megabyte as PC drives.

Some mainframes support SCSI.  If so, you can add a SCSI drive without
problem.  I don't know of any mainframe where you can use IDE/EIDE
drives, though it conceivable that somene could have built a
controller for that purpose.

SCSI drives will usually be slower than the high priced mainframe
drives.  If this is for a high performance database, that's a problem.
For most purposes, having more drives can reduce contention, and
that's the real performance issue.

Two words: Reliability and Throughput.

The most current mainframe disk storage technology actually uses 3.5"
hard drive modules, and while these undoubtably have lower access times
and higher reliability than run-of-the mill PC hard drives, it is the
way they are organized into functional storage subsystems that is the
most significant difference.  You don't add a disk to a mainframe, you
add a disk subsystem, consisting of many logical drives, subsystem
controllers, cache memory, nonvolatile cache memory, multiple channel
interfaces, etc. The emphasis is on error-detection, error-correction,
no single points of failure, parallel operation, isolation of failing
components while maintaining concurrent operation, concurrent
maintenance, etc.

The first indication you have of a drive failure on your typical PC
platform is when your system is toast and won't reboot,followed by
extended down time. This can be caused either by a failure in the hard
drive or by a failure in your single SCSI or EIDE interface to the
drive.  The first indication you typically have on a modern mainframe of
disk subsystem problems is when the IBM CE (customer engineer) arrives
unexpectedly to service a unit that is still reliably running production
work, but which had automatically "phoned home" to report a component
that needed servicing.  The CE does the repair, and while the system
operator may be aware there was a problem, the the 10,000 users of the
system are unaware any problem existed!

Modern mainframe subsystems use either mirroring or RAID-5 storage
techniques to avoid data loss in the event of single hard-drive failure.
Cache storage in the multi-GB range is used to speed up read and write
access, and this is managed so as to avoid data loss even in the event
of power failure.  Redundant power supplies and power connections are
used. A single subsystem may consist of 64 - 1024 logical drives, with a
total data capacity of 300 GB to several TeraBytes.  There may be 8
independent data buses between the controller and the physical DASD
devices, and 8 independent 17 MB/sec channel paths between the
controller and the mainframe.  All logical drives may have I/O
operations in some state of progress at the same time, with up to 8
concurrently transferring data to/from the mainframe.  All devices are
accessible over any internal bus and from any of the mainframe channels
using whatever paths are free at the time. Bus or channel failures are
detectable, and a failing component can be isolated and the operation
redriven over functional paths.  There are extensive checks and balances
and use of error detection-correction codes to insure that undetected
errors do not occur within the disk subsystem or in transferring data
to/from the mainframe.  These subsystems can easily sustain 100%
reliable I/O rates well in excess of 1000 operations per second, 24 hrs
a day, 7 days a week, and these are not your 512 byte PC file transfers,
but more typically in the range of 8K to 28K blocks of data and
sometimes much larger.  And then of course one mainframe may have many,
not just one, such disk subsystems attached.

Disk storage on a mainframe is more expensive per MB than a PC hard
drive, because it takes much more than just the hard drive to achieve
reliable 24x7 sustained operation.  This is just one of the many reasons
why comparing PC resources with mainframe resources by numbers alone is
like comparing grapes and grapefruits.  You will find there are many
people today whose computing experience has been so limited to the PC or
workstation environment, where Operating System failure or occasional
hardware failures are the norm, that they have no concept of what
reliable operation really looks like, or any concept of the costs
associated with unscheduled downtime of critial online systems in the
real world.
