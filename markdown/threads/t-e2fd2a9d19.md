++++++++++++
From: John Meyer
Date: Fri, 21 Apr 2000 15:30:34
------------------------------------------------------------------------
--------

My first use for the FAQ:

I want somebody to help with my homework, but I only get smart-ass
remarks?  What gives?

Okay, let's define help here.
Certainly, C++ and C programmers know what its like to be banging your
head against the wall at 2 a.m. in the morning, trying to figure out why
that subroutine or class won't compile properly.  If you've tried and
you tried and you tried and you tried, and then come to help and post
your code, certainly you have a right to get miffed.

 However, most of the time, what it looks like is this:

Dear group:
I have a problem due tomorrow by my dumb teacher who doesn't know
anything and hasn't taught us anything.  Here are the requirements.
Thanks.

See what this looks like?  It looks like plagarism, and programmers
don't like it for several reasons.

First off, it's dishonest on a personal scale.  The teacher may have
been some bum hired off the street giving out last year's assignments,
but you still have an assignment and it's up to you to have the
integrity to do the work.  In the end, you may be the most talented
programmer around, but you sell yourself on your integrity first.  Do
the assignment as best you can, then go to the school and get the
teacher fired if he's such a know-nothing.

More than likely, however, he does know what he's doing, since he was
hired by a university to teach it and universities don't specialize in
hiring people off the street to teach random courses.  It's more than
likely that you didn't understand something he or she said, or you
didn't go to class and take the proper notes.

Secondly, these programmers went to school and did all of the work (many
times, more than what the school did; I'm always assigning myself little
projects) to earn money in this field.  Why would they write a program
for you for free, and let you take the credit, when they earn $200 or
more an hour?

Finally, there's a chance that you will end up with a degree (not
likely, but possible) and out there trying to sell skills that you don't
even have.  And then there's the chance that they'll have to work with
you and will end up doing all of your work then as well.  Or there's the
chance you'll have to design some program that could endanger their
lives, property, or whatever.

With that in mind, here are some guidelines for posting homework
assignments and problems:

* Try the assignment first hand.  Write some pseudocode down, at the
very least, if you don't know what all to do, then write as much code as
you possibly can, filling in the pseudocode

* Post your assignment in reasonable time.  Most people don't check
their e-mail at 3 in the morning looking for some student to help out.
If you don't have it in by 9 your time, don't expect to have the answer
for at least 12 hours, if not more.

* If you have problems for a long time, walk away from the computer and
do something else for an hour or two.  Pounding your head against the
wall after a couple of minutes does nothing but give you a headache and
the wall a dent.

In short, do some code first.
++++++++++++++++

Maybe John's response is longer and kinder than Boris's but the jist is
the same.
