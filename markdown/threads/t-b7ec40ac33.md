```
In a word, no.

Take a G6 chipset IBM mainframe. CPU bus to C-Store (RAM for you 
PC types) is 256 Bytes wide. Compare that to 64 bits wide. Cache 
lines are 256 bytes in the G6 chips. A CPU's Cache is > 256K and 
there are two of them (I bank and D bank) -- I can't remember 
what the actual cache size is, been working too many late nights.

Speaking of memory, mainframes have memory CENTRAL to the system, 
not the bus. That is, most non-mainframe systems make the bus 
central to the machine. As a result, n CPUs are able to 
communicate with C-STORE simultaneously (not pseudo 
simultaneously) with y I/O channels (typically connected through 
an IOP - I/O Processor).

1 => n <= 16    CPUs
1 => y <= 128   Channels

Now, given this, how many transactions a second can the mainframe 
do compared to an AIX box?

Well, where I am right now, a z/800-02 is blowing the doors off 
every RAPTOR (biggest AIX box IBM makes) in the house. And they 
are doing everything they can to handicap the mainframe in favor 
of "open systems".  BTW - we are doing in excess of 2M 
transactions in a business day, and the other guys can't hit 50% 
of that (note our transactions are complex and involve 
communications to outside entities). And, we can't seem to load 
the production LPAR beyond 35%!
```
