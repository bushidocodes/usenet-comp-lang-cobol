the domain of opinion).

I think it is a good idea to monitor personal growth as well. About once a 
year I like to reflect on how I feel about life now as opposed to how I felt 
last year :-) Usually I can see where lessons were learned (and try to make 
sure that it wasn't the wrong lesson), and I can see areas that need work 
and areas where progress was made. Age is a great mellowing influence and 
also helps with perspective. I can't prove it, but I would imagine that 
civilizations which last for a very long time are probably getting it mostly 
right. Then some completely unexpected event occurs (or they become so 
accepting of their own success that they stop monitoring themselves) and the 
civilization falls.

It is unthinkable that the USA could run out of money, or that nobody would 
be prepared to lend to them.

Yet there is a small but finite chance that it could happen.

It is hard for outsiders like me to see how much of this is political 
posturing (like Republicans forcing the Government to admit that they can't 
manage) and how much of it is actually real. Either way, I should think that 
many Americans are considering and reviewing their options right now.

They showed Al Gore's "An Inconvenient Truth" here on TV a few nights ago 
and I found it very interesting. I hadn't seen it and it is a bit dated now, 
but the facts are pretty much inescapable. America consumes most of the 
worlds resources and much of that consumption is not necessary in order for 
people to have a reasonable standard of living. Of course, many Americans 
are concerned about it, but it is hard to see how much progress is being 
made towards less waste and better utilization.

I can remember when the NZ economy rested on sheep. (We had around 60 
million of them at a time when the population was under three million; if 
the sheep had ever become organised it could have been decidedly unpleasant 
for us...). At that time it was just assumed that the wool cheques would 
keep rolling in and could prop up our welfare state indefinitely. We sent 
lamb and wool to England and they sent large sums of money back. Then Ted 
Heath became enamoured with the European Union and had visions of everyone 
in Europe driving mini-minors and the sun never setting on a bright 
industrial future for the UK, so off they went. (Heath's dream didn't 
eventuate; everyone bought volkswagens because they were better cars and it 
was Germany that did so well they were able to buy the Eastern bloc back and 
get reunited...)

It seemed that we were heading for bankruptcy on the withdrawal of support 
from the Motherland. Then people kind of woke up and scratched their arses 
and started thinking there was a lot more we could do besides raising sheep 
for dear old Blighty. Today, we are a much more diverse nation with twice 
the population and many more important "industries", although agriculture 
and farming are still extremely important for us. The economy is supposed to 
be knowledge based (although judging by some of the experiences I've had in 
certain local establishments, I think there is still work required in that 
area) and we send our young people to Academic institutions where they get 
saddled with huge debts that require them to leave the country so they can 
make "loadsa money" in real foreign currencies and pay off their student 
loans. Most of them eventually come back (a bit like me, really...) and 
bring their assets with them so it is all good in the long run.

Although life here is far from perfect financially (like most nations we are 
incurring debt at an alarming rate, but the lenders seem to think we are 
good for it and investment is coming in - the Chinese decided to invest 6 
billion dollars in NZ a few weeks ago; I might have to start learning 
Mandarin...), the sudden pulling of the fiscal rug back in the 60s was 
actually a Very Good Thing. It made us get into other things and learn not 
to put all the eggs in one basket.

Maybe the current situation in the USA will jolt the population in a similar 
way, and in 10 years time, when everyone is driving hybrids and using 
renewable energy, when the US has signed up to Kyoto, when people will pay a 
little more to have things produced responsibly, everyone will look back on 
this time as a needed stimulus which producd an overall good result.

Of course, by then I'll be living on Chow Mein and watching Kung Fu 
movies...
