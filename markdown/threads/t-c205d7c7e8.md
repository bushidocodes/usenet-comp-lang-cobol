a poem while many (most) think it is hen scratchings.

They are not using END- constructs because they are slavishly
following what they were indoctrinated with, they are doing
so (IMHO) because they find it more readable, and hopefully
less bugged.

it far less meaningful.  I would guess that some who are used to
colour coded editing would see just a mass of meaningless words
if they had to work on a monochrome terminal.

END- or '.' or upper case or colour are not inherently more
meaningful or readable, it is all just a function of the
reader.

You need to know what PC COBOL package you are using. They are different 
form one another in syntax, how to compile and run.

Here are advice on programming only.

1- The SELECT statement is one sentence. It should have only one period at 
the end. The period cannot be anywhere in the middle:
SELECT EMPLOYEE-DATA
ASSIGN TO "A:EMP.DAT"  (There is no period here)
ORGANIZATION IS LINE SEQUENTIAL. (There should be a period here)

2-  I am not sure whether you may assign the output the printer.  Please, 
check your PC COMPILER Manual. The best way is to assign to an output file:
SELECT PAYROLL-LISTING
ASSIGN TO �A:\REPORT.TXT� (There is no period here)
ORGANIZATION IS LINE SEQUENTIAL. (There should be a period here)

3- In the FILE SECTION you need not to declare variables here. The best 
suitable place to declare variables is in WORKING-STORAGE SECTION. The 
variables here have no value in processing.  You may get empty results. If 
you declare here, you should declare again in the WORKING-STORAGE SECTION. 
Why bother? Just enough here:
FD  EMPLOYEE-DATA.
01  EMPLOYEE-RECORD.
FD  PAYROLL-LISTING.
10  LISTING-RECORD.

4- In WORKING-STORAGE SECTION, you need to declare all varibales, 
including working variable, which means an immediate variable in editable 
format, before your move it to output in un-edit-able format. 
01  EMPLOYEE-REC.
    05  EMPLOYEE-NAME-IN PIC X(20).
    05  HOURS-WORKED-IN  PIC 9(2).
05  HOURLY-RATE-IN   PIC 9V99.
01  PRINT-REC.
    05                   PIC X(20).
    05  NAME-OUT         PIC X(20).
    05                   PIC X(10).
    05  HOURS-OUT        PIC 9(2).
    05                   PIC X(8).
    05  RATE-OUT         PIC 9.99.
    05                   PIC X(6).
    05  WEEKLY-WAGES-OUT PIC 999.99.
Pay attention to 2 spaces between level number and variable name. In some 
compiler, one space may cause compiling errors.
77  WORKING-WAGES        PIC 999V99.
77  HOURS-WORKED         PIC     99.
77  HOURLY-RATE          PIC   9V99.
This is the intermediate varibale used in the mathematic calculation. They 
are independent, that can be in 77 level.

5- In the PROCEDURE DIVISION you may apply structured programming, that is 
useful in Objective Oriented design:
PROCEDURE DIVISION. (Main routine is in C programming. No need here.)
   PERFORM INITIALIZATION-ROUTINE.
   PERFORM PROCESSING-ROUTINE UNTIL ARE-THERE-MORE-RECORDS = �NO �.
   PERFORM CLOSING-ROUTINE.
   STOP RUN.

Try to avoid PERFORM .. UNTIL . . END-PERFORM approach, not OO design.
6- In processing routine, every statement should have a period at the 
end.  For example: MOVE SPACES TO PRINT-REC. (period here)
7- You should move all edit-able-format variables to non-edit-able-format 
variables prior and after the calculation. In Mathematic calculatioin, all 
variables in the equation should be in edit-able format:

MULTIPLY HOURS-WORKED-IN BY HOURLY-RATE-IN GIVING ...
(non-edited format variables cannot be used in mathematic calculation).
MOVE  WORKING-WAGES-IN  TO WORKING-WAGES.
MOVE  HOURLY-RATE-IN    TO HOURLY-RATE.
COMPUTE  WORKING-WAGES  =  HOURS-WORKED * HOURLY-RATE.
MOVE  WORKING-WAGES     TO WEEKLY-WAGES-OUT.

I hope this will help you in your first few steps.
Everything is easy after that. I still remember my first days with COBOL.
