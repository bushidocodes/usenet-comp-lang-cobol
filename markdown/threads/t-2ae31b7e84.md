It surprises me a bit the general trend against COBOL SORTING.
Contrary to this anti-COBOL SORT tide, I very often take advantage of
such a tool, a quick and reliable one - without the burden of further file
description and handling. Of course, the main way of retrieving data
from files should always be by means of their keys (primary or alternates -
the subject of another thread in this n.g.). But sometimes (very frequently
in
my case) you need different ways of sorting your records and COBOL SORT
is up to the job as far as I modestly know.

As for the question in the first post, it looks a little messy the author's

record and
follow something like this:

Main.
   move start-your-file-key-value to your-file-key
   start your-file
   if ok-status 'I rather use declaratives'
      perform sequential (next or previous) read of your-file
      if ok-status
____________'added field'____________
         sort s-sort-file                                          |
         on ascending key s-location-code, s-class-code, s-id-number,
s-last-name
         with duplicates in order 'why not ?'
         input procedure sec01-input
         output procedure sec02-output
     end-if
   end-if

sec0-input.
   perform until eof-status or your-file-key not =
start-your-file-key-value
      initialize s-sort-record
      move your-file-location-field to s-sort-location
      if s-sort-location = 15
         move your-file-class-code to s-class-code
      else
         move low-values to s-class-code 'for instance'
      end-if
      make the other moves
      release s-sort-record
      perform sequential (next or previous) read of your-file
   end-perform

sec0-output.
    in the return paragraph you'll get the 15 location type sorted by
    their class-codes and thus you'll be able to handle/report them
    accordingly.
