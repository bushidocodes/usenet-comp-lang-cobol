Here we go again!  My brother and I shot and killed  seven deer with one shot each from a
Mini-14 or a Ranch Rifle.  They dropped on the spot each time and we never had to track
them.  The shortest range was about 40 yards, and the longest was 257 paces, which hit the
deer in the spine.  A deer has rather small rib bones and the .223 went through them quite
easily every time.  In the chest cavity there was massive trauma because the bullet usually
blew up.  We were using hollow point target bullets most of the time, which are not
recommended for hunting because of their frangibility.  Hunting bullets would have held
together even better, possibly permitting shots that we refused to take.  We only took
standing, broadside shots.  I would imagine that FMJ would easily penetrate deer on
broadside shots.  Humans and deer have comparable size torsos, and if a caliber is
effective on humans, it will probably work on deer about the same.  Is the .223 the best
whitetail cartridge?  No, probably not, but it will kill deer if you place the bullet in
the vitals.

For 7.62x51 (.308) Battle class infantry rifles, I recommend first the FN-FAL

very informative. If a person is going to spend close to $1K on a rifle, I
would think it would make sense to spend $100-$150 on a very good book that
tells you everything you want to know about it- vs. these $20 books that are
about half wrong on a number of issues.

I recommend that maybe you post some *generalized* facts about your plans and
situation - then maybe we can more precisely match up your needs with the
proper chambering and make/model of rifle, and handguns. Some people are well
served by a 9mm handgun and a .223 rifle, other people need something
different.

About the only things I recommend to all people when choosing firearms is:

1) Stick with a chambering that is common and easy to get ammo for. E.g.,
7.62x51 (.308), 7.62x39 (the common chambering for an AK), 5.56x45 (.223) - the
common chambering for the AR15, 9x19mm and .45 ACP (the two common chamberings
for military semi-autos, .22 LR, .357 Magnum, .44 Magnum and so on.

2) Stick with common makes and models - if you are in the US, AKs, AR15s,
Min-14, FN-FAL, M1A, and so-on. Just how many people in the US have Yugos in
308? Can you swap out parts with a Valmet or Galil? A lot of people have
Norinco/Maadi/etc. AKs in 7.62x39 and to a somewhat lesser degree chambered for
.223.

3) Stick with something that is simple, reliable and robust. Here you get into
trade-offs; the AK is supremely reliable compared to the AR15 which has some
problems in this area. But the AK has poor ergonomics, and this is definitely a
consideration for many people. It doesn't do any good to have a rifle that
always goes bang, if you cannot hit anything with it, fumble with the controls,
or you fumble reloading it (all of which some people do with the AK). At the
same time the AR15 can break or malfunction if you do not keep it relatively
clean, or fall down with it.

4) Finally, and most importantly, do not just buy the firearm and stick it away
in a closet thinking you are now prepared. Be willing to spend some time with
the firearm. If you have never owned firearms before then definitely take some
safety training and some basic marksmanship training. Then be willing to take
some more advanced training. This is where your money is going to be spent -
these courses can cost several times the expense of the firearm, but are well
worth it. Definitely be willing to regularly practice what you have learned. I
assume you are buying a firearm for self-defense. There are a number of good
books out there on the issue, two of which I would recommend you start with;
"In the Gravest Extreme" by Massad Ayoob, and "On Killing" by Lt. Col. Dave
Grossman. The latter book is not a book about how to defend yourself, but if
you have never been shot at, and/or have never had to shoot at someone, or even
if you have, I highly recommend it. It made a lot of things I have experienced
a lot clearer to me.
