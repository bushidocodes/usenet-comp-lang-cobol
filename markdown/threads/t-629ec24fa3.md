Should anyone care ...  (or to put it another way)
 If one IS interested in the topic, ...

Grammatically, the pronoun "ONE" is third person singular.  Hence, it is
correct to say

   One is often surprised by ...

If it were first person plural (as suggested elsewhere), the it would be
correct to say (instead),

   One are often surprised by ...

Similarly, the correct and corresponding possessive pronoun is "one" or
possibly even "his" (or in a politically correct environment his or her).
Hence it is correct to say,

    One is often worried about his or her own concerns ... (informal
American usage)
        or
    One is often worried about one's own concerns ... (more "correct" usage)

rather than (again if it were first person plural),

   One are often worried about our own concerns ...

    ***

Having spoken about its GRAMMATICAL definition,  it is also true that it is
often used with a "first person" (either singular or plural) semantic
meaning.  (In an earlier generation of "proper" British English, the use of
the pronoun "ONE" to replace the pronoun "I" is medium-common.)  One also
finds it used with a second person "semantic" meaning.

For various "interesting" web discussions of "one", see:

 http://www.bartleby.com/64/C001/043.html

http://webster.commnet.edu/grammar/one.htm

http://www.fortunecity.com/bally/durrus/153/gramch19.html
```

Grammatically it is not 'third person' at all but is an indefinite
pronoun (and thus not any 'person' which apply only to personal
pronouns):

"""indefinite pronouns:

  one    ... """

You may have taken:

"""All of the pronouns listed above take verbs in the third person
singular."""

to mean that it is third person, but it only says it uses the same
form of the verbs as third person personal pronouns do.
 
> If it were first person plural (as suggested elsewhere), the it would be
> correct to say (instead),

What _I_ actually said is:

>> 'One' is first person inclusive and singular.  It is used to
indicate
>> 'we' (inclusive) but individually one at a time.

That is to say that it is singular and 'inclusive of the first
person'.  It is indefinite (which I didn't actually say, but I thought
everyone knew that ;-) and thus (potentially) refers to a group.

      One must understand one's limitations.

May mean:

      I must understand my limitations.      
      You must understand your limitations.      
      He must understand his limitations.

or some or all of these at the same time for some group.
```
