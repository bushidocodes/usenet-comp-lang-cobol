```
Given some private conversations, I would be interested in some input from
any of "you" who are actually doing "day-to-day" coding in COBOL on IBM
mainframe environments.  I am *particularly* interested in those who are
employees rather than "contractors" or "consultants" - but any input that I
can get will be gratefully received. (Please pass this on, if there are
others you know that you think might provide me with some "real-world"
input.)

It seems to me that few IBM mainframe COBOL programmers are planning on
attending the upcoming (end of June) "COBOL Expo 2002".  I have some guesses
of my own on why this might be, but I am interested in input from others.  I
am also interested in any "relevant" input on any of these topics:

1) It seems to me that VERY few IBM mainframe COBOL programmers
"participate" in the comp.lang.cobol Usenet newsgroup or the IBM-MAIN
list-server.  Why do you think this is?  I can think of a few possibilities
(NOT mutually exclusive), but I don't know how realistic they are:
  - IBM mainframe COBOL programmers want to put in a "day's work" and then
stop thinking about COBOL
 - IBM mainframe COBOL programmers are "old-timers" who are just "waiting"
for the day they can retire and aren't interested in other forums for
discussing "recent" or "new" techniques and issues
 - IBM mainframe COBOL programmers get enough "inter-action" with other
COBOL programmers at work, and don't think they need other venues
 - IBM mainframe COBOL programmers *really* want to get into some other
field/job and see COBOL as a "dead-end"
 - The estimates of how many programmers there really are still doing COBOL
coding on IBM mainframes is really highly exaggerated.
 - others?

2) From what I hear, the COBOL (and LE) projects at SHARE aren't
particularly large/active (but certainly more alive than the now defunct
GUIDE projects).  What *would* interest IBM mainframe COBOL programmers in
the way of "presentations," discussions, forums, etc?

3) How much of the time of IBM mainframe COBOL programmers are spent with
"mainframe-only/primarily" applications and how much is spent with
applications that (now) normally have PC-ish "front-ends"?

4) What do you think can/could be done to "improve" the image (both
self-image and image from outside) of those doing IBM mainframe COBOL
programming?

  ***

*OBVIOUSLY* some/most of this applies to other mainframe COBOL programmers,
but I am particularly interested in those in IBM environments.

Feel free to respond to me publicly via CLC or IBM-MAIN - or via private
email.  This is *not* an official (much less paid for) survey - but is aimed
at my trying to get in touch with those "on the front line" today.  Anything
posted publicly - will obviously be available to others; things sent to me
privately will NOT be generally distributed - unless explicitly allowed by
the sender.
```

```
wagers anyone? (Hehehehehe).

complete re-install, he might start taking them seriously.  I certainly
couldn't disagree with him.  (Before I get lots of flames, I know there are
some platforms, such as UNIX, that have demonstrated a remarkable stability.

department).
```

```
slave at each turnstile with a wax tablet and a stylus. For each revolution

regions, causing him to jump and thus make a mark on the tablet. The idea
was to stop some of the pocketing of revenue  by the Gatekeepers, which was
suspected to be going on. It was felt that if the number of people who
attended could be calculated, this could be multiplied by the unit price at
each turnstile, and the total revenue collected could be matched against
this figure. If the actual amount collected was less than what should have
been collected, certain Gatekeepers would be lion fodder at the next
performance... Tightfistus, (who had been promoted after the Forum debacle

allowed to unroll under its own weight. Any slave who had not totalled the
marks on his tablet by the time the reel was completely unfurled would be
executed by Hittus (who really enjoyed the work).

unfurling of the silk from off the reel, but these were countered by
efficency experts from the far-off land of Candle, who always detected and
removed the various inhibitors which slowed the spinning of the reel. (The
cost of these had not been considered by Tightfistus, who called in Arturus

History has shown that a reel time system NEVER pays for itself, so the
```

```
It is not the tool but rather the use of the tool that influences
our thinking. Wiliam A. Wulf expressed it differently,
"There is an implicit Whorfian hypothesis that the nature
of language shapes the ways in which we think about
problems. Thus, although we may not be able to measure
it directly, most experts believe that the user of one of the
more modern, structured languages is better equipped
to think about complex problems than the user of one
of the older languages (e.g., Fortran)."[1]

Also,
"The antistandardization argument holds that language
evolution is essential to incorporating new concepts into
the field, and further that the most important impact of
language is not easily observed because it is in the
mental set of its users."[1]

COBOL 74 provided a "vocabulary" and means for
structuring solutions. COBOL 85 extended both
"vocabulary" and structure. The same applies to
COBOL 2002. It is this "vocabulary" and structure
that shapes the ways in which we think about solutions
to problems when using these particular tools.

One who knows only COBOL 74, is less likely to use
"structured programming," "modular programming," or
recursive data structures than one who knows the later
COBOL standards, or who knows other languages.
Thus, one's use of a tool (COBOL 74) influences one's
thinking habits. The absence, from a language, of
constructs, for expressing some concepts, limits our
ability to think about solutions when using the language.

Therefore, in general,
"The tools we use have a profound ... influence on our
thinking habits, and, therefore, on our thinking abilities."

But, it also seems true that evolutionary improvements
in a language have a positive influence on our thinking
habits and on our thinking abilities.
```
