During the few years I have been following this group,
I have seen no one provide justification for the continued
availablility of PERFORM ... THRU in the COBOL language.

The use of PERFORM ... THRU should be made archaic.
It is a poor programming practice that leads to and causes
problems in maintaining source code.

Removing any item from the COBOL standard takes many
years. First, the item must be made archaic or obsolete in
the next revision of the standard. Second, if the item was
made archaic, then it must be made obsolete in some
subsequent revision. Third, the item is removed in the
following revision.

Making the item archaic, initially, is a more conservative
approach to its eventual removal and gives more time
for conversion of those programs which still use
PERFORM ... THRU.

Those who wish to have PERFORM ... THRU removed
from the language may request such removal by commenting
on the Final Committee Draft (FCD) for the draft COBOL
standard. Information on where to direct such comments
was provided to this group recently in the thread "Press
Release on COBOL Public Review".
------------------
Rick Smith

In reverse order,

Yes, EXIT PARAGRAPH is in the draft of the next Standard (so should be
available in *all* standard conforming compilers - when/if such become
available.

Now for making something "archaic".

There are 3 "levels" of support for features that are *in* the (draft)
Standard:

1) "Fully supported now and forever" (no other classification)

2) "Obsolete" - fully supported in this "version" of the Standard - but will
come out when/if there is one AFTER this one (i.e. 201x).

3) "Archaic" - fully supported in this version - and "guaranteed" to also be
supported in the next Standard, but they really are "not nice" things to
do - and there are better ways of doing it.  So when/if programmers STOP
using them, then they will be made obsolete in some future Standard - and
will go away in the Standard *after* that.

   ***

Vendors are required to provide a "flagging" mechanism (that programmers can
turn on OR OFF) - for either OBSOLETE or ARCHAIC features.

Therefore, do *not* worry about THRU going away "soon" if it were made
archaic.  However, it would be possible for you to start a *new* shop
standard to move away from it - both in new development and maintenance -
and at some VERY distant time in the future, it may drop out of the Standard
(but even then vendors are allowed to keep it as an extension - as IBM has
already indicated that they will do with some features that were marked
"OBSOLETE" in the '85 Standard - and which are "dropped" in the draft
revision).

using PERFORM ... THRU with multiple paragraphs

Personally, I use PERFORM...THRU.  But let me explain why, which is
the crux of the issue.  In the vast majority of my COBOL programming,
even without EXIT PARAGRAPH, I could and would be quite happy without
PERFORM...THRU.  But as I have stated here several times, there is
a particular type of program, frequently used by my clients, in which
there are long series (dozens) of field validation tests, the failure
of any of which should cause a paragraph exit.  In addition, there is
no logical point where one would be justified in breaking up the
paragraphs.  Also, my clients still use COBOL 74 because the vendor's
COBOL 85 compiler is much slower, so we do not even have END-IF, etc.
There just is no simpler, clearer or as efficient way to do this as
PERFORM...THRU / GOTO EXIT.  With newer technology, this particular
type of code will probably be abandoned by my clients, and/or EXIT
PARAGRAPH will be available.  In either case, I will happily abandon
PERFORM...THRU.  But even if 99% of all programmers wanted to get rid
of PERFORM...THRU, it would in no way lessen my client's need for it
right now.  In fact, we don't know when, or even if, the new draft
standard will ever be implemented.

The simple fact is that it has been determined over the years that
good structured programming requires an exit code block construct to
enable writing clean, clear code.  This is why (AFAIK) all structured
procedural languages provide one.  Because COBOL was designed before
these principles were realized, PERFORM...THRU/GOTO EXIT is the only
standard COBOL construct to provide this capability.  The number of
people who agree or disagree doesn't change this reality. :-)

The debate missed some points.  1) What is the program doing?  2) What
is the most effective to support?

Support groups often take ownership of code, for many years after, so
their view is probably the most important of all.  I think the support
people deserve the right to tell the developers how to structure a
program - with exceptions for solving particular problems.

Would also point out that section *numbers* were extremely usefull in
early PC COBOL, as we know they were in the early days of mainframe
hardware.   Interesting how they came back for a while.  So it's
important to note they were not removed from the language just because
segmenting fell out of favor.

I have vivid memories of debugging 12,000 line on-line programs.
Section made those programs exactly what the words says - sectioned.
A modular approach with source code at a higher level.  They served as
a kind of wrapper for pages of code that had to, for example, edit and
validate terminal forms input.  They also helped us move sections of
code around.  With a bit of revisionism, I think of it as a form of
containment.  Eventually I saw these 12,000 line PERFORM monsters
replaced with 12,000 line structured GO TO DEPENDING monsters.  They
still sectioned the programs because the need to contain code simply
didn't go away.  Can't say which is better, though the later loaded a
heck of a lot faster.  

To use GO TO DEPENDING with these programs required the invention of
control switches which took over the focus of the application.
Typically something like *STATE* as in a state driven program.  I
think some folks might call it *casing* a program.  You always had to
ask yourself, what is the current state?  What is the current mode?
What is the current keypress?   Not clear where I came from - not sure
where it's going.  Very easy to get lost.  In some ways it was easy to
modify, like adding functionality to an F-Key, but it was always hell
to support. 

Then one day I took over an EDI interface.  The program was written
using typical PERFORM structure.  The incoming flat file records were
highly optional.  Sometimes two number 005 record types, sometimes
none.  The PERFORM structure was just nuts trying to deal with *not*
doing anything - sometimes.  Rewrote it using go to depending.
Beautifully simple.

So we must always ask ourselves, what is the program doing, and choose
the best approach.   Good idea to keep options available and much
prefer a tool box brimming over when rolling up the sleeves.  Removing
program flow control contructs just because someone can demostrate
another way to do it in a given example lacks attention to the diverse
business problems programming must solve.

Perform thru was essential, even non conditional.  The whole idea was
to give the switches an elegant back row seat.  The point is, PERFORM
THRU is the basis for PERFORM THRU UNTIL, so debate (as someone
suggested many threads ago) is moot point.  It really made sense with
those online programs.  Granted, we can do things differently today
but the host of business problems to solve are still before us.
