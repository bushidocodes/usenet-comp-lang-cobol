would be *no* symbol for the word "NOT" in relational conditions, e.g.

  If  A not < B
     could only be "changed" to symbols by "switching" the logic to
  If A >= B

  If Not A < B
    could be changed to
  If ! A < B  (or one of the other "suggested" symbols for NOT)

twelve pages of the most gawdawful code, and right in the middle of it was
the comment "Yes, this is what you think it is!".  Took me three weeks to
figure out how to get there; turned out to be a compile-time optimization
for SUBTRACT X FROM X.  I ripped out the entire optimization and the problem
went away.

I also remember a COBOL cross-reference utility written in COBOL by a
Fortran programmer who had just discovered the joys of the ALTER verb.  I
submitted a patch to the plant and they rejected it on the grounds that
nobody knew enough about the utility to judge the patch; they discontinued
the product about a year later.

The authors of the standard can't be expected to prevent people from
shooting themselves in the foot.  It's up to the programmers to learn how to
use the weapon wisely.

statement.  With a little adjustment (to use COPY ... REPLACING instead of
REPLACE) I can do so with a '74 compiler of my acquaintance as well.  That's
