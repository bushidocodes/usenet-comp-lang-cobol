But that was, at least partially, my point. The program was not really working
before. It may have been just luck of the draw that the existing, underlying
bug never surfaced. For example, suppose you use PIC S9 for a page counter. If
your report never goes over 9 pages, then you consider the program to be
working. If the report grows to 10 pages what happens depends on several
variables but there are these possibilities in the end: 

* the program will abend (the bug is now drawn to your attention) 

* the page number wraps to zero (subpossibilities: a) no one notices; is this
then a bug or not? and b) a user notices but decides to ignore it; is this a
bug or not? and c) a user notices and requests a fix; this is a bug)

* the page number shows up as 10; the program is now not performing according
to the rules of the language; is this a bug?

>Your original post stated that one of the justifications upgrades was
>the ability to use new features.  However, you have to weigh that
>against the cost of destabilizing the programs.
>
>

Point taken. But you have to also consider the benefits of uncovering hidden
bugs that would have cost the customer later.

>The people that you are addressing and this newsgroup and IEEE are all
>techies who recognize the personal benefits of learning the latest and
>greatest software.  We benefit regardless of what happens to the systems
>we convert.  So your proposed article is preaching to the converted.
>

Well, the orientation is to try and make the business case for doing the
conversion. We need to point out factors that non-technical people may not see,
in a way that doesn't require them to be technically competent (just literate,
I hope).



>However, the user department who paid for the upgrade still feel that
>they got no benefit from the project. 

Someone has not been doing their marketing, then. When I worked for IBM over 25
years ago, they taught us "everyone sells". This was an uncomfortable message
for most of us techies, but over time I have come to believe it. It is OUR
responsibilty to ge the users excited about updates and aware of the costs of
doing thing poorly. And we need to do it on their terms. That's the way things
work and how we make life better for ourselves. We cannot just stay in our
cubicles and bemoan the shortsightedness of our users. We must show them the
big picture.
