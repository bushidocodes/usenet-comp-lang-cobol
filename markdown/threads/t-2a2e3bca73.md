What changed was the technology and the expectations of people using it.
(Not just people in the company but also the general population. Nobody
would wait for a Bookkeeper to carve notches in a stick when they can
use a cellphone to get what they want...NOW!) Astute managers saw this
and invested in re-training before new equipment arrived. Good
technicians realized that the whole paradigm had changed (no fault of
COBOL) and acquired new skill sets in addition to COBOL. (I was
advocating that people do this back in the mid-1990s).

they were proven correct. But it was Management decision that allowed
that bet to pay off. Instead of investing in their people and phasing in
New Technology, it was easier and cheaper to do nothing and just let
things go, for some companies. As generations of skilled people moved
on, the people left with the maintenance burden became increasingly
under-staffed, demoralized and demotivated.

Now we have a sudden surge in demand and no people to deal with it.

after years of neglecting their people and failing to build support
systems for just such a demand surge, to now call for volunteers to come
and bail them out.
