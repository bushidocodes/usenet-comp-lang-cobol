Here's a hypothetical manageent dilemma which will be real for some of you
and a, hopefully, interesting intellectual exercise for others...

Anyway, please consider the following situation:

1. I am currentlydeveloping a very large package that will automatically
transform PowerCOBOL screens into Windows Forms, and salvage all the COBOL
parts (scriptlets) so they can run as Windows Forms "code-behind". (They get
converted from PowerCOBOL into NetCOBOL (which is really just OO COBOL) so
that people used to maintining their GUI applications with COBOL can
continue to do so, and/or they can slowly transition to other languages.

2. The "first cut" of this is necessarily a feasibility study and there are
various "proofs of Concept" embedded in it. Release 1 does some amazing
stuff but it isn't definitive; there is more required.

Several things have emerged from this exercise:

1. The process is feasible and with the right tools it can be easy.
2. There will be exceptional cases that cannot be automated and will require
some manual work. However, we can reduce the work required into a consistent
format with tools assisting as far as possible so it isn't too bad. (Mostly
it means changing COBOL code-behinds; standard COBOL maintenance)
3. Just like all migrations, there is a fair amount of effort required to
achieve this successfully.

BOTTOM LINE:

It is possible to migrate your existing PowerCOBOL into the modern Windows
environment, salvaging your years of COBOL investment, and continuing in
COBOL or using a different language for the future, BUT, there is a process
involved and the process requires some effort. (You can't just click a mouse
and get everything (screens and code-behind) done 100% with no further
intervention. This will be possible after Release 2, but it still won't be
for 100% of cases. The variations on what is in the PowerCOBOL (including
PowerCOBOL-specific and third-party-provided controls make it impossible to
predict, but we can still automate much of the work required, even in these
cases.)


Now, imagine you have a fairly large investment in PowerCOBOL development.
(Say, 1000 screens that need conversion...)

Here's the dilemma:

1. There is no guarantee that PowerCOBOL will continue to be supported.
2. The screen controls available in it are tired, and limited, compared to
those available with Visual Studio for Windows Forms.
3. You are moving to networked systems and the long term goal is to move on
from COBOL.

BUT:

1. You have business rules buried in your PowerCOBOL scriptlets.
2. You have a team of people who know PowerCOBOL.
3. The manual re-design and rebuild of all 1000 screens would be
prohibitive.

Now you hear about a toolset that can help you move to a modern Windows
environment, without losing what you have. Your existing team can lever
their PowerCOBOL skills into NetCOBOL easily.

So you make some enquiries and are told the following:

"Release 1 is available and it implements the full process, but there are
some facilities missing. (You can write them manually now, or retrofit them
automatically after you get Release 2). If you buy it now, you will have
extended free support, and you will pay an "early adopter" price. When
Release 2 comes along (expected around April 2016 and it will cost about
40% more than you paid), you will receive a free full upgrade to Release 2,
in recognition of your early adoption."

Would you decide to bite the bullet and get started now on the migration
process?

OR...

Would you decide to wait until Release 2 is available before even beginning?

I'd be very interested to hear your votes on this.

Pete.
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."

› All come down to total cost for the work to migrate !

The cost of doing any migration is considerable. The quicker you do it, the
less it costs. Tools help you do it quicker... In some cases it isn't even
viable WITHOUT tools. We had a Canadian client who had around 850 PR1ME
COBOL programs that needed to be migrated from using indexed files to a
modern Relational Database. They estimated the time to manually go through
these programs and change all the IO, to be over 2 man/years. They did it
with tools in under 3 weeks... (And they got the added bonus of an optimized
RDB with a separated Data Access Layer, rather than embedded SQL in the
applications.)

For PowerCOBOL migration there is a large amount of work in just redefining
all the existing screens to be WinForms (or whatever else you decide to move
to) and then re-building all those screens. New forms are generally built
using the design surface in Visual Studio and this is not a batch process.
If you have hundreds of forms it will take a considerable amount of time.

If you can actually achieve that, then you have to deal with all the event
and procedural scriptlets that drive the PowerCOBOL screen. They have to be
salvaged somehow and wired into the new Form you created. It is a mammoth
task. So much so that most people won't even consider it. Many sites are
just "locked in" to PowerCOBOL after years of using it, and watch the world
moving on past them.

(The sad thing is that PowerCOBOL is actually a very good system. It suffers
from not having the richness of controls that WPF or WinForms provide, and
you cannot currently use the new 64-bit COBOL compilers with it. GT
Software have announced they will be supporting the latest release of it
next month, and it can now be used with the latest compilers, but it is
still 32 bit and there are no new features. At least this will reassure the
customer base that it isn't being dropped...yet. It also gives them a bit
more breathing space to consider moving on.)

I'm trying to offer them some options, that don't involve throwing all their
COBOL away...

PowerCOBOL is totally event driven.

It provides a useful platform for COBOL people to get used to this type of
programming. (and it is fun to do this).

The Windows loop itself is hidden under the covers and PowerCOBOL people
don't need to worry about it. What they see are raised events and they can
write the handling of these events in COBOL. (Actually, it is PowerCOBOL and
is really a dialect of COBOL, but it is so close to Standard COBOL it is
easy to pick it up.) You have a choice between using OO COBOL, or standard
COBOL for your event procedures, but both of them are extended by
PowerCOBOL to provide a higher level API. At the lowest level, the
processing is all in OO COBOL and is transparent to PowerCOBOL users. When
PowerCOBOL users see the actual source generated by PowerCOBOL, they are
usually shocked... it seems extremely complex. But it is all hidden and,
mostly, PowerCOBOL just works.

PowerCOBOL is suffering from a lack of commitment to support it, and by an
aged IDE and GUI templates.

It is really a "dead end" going into the future and there is no provided
path to move off it.

Hence, a package is being provided by PRIMA to give people more options.

I don't know of any sites where PowerCOBOL was used to "GUI-ize" green
screens (although there could be some, of course), so the paradigm clash you
mention was largely avoided.

As far as our package goes, we are converting PowerCOBOL to other event
driven environments (Windows, the Web, etc.) so we don't encounter that
particular paradigm clash either.

Nevertheless, there were some major technical challenges to be overcome
(they are documented, with the solutions, at:
http://primacomputing.co.nz/PRIMAMetro/pwrCOBMigration.aspx , on the
"Solutions" button.)

Thanks for your post, and the very good point you raised.
