I don't know anything about the company you have mentioned. I noticed that
no one has responded, so if you can tolerate it, I can offer some general
guidelines. Some may be very obvious, so pardon me in advance.

First, and most important, get a copy of the contract in writing up front,
before you move. If you have a friend who is in the DP business let them
look it over. Better yet, let a lawyer who is familiar with employment
contracts look it over. Yes, it will cost you about $100 bucks, plus or
minus depending on your lawyer and area. Make sure that the lawyer is
knowledgable about legal nuances that may exist in the state to which you
are headed. Believe me, this is money well spent. (I am not a lawyer...
don't get me started...) In some cases you may find a lawyer who is willing
to talk to you about these issues, in a very general way, over the phone for
free.

Pay particular attention to "non-compete" clauses. Pay attention to
promissary note clauses. You may be subject to these if the company is
connecting your relo package to your employment. If you leave the company
too soon, you may have to "buy your freedom".

Regarding the relo. Relo packages can be worth up to $30,000 - $60,000. If
you are selling a house the relo package usually covers real estate fees on
both ends, mortgage origination, points, temporary housing, travel (airline
tickets, taxis, car rental, food, etc). It can add up... since you are a
recent college grad, I assume that you do not personally have a house to
sell, so the company is getting off cheap on the relo. Don't feel bad about
pushing them a little bit on this point. Like maybe flying you and a friend
out for an all expense paid week to look the area over. Be creative!!!

If you are taking a position as a contractor, make sure you are compensated
for every hour your work. Comp-Time sound great on paper, but it never
works. What about medical / dental / optical insurance, is it paid or
contributory? What about retirement savings? What about waiting periods
before benefits begin? What about vacation?

Last and most important. If you are choosing the life of a consultant, you
need to have money in the bank. Nothing is more liberating than having
several months of living expenses stashed away. This is a top priority if
you are going to be a contractor. This allows you wlak away from a bad
situation. It allows you to negotiate with more confidence when you are
looking for a new contract. And if the project that you are working on gets
cancelled, you don't end up eating cat food until a new one comes along.

Good luck.
