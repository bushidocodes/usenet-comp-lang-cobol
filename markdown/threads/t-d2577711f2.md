Despite a number of people telling me that  Symantec's Norton is a heap of 
crap, I persevered with it for a couple of years and found it very good.

It WAS pretty terrible a few years back but I persuaded myself they had got 
it right and it was now a good product.

Then, a couple of weeks ago, it updated itself and automatically extended 
itself for another year. (It's OK, I had authorised it...). About this time 
I started having trouble with the LAN. Norton support said to reinstall it. 
(Yeah... the usual bullshit from people who are at a loss to fix something.)

So I did.

At least, I tried.

Taking it off through the usual add/remove resulted in it freezing the 
system.

After searching the Net from a different computer I found there is a program 
provided specifically to remove Norton. Downloaded it and ran it on the 
system that froze during add/remove.

It looked like it was going to be successful and then it got to a point 
about a third of the way through the process where it said it was "removing 
service dependencies".

It hung.

I couldn't kill it because it had suppressed the Task Manager.

I powered off and rebooted.

Windows hung immediately after logon.

Booted into Safe mode with the idea to run system restore... Safe mode hung.

So now I have a computer that cannot be booted except from an external 
Optical drive. Sadly, the ODD on this machine is broken...

I go into the BIOS with a view to getting the Boot order changed and find I 
cannot boot from a USB drive...(I could from the Network, but the network 
was not obtainable as the wireless drivers weren't being loaded.

So my computer is now a brick.

Of course I have backups and I even have a partition image that was taken a 
few weeks ago.

I get a local computer store to copy the partition image from the external 
hard drive it is on, onto the HDD of the brick. (Obviously they had to 
remove the HDD and but it into something else so they could access it...) 
Mark the newly copied partition as active, boot up and then we can see the 
destroyed partition as another disk drive.

I got them to replace the ODD while they were at it.

The task of restoring and organizing 2.7 million files from the "bad" 
partition into the new good one begins...
25 hours later I have my system back.

Guess what I did first?

Yep, manually removed every trace of Norton and Symantec from the file 
system and the registry. There were just under 400 objects that I took out.

Then clean the registry and defrag,

Now do a partition copy of the active partition to an external backup.

The system (win xp SP3) boots in half the time it did previously (now around 
2.5 minutes), but it seems a long time because I've become used to Win 8, 
which boots in seconds.

I don't know how a software company can sell a product, that can't even 
uninstall itself cleanly without destroying a system. I take good care to 
ensure that releases of my stuff leave your system exactly as they found it, 
should you decide to remove them. It isn't rocket science, it's a duty of 
care to the people who give you money.

My advice: NEVER install Symantec or Norton.

(PS, I'm not talking behind their backs. I said all of the above (and much 
more) to them. They know what I think. There was never any suggestion from 
them that I should get a pro rata credit for the 250 odd days of 
subscription I have paid for though... :-))

Pete.

Er ... that's more or less the definition of a rootkit. Rootkits are 
malware that hook or replace system APIs and alter their behavior to 
make it difficult to detect and remove the rootkit.

And requiring complete reinstallation is not atypical with APTs and 
other contemporary rootkits. Certainly there are older ones around 
with less-sophisticated defenses that can sometimes be removed by 
remediation software, but newer ones - for Windows that might include 
BDS/ZAccess, Rootkit.Boot.Pihar, MBR:Alureon-L, etc - are very 
difficult to detect, much less remove. Some can infect the BIOS; 
others create hidden boot partitions and mask their changes to the MBR.

> I'd be inclined to boot from a clean disk (I use Windows PE) then do some 
> investigation as to what is actually there.

Even booting from a separate copy of the OS on read-only media (eg a 
boot CD or DVD) isn't always sufficient, particularly for the newest 
variants (which may not be detected by remediation software that 
hasn't caught up yet) or for malware that infects BIOS. It's worth a 
try, though. Windows users who don't have access to real Windows PE 
(Preinstallation Edition) can use one of the free third-party 
equivalents, such as BartPE. (Computer-forensics researchers might be 
more inclined to boot a different OS entirely, such as one of the 
more-secure Linux configurations, but most Windows users probably 
aren't that ambitious, and that makes it much more difficult to use 
Windows-specific remediation software.)
