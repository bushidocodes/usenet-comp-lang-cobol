Here's a suggestion:
Write some COBOL to do something like access a couple of files (maybe a 3
way split) and
print some reports. Write it as modules or stand alone programs, whatever
you feel comfortable with... Lay the code out clearly, indent it, and
comment it. Make it look professional. Next time you have an interview for a
job, take along the listings and say, "Well, here's some COBOL I wrote. As
you can see I have a good grasp of the basics and am keen to learn."

It worked for me a long time ago when I couldn't get a job because they
wanted experienced people, and I couldn't get experience unless they gave me
a job. We've probably all been there.

Since then I have been in the position of having to employ COBOL people from
time to time, some of whom are relatively junior without in-depth
experience. I would take samples of work over glowing references every time
(I back my own judgement as to their character and work habits; references
are too easily obtainable and most people are loth to say anything bad about
you...). The clincher is attitude. Enthusiasm and keenness cuts a lot of
ice. If you really believe VB is a skill in demand, learn that too. It's a
market place; sell what people want to buy.

I know there is still a huge demand for COBOL people; work on all your
skills, including your presentation and interview skills and your attitude
(project confidence without arrogance, and a desire to learn), and show them
something solid like a printout of code you have written.

If they won't even talk to you because you haven't enough experience, then
they're very short sighted and probably don't deserve to have you. Or else,
they're desperate and need experienced people who can bale them out...Wonder
how they got into that state? (Probably by ignoring in-house training and
not retaining people with knowledge of their systems...)

Keep trying. I remember applying for dozens of jobs before I got one. In the
end I was ready to sweep the floor or punch cards just so long as I got
access to a system where I could read the manuals and continue my
self-education...(That was in 1967...it's no different today...)

Good Luck!

Pete.
