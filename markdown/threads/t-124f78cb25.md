the claim above.

Fujitsu (either their .NET COBOL or there NetCOBOL for Windows product line)
is about as far away from 2002 conforming as you can get.  It is true that
their OO support is "medium close" (if you ignore local-storage issues,
exception handling, and everything outside the "basic" OO syntax), but as
far as BASIC portions of the 2002 Standard goes outside of OO, they are at
least as far behind as most of the other major compiler vendors that I know
of (at least as far as I can figure out) - and possibly FURTHER behind than
some.

For example,

 - Local-Storage and Recursion (for non-OO programs)
 - Table sort
 - Unicode / ISO 10646 national language support
 - Locale support
 - Standard arithmetic
 - Common Exception handling (outside OO)
 - Validate statement
 - user-defined functions
 - SUPPORT for GoBack statement
 - Most of the new 2002 intrinsic functions
 - 2002 Standard "split-key" syntax
 - Stop Run / Exit Program WITH STATUS

etc, Etc, ETC ...

If you look at their .NET product, it doesn't even include full support for
ANSI '85 syntax (with such things as file SORT's and printer output)

I am *not* saying whether Fujitsu's current products do or do not met THEIR
customer needs - I simply find it VERY disturbing when people think that
their current products are particularly "close" to the 2002 Standard
*required* features.

Well that's fair comment, Bill.

It does presuppose that one cares... (I don't...).

It comes down to the fundamental argument of whether a vendor should support
the Standards Committee or the perceived needs of the Market Place.

Ideally, we would have a responsive Standards Authority who could actually
produce something within a reasonable time frame, and would take into
account the requirements of the Market Place by liaising with vendors and
soliciting feedback from users in a simple accessible way (maybe via the
Internet). That didn't happen (despite protestations that it did) so vendors
HAD to meet the market need.

Do you think I'm going to beat up Fujitsu for providing me with OO, three
years before the Standards people got their act together?  (It STILL isn't
resolved...). No way. On the contrary, if they haven't conformed totally to
the proposed standard I'll cut them some slack and say: "Who needs it?". I
can use what they've provided and I couldn't care less whether it conforms
to some "Standard" that has no credibility, is still being debated, and is
largely irrelevant.

There might have been a case 20 years ago when source code was king and we
couldn't transport anything to another platform unless it conformed. That is
no longer true (at least for me it isn't...I write componentized systems and
the components can run anywhere and be written in whatever language is
appropriate for the function they are required to perform...). The COBOL
standard has demonstrated that it is pointless in today's world by us
waitiing 17 years for it and then finding it doesn't give us anything we
really need. The last effective standard was 85. If the vendors had done
this, we wouldn't have a COBOL language today.

I won't be discarding Fujitsu (standard or non-standard) any time soon.

Pete.
