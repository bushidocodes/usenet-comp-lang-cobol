The general consensus among most programmers who are working on Y2k
(including myself) appears to be that there will be some problems,
but probably nothing catastrophic.  I believe that government is the
most likely place to have catastrophic problems, if any.  There is a
bureaucratic mindset that simply cannot understand that meetings and
planning do *nothing* toward fixing the problem.  Some agencies have
had meeting after meeting, committee after committee, study group
after study group, wringing their hands and accomplishing nothing but
wasting the time we have left.  It will be the troops in the trenches
fixing the code which will make it work, if it works.  Unfortunately,
many bureaucrats have no clue about this.  I know one local bureaucrat
who stated that "Y2K is a hoax."  Can you believe that?!?  If these
people don't get it straight Real Soon, they will be paying the piper
come early 2000.

Bankers hate spending money, but they hate going out of business or
facing a court trial for negligence even more.  I'm expecting they
will have their house in order.  I simply don't believe the power
system is as vulnerable to shutdown from Y2K problems as some think.
According to a number of posts by people who should know, the power
grid has virtually no concept of date or time.  Some people worry
about getting fuel to the power plants, but I would think the people
who do the transport are not fools, and that most of the schedules are
regular stuff.  Just keep doing what you did last week and the week
before that, etc.  If Europe could continue to function under the
unimaginable damage and disruption of WWII, when the infrastructures
themselves were destroyed, this should not even be a bump in the road
by comparison.  And consider that millions of very intelligent, clever
and creative people will be thinking of every conceivable way to 'get
by' any disruption.

We don't have to read any farther than that to see we have an extremist
here.  The statement "less than 1 percent of the 50 billion integrated
chips used in computer systems around the world are Y2K-compliant" is
very misleading.  Only a very small percentage of those chips *need* to
be Y2K compliant, because the date has absolutely nothing to do with the
devices they control.  The CPU(s) in your printer, for example, could
care less about date.  To say that they "aren't Y2K compliant" verges
on a lie, it presents so distorted an impression.  Ask yourself "How
many devices do I use that I must enter the date when I buy it, or when
I change the batteries?"  Then ask yourself "If those devices failed,
how much problem would it be for me?"  Yes, most VCRs do know the date,
and many of them won't time record properly after 1/1/2000.  But does
it explode when the power fails and it comes up blinking 12:00?  Or if
you neglect to set the date properly is your life ruined?  If your VCR
doesn't accept dates after 1/1/2000, then simply select an earlier year
which has the same weekday configuration (starts on the same day of the
week and is/is not a leap year) tell your VCR it's that year.  You and
the VCR will be just as happy.

A number of technical people who have worked in the power generation
industry for decades have posted that there is nowhere in the power
generation system which is even aware of the date.  The systems are
responsive moment to moment; date is of no consequence.  When you
think about it, this is completely logical.  Administrative computer
systems are date aware, just as in any management function of any
company.  But those systems don't produce the power, they produce
reports for management.  The power system won't fail if the CEO does
not get his P/L sheet on time!

How many of you have ever experienced your automobile refusing to run
because you failed to enter the correct date?  How many of you have
ever *heard* of somebody ever entering a date into an automobile,
other than the dashboard clock?

Yes, there is no doubt that when 1/1/2000 rolls around, every farmer
in the world will become paralyzed, their tractors won't run, the
produce will cease to grow, and all the meat animals will immediately
cease eating and die.  The truck drivers will forget to come to work
to deliver the groceries to your shelves.  All the people in the food
production and distribution industry will completely forget to come
to work and follow the schedule they followed the week before, and
the week before that, etc.  Yes, we are all going to starve to death
from Y2K.  Sheesh!  God save us from simpletons!

Do you *really* think that nuclear weapons are designed such that a
date error will launch them?  If the people who handle nuclear weapons
were that stupid, we would have been blown to bits long before now.
Not to say that they are all Y2K compliant, just that it is not that
easy to set off a nuclear weapon.  Several years ago there was an
accident in a U.S. ICBM silo which caused the rocket engine to explode.
It blew the top off the silo and the warhead was thrown hundreds of
yards away.  However, the warhead did not go off.  Not a nice thing,
but remember that these weapons aren't stored in residential areas,
they are miles from anything else.  In fact, there have been many
occasions when planes carrying nuclear weapons have crashed, and none
of them have ever gone off.  It is difficult to create a nuclear
explosion, even when you want one, and those devices have many fail-
safe features.  Consider that there have been something like 60,000+
nuclear devices around for decades, handled by thousands of people,
and not one of them has ever gone off by accident.  Don't you think
that, in all that time, there have been all kinds of failures of the
electronics in those things, far worse than a date failure?  It is
not an accidental launch or detonation of weapons which is the problem
with Y2K, but the fact that some of the guidance systems depend on
precise time measurements.  So, if we just happen to get into a
wholesale nuclear exchange right after 1/1/2000, the missiles may not
all strike their target.  Considering that the targets are often high
population areas, probably *less* people would be killed, and *less*
damage would be done.  Not that there would be that many of us left
around to keep track of it.  So let's keep our perspective on the Y2K
issue.  We may see a nuclear Armageddon, but it won't be from Y2K.  A
nuclear terrorist attack is almost infinitely more likely, almost a
probability.  If you want to worry about something, worry about that.

Banking Collapse versus Economic Collapse:

The problem with massive withdrawals isn't, so much, the
crash of the banking system as with the economic collapse
that would occur due to banking reserve requirements. I think
the current reserve requirement is around 4 percent; it may be
less. This means that banks may loan up to 96 percent of their
deposits.

Since these loans are redeposited into the same or different
banks, the bank receiving the deposit may loan up to 96 percent
of that deposit and so on. Looking at it another way, this means
that for each dollar removed from the banking system, the banks
must reduce their outstanding loans by 24 dollars. A massive
withdrawal of cash from the banking system will cause a
corresponding massive decrease in economic activity since
banks would not normally be able to write new loans until their
reserves come into compliance with the reserve requirement.
Alternatively, loans would become much more expensive.

It is this type of contraction in loan volume that led to a massive
decrease in money supply in the US and subsequently to the
world-wide economic collapse in the 1930's; even though
there was no reserve requirement at that time.

Having said that, both the US Federal Reserve and the US
Treasury Department have taken steps to prepare for the
worst. The Treasury has printed billions of dollars of cash to
replace any massive withdrawals from banks and the Federal
Reserve is prepared to reduce the reserve requirement and
interest rates to whatever level is required to prevent
economic collapse.

A direct result of these actions is that the banking system
will survive. It would require a prolonged economic downturn,
IMO lasting perhaps 5 to 10 years, before the banking system
would have serious problems. I just don't see that happening.
