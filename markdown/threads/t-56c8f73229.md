Unless you have some compelling reason to use
COMP-2 fields, don't. You will encounter loss of
precision, stuff won't add correctly, division
kills your computation, etc. Your results will be
accurate with floating point, but not precise. If
you are unsure of the difference between accuracy
and precision, here's an example:

The moon is 240,000 miles from earth (precise to 2
significant digits).
The moon is 243,127.544 miles (precise to 9
significant digits).

Commonly. COBOL program deal with money. People
count money to pennies. They get really, really
upset when you round off their paychecks, even
thought the paycheck is 'accurate.'

There is a WORLD of difference between ANSI/ISO conforming COBOL source code
(portable between most compilers) and "perfectly valid vendor extensions" to
COBOL.

Things like the optionality of the period after the program name in the
Program-ID paragraph is a "medium common" extension - but certainly not
universal.

For what is "portable" and what is an "extension"

A) Check out your vendor's documentation.  It should indicate what is an
extension

B) run your compiler with an ANSI or FIPS flagging mechanism.  (how to do
this is also different from vendor to vendor - but should also be in their
documentation)

  ***

Currently *all* floating-point support is an extension - and works
differently from compiler to compiler.  (The next ANSI/ISO COBOL
Standard -expected to be approved at the end of this year - DOES provide
support for both "internal" and "display" floating-point data-types.)

Currently *all* methods of specifying "linkage" between COBOL and non-COBOL
(e.g. C) programs is also an extension and QUITE different from vendor to
vendor.   If you check out both of your vendors' documentation, they
probably have "entire sections" on how to CALL and be CALLed by C programs.

One common way to "get around" this difference is to be "vendor extension"
code in COPY members, so you can COPY in the specific "variation" that you
want for each compiler.

P.S. "MYFUNC-RETURN-CODE" should be valid for any/all compilers.  Exactly
WHAT message did you get when you used it - and how did you define that data
item?  My guess is that there was something ELSE wrong - besides the length
of the field name.
