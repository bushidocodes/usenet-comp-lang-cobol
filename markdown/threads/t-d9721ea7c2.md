So... I was reading someplace else, got directed to 
<http://bucks.blogs.nytimes.com/2010/09/14/chases-online-banking-outage/?ref=business> 
and read:

--begin quoted text:

Potentially millions of Chase retail and small-business customers were 
frozen out of their online bank accounts for more than 24 hours because of 
a technical glitch, officials of the bank said.

[snip]

Chase, the retail banking arm of JPMorgan Chase, is the nations 
second-largest bank. More than 16.5 million of its customers have online 
accounts.

--end quoted text

... and then a guy posted about his experience dealing with Chase 
(permission to repost without attribution was given):

--begin quoted text:

I don't think we were impacted by their outage, but we've run up against 
their lousy app development practices time and again.

Way back when we were first rolling out W2k, we couldn't upgrade our 
treasurer's PC for nearly a year because the banking app we had to use 
wasn't compatible with W2k.

Recently, we've discovered their online apps frequently require the use of 
outdated (i.e., insecure) JVMs.  Upgrading to newer JVMs and removing the 
older JVMs requires some quasi-supported registry hacking.

And we have a check-deposit system that allows us to do the check scanning 
at our site.  But the web-based check-scanning app isn't yet compatible 
with Win7 at all, and they're only working on a version that works in 
32-bit Win7, not 64-bit.

--end quoted text

... and it got me to musing.  Recently there have been discussions about 
the Oldene Dayse, code that's old enough to vote and was last touched to 
take out the EXHIBIT NAMED statements during Y2K recompiles, sequestered 
programmers whose only glimpses of women were tke Keypunch Girls they were 
permitted to visit every other week with stacks of Hollerith cards...

... and then there's the above.  W2k (Windows 2000) came out a decade or 
so back and code that breaks because of an Operating Systems change is (as 
I was taught) the realm of Systems Jockies, not Applications Coders... and 
yet here's a fellow mentioning difficulties with folks relying on outdated 
Java Virtual Machines (outdated Java?) and check-scanning applications 
that have trouble not with the Operating System (Win7) but with the 
architecture (32- vs 64-bit) it runs on.

I am not advocating a return to the Oldene Dayse... but these are truly 
different times.  Twenty-five years ago or so a new-Standard compiler was 
introduced... but outside of that the 9-track tapes that the Payroll 
Office had been keeping (in accordance with The Law) since the early 1960s 
could (if the magnetic-particle-containing top coat wasn't peeling off the 
substrate) still be read and re-saved on more 'modern' media.

Twenty-five years ago or so IBM introduced the 96-tracks-per-inch 
5.25-inch floppy disk for the PC/AT and I remember marvelling at how over 
a meg o' stuff' could be stored on such a thing.  Were the Payroll Office 
to need the datasets... errrrr, files that were stored on such things now 
there'd be a hurdle or two to overcome.

DD

Chase may not be a typical example. They have always struggled with IT and I 
endorse what the poster says.

Part of their problem is that they have always outsourced their IT to 
consultancies and not developed significant development expertise in house.

You can see their point. If you have virtually unlimited money it is 
tempting to simply buy a solution. The argument would be:"We are bankers, 
not computer people; as long as we buy the best available we should be fine 
and if there are problems we have the leverage to get them sorted."

It is a convincing argument.

I worked for them in London (when they were still "Chase Manhattan") in the 
mid 1970s.

They decided that they wanted a state-of-the-art Forex (Foreign Exchange) 
system so they retained Arthur Andersen to design it and IBM to provide the 
latest hardware for it.

I think I have described the ensuing fiasco here previously so I won't 
recap, but the main points were:

1. The Arthur Andersen consultant was incompetent.
2. IBM had not yet produced the hardware (3790) and it existed as a few 
prototype machines back in the States.
3. Nobody knew the 3790 Assembler.
4. Testing and debugging for it had to be done on a mainframe using an 
emulator.

Obviously, as it was a high profile project with a bottomlless pit of money 
attached, the pimps were on it like flies round a cowpat.

They wanted people who could learn Assembler overnight and "hit the ground 
running". Apparently I qualified and found myself pulled off another project 
and sent to join the prestigious "Front End Team". I was still a relatively 
new contractor and hadn't yet learned the ropes of contracting. (I was still 
trying to do my very best to deliver what was wanted...)

An Irishman and I very quickly realised that the system as designed could 
never work and the hardware could never handle it. He was more experienced 
at contracting than I was, and was using his contracting role to feed an 
expensive Raj Nish habit that required him to live with his partner and 
their baby in a London squat while he earnd about 5 times the national 
average, so they could go to Poona once a year and hand it all over to their 
guru.. I suggested we should raise an alarm and he said "No, say nothing. 
Just do the work and take the money for as long as it lasts...".   I wasn't 
having that, so I did some emulations and took them to the AA guy. He 
dismissed it as nonsense and told me to get on with it. I went to the IBM 
rep. and she at least had a look and agreed to do some more testing back at 
IBM.

(The transaction volumes for the system had never been estimated or 
investigated (Hooray for AA!) and the system simply didn't have enough 
registers or memory to support the task switching that would be required.)

IBM came back and said their tests had showed that the system as ordered 
couldn't handle the workload, so they upgraded it (at a special price.) We 
still never saw any hardware.

AA guy said I was a troublemaker (which was probably true) and I was fired. 
My pimp was very angry with me and told me I would never work in London 
again. (He was wrong.) I learned some valuable lessons.

A few months later I was having a beer with the Irishman in a pub and asked 
him how the implementation had gone. He said they didn't implement the 3790 
solution but went with DEC instead. He was still there and still raking it 
in...

When you look at this in terms of the original Management decision to buy 
the best, you can understand there must have been some wailing and gnashing 
of teeth at Chase. When the "best" isn't good enough, what do you do?

Nevertheless, they eventually got their system and they probably counted it 
as a success.

Although IT people may see inadequacy and inefficiency, the rest of the 
organisation only sees results.

"It's an IT problem. Man those guys are useless... takes them forever to get 
anything done and we never get what we need..." I'm sure you've heard the 
mantra...

Another very well known American Bank I worked for in London had a slightly 
different policy. They trained their own people into IT and made sure that 
if systems were outsourced they had an IT savvy person, hands on, who 
understood the Bank's requirements and could resolve design issues 
immediately. They were a pleasure to work with and I ended up doing several 
projects for them. It got to the point where we would discuss requirements, 
they would give me a budget and I'd recruit the necessary people and deliver 
it. I reported regularly  (informally on a daily basis, and formally once a 
week) to their liaison guy and if we had problems they would immediately do 
what was needed to resolve them.

Corporate culture has very much to do with success, in IT and everything 
else.

Pete
