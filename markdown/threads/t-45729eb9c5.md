This question is for everyone who would care to respond. The question is
this: Would you attend a Cobol conference? Also, take a stab at how many
other Cobol programmers from your company might attend.

I believe, in many ways, Cobol is shooting itself in the foot. We have
one of the largest groups of programmers in the world, four times the
installed code base of every other language combined, yet we don't have
our own conference.

Merant has a conference, but it's not a Cobol conference, it's a Merant
conference. The same holds true for Acucorp and others.

Jave One, presented by Sun, has every major Java language supplier in
the world. As a result it draws tremendous crowds, good press coverage,
and converts. It is the philosophy behind car-marts, where you can buy a
Buick next to a Toyota, next to a Chrysler, next to a Volvo. Shopping
malls work the same. Sears doesn't say "count me out if J.C. Penny is
there."

A group of us have been discussing the possibility of putting on a Cobol
conference for a number of years. I envision the conference to be from
2-4 days with the world's leading experts providing talks, demos, birds
of a feather sessions, roundtables, etc. The conference would focus on
the Cobol programmer, rather than on the tools of one vendor. It would
also be Webcast so that those unable to physically attend the conference
would not miss out.

I am very interested in your thoughts. If you would like to discuss this
further you can reach me at earranga@objectz.com.

Edmund C. Arranga
Editor - The COBOL Report
earranga@objectz.com

Dave -

Before addressing the conference issue let me ask you if you would like
to file an article about the SHARE conference for the COBOL Report? If
so you can attend the SHARE conference for free, plus we can work out
some amount to pay you for the article (not much, but something). You
can contact me at earranga@objectz.com.

The same offer goes for anyone else planning to attend a conference that
would be of interest to the COBOL Report. Every conference I am aware of
allows the press to attend free of charge. Drop me an email and we can
discuss it further.

On to the matter of SHARE vs. a COBOL conference. An analogy. In its
infancy computer science was a series of classes generally found in the
school of electrical engineering. As a result if you wanted to become a
tenured EE professor, but your interests lay in computers, you studied
and published topics such as "The Propagation of Electricity in
Metal-Dioxide Memory Banks." CS became a discipline unto itself in the
mid-'60s when Carnegie Mellon University made CS a fully funded
recognized department whereby students could graduate with a degree in
computer science. To become a tenured CS professor, even though your
interests lay in the filed of software languages, you studied and
published works such as "The Church/Turing Theorem Proof Re-Analyzed."
Computer science is really computing science - the study of what can be
computed. Again Carnegie-Mellon led the way and in the early '70s made
software engineering a discipline in its own right. Out from under the
yoke of EE computer science flourishes. Out from under the yoke of CS
software engineering flourishes.

My point is this. At the SHARE conference Cobol is "computer science"
stuck in the bowels of the electrical engineering department circa 1960.
A conference with Cobol at the core creates its own weight, gravity and
light. We (and by we I mean the Cobol professional) deserve and need our
own conference. Imagine a Cobol conference with 5,000 attendees; with
10,000; or 15,000 (by the way the Java One conference draws 16,000).
Imagine the amount of knowledge generated, of new articles, books,
ideas. Why has Gamma's books "Design Patterns" never been translated
into Cobol? We are a fragmented community. There are probably more
design patterns waiting to be exploited in Cobol than in any other
language. But because we don't present a unified force we are treated
like the redheaded stepchild. Gamma's book is but one small example.
There are thousands.

Best regards,
Ed

Steve -

The conference would not necessarily be just for the Cobol programmer,
it would be for the entire Cobol community and that includes managers,
analysts, designers, consultants, professors, students, trainers,
authors, research software engineers, and anyone else who has a
interest, vested or otherwise, in Cobol.

I received an email about this topic from a gentleman who mentioned
context. He stated: "�people could get an idea of other technologies in
context." I think he hit the nail on the head. Context is everything.
Most software vendors are tribal villagers and they want you to join.
Read the marketing spiel on any software vendor's Web page and then tell
me what it means. It means absolutely nothing. There is no context.
Nothing is a product; everything is a "solution." A solution to what?
Inprise is a perfect example. There is not one place on the Inprise web
site that mentions object Pascal - and that's what Delphi is. Inprise
now sells for about $2 a share - soon to be purchased by a larger
company. Inprise attempted to put a fence around themselves to protect
market share. They succeeded in becoming irrelevant. Does it remind you
of anyone?

Doesn't XML remind you of the Data Division? Isn't it something that
Cobol folks should be exposed to sooner, rather than later? XML and
Cobol go hand-in-hand. Where are the seminars at the Merant conference
that explain IBM's MQ Series or Microsoft's Transaction Server both
fundamentally important to Cobol folks? Where are the middleware vendors
explaining object request brokers? Where are the folks talking about UML
or OPEN? The Cobol community has absolutely no ties to any of the other
software engineering communities. Where is Linux at the Merant
conference? All these technologies require context to make sense. Folks
need to be able to contrast and compare. They need background so that
they can say, "Oh, I see how that works."

So to finally answer you question, folks would gain knowledge and
insight and a clear understanding of not what the future "holds in
store," but how to create the future.

Best regards,
Ed

OS Cobol is IBM's implementation of the Cobol-68 standard. VS Cobol II
(version 3.2, I believe) is IBM's implementation of the Cobol-74
standard. Come 2001 unless you have migrated to Cobol for MVS you will
no longer have IBM support. IBM will be glad to sell you a special
support deal on their older compilers. In the early '90s it was $50,000
a year per site. I'm sure it's more expensive now.

I'm suggesting would be all about training and education. As Bob Wolfe
correctly points out this type of conference would not be dictated from,
"One vendor's point of view," but from the point of view of the
programmer. Ask the programmers you work with which version of Cobol
they use. The answers will probably be in the IBM vernacular, that is,
folks will say they use VS Cobol II without realizing that is a subset
of the Cobol-74 standard. IBM likes it that way. The more you talk in
terms of their products the more "stuck" you are in buying solutions
from them.

At a Cobol conference where the vendor did not control the show there
would be multiple tracks, one covering how to migrate to Cobol for MVS
(the IBM implementation of Cobol-2001). The track would demonstrate how
to migrate to half a dozen other solutions (non-IBM), each one competing
against the other for your business. There's probably a company out
there who would support older IBM Cobol compilers at a fraction of the
cost IBM charges. And a company that would migrate your entire code base
in a matter of weeks. A great position to be in for you. Check each

drain.

straightforward answer? Wouldn't you rather have an expert who was not
beholden to IBM for a paycheck answering your questions.

Cobol standard, Cobol-2001, has more cool stuff than all the previous
Cobol standards combined. In fact, read the opening paragraph of the new
standard. It talks about how Cobol is no longer simply a business
language, it is now a general purpose programming language capable of
effectivly handling all kinds of new problems. There are pointers and
Boolean data types and a new data validation routine that is so powerful
it will knock your socks off, plus a host of other best-in-breed
language features.

by a vendor.
