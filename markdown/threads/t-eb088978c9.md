```
I posed this same question to IBM just a week or two ago. The problem is
with your index. Your index cannot access two different tables, even
though they have the same definition. From IBM, "An index is not an
actual data item and the name is not a data name, so it cannot be
qualified to make it unique, it must actually be unique. It is more like
a special register than anything else."

Their suggestion was to change the index name in one of the tables so
you have unique index names, or use subscripting.
```

```
The CORRESPONDING phrase (CORR) allows ADD, SUBTRACT, and MOVE
```

```
I group move is an alphanumeric move (mostly).  However, it won't "mess up"
COMP-3 (or other non-DISPLAY) fields *if* they are defined the same (same
offset from the beginning and same PICTURE/USAGE within the group).  This
(ignoring the indexing/subscripting issue) would be a good candidate for a
group move. The "normal" time to use

  MOVE CORRESPONDING

is when the elementary items are NOT defined the same in the sending and
receiving fields. (For example to change all COMP-3 and BINARY fields in the
sending group to DISPLAY in the receiving group) - or when there are OTHER
fields in one of the groups that you do NOT want moved (or when the order of
the sending/receiving elementary fields are different).

My personal guess is/was that Skippy was showing a "simple" case to
demonstrate his problem, not that his actual record layouts looked this way.
If they did/do - then a group (including the entire table) would probably be
the easiest way to do it. (Depending on the compiler's optimization, that
might also be the best performing way to do it)
```
