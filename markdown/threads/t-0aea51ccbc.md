So, here's a few rules of thumb I use in order to minimize aggravation
and the loss of time:
1. Only look when in a job, never be out of job unless by your own
volition.
   (when you are, avoid recruiters as a plague.)
2. Never pick up the phone, let them talk to the answering machine first
3. In your greeting indicate clearly that you will be only able to 
   return those out-of-state recruiting calls for which a toll-free
number
   is given.

4. Listen to the message: does the guy sound like a piece of trash out
of
   a trailer part? If you don't like the sound of it (for any reason),
or
   there's no toll-free number (or invitation to call collect) do not
   return the call.

5. Expect the worst, don't waste time on moral disputes. 99% of rec
firms
   will do exactly like the one Randall dealt with this time. Therefore,
   when calling back, make sure that you ask rather soon about what
   specifically they are working on that prompted them to give you a
call.
   If they reply with "what are your top 3 skills" or "how about an
updated
   version of your resume" you're safe to hang up--they're fishing for
   resumes, the recruiter is probably a new hire. If you have you CV
   on-line somewhere, give the the url.

6. If they do share (may be a lie of course) see if that's
   a good fit in your opinion. If so, try to get them to
   connect you with the client on the phone so you can: 
   a) make sure it's not bullshit and there's a client
   b) have a chat with a real consumer so as to remove the
      last possibility of a mismatch
   c) test what relationships this rec firm has with the client,
      if they can talk to a tech party there, or all they know
      is an HR imbecile who doesn't know a shit.

   If they can't do that, that is a very bad sign. I personally
   always excuse myself if a phone interview cannot be arranged
   for.

7. Ask if they have a "standard" contract agreement that they "normally"
   ask "their" consultants to sign. (Ha ha. Yes they do <g>.) ASK FOR
   A SAMPLE COPY before you spend any more time with them. If refused,
   drop the fuckers right there.

8. Take a look at the document. If there's something you'd like
   to change or add some of your own--ask them about it. Some will 
   work with you on that, some won't. If the latter, drop the fuckers.

The idea is not to fight them down or outsmart somehow or change the
world. The idea is to avoid wasting time. Therefore in your discussions
with a rec. firm it is important to maintain a constant discovery,
prodding for crucial information. The earlier you see the red flag (or a
brick wall rather) the less time you'll waste on this dead-end
"opportunity." Of course, one will eventually see it anyway, but you
might first have to go for a couple of interviews <g>, and that's a lot
of time, travel expenses, and extra dry-cleaning.
