Given another thread (clearly marked "OT") going on, I decided that I would post 
this (for what it's worth - or not worth)

First let me say, that I am not (personally) offended by any of the "faith 
based" or other politically correct or incorrect posts that are going on in 
C.L.C. - as long as they are clearly marked "OT".  Although they certainly have 
NOTHING to do with COBOL or programming, I think this forum is a "social 
interface" for many of us. If others want to expound on their "world view," I 
don't see any reason that I shouldn't too.

Having prefaced with that, I just thought I would put in one (medium-sized) note 
many of the things that "make me -  me" - that I am certain will offend some; be 
similar (in part, probably not in whole)  to others; and generally show why such 
posts (with opinions and beliefs) either will or won't impact how some people 
"read" other people's posts (that ARE on topic).  Obviously, anyone who reads 
this and wants to explain how they "differ" may feel free to do so.  Anyone who 
tells me that I am "wrong" is free to believe and/or live their life as they 
want, but this will have little or no impact on me.

"Begin controversial topics"

1) I am openly gay - have been for over 30 years and (luckily) this didn't 
impact my business life (getting or keeping jobs) at any time (that I am aware 
of).  I had an interesting experience when I was hired by the company that 
"invented" Xpediter - as it was (at that time) owned and managed by some "born 
again evangelical Christians" - who were told by their (equally fundamentalist 
boss) to either "live with my life style - when kept out of the office" or quit.

2) I was actively opposed to the US participation in the Viet Nam war and don't 
(personally) see much more justification for the current US participation in 
Iraq (possibly less as Viet Nam had SOME serious international support).  I 
applied for "conscientious object" for Viet Nam (with a supporting letter from 
my Bishop) but when this was turned down, I was (again luckily?) classified as 
1F.  I was, am (and probably always will be) against war, capital punishment, 
and all other premeditated killing.

3) I am currently an Episcopalian (US part of Anglican Communion - for those 
outside the US) and am INCREDIBLY proud of our recent consecration of the first 
OPENLY gay Bishop.  (Having been in Seminary at one time, I won't comment on the 
number of closeted bishops).

4) Although I am Episcopalian, I come from a German (and/or Russian) Jewish 
background.  I have no idea what I would have done in WW II - especially if I 
had known what was happening to Jews (and others - including gays) in Germany, 
Austria, Poland, etc.  My "hope" is that I would have been a conscientious 
objector then too, but who knows (I was born in '50 so this wasn't an issue for 
me).  I do know that had I been "called up" for Viet Nam (and I was eligible for 
the draft), I would have either left the US or accepted jail time rather than 
"fight".  (I believe in "civil disobedience" - but only if one is willing to 
accept the consequences.)

5) I am a NOT a -recreational drug user (MINIMAL wine, nothing else) and 
personally don't understand why the US treats marijuana as illegal but tobacco 
as legal.  (I sort-of lean to making both illegal - if I thought doing so might 
be useful - but I also accept "personal responsibility" as a reason for NEITHER 
being illegal.)  Treating the two differently just makes no sense to me.

6) I fully accept that the history of the US and "church and state" really 
started with trying to ensure that there was no "state mandated religion" and 
NOT that it was really trying to "totally take religion out of the state".  I am 
HAPPY that over the last 230+ years it as moved much more to the total 
separation of the two.  (I am a "card carrying member of the ACLU).  I:
  - think we should take "under God" back out of the pledge
 - think we should take "in God we trust" off our currency
 - should prohibit "God save ..." from courts, congress,  and all other 
governmental bodies
 - should insure that all organizations (such as the Boy Scouts) that require 
religious or sexual orientation beliefs do NOT receive any government assistance 
(or approbation)
 - should make marriage (to the extent the law deals with it at all) available 
to any 2 (or more?) people wishing to enter such a "contract" - with not LEGAL 
distinction between the results.
 - should make discrimination (in all "public" activities) on the basis of 
sexual orientation punishable to the same extent discrimination for other 
reasons is punishable (and illegal to the same extent)
 - should pass the (old) ERA amendment and in general insure that women are 
treated equally to men in employment as well as other "public" activities, etc

7)  I am not a "Libertarian" although I agree with SOME of their beliefs (i.e, 
LESS government telling others what is right for them - is better) .  Rather, I 
am almost the "opposite" in that I once described myself as a "Christian 
Communist" - in that I do believe that it is the governments (and society in 
general) responsibility to insure the public "welfare".  (From each according to 
his/her ability, to each according to her/his needs).  But think that the Soviet 
*anti-* religious indoctrination was as bad as the "Christian right's" thought 
that "evolution should be taught as an 'option' to creation" (see recent Texas 
school book decision.

8) I am on long term disability as a result of having AIDS (diagnosed in 1984 - 
possibly contracted earlier) and its side effects. I am *thrilled* that other 
than my eyesight, my health has been great since the current regime of medicines 
became available.

   ***
So, I am certain that at least SOME of what is listed above differs 
(significantly) with the beliefs of most (all?) of those who participate in this 
forum.  (Where each participant differs will depend upon their OWN life 
decisions and style.)  I am more than willing to discuss WHY I believe and live 
as I do (preferably in a more appropriate forum).  However, I did think that as 
long as others are posting their beliefs, that I might as well make some (many) 
of mine public too.

This top-posted response to a thread last month is a posting which sets
forth where I am at the moment in regard to the issues discussed.  It is
interesting and perhaps appropriate that this type of discussion appears
on a computer programming newsgroup because it has as members people
whose approach to problem solving is at least somewhat akin to my own.
I do not expect to change anyone's point of view and am posting this as
much to better understand, clarify and possibly change my own views.
The discussions in this and the related thread on science and religion
have been of real interest to me and useful in considering my own beliefs.

I am a member of a church in the Canadian Baptist Convention having
transferred my membership from a church in the American Baptist Churches
of the USA when I moved north.  The American Baptists cover a broader
spectrum of belief than the Southern Baptists which Daniel belongs to
but are far less numerous.  I have come to believe that the Bible is the
inspired word of God but is not infallible.  The forwards to most bibles
about the accuracy of translation makes it impossible for me to believe
in infallibility not to mention the different understandings of a given
text.  The text of the book of Job leads me to believe that it was
intended as fiction in the same sense that Pilgrim's Progress is
fiction.  The ignoring of various strictures by most churches also is
something that makes it hard to be dogmatic.  I also am aware that many
old testament strictures were overturned by Jesus, one of the more
striking examples being the replacement of an eye for an eye attitude
with the command to love your enemies (one which I don't claim to do a
good job of following).  I have trouble completely believing that my
salvation is based solely on belief when I remember the passage in
Matthew about our entry to heaven being based on how we treated the
least of these, our brethren (feeding them when hungry, visiting them
when sick and when in prison, clothing them when naked).  That passage
gives me the uncomfortable feeling that Sufi Muslims who practice their
beliefs may well be ahead of me in line, assuming I make it in that line.

We have "judge not lest ye be judged" on the one hand and Paul on the
other as one of the major church organizers having a whole set of rules.
I am well aware of his writing that our natural selves are sinful and
when he list sins, he does not elevate one above the rest.  In the
passage in First Timothy chapter 1, verses 9 and 10 where homosexual
practice is mentioned, it is equated to "anything else contrary to sound
doctrine".  In another of the places, it is in the same list as gossip,
something which has probably caused more serious grief to the community.

Having read at least two of Episcopal Archbishop Spong's books, I find
him too cute by half and wonder how he escaped expulsion for heresy.  It
would be interesting to see how well his views would have been accepted
in an American Baptist Church (note that the decision would be at the
church level since churches are separate entities belonging to the
denomination  with no formal creed rather than being a division of the
denomination).   Thus as a member of disorganized religion (three
Baptists get together and have 4 different theologies) I am free to hold
my own beliefs as we in the individual churches may covenant regarding
certain things but are free to interpret the Bible as each of us
prayerfully understands it.

I believe that most of the major religions have much to answer for in
terms of atrocities committed in the name of God.  I also believe that
religious believers hold no monopoly in that regard as the Nazis and
Communists proved.  The Bahai faith hasn't gained majority status or
control of the government anywhere to my knowledge so it hasn't been
tempted.  My knowledge of the acts committed by people outside of the
Jewish, Christian and Islamic faiths is so limited as to be sheer
guesswork about the actions.

Given that same sex marriage is coming to a decision point in Canada
with legislation being proposed in the House of Commons and that the
issue is coming up for a vote in my local church, I am being forced to
consider the issue more closely.  I find myself in serious disagreement
with my wife and several members of her family on the one hand because
they belief in same sex marriage and with those in my church proposing a
change to the church rules on the other hand because I don't believe
that the bible is infallible and further that it defines marriage as
between one man and one woman.  In the following paragraphs I will
explore first the civil issues and then where I am in the struggle
regarding what I believe my local church's stand should be.

In the civil area, you could make a good case for retaining the current
definition if you were to claim that the major concern for the
government is regularizing the reproduction of the species and
protection of the children.  In this regard, the major effort should
have been restricting adoption to only married couples.  In Canada,
single persons and same sex couples are allowed to adopt children and I
don't recall any major campaign on the issue to overturn this.  Given
this I believe that failure to have marriage available to any group of
people allowed to adopt children as a group is straining at the gnat and
swallowing the camel as well as discriminating against the children
involved.  However, Prime Minister Paul Martin is being intellectually
dishonest when he claims that the court believes that it is a Charter
Right (Canadian Charter of Rights analogous to US Bill of Rights).  The
Canadian Supreme Court has ruled that the government COULD change the
law to allow same sex marriage but in the request for opinion
deliberately avoided saying it SHOULD or MUST do so.  The government has
been unwillingly to vigorously and fully defend the status quo in the
lower courts and has been unwilling to allow intervenors who have an
interest in doing so.  Thus in six provinces including the province of
my current residence same-sex marriage is legal.  In typing this, I have
come to the belief that there should be phased changes.  The first step
in both the US and Canada would be to change the word marriage to civil
union in all statutes where the word occurs and to extend the definition
of civil union to mean both same-sex unions and any multiple partner
relationship currently recognized in other countries.  The change of the
wording is deliberate and is recognize that marriage is a religious
concept that has no necessary relation to the civil concept.  The
separation of civil and religious wording allows religions to define who
are valid partners from there perspective without government
interference.  In the United States there are already cases wending
their way through the courts claiming that restricting the number of
partners to two is discrimination.  Failure to have provision for
multiple partners is to deny protection to women in Islamic and other
cultures.  Presumably all of the partners in the relationships that I
have mentioned covenant to restrict sexual intimacy to the members
within the relationship and failure to do so would be grounds for
exclusion from the relationship and any benefits thereof.  The next step
is to determine which other groups should be recognized for civil unions
(if any) where physical intimacy may or may not be part of the
definition.  For example, had I remained single should I have been
allowed to have my sister or brother as a survivor on my pension 
benefits?  Another reason for changing marriage to civil union is that 
adultery is no longer grounds for divorce in many countries (I THINK 
including Canada) and I doubt the laws prohibiting adultery exist and if 
they are, I doubt they are enforced anywhere in North America.

 From a Christian point of view and the point of view of many of the
other major religions, same sex intimacy is forbidden.  The high priest
in the Sikh religion in India has brought up the issue and urged all
Sikh members of the Canadian Parliament to vote against same sex
marriage.  In many of the world's religions, the only purpose of
physical intimacy is for child bearing and there are passages in Paul's
letters to that effect.  From what I have read in the newspapers, there
are Roman Catholic churches in Nova Scotia that will not allow a couple
to marry if they can not or plan not to have children.  Given my wife's
and my ages at time of marriage, I am grateful that the American Baptist
Church where I was married did not hold that interpretation.   However,
if marriage is allowed for the purpose of sharing intimacy without the
purpose of having children, then other questions arise.  For those who
define marriage as between one man and one woman there are the
inconvenient examples of Jacob and his two wives (plus concubines),
David and his wives, Solomon and his wives, etc.  It is true that Christ
described marriage as a man and a woman leaving their parents and coming
together as one but you have the interesting injunction in Acts against
men with more than one wife becoming officers of the church.  The
existence of the double standard where the man could "play around" 
before marriage and having a mistress was a "tut tut" issue while the 
woman was expected to be virginal before marriage and faithful after 
seemed hypocritical to say the least.  The issue from a religious point 
of view is leaving me in a state of wanting to explore the issue more 
seriously than I would have 2 years ago.  I also am well aware that most 
churches have moved to condemning acts that Paul approved (owning of 
slaves) and accepting divorce with eligibility for remarriage when the 
grounds are other than adultery or the partner not being in the faith 
and wanting out.  I support both changes although we may have made the 
termination of the marriage relationship too easy.

In a not too related note, for those of you who feel that massive 
problem of AIDS in Africa and related problems are getting further lost 
due to the tsunami, I recommend looking into the Stephen Lewis 
Foundation if you are Canadian and ICROSS international if you are not. 
  Stephen Lewis is the UN special envoy on AIDS.  ICROSS is an 
international organization which has Canadian and US chapters among 
others.  I became interested after watching the CTV (Canadian Television 
network) news show W-five which did a special on the organization.  It 
has attracted the support of Stephen Lewis and retired Major General 
MacKenzie (spelling may be wrong) who had headed various Canadian 
peacekeeping missions.  The url is www.icross-international.net.
