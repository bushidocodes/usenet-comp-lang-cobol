This seems to be a perfect case of what Don was saying about the
morality of big Business.

If a company makes $3.5 billion profit, do they have any more
obligation to their employees than a company that makes $3000 (or,
indeed, .30c profit...)?

The Doc says their obligation is to do their utmost for the
Shareholders.

(As a shareholder myself in various Companies, I have great sympathy
for this point of view, and it would be a brave person indeed who
would argue against it...<G>)

But should this pursuit of profit be to the exclusion of all else?

Personally, I don't think so...

Has anyone actually ASKED the shareholders of BOA whether they are
comfortable with this action, or is it simply assumed that
maximisation of profit MUST be done?

Some years ago I attended an AGM of the German Company BAYER (they of
aspirin fame...although many people would not realise that Bayer are
into dyes, fertilisers, plastics, all kinds of stuff as well as
pharmaceuticals, and all around the World..), held in Koeln (Cologne),
Germany.

I was amazed to see on the agenda the question of wages for African
workers. It had been raised, as these workers were receiving less than
the minimum guaranteed in Germany. (In fact the workers were well paid
by local standards, and had good conditions, staff canteens etc.) The
Board responded to this question by making the point that the money
being paid was adequate for the local conditions. The response from
the floor was that the company had made significant billions of DM
profit, and therefore had an obligation to its workers. There was some
heated debate and finally it was put to a vote. It was carried, and
the workers were granted the minimum wage payable in Germany (a
princely sum by the local standards.)

I was surprised by this because you wouldn't think that shareholders
would vote against their own interests. Nevertheless, on that
occasion, they did.

(I don't know whether it was actually implemented, but I have no
reason to believe it wasn't.)

If a Company pays somebody $2000 a week to maintain their IT and
another Company (or Country)comes along and says "We'll do it for $30
a week..." it would be foolish to be surprised when a deal is
struck... The immediate solution is a no-brainer. But the implications
of this can be much more far-reaching.

It would be unreasonable (and unfair) to place a jobs embargo on India
(or any other Country or Company which is simply trying to make an
honest living) but it should also be unacceptable to destroy the local
community. This is where, I believe, the size of the profit DOES have
a bearing. To demand unlimited profits, irrespective of the Human
Cost, is just bad economics. What point is there in having all the
money if you cannot enjoy it, because your community has been
destroyed?

I believe that outsourcing is morally and economically inevitable. But
it should be done over an acceptable period of time (say 2 years
minimum and 5 is better), every effort should be made to help staff
who will become redundant, funds should be allocated for retraining
and redeployment. Severance payments should be generous. People who
volunteer for early redundacy should be given an even better package.
All of this can be financed from the large profit.

I would stress that this is not just from motives of generosity; it is
the most effective way to minimise the effect of outsourcing on the
local community.

Shareholders should be apprised that a percentage of profit is being
witheld to ease the pain of redundancy, and to minimise the impact on
the local community.

If we take a long term view, we can see the economies of the 3rd World
being improved, standards of living rising, and eventually they will
price themselves off the market, just as we have. At that point we
should have a level playing field, but by then, it will be irrelevant
as far as "computer programming" is concerned...

Finally, if I were a BOA shareholder I'd be thinking about the
AGM...<G>
Pete.
