### Trailing Zs in PIC

- **From:** Barticus@att.spam.net (Randall Bart)
- **Date:** 1998-12-16T00:00:00
- **Newsgroups:** comp.lang.cobol,comp.sys.unisys
- **Message-ID:** `<36775cb4.12386466@netnews>`

```
I recent have had a problem with Unisys A Series Cobol85.  The problem
turned up in a check printing program, and related to the picture
character *, frequently called the check protect character, and Z.  

The PIC in question was "***,***.ZZ".   There is consensus that this PIC
is invalid according to every ANSI standard and according to the current
ISO draft.  The question is how Unisys interprets it.  A long standing
Unisys rule is that any character which is invalid in a PIC according to
ANSI, may be used as a fixed insertion character.  Thus the (then)
Burroughs Cobol-68 compilers accepted /, while it wasn't added to IBM
compilers until ANSI-74, and a host of miscellaneous insertion characters
such as =, :, [, >, 4, ?, and W can also be used.

But what about ***,***.ZZ?  Until a recent change from Unisys, the COBOL74
and COBOL85 compilers, interpretted those Zs the same as 9s, with the
peculiar exception in COBOL74 that a value of zero caused the ammount
field to be completely blank (on a CHECK).

Now,  Unisys says that it is incorrect to interpret those Zs as digit
positions, they must therefore be insertion characters.  Therefore a check
printing program (which was recompiled on Y2K implementation weekend)
started printing amounts like *****23.ZZ.

This is why I say that the Supreme Court of Unisys Cobol has ruled
incorrectly:  

This behavior is new.  Cobol74 and Cobol85 always took Zs as digit
positions up until and including the 44.1A base release.  This behavior
was introduced in patches late in 1998.  It has not yet reached most
users.  To the best of my knowledge, no Unisys A Series or Burroughs Cobol
compiler ever exhibited this behavior until 1998.

This behavior is non-ANSI.  Any action other than a syntax error is a
Unisys extension.  Changing the behavior to comply with ANSI is an
acceptable change.  Changing bahavior of non-ANSI syntax to comply with
Unisys standards is potentially acceptable.  But to interpret them as
non-digit insertion characters is A Bad Thing.  To take non-ANSI syntax
which has had certain Unisys semantics since the beginning of time, and
suddenly change it to do something else (and not even something useful) is
just making trouble for the users.


I therefore request that all Unisys A Series users express their opinion.
Which should it be:

A)  The Zs should be insertion characters (the new Unisys interpretation)
B)  The Zs should be digit positions (the old Unisys interpretation)
C)  A syntax error (ANSI)
```

```
Randall Bart has asked for user input on the behavior of Unisys A Series
COBOL85 in the context of a data item declared "PICTURE ***,***.ZZ", but he
didn't post Unisys' reasons for rejecting his assertions that were provided
to him in private correspondence.  This is an effort to put forth the
reasons that the architects of the Unisys COBOL85 compiler (as well as the
COBOL74 and COBOL(68) compilers)  disagree with Mr. Bart's assertions and
position.

Unisys agrees that the PICTURE string in question exceeds what is permitted
by ANSI-85 (or for that matter ANSI-74)  rules.   That isn't the point here.

Unisys' current COBOL85 reference materials states, in "Simple Insertion
Character":   "Also, any characters that are not defined PICTURE characters
are used as insertion characters.  These include right parentheses, left
parentheses, and any digits that are not in the strict syntax to indicate
consecutive occurrences of a preceding symbol.  Precedence rules for these
characters and rules for determining the categorty are the same as those for
the simple insertion character 0".

This notation is encoded in such a way as to identify it as an extension to
X3.23-1985 COBOL standards.  This feature is similarly noted in the Unisys A
Series COBOL74 and COBOL(68) reference manuals, and identified as extensions
to the standard.  .

The underlying philosophy behind the implementation of this extension is
this, stated a little more informally:

"If you're parsing a PICTURE string, and encounter a character that doesn't
fit the narrowest rules that apply where you are in that PICTURE string,
treat that character that doesn't make sense in that context as an insertion
character."

It doesn't matter whether the character can or cannot be used elsewhere in
the PICTURE string and be identified as a PICTURE character in that context,
and it doesn't matter what the meaning of that character, if it is
legitimate in another context, is in that context.  If it doesn't fit in
context, it's *supposed* to be treated as an insertion character, as an
extension to ANSI rules, instead of receiving a syntax error.

This rule dates from the introduction of COBOL (68) on the Burroughs Large
System, and was carried intact into COBOL74 because that compiler was based
on the same symbol file as COBOL(68), and is maintained there for
compatibility between those two languages.  And it is maintained as an
extension in Unisys A Series COBOL85 for compatibility with both of that
compiler's predecessors.

Whether the documentation completely, correctly and clearly reflects this
implementation design feature is a separate issue (I personally suspect it
could perhaps be clarified some).

This rule was *originally* intended (circa 1970!) to provide a solution in
(then) Burroughs Large Systems COBOL to such problems as phone numbers and
social security numbers; according to this rule, the appropriate encoding
for such purposes would be "PIC 999-99-9999" and "PIC (999)999-9999"
respectively.  But neither the implementation nor the intent was limited to
dashes, parens, and digits other than "9" or "0" in PICTURE strings.

Other mechanisms do exist to provide this capability on other systems.  I
have been told, for example, that "I" has been used as a PICTURE character
to introduce a following insertion character outside of the strict syntax,
which implies that PIC 999I-99I-9999 and PIC I(999I)999I-9999 produce the
desired results in those environments.  We think, for example, that "PIC
***,***.IZIZ" would provide the functionality on such systems that is
correctly provided by "PIC ***,***.ZZ" on Unisys A Series.

Personally, I think the use of "I" as an insertion flag would have been a
vastly superior approach to the provision of arbitrary insertion characters
in a PICTURE string, and think the Unisys A Series community would have been
a whole lot better off had that approach been implemented in the late 1960's
in Unisys A Series COBOL(68).   But it wasn't, and it's now some thirty
years, three generations of compiler implementation, and who knows how many
programs and corporate coding convention decisions, later.  This extension,
and the rule that underlies it, is here to stay.

Mr. Bart is indeed correct; the behavior of "PIC ***,***.ZZ" did indeed
change, in HMP 3.0 / SSR 44.2.  Prior to that release, COBOL85 failed to
treat "Z" characters as insertion characters in contexts other than
floating-Z, and also failed to treat trailing asterisks as insertion
characters in contexts other than floating-asterisks.   This behavior was
corrected, *in response to a customer UCF*, in HMP 3.0 / SSR 44.2 and
subsequent releases, and in all prior supported Interim Correction streams.

We stand by the correctness of that modification, in the light of the
standards prohibition and Unisys' documented extension to that prohibition.
Given the documented extension to the ANSI standards in this area, and the
three choices  Mr. Bart has proposed for the community, namely,

>A)  The Zs should be insertion characters (the new Unisys interpretation)
>B)  The Zs should be digit positions (the old Unisys interpretation)
>C)  A syntax error (ANSI)

Unisys continues to take the position that (A) is the correct approach, and
furthermore that (A) is the *original interpretation*, not a new one.  The
failure was in the original implementation of the design, and that failure
has been corrected.

There may well be cases in which the implementation cannot meet the
expectation of treating the character as an insertion character, and in
those cases the correct "fallback" position is a syntax error, not on the
basis of ANSI violation, because we already permit this encoding, but on the
basis of conflicting requirements or even simply on the basis that the
combination wasn't considered.  Any case that does not fall into one of
these categories represents an error in the compiler that should be
corrected.

Had Unisys made the decision to use "I" as an insertion flag as an extension
to the applicable standards on Unisys A Series systems nearly thirty years
ago, "PIC ***,***.ZZ" would indeed be receiving a syntax error today.  But
that path was not taken at that time, and one of the implications of the
path that *was* taken back then is that, in "PIC ***,***.ZZ", the "Z"
characters are correctly treated as insertion characters.
```
