In an important way GMH did pioneering work in compiler bootstrapping. She
insisted that the UNIVAC COBOL compiler be written in FLOW-MATIC, with
initially no mods to FLOW-MATIC and later with minimum mods (instruction image
emits: analogous to BASIC POKEs, from what I can tell). Newspaper editors and
biographers might not have the background to perceive the importance of that.

I do not know what her motivation was.  It could have been pride in FLOW-MATIC,
or comprehension that it (FLOW-MATIC) was slightly cross-platform, so a
compiler bootstrapped from there had potentially wider availability. One can
guess that she understood that if the building blocks were a restricted set of
functions then they could reach other platforms, and be positioned well as
their own company's hardware product line evolved.

Does anyone know if IBM used the earlier translators much to develop their
early COBOL? (Was this called COMP-TRAN?)

I do not know if anyone has ever made a claim that Grace Murray Hopper did the
first bootstrap compiler; but she atleast possessed one of the earliest tools
that could have used that way.  


There is an interesting note about Grace Murray Hopper at
http://www.cs.yale.edu/~tap/Files/hopper-story.html

I don't remember that many details, Bob, but Flow-Matic had been used to
bootstrap to Amico (?) a FLOW-MATIC VERSION that generated object code for
Air Force Logistics Command 1100 systems, and that group along with my
employer and others worked on an extension to use on the IBM 705.  This
design work was one of the inputs to the COBOL short range committee.

Regarding copies of the first Univac COBOL compilers, at the demo to the
Executive Committee a tape containing this compiler was presented to each
member.  My boss, a member, gave me his copy.   Beyond the nice paint job
that they did to the real of tape, I can not swear that it still or ever
had a copy of the first compiler on it.  Many times I had wished that I
could read that tape. However, finding a Univac I that could process the
code is beyond hope, and so I have it on a book shelf where I can look at
it several times a day.

Another thing I know about it vs the FLOW-MATIC compiler is that:
    Flow-matic was one program that read into memory overlays when extra
memory was needed and used just 1000 words of 12 characters each.
    COBOL '60 for the Univac was a string of programs (over 100 as I
recall) that processed the input, created output, and loaded other programs
until all was processed.  Compared to FLOW-MATIC it was much faster.

Of course, those are my reflections only, and aside from actually having
the real of tape and pictures of the demo I can't say what is fact or what
is fiction.

Warren Simmons
w.g.simmons@worldnet.att.net
