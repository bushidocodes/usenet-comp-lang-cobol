Figuring it out the old fashioned way - the logic would be:

   Three rows of three with each position able to assume one of 4 possible values:
   In each row, that means there would be 4^3 possible choices. (64 permutations
   in each row.) Three rows would mean there are 64^3 possible permutations in all,
  meaning you had 262,144 possible permutations.

  The interesting question is how many of those permutations are winning permutations?
  For each possible value, there would be 81 possible winning combinations ( I think,
  someone check me on that please?) and that means four values per slot * 3, or something
  like 972 possible winning combinations. That means you had a 1 in about 270 chance of
  winning something. The odds are much much lower of winning a particular prize of course.
  Then things shift when you count in a large number of cards.

   Back in the 80s a friend of mine got an idea about winning sweepstakes from some
   teenage movie where a 'computer genius' had the computer figure out the most likely
   combinations and sent in all the most likely winning combinations. He came up with a
   program that would do things like calculate the above stuff and come up with the most
   likely combinations. It sometimes came amazingly close, but when analyzed, it turned
   out to be just slightly better than pure chance. That turkey makes a lot of bucks working
   for some securities firm now.

   Anyway, if I goofed up the math above, it is purely because I am an idiot, and was amused
   by it. :)

-Paul

On Sat, 27 Apr 2002 10:06:04 -0400, Paul Raulerson wrote
>   The interesting question is how many of those permutations are winning 
> permutations?

You can't assume that the symbols are randomly distributed, because 
they aren't. These kinds of "games" are carefully rigged so that they 
know in advance what prizes they are going to give out. In this case 
they probably give a lot of $1 prizes and very very few Sony Home 
Theater Systems.

The only way to determine the probability is to read the very fine 
print where it says how many cards they will give out and how many 
prizes they expect to give away. Otherwise you are just guessing, since 
you don't know how the game designers have set it up.

This kind of game plays on psychology. Lot of cards will have TWO Home 
Theater Systems and TWO Mini Component Systems -- the prizes people 
want to get. Funny thing, that's exactly what Jimmy's card had. Thus 
people see these symbols a lot, and psychologically they feel they are 
close to winning one of those prizes, and so they go and buy more of 
the product that the cards come with. They don't stop to think that it 
makes no difference how often they see SINGLE symbols -- only how many 
cards have enough symbols to win. And that number is limited by the 
game designers -- they print enough of those so that when multiplied by 
the probability of actually scratching off the right three (1 in 84), 
they'll award the expected number of prizes. Most likely they also know 
which combinations of three people will scratch more often -- for 
example, few people will scratch all three on the top row.

Nah, come to think of it, I'm sure that they determine the results even 
more tightly. They decide how many Home Theater Systems they will give 
away, and then print that many cards with ALL "H" symbols. Same with 
Mini Systems and "M" symbols. No one will notice, since after all, 
anyone who scratches off three alike isn't going to scratch off another 
and thus won't realize that the card was a predetermined winner. Every 
other card has exactly 2 Hs and 2 Ms -- no more, no less. They let 
probability manage the larger number of small prizes -- for every 84 
cards they print with 3 "$", they'll give away one prize 
(approximately). But no probability in the large prizes -- they know 
how many winning cards they printed, and that's how many they'll give 
away.

Edward Reid

Other people have answered the odds, so I'll take a crack at the code:

*  table of the card combinations that our sponsor wants
*  total of 106,175 card created
*  total of 1060 guaranteed winners
*  total of 5115 others that can win
*  100,000 cards that have no chance
 01  possibility-table.
*  our sponsor has guaranteed 10 home theatre winners
    05 pic x(18) value 'hhhhhhhhh000000010'.
*  our sponsor has guaranteed 50 mini component systems
    05 pic x(18) value 'mmmmmmmmm000000050'.
*  our sponsor has guaranteed 1000 money winners
    05 pic x(18) value '$$$$$$$$$000001000'.
*  our sponsor has allowed for 15 other possible home theatre winners
    05 pic x(18) value 'hhhmm$$ss000000015'.
*  our sponsor has allowed for 100 other possible mini component winners
    05 pic x(18) value 'mmmhh$$ss000000100'.
*  our sponsor has allowed for 5000 other possible money winners
    05 pic x(18) value '$$$hhmmss000005000'.
*  the rest can't win, but it will be close (let's make 100,000 more)
    05 pic x(18) value 'ssshhmm$$000100000'.
01  filler redefines possibility-table.
    05 possibilities             occurs 7 times.
        10 tbl-values            pic x(9).
        10 tbl-remaining       pic 9(9).

*  work table of each value
01  work-values.
    05 work-value             occurs 9 times
                                        pic x.

*  the card layout (3 rows with 3 columns)
*  we redefined it to make it easy to move to (9 possible places)
01  card.
    05 rows                       occurs 3 times.
        10 columns              occurs 3 times
             15 card-value     pic x.
01 filler redefines card.
    05 card-placement       occurs 9 times
                                        pic x.

*  the number of possible card combinations
01  poss                           pic s9(9) comp value +7.

*  the subscript of the card combo table that we have randomly selected
01  tbl-sub                        pic s9(9) comp value zero.

*  subscript for the actual values
01  work-sub                    pic s9(9) comp value zero.

*  subscript for the placement on the card
01  card-sub                     pic s9(9) comp value zero.



*  there are seven possible card combinations
move +7 to poss.

*  run through card combinations, creating as many cards as requested
*  (note) - there should be code above to make that tbl-remaining for the
first card type is not zero
perform until tbl-remaining (1) = zero
*  space out the card values
    move spaces to card

* determine which card combo to use (randomly, unless only one left)
    if poss > 1
        compute tbl-sub = function random(poss)
        end-compute
    else
        move 1 to tbl-sub
    end if

*  if that combo did not come in zero (which really should be handled before
this routine),
*  move it to the work area (mostly for readability, you could have defined
the work table
*  within the possibility table)
    if tbl-remaining (tbl-sub) not = zero
        move tbl-values (tbl-sub) to work-values
*  subtract one from the remaining count
        compute tbl-remaining (tbl-sub) =  tbl-remaining (tbl-sub) - 1.
        end-compute
*  move the values, one by one, to randomly determined placement on the card
*  (there are faster ways to do this, but this is easy to read)
        perform varying work-sub from 1 by 1 until work-sub > 9
*  get random placement
            compute card-sub = function random(9)
            end-compute
*  make sure we haven't already put something there,
*  if we have, set the work-sub back down and try again
            if card-placement (card-sub) = spaces
                move work-value (work-sub) to card-placement (card-sub)
            else
                compute work-sub = work-sub - 1
                end-compute
            end-if
        end-perform
    end-if

*  if the remaining is zero, move it to the end of the table and set poss
down by one
    if tbl-remaining (tbl-sub) = zero
        move tbl-values (poss) to tbl-values (tbl-sub)
        move tbl-remaining (poss) to tbl-remaining (tbl-sub)
        compute poss = poss -1
        end-compute
    end-if
end-perform.


This would be for a "fixed" setup.  If we were to put truly random values on
the card, then you could do this:

01 chars                                 pic x(4) value 'hm$s'.
01 char-sub                            pic s9(9) comp value zero.
01 number-of-cards                pic s9(9) comp value zero.
01 number-of-cards-wanted   pic s9(9) comp value +100000.
< using card description above >

perform varying number-of-cards from 1 by 1 until number-of-cards >
number-of-cards-wanted
    move spaces to card
    perform varying card-sub from 1 by 1 until card-sub > 9
        compute char-sub = function random(4)
        end-compute
        move char (char-sub) to card-placement (card-sub)
    end-perform
end-perform.

This should give you the general idea (at least one way to do it)....
