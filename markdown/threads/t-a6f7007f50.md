You can always buy a new VS, but you're still faced with the y2k problem
inside your code and within your data. A platform change alone won't
solve that one.

The least painful way to get off the VS is by using a migration product
to convert your VS COBOL. There are two schools of thought on this:
Migrate your code to the COBOL of your choice in "native" format (i.e.,
no Wang extensions, etc.), or use a middleware product to emulate the VS
"shell" so the move off the VS is accomplished with minimal
code/environment changes. There are pros and cons to either method.
Three years ago we chose the middleware approach to move from our VS to
a UNIX box (RS/6000) using Micro Focus. No matter what you do the VS
terminals are history.

I suggest you look at "VuPort" from SanSoft, Inc. Good luck.

All Wang-VS systems can be made Y2K compliant by licensing the operating
system and upgrading to version 7.52 or later. I work in a shop with 5
VS systems all of which are on this release of OS and soon to be on
v7.53. The Date handled internally has been changed from DATE to DATE4
which handles a 4 digit year. The subroutines (VSSUBS) are now all Y2K
compliant with the exception of the DAY VSSUB which is supplied with the
latest version of the OS but has also had it's functionality replaced
with the VSSUB's DATE2 and DATE4 (not to be confused with the DATE4
reserved word System Date.

You can migrate from the VS but this is not an easy task. The Wang
Procedure code is far more powerful than DOS Batch file language - it
more resembles REXX. The Wang Extensions to the COBOL dialect are many
(I have been working on a prototype for a Wang to MF Cobol convertor for
quite some time) and many cannot be easily mapped to the PC
environment. One thing people whould understand when dealing with a
Wang-VS system is that the COBOL has been extended to include "video"
which is not in the ANSI standard and thereby subject to the platform
and environment in which it is resident.

When I first came to work here 3 years ago I was astonished to find out
that Wang computers are not only still alive, they are very healthy and
producing new hardware and software.

You might want to visit the following site for more information:
http://www.phoenix.net/~tjunker/wang.html

or

http://www.wang.com/

Hope this helps...

I admit that I'm a out of touch with the Wang World, but unless things
have changed I don't believe your statement is entirely correct. We
migrated off the VS a few years ago from an ancient VS-100 system.

You are correct that migrating from the VS is not an easy task (is any
migration ever easy?), but there are tools out there that duplicate many
VS functions to a high degree--although UNIX is a better alternative to
the PC environment for a VS conversion (IMHO).
