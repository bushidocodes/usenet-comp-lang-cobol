We, too, recently began having problems with the hard drive in our server.
Fortunately, the NT system detected the early failures and started fussing.
The drive had been in service for almost three years (after it replaced a
failed drive which, too, had been in service for three years).

Even with backups, one is faced with from one to several days to set things
right: get new drive, install OS, install updates to OS, install drivers,
install programs, reconfigure, passwords, etc., THEN restore the backup.

Our server now has TWO drives and a RAID controller running in MIRROR mode.
Every write to the drive is replicated, via the hardware path, to the second
drive; reads are split between the two drives (providing faster access and
transfer rates). But here's the neat part:

When one drive fails, or starts getting flakey, the controller detects the
condition and starts running on the spare. Meanwhile, the controller sets up
a din to warn everybody of impending doom unless the suspect drive is
replaced. We hot-swap the bad disk using drive caddies (about $12). The
controller applaudes our move and begins rebuilding the bad drive to match
the existing drive as a background task. This rebuilding, depending on
system load, may take as long as four hours. Meanwhile, during all this
to-ing and fro-ing, the system stays online.

This is, of course, not a substitute for a rigouous backup scheme; but
MIRRORING does prevent the crisis when a drive fails.

You need: 2 identical hard drives (20G = $80/each), a Promise Technologies
RAID controller (about $80), and, optionally, one or two drive caddies ($12
each). With THREE drives, you can routinely swap the secondary drive and let
the controller re-mirror the spare. This gives you an almost instant backup
of the whole operating system.
