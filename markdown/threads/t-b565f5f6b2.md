First, I haven't seen more than 10 lines of COBOL code in my life,
which explains the subject title and could do the same for my
post.

I consider the Y2K problem and wonder how many companies
could/would be better off just replacing their mainframe systems
with Unix or PC networks.

As I see it, COBOL programmers are getting say, $50 per hour.
$104,000 per year avg. Say someone with good UNIX or C++ or NT
skills is getting half that (or less in quite a few regions.) When
does it become more cost efficient to just put in a UNIX network?
Or an even cheaper NT network (at a sacrifice of security.)

Even if it costs more to change over after factoring in new
hardware costs & programs getting rewritten, afterwards the
maintenence costs are greatly slashed. C++/Java programmers are
going to be coming out in droves as the universities keep pumping
out those CS degrees. C++/J programmers market price may stay
static (or even drop?), while COBOL programmers will always
demand much more, just 'cause there are so few in comparison.
Even after the Y2K crisis ends (around 2001, 2002? :) and the
COBOL programmers need (and wages) decrease, they'll
still be getting more than C++/J people.

Besides the slashed maintenence costs, there is the addition that
UNIX/NT systems can be used for many more purposes. Last time I
checked there were more apps to be run on a Unix box then there
is on a mainframe. And if it's NT/95/98, the apps available are
about limitless and growing.

Like I said, I'm foreign to COBOL, but what's written in COBOL that
can't be written in C++/Java?? As I look into COBOL, they are making
programs to make calls on C++/Java/VB scripts, which means they
are taking COBOL more and more out of the loop (though increasing
productivity now.) Is that to make COBOL semi-OOP by incorporating
OOP programs into it or is the latest COBOL OOP itself?

And I would wonder how the mainframe processing power ranks vs.
today's avg PC. Let alone tomorrow's PC, which will continue to get
faster and cheaper. Even if the mainframe has more power, how badly
does it lose the bang/buck battle?

As an example, I used to work at a military testing base. When
I first started, much of the processing was done on an old
Encore system. It was clunky and cumbersome. Management made
it a priority to convert everything to C so all processing could
be done on the SGI's. When I left, the next planned initiative
was to convert all the C stuff to Java for the PC's.

Many companies may be too entrenched into their current system to make
such a large switch, but the latest reading I've been doing says a
big problem will be middle-small sized companies who are doing little
preparing for Y2K now. Would it be more effective for those companies
to just scrap their mainframe and COBOL programs instead of paying
COBOL programmers $70/hr next year?

To note, I'm not trying to slam the COBOL language, but rather learn
more about it. In fact, I'm highly considering how much I could pick
up if I wanted to try to learn it! But if I had a business, I'd
have to really look into what it'd cost to replace any COBOL stuff
with C++/Java.

Also to note, during my 4+ years at a base, I never met one ADA
programmer. Go figure.

Again, young and naive...

Chris L.

Again, it depends on the needs of the application. You're missing an
important aspect: Scalability. That is, the number of users that can
be using a specific application. Unix isn't as scalible as
mainframes. NT isn't as scalible as Unix.

The next aspect that must be considered is the cost of replacement.
Replacing an application averages 5 times as much cost than using the
existing code.

› Even if it costs more to change over after factoring in new
› hardware costs & programs getting rewritten, afterwards the
› maintenence costs are greatly slashed.

Maybe yes, maybe no. So applications are very well written and
maintenence isn't a problem. Others have become a tangled web of
spaghetti code that need replacement.

› C++/Java programmers are
› going to be coming out in droves as the universities keep pumping
› out those CS degrees.

Yes, I know all about this. Academia has always hated COBOL because
it's boring to them. 20yrs ago, it was PL/I that they were pushing.

› C++/J programmers market price may stay
› static (or even drop?), while COBOL programmers will always
› demand much more, just 'cause there are so few in comparison.

There is more than twice as much COBOL code out there as C and C++
combine. And because academia's real world myopia, people like me who
were trained in PL/I, get jobs programming in COBOL. Locally, I know
of 3 shops that train people to become COBOL programmers.

However, it's important to know that each language is better than
other languages for a specific use. The best language for business
data processing is COBOL.

› Even after the Y2K crisis ends (around 2001, 2002? :) and the
› COBOL programmers need (and wages) decrease, they'll
› still be getting more than C++/J people.
›

I was told COBOL was a dead language when I started. After 20yrs, I
still hear it, but I've seen no evidence of it's demise.

More C++/J people in 2002? That's not a sure bet. By then,
academia's latest craze could be over the new ultra cool cure all
programming language called something like Turbo Visual Mega T+++.

› Like I said, I'm foreign to COBOL, but what's written in COBOL that
› can't be written in C++/Java??

Any application can be re-written in any other language. The
questioned that needs to be asked is: Will changing the language
increase the utility and/or reduce the costs.

COBOL is best for custom business applications that are in a constant
state of change do to user demands, market opportunities, and
government requirements. The greatest feature of COBOL is
maintenance. Wordy COBOL allows someone who's never seen the program
before to more quickly figure out what the program is doing. Which
becomes very important for apps that cannot be down for sustained
periods. Wordy COBOL doesn't get tangled in spaghetti code as fast as
other languages. Keep in mind, that custom business application in
constant change will have dozens of programmers adding code, each with
different styles and many of those programmers in turn move on to
other shops, and are no longer resources. I've seen COBOL programs
that are 30yrs old with multiple enhancements, still in production
doing exactly what they are suppose to do, and very easy to read.

COBOL's biggest rap is it's strength. COBOL is seen as legacy. It's
legacy because dozens of languges have come and gone and it's still
there. And many forget that COBOL successfully replaced much of it's
predecessors, FORTRAN and Assembler.
