"8.2.1 Decimal-Arithmetic Instructions

The decimal-arithmetic instructions perform addition, subtraction,
multiplication, division, comparison, and shifting. 

Operands of the decimal-arithmetic instructions are in the packed format
and are treated as signed decimal integers. A decimal integer is
represented in true form as an absolute value with a separate plus or
minus sign. It contains an odd number of decimal digits, from one to 31,
and the sign; this corresponds to an operand length of one to 16 bytes. 

A decimal zero normally has a plus sign, but multiplication, division,
and overflow may produce a zero value with a minus sign. Such a negative

(Appendix A - please note the first sentence and let us know if you
still maintain that the compilers "add a sign" because IBM is a bad

A.1.2 Decimal Integers

Decimal integers consist of one or more decimal digits and a sign. Each
digit and the sign are represented by a 4-bit code. The decimal digits
are in binary-coded decimal (BCD) form, with the values 0-9 encoded as
0000-1001. The sign is usually represented as 1100 (C hex) for plus and
1101 (D hex) for minus. These are the preferred sign codes, which are
generated by the machine for the results of decimal-arithmetic
operations. There are also several alternate sign codes (1010, 1110, and
1111 for plus; 1011 for minus). The alternate sign codes are accepted by
the machine as valid in source operands but are not generated for
results. 

Decimal integers may have different lengths, from one to 16 bytes. There
are two decimal formats: packed and zoned. In the packed format, each
byte contains two decimal digits, except for the rightmost byte, which
contains the sign code in the right half. For decimal arithmetic, the
number of decimal digits in the packed format can vary from one to 31.
Because decimal integers must consist of whole bytes and there must be a
sign code on the right, the number of decimal digits is always odd. If
an even number of significant digits is desired, a leading zero must be
inserted on the left. 

In the zoned format, each byte consists of a decimal digit on the right
and the zone code 1111 (F hex) on the left, except for the rightmost
byte where the sign code replaces the zone code. Thus, a decimal integer
in the zoned format can have from one to 16 digits. The zoned format may
be used directly for input and output in the extended
binary-coded-decimal interchange code (EBCDIC), except that the sign
must be separated from the rightmost digit and handled as a separate
character. For positive (unsigned) numbers, however, the sign can simply
be represented by the zone code of the rightmost digit because the zone
code is one of the acceptable alternate codes for plus. 

In either format, negative decimal integers are represented in true
notation with a separate sign. As for binary integers, the radix point
(decimal point) of decimal integers is considered to be fixed at the
right, and any scaling is done by the programmer. 

The following are some examples of decimal integers shown in hexadecimal
notation: 

   Decimal 
   Value        Packed Format     Zoned Format 

   +123         12 3C             F1 F2 C3 

                or                or 

                12 3F             F1 F2 F3"

Perhaps I should explain the point about remainder etc a little
more. COBOL requires rounding to the nearest *decimal* digit, and
similar rules for truncation. That is not the same as round to
the nearest binary digit and convert to decimal. It is quite
painful. Have a look at the instructions the IBM mainframe

Merced have any hardware to help with this problem.

This is one of the problems we have had with various free cobols,
people assume that binary arithmetic semantics will be OK. There
aren't.

On the mainframe, the compiler handles display data in this way.

1. Pack 
2. Decimal Arithmetic
3. Unpack

Steps 1 & 3 have one instruction per operan and step 2 is one
instruction (unless you have really long operands or specificy
non--standard signing).

In this case the decimal arithmetic is in hardware. There is no
byte by byte processing in software. The tests I did showed to my
surprise that packed was as fast as binary.

If it is treated that way, then all truncation will be done correctly, and
rounding is trivial.  The only real problem is how many bits of integer are
required.

The BCD opcodes work well because they are integer oriented, not because
they are decimal oriented.

A small point, though, and perhaps a big philisophical difference. It is not
*Cobol* that requires the handling of decimal math.  It is the world we live
in that requires it.  Cobol is simply a means to impliment it.

The fact that a lot of computer languages force you to go through hoops to
take one third of a dollar, and give you the "correct" answer, is precisely
why Cobol is designed the way it is.  It is designed to reflect the real
world, not a specific hardware architecture.

I have been writing software for a long time, and the one thing that i have
learnt is that attempting to change the world to fit the software rather
than changing the software to fit the world usually fails. <G> In
fact, the real world has a habit of coming back and pounding you into the
ground, as anybody that ever tried to write a financials in a Basic that
insists on floating point has probably found out.

One of the main reasons for having a compiler is to place the various levels
of the software problems into their respective places.  The person writing
the application should not have to worry about the fact that the hardware
has no decimal operands; the person writing the compiler should not have to
worry about a ledger balancing.

Perhaps that is the real reason that your open remarks way back at the
beginning of the thread were jumped on so hard ... Cobol was not designed to
make efficient use of a specific set of opcodes. It was designed to take
care of a specific set of real world problems. How long the language lasts
should have nothing to do with how long a specific set of opcodes lasts.  It
is related, instead, to how long the problems last, and how good Cobol is at
solving them.

/* translation of a COBOL program */

/*-- relevant COBOL statements

 01 A PIC 99 VALUE 6.
 01 B PIC 99 VALUE 10.
 01 C PIC 99.9999.

 DIVIDE A INTO B GIVING C ROUNDED
 DISPLAY A
 DISPLAY B
 DISPLAY C
*/

#include <stdlib.h>
#include <stdio.h>
#include <string.h>

static long CharsToLong ( const char * s, const size_t n );
static void DisplayChars ( const char * s, const size_t n );

static char A[2] = { '0', '6' };
static char B[2] = { '1', '0' };
static char C[7];

int main ( void )
{
    long a, b, c;
    char cf[8];

    a = CharsToLong ( A, 2 );
    b = CharsToLong ( B, 2 );
    c = ((b * 10000) + (a / 2)) / a;
    sprintf ( cf, "%02d.%04d", (c / 10000), (c % 10000));
    strncpy ( C, cf, 7 );
    DisplayChars ( A, 2 );
    DisplayChars ( B, 2 );
    DisplayChars ( C, 7 );
    return 0;
}

static long CharsToLong ( const char * s, const size_t n )
{
    long r = 0;
    size_t x;

    for ( x = 0; x < n; x++ )
    {
        r = (r * 10) + (s[x] - '0');
    }
    return r;
}

static void DisplayChars ( const char * s, const size_t n )
{
     size_t x;

There are about a dozen points that he missed.  For example, thirty years
ago, the machine that I was coding on did not *have* packed decimal.  The
most efficient storage on that particular machine was called "usage
display-6" and was sixbit packed 6 bytes per 36 bit word. That was a PDP-10,
running Tops-10, circa 1968.

The fact also remains that there is absolutely no reason to convert, with
the exception of a few platforms that convert to do arithmetic.  That is
certainly not true on a number of machines.  On several, stuff is converted
to integer, then converted back.  Packed takes *extra* time on those
platforms, not less.  On others, arithmetic is done in Ascii, digit by
digit.  The assumption that Cobol always converts to packed decimal to do
math is simply erroneous.

Thirdly, lets look at that 10:1 figure.  It assumes that the only thing done
in a program is mathmatics.  In fact, a typical Cobol program is about 3-4%
clculation.  I-O accounts for about 90%, typically.  Moves typically take up
another 8-9%.  Most Cobol is looking up data, and formating it for I-O.  If
one takes that into consideration, then packing all the data may very well
cost, rather than save time.  If I look up a figure and put it on a page or
screen, rather that look up a figure and do a multiply, then put it back
without printing or examining it any way, then anything gained by lack of
conversion for the one function is lost by the time taken to convert for the
other.  What do you do most in Cobol?  Calculations or reporting and
updating?  The 10:1 figure might apply if you are doing large sacle matrix
arithmetic, but *that* I would not use Cobol for.  Even if I did, I would
make the data floating point, not packed. I doubt you could measure the
execution time difference in 95% of all Cobol programs.

Then there is storage efficiency.  The gain there is roughly 50%, if all
your numbers are an even number of digits, and none of your data is anything
but numbers.  However, the ratio of text to numbers in most data processing
is about 10 to 1.  When you look at actual figures the savings are not that
great.  At todays prices, probably about dollar per system, in my case.  My
systems sell for $20,000.  A buck of disk I do not worry about.  If it costs
me a day every five years, then it is not justified.

Next we get to machine compatability.  Every system that I have worked on
for the last 25 years has involved at least two platforms. Usage clauses, of
various sorts, are begging for trouble in that kind of situation.  A simple
standard of usage display, sign separate, gets rid of about 50% of all
programming problems, not only for mixed platforms but also for mixed
languages.  How often, in the last year, have we had to deal with people
attempting to read Cobol files from another language and getting screwed by
nibble oriented system designs?  Ascii is standard.  Bytes and overpunches
are not.  I stick to standards because they work.

There are several other points I could make, but that should be enough of a
rant to get the talk going.
