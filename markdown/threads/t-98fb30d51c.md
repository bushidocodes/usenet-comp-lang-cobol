› John Allman

Please explain what is so dubious about it -- it's not obvious to
me.

I use only a single STOP RUN in a given load module, and I put it at
the bottom of the top-level paragraph of the top-level module. Hence
it is immediately obvious where normal control flow exits, and neither
PERFORM nor GO TO is necessary to reach it.

If I encounter a fatal error condition, I PERFORM 9999-ABEND. GO
TO offers no advantage in this context. It does, however, offer a
significant disadvantage, quite apart from style or esthetics.

Suppose I code twenty or thirty GO TOs, all of them to 9999-ABEND, or
to other seemingly innocuous destinations. Further suppose that you
have to maintain this program.

Before you can safely make any substantive change to the logic, you
must establish the impact of the change, i.e. you must determine the
contexts in which the changed code will execute. If there is even a
single GO TO or PERFORM THRU, it raises the possibility that control
falls into your paragraph from the preceding one, possibly to fall
further into the next one.

To examine this possibility you must analyze the scope of the PERFORM
THRU, or the destination of the GO TO. If there are twenty or thirty of
them, you have to analyze every single one of them. Even if they are
all innocuous, you have to look at all of them before you can be sure
that they ARE innocuous.

Will you really undertake such a tedious and error-prone analyis?
Maybe, but most people won't. They'll take short cuts, and make
reasonable assumptions. Usually they'll guess right -- and sometimes
they'll guess wrong.

If there are no GO TOs and no PERFORM THRUs, then it takes only a few
seconds in the editor to establish that none of this analysis is
necessary.

Michael C. Kasten mc··.@swb··l.net

Consider the context of this discussion. 9999-ABEND aborts the program
and produces a formatted dump. It can only execute once in a given run,
and if it ever does execute, you will have much more to worry about than
the loss of a machine cycle or two.

There may well be cases where performance considerations justify the use
of a GO TO, but this situation is not likely to be one of them.

When performance is critical, I would rather start out by writing a
well-structured program, with no GO TOs or PERFORM THRUs. If it turns
out not to be fast enough, I'll identify the bottlenecks and optimize
the
hell out them, using GO TOs or whatever else is necessary, and leave the
rest of the code alone. If it's STILL not fast enough, and I have to
corrupt the structure of the entire program, then I'll do so, but such a
project is probably in trouble from the outset.

It's easier to make a working program efficient than to make an
efficient
program work.

Charles F Hankel wrote:
› 
› Michael C. Kasten wrote:
…[9 more quoted lines elided]…
› should you imply a return from it by using PERFORM?

True -- PERFORM implies a return, whereas 9999-ABEND implies a
non-return. Unless one does a really terrible job at naming one's
paragraphs, it's still pretty clear in context that 9999-ABEND
will not return. I'm willing to accept a minor contradiction, which
has never bothered me in practice, in order to avoid the more
substantive problems caused by GO TO.

Some GO TO's are worse than others, and GO TO 9999-ABEND is among the
most innocuous. Still, even an seemingly innocuous GO TO requires me
to verify that it is innocuous before I can understand or safely
modify the program. (The same is true of PERFORM THRU.)

The innocuous forms of GO TO are also the easiest ones to eliminate
by trivial rearrangements of the code. Hence I have little patience
even for innocuous GO TOs, except where performance considerations
may justify them in a particular case.

I discuss GO TO at length, along with various other topics, at:

http://home.swbell.net/mck9/cobol/cobol.html

Tim -
I'll echo J.S.'s comment 1 above. The use of the Perform-Thru is/was a
part of the structured method of coding for COBOL. It also has one
benefit that I've found in that it "can sometimes" prevent a program
from going off where you don't want it to. Unless one uses the GO TO to
branch out of the performed-thru paragraphs, the program will usually
return from whence it came as the coder intended. I say usually, in
that I've seen badly coded programs that went into unintended places due
to the improper use of perform-thru. Once such example is:

PERFORM SECTION-NAMED-1 THRU NAMED-1-EXIT.

SECTION-NAMED-1 SECTION.
...a bunch of paragraphs doing some stuff...

NAMED-1-EXIT.
EXIT.

PARAGRAPH-NAMED-3.
...code...

PARAGRAPH-NAMED-4.
...code...

SECTION-NAMED-2 SECTION.
...a bunch of paragraphs doing some other stuff...

When perform-thru is used for performing a section thru something, I've
seen the program fall on down until it found another section before it
would stop. I'll grant that the above example is bad coding, but I've
seen it happen. By inserting a section name of say SECTION-NAMED-1-EXIT
SECTION above the NAMED-1-EXIT paragraph, there is no chance of it
working incorrectly, unless the coder does a GO TO branch out of the
performed-thru function, a practice that is frowned on by me anyways.
I'll grant that your method of performing a section by itself is good,
assuming that you always have a section for it to find as the
"end-marker", indicating where it needs to stop. In my example above,
if you were to perform just the section, it would also perform the 2
paragraphs below, probably not what was intended. There will still
always be cases for branch-and-return functions (more PERFORMS or
PERFORM-THRUs) going out of a section, but as long as they return
properly to the calling place, then I don't see any difficulties. Just
my $.02 and YMMV.
Thanks, Bill Wagner.
A COBOL programmer/system analyst/designer since 1975.
