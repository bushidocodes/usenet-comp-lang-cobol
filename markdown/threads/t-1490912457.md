```
>If I were hiring a CoBOL programmer, it would be a plus if I saw that the
>applicant was familiar with other languages.

It's more than a plus IMHO.  If you ever want to have the attitude that "I am a
professional programmer" then you should think of COBOL as merely a tool. I
other words, I am not a COBOL programmer, I am a programmer who uses COBOL to
craft solutions to problems and tasks. 

I have seen "COBOL programmers", 15 - 20 years programming computers and they
didn't understand squat about variable types, parameter passing, scope, etc.
etc.  God help them if they ever loose their current jobs.  When we took an
overview course on Ada, I was appalled that they simply did not understand this
stuff.

Second, professional programming goes beyond simply learning the syntax of yet
another programming language.  A language does not a decent programmer make. 
And experience is meaningless in this context too.  Those same said "highly
experienced" programmers I talked about didn't understand diddly about
structured programming.  Have you ever heard "maximize cohesion and minimize
coupling?" If not you better find out darn quick (I suggest "Code Complete" by
Steve McConnell).

I was hired 2 years ago into a COBOL shop that was primarily a maintenance
shop.  Honest to God I had not written a line of COBOL code in almost 20 years
when I was hired.  Yet I recently finished rewriting THE program from hell, a
notoriously awful program that no one, not even the ones who have been at this
place 23+ years and understood the accounting system inside and out could deal
with.  Why?  The fact that they'd been using a COBOL 85 compiler for 5 years (7
now) and NO ONE knew any COBOL 85 (and still don't), only the old COBOL 74
standard, is a big clue.  30 years of programming unhindered by progress. 
COBOL is not a dead language, it only looks like it when you meet the
experienced, but utterly stagnent programmers using it.  Forgive me if this
sounds like bragging (well, I am damn proud of my work) but this really makes
the point.

If you are not constantly learning new stuff about the *craft* of programming,
you're dead meat.  And when you define your self in terms of "I'm a <insert a
language here> programmer" you automatically and subconsciously limit yourself.
 Beware.
```
