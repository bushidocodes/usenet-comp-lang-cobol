The most accurate method is to ask the programmer and add 25% -
and track the programmers so you know the fudge factor for next
time. The most common method used is to pick an arbitrary number
usually based on some political compromise with no input from
those who have to deliver it.  Or take the programmer's estimate
and subtract 30% - this is not accurate but it makes people happy
(for a while). 

Seriously, there is a whole literature on estimating projects -
do a search at Amazon. Look for "function points" if all else
fails. Make sure you are clear about what they have to do (eg how
much testing and documenting). One book I liked was called "Rapid
Development".

Tim Josling

As a former Systems & Programming Manager and now a loner, (call me
Lonesome Dove if you like - they film the TV series 30 miles west of
Calgary!), very briefly :-

1 - If anybody suggests using formulae, ( x lines of code equals y
man-hours), file it where it belongs - in the garbage bin.

2. There are essentially three criteria :-

  (i) In-house standards :-
       - for specifying the description of the problem to the 
	 programmer
       - shop standards on how programs should be written

  (ii) Program difficulty :-
	  (a) Complex (possible candidate for simplifying)
	  (b) Intermediate
	  (c) Simple

 (iii)  Programmer Proficiency :-
	  (a) Highly skilled
	  (b) Intermediate
	  (c) Beginner

What you are doing is matching up group (ii) against group (iii). Before
you become a target for attack by prima donnas, (God knows there are
plenty in data processing), try and seek consensus on what you come up
with. Your three years is OK but hardly makes you a seasoned programmer.
If necessary contact local user groups. Discuss with your own people why
it took x weeks/months to do a particular job, mentally grading programs
and people into the categories I've indicated above.

In the end it becomes a judgement call - the person dishing out specs
to programmers makes the decision on how long it should take. Make sure
your Manager/Supervisor is behind you before you take the first step.

You may get other suggestions to look at text books. Certainly read
them, but keep an open mind - these consulting guys write lots of blurb
for a living. (Consultant - somebody who borrows your watch to tell you
the time. Place the emphasis on the CON part of that word !)

Time Estimating is a topic one could write reams about - hope the above
helps.

Jimmy, Calgary AB

Judson, 

Not sure I agree with your addition, "super-complex". Bear in mind I'm
coming at this from a system's perspective.

Initially the Systems Manager/Analysts, (back when analysis and
programming were separate jobs) are between a rock and hard place,
trying to get a clear-cut request from the users and then doing the
impossible, trying to match the users' demand for when the system will
be up and running and at the same time being aware of what programming
resources will be available, (on-going maintenance and finish-off for
current systems). A seasoned analyst has to stick his neck out and come
up with a target date for the users, before he has even talked to the
Chief/Senior Programmer. 

Yes systems can be complex - but this is the result of using egg-heads
to design, when they should be restricted to a think-tank role. Such
people can be an asset to a DP department when introducing new
innovations for the first time, e.g. optical character recognition,
point-of-sale, automation of warehousing etc. BUT, these people should
never be allowed to get too close to the users, and God forbid that you
actually let them design the system - Pass their brilliant concepts on
to a pedestrian analyst who will arrive at a workable solution. 

The point I'm making is that your category "super complex" evolves
because it was an egg-head who arrived at the original system concept.
As soon as a Chief/Senior Programmer sees the system divided into
program specs - he should immediately be banging on the DPM's door -
"This aint gonna fly". And as I wrote, even my category "complex" should
perhaps be given some analysis to see if it can be broken down into
acceptable modules.

Just throw this in for good measure - if and when the COBOL fraternity
gets into OO - then this is a completely new ballgame - conceptually it
is a new way of thinking, and with its reusability can take away a lot
of that complexity we are both referring to. 

An example of things that can go wrong because of complexity - I believe
it was the London Stock Exchange. I think we can all appreciate how
complex the LSE or NYSE systems would be. Some guy was appointed Manager
and over a period of years built up a team of analysts and programmers -
from memory I think they hit 300. They were buzzing around for about 5
years discussing the whole thing conceptually, and presumably started
programming. The Chairman got PO'd at constant delays and called in
consultants. The good news, "It's cost you 5 million so far and is
coming along", the bad news, "You are going to have to spend another 5
million before it's finished!" The chairman fired everybody.

I understand your comments about getting "egg-heads" involved. :-)
But I am talking about systems where the user's requirements are so
complex and difficult to meet, that the challenge can be met, if at
all, only with the best, most experienced people.  Anything less and
it simply will never fly, even poorly.  These systems are by nature
highly complex, and cannot be built with a simple design, though of
course, you simplify the design as much as possible.  KISS has been
my motto for a long time. :-)

Let me give you a real world example that I have mentioned in the
newsgroups before.  In late 1996 I was contracted by the local county
government to rewrite their automobile license system.  I did the
design of their old system in 1977 and knew it would be a very large,
tough system.  A long time friend and associate who has a masters in
CS, has taught university level CS, and 20 years experience in and
around NASA, agreed to help.  My friend was totally flabbergasted by
the complexity of this system.  Legislatures think in terms of what
is politically expedient, not what will work in practice, and
bureaucracies are even worse.  The maze of rules involving issuing the
well over 200 different kinds of license plates in Alabama, with new
types of plates being created almost every month, is bad enough, but
the unbelievably vast number of exceptions, and exceptions to the
exceptions, is truly mind-boggling.  The county had seven programmers
totally dedicated simply to maintaining the old system.  To address
this amazingly complex situation, we designed as much flexibility into
the system as possible.  There is a data dictionary which defines the
basic field edits and valid values, and a host of table files used to
largely define how a given type of license is calculated.  The system
has help available for every field on every screen.  The help system
can search through lists of codes or valid field entries, and allow
the user to select, and the value is automatically inserted into the
screen.  I have a reputation for making complex things simple to use,
but it was a major effort just to teach the users how to maintain the
description tables.  Not from complicated user interface, which is
clean and straightforward, but because the concepts were so difficult
for non-techie users.  And this table driven approach was an important
design goal the users really wanted.  Now they can usually create a
new license type on the fly with no programmer overhead.  But that was
the easy part, for us.  There are areas in that system where field
validation edits and procedural algorithms must meet so many illogical
and often conflicting laws and regulations, that we are still not sure
that a logic which would entirely meet all requirements for a few of
those algorithms can even exist.  Some of those algorithms took weeks
to develop, and they were so complex that you couldn't grasp the whole
thing in your head at once.  We had to prototype those and keep working
on them until they were correct.  Believe me, this is something I
seldom have to do.  Yes, I may prototype an algorithm to debug it, but
not because I can't understand the whole thing.  Even so, the system
came up and flew the first time, with an extremely low count of minor
bugs (my friend and I said many a prayer of thanks over that! :).  When
we were through, I asked my friend how it compared with scientific
projects at NASA.  He thought a while and said "The only thing I can
compare it to is a stress analysis of an huge, complicated structure
for the space station.  Two successive teams of programmers literally
refused to continue working on it, saying it couldn't be done.  The
analysis would not have been done, except NASA found a programming
genius with a Ph.D. in math, who finally made it work."

The design requirements were created mostly by the users, the county
MIS staff and us.  I did all of the design myself, with a great deal
of assistance from my friend.  No 'egg-head' was involved. :-)  I've
built over 60 computer systems, including some that I was told were
impossible.  But I have only seen one this though.  The second toughest
was designing the old system this one replaced, back in 1977.

The truth is that we must quote to get the work. The basic problem
is false precision.

Do you quote 365 days or 1 year.  If you quote 365 days then it
had better be correct.  If it is one year there is an implicit
plus or minus a month in there.

The project will start at 3 man years, go to 34 or 38 man months
as things progress, and only on the final day will you really get
the number of days.

The uncertainty funnel comes into play here.  As the project
progresses the certainty of your quote must become more accurate. 
The experience you have learned and understanding what the user
really wants (or how little he really understands his wants)
generates a certainty factor.
