We tend to associate "legacy" with "mainframe".    But it ain't
necessarily so.

From my limited exposure, the large new systems I have seen have lots
of code which doesn't fit new standards, but is kept around because
redesign and replacement is much more difficult when anything can
touch anything.

I'm calling it "accelerated legacy code".

I used to think "legacy" only applied to mainframe until I was given a task 
to "keep legacy running while we develop and implement a new solution" for a 
major Utility in the U.K.

They were replacing their existing systems (which did include an IBM 
mainframe, but were mainly client/server) with the Siebel package. 
(30,000,000 UK pounds). I had a team of around 10 people and found to my 
astonishment that most of the applications were written as stored procedures 
on a number of  distributed SQL Server DBs. All of this code was considered 
"legacy" and this was in the late 1990s... The Business were generally out 
of patience with IT, the team were dispirited and demotivated because they 
had not even been offered the chance to work on the New Technology, and most 
of them considered their jobs were gone.

We not only kept it running, we improved it out of sight and the Business 
became very fond of us. (I spent a deal of time lunching with Business 
managers and listening to them.)  I managed to get most of the team (the 
ones who wanted to; two were pretty disillusioned with the company and left 
as soon as they could get another job) redeployed (after training) onto 
Siebel support and, as the existing systems stabilised, they were able to 
spend time with the Siebel implementation. By the time my contract finished 
this "discarded" group had made some major contributions to the Siebel 
development, contributing their business experience and gaining Siebel tech 
skills.  I don't know how successful the overall Siebel implementation was, 
but the "legacy" code was in good shape having fallen from over 900 known 
problems to around 40, and despite a very limited budget (they were 
understandably loath to spend money on existing systems), we had managed to 
meet several urgent business requirements for new functionality.

I never saw "legacy" in quite the same light again, after that exercise.

Pete.
