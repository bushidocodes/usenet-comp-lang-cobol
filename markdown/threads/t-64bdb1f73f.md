You might start with something like "Teach Yourself COBOL in 21 Days", then
continue with your compiler manuals. "COBOL for Dummies" will give you an
easy introduction, but probably won't carry you as far as you want. However,
it comes with several COBOL compilers on a CD-ROM, and might be worth the
price just for that. Do a search for "COBOL' at www.bookpool.com and
www.amazon.com. Bookpool has better prices, but Amazon has a better
selection.

the Murach books I've seen are quality texts. They have a web

It seems some emotion is creeping in to our cave... If you want a
very technical dissertation on intricate material, then I agree that
Murach books don't do it == and so would they (Yes, I've talked with
them).. but if you're a programmer in the trenches who needs to know
how to do function xyz, and needs to learn it quickly, then their
books are a good choice. And for those of you complaining... remember
that Murach offers a LIFETIME guarantee on their books. So what are
you griping about. Mail the book back to them.

I have met many programmers who learned their trade from Murach's
books... so while your opinion is always welcome, remember there are
others who swear by what you swear at.....

There is a problem today in mainframe books. Publishers can receive
more revenue by publishing PC books... and advertising isn't an issue
for PC books. Good books are pricey (e.g., my CICS for COBOL
Programmers , published by John Wiley -- is approx $55) I've met many
programmers who feel spending $100 a year on their profession is about
$95 too much... That is keeping publishers from staying in the market
with good writers. At least Murach is keeping to the mission -- and
even their books are falling behind. I'm currently revising my
'COBOL/370 for Power Programmers' to include more millennium material
and seeking a new publisher.... they just don't want the headache
because we're not buying....(yes, that book is now out of print, but
Amazon books is keeping a 6 month backlog for orders for used books,
if they can find them...)

A 'good' mainframe book should refer to cobol/370 or higher, cics 4 or
higher, and have information that does NOT include the basics of cobol
or of cics. we're a niche market --- bookstores don't sell what we
need. and it won't change if we keep trying to get the information
for free.

So... to the beginner who wrote this post.... my advice is to get the
Murach books, read them well, practice the design techniques in them,
and take good care of the books.... they'll do you well.
