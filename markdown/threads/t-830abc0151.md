The first comment I would make is about tools. The more powerful they are, 
the more damage they can cause in unskilled hands. If you let your 6 year 
old play with your power tools, there's not much point in crying over the 
holes in the furniture. Put the same tools into the hands of a cabinet 
maker, and you can take pride in your new dining table...

You didn't mention what language or tools you are using for "object 
programming", or what the company has failed with.

Certainly, if OO programming is thrown onto COBOL people without proper 
training and orientation, and if the same old waterfall approach is adopted 
with OO as was used with COBOL development, the results are likely to be 
disastrous. Object Orientation requires a shift in concept as well as 
technology, to be successful.

The Project Management approach to OOP needs to be completely different from 
that used with procedural development. (I found this out by experience...) 
If the OOP is managed properly it is very successful (or, it has been every 
time I have used it...Nowadays, I would need a special compelling reason to 
depart from using it - maybe batch development... difficult for me to 
envision...)  People used to managing COBOL development, for the most 
part... (I can thinkof one particular person who was astute and insightful 
enough to realise a change in methodology was warranted), simply don't get 
it.

Training and concomitant change in development approaches are essential 
ingredients for successful OO system development. (As not everyone embraces 
change, this is one of the reasons why OO COBOL was resisted by the COBOL 
community.)

Leaving aside the arguments for training and management adjustment, I stand 
by my statement above. The future is in objects, and, to a lesser extent in 
layered/tiered systems. (This has more to do with the granularity of systems 
and reduces the impact of system software changes on the applications. 
Separation layers, at least at the three basic levels of Presentation, 
Business Logic, and Data Access, are well worth the small extra overhead 
they incur.) When properly implemented, component based systems (based 
around objects) offer HUGE benefits that simply can't be ignored. The 
ability to encapsulate functionality and re-use it anywhere you need it, 
plugging systems together like building with Lego blocks, cuts development 
time dramatically, reduces maintenance and regression testing to almost 
non-existence (in my systems it achieves non-existence, but not everyone 
will develop the way I do...), and eliminates unit debugging. The code is 
smaller, tighter, and more powerful; this makes it easier to extend or 
enhance. Also, as time goes by and the library of useful components grows, 
systems get built even faster. There is a synergy to the whole business. 
Components can be built in any language that is appropriate and supports OOP 
(including COBOL). The difference is that the OO approach in COBOL is not 
the same as the non-OO approach in COBOL. Sometimes this gets overlooked.

The fact that some sites experience disasters with Object based systems, 
does not deter me or change my mind in any way. Until it happens to me, (and 
I have a successful track record of around a dozen small to medium (up to 
$15,000,000) OO projects successfully implemented) I will continue using 
objects/components.

analyse what went wrong and where exactly it went off the tracks. It isn't 
about blaming people, it is about ensuring that lessons are learned. If 
there was proper financial management in the first place, there would not be 
significant loss, even on "failed" projects.

3. "never" be done?  That seems too strong to me. I see no reason why a Web 
application should be any different to any other application in this regard. 
Provided the proper checks and balances are put in place there is no reason

If your concern is about security, modern Web applications with compiled 
code running on secure servers ("code behind") are far less prone to 
penetration than the good old days when everything was in HTML and 
JavaScript. The fact is that ANYTHING can be cracked, as long as there is a 
legitimate access pathway to it (and if there isn't a legitimate access 
pathway to it you might as well put it in a safe and dump it in the deepest 
part of the Ocean...); it all comes down to relative risk. You may consider 
the Web in general as more risky than your desktop (many people do...) but 
this is not necessarily the case. Much of this perception arises from 
journalists reporting horror stories. While these stories are usually true 
(at least, underneath the journalistic spin...) they are not necessarily 
about things that are likely to happen with well designed, modern web 
development. The fact that HTML is so easy means that anyone who can read 
and write can create a web page. BUT, not all web pages are equal... I know 
of sites that take hundreds of thousands of hits a day, against sensitive 
databases, without problem. (My bank is one... :-))
