(Response - July 31, 1997
Subj: COBOL is Alive and Well

Marie,
Your, note was forwarded to me by Mr. Stephen Burns of Boeing
(Ste··.@PSS··g.com). I will try to help you and your boss
and the editor of the New Zealand magazine.

The modern COBOL language, which I refer to as COBOL 2000,
is very different than the original COBOL first published in 1960.
COBOL runs on nearly all modern computer systems that feature the
latest technolgy. To be more specific, modern COBOL:
a) is object oriented,
b) is very well structured,
c) contains many intrinsic functions (both business and mathematical),
d) supports Year 2000 maintenance and conversion projects, and
e) facilitates both mixed language and cross platform environments,
(COBOL programs easily "talk to" programs written in other languages
(C, C++, PL/1, Fortran, even Visual Basic, Power Builder, MS Word,
MS Excel, Lotus, etc......).
The modern COBOL language runs on nearly every platform found in the
business world - mainframe, midrange and micro computers - including
among others: MVS, DOS, Windows 3.n, Windows 95, and UNIX.
(COBOL compilers are available for nearly every UNIX computer).
COBOL is truley the most universal language to be found.
This feature of COBOL programs, their ability to run, virtually
unchanged, on nearly every mainframe, midrange, and micro platform has
never been more important than it is currently given today's distributive
systems and client server applications with mixed platform environments.
And still the essence of the COBOL language, its clarity, has not changed
in 38 years. These core values, clarity and maintainability, made COBOL
the most popular application development language in the history of the
computer industry. These same core values thrive today in COBOL 2000
- only they are better than ever. Today's COBOL produces the most
readable and maintainable application source code possible. Why, then,
has COBOL been declining in use over the past decade in favor of languages
like C, C++, etc., which are preferred by many programmers today?
The answer has much to do with the new generation of programmers who
entered the commercial IT industry during this past decade - a decade
identified with the explosive growth of personal computers.
These languages are closely tied to their PC environments - UNIX, DOS,
etc. and these are the environments with which they are most familiar.
Unfortunately, they ignored the lessons learned by the IT industry over
the past 30 years. This was the era identified by the explosion of
commercial computer applications, running mostly on mainframes and written
mostly in COBOL. The lesson we learned then was how important computer
application maintainability is. Applications written with low level
languages like assembler were too difficult and too expensive to maintain.
Ultimately, the use of low level languages like assembler declined in
favor of higher level (and more maintainable) languages such as COBOL
(and Fortran, PL/1, etc.). Of course there is still a valuable need for
low level coding, especially for system software. But it is less
appropriate for commercial applications. Now in the 1990's we find
ourselves ignoring this wisdom and reversing the progress we made.
This will result in higher maintenance costs for our new computer
applications in the comming decade and beyond. So to answer the
question "...what significance does ...(COBOL) have in the forseeable
future for computer uses?" - COBOL IS PERHAPS THE MOST RELEVANT
PROGRAMMING LANGUAGE TODAY FOR APPLICATION DEVELOPMENT.
What about COBOL's future? As a member of the International
and American COBOL Committees(ISO/WG4, ANSI/X3J4), I can assure you
that these committees are constantly reviewing new trends in application
development technology to assure that COBOL stays relevant to the modern
IT industry, with state-of-the-art features. I expect that COBOL will
be around for a very long time.
Marie, I hope that this is helpful.
