record and especially for COBOL, they are not complex. If you recruit
through agencies who simply use software to match keywords on resumes, you
might well find COBOL people "thin on the ground". If you are so dumb that
that's the way you work, then you SHOULD pay a premium for people with a
resume that includes all the keywords you (or your recruiters) specifically
think you need. These days I find myself referring occasionally to language
specs for various scripting solutions in languages I have never learned, and
yet my scripts work fine and do what I require. I'm a programmer (among
other things...)

Getting people who actually understand your business and your applications
(in ANY programming language) is much more troublesome and that's why, if
you are nailing your colours to the COBOL mast, you should be prepared to
train your OWN people.

resist change, adopt a policy of :"If it ain't broke, don't fix it..." which
ensured you never enhanced, extended, replaced or reviewed what you were
doing, and now blame the world for moving on?

bandied about by managers as an "argument from authority" or justification
for their own tunnel vision, deserves whatever it gets.
