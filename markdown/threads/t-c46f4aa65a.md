Matthew L. Molnar wrote:

› In the next 2 months, my current employer (a major bank in the
› Northeast) will be offering a 12 week crash course in COBOL programming.
› I would like to know if anyone in the group could provide me with some

It doesn't surprise me. There are many companies that are doing the
same thing here in the Midwest. There is a shortage of programmers
among just about all major application languages.

COBOL is the most used language in the world. The reason why is
because it was developed specifically for business applications, and
businesses, governments and data processing institutions write a huge
amount of custom code to pursue there business needs. And because of
it's ease of use and maintainability keeps the written code around a
lot longer. Businesses are very keen on the fact that is cost 5 times
as much to develop new code, than to re-use existing code.

COBOL is strongest in the banking, financial, accounting, and
insurance industries. And this is where I've spent the vast amount of
my 18yr career in, and most of that is new development work.

If you want to be a games programmer, or write embedded code, COBOL
would be a poor choice. But, for business data processing, there
isn't a better language for that purpose.

› I have seen much debate in the group regarding whether or not COBOL is
› here to stay. I would not want to make this career change and have this
› language obsolete in the next 10 years.

Matthew, I was told 20yrs ago that COBOL was a dead language, or that
is would die in 10yrs. First, PL/I (bet you never heard of that one),
was going to replace it. Then, Pascal. Lately it's C or C++ or Java
or VB. And COBOL is still the most used.

› I personally believe COBOL is
› not going anywhere -- it has been around 40 yrs., and still going.

COBOL isn't a fashionable language, it never was, never will be. It's
not the Porshe, more like the 18 wheeler. It isn't the only language
I've use, but being in the financial and banking industry, Cobol is
the right language, and I don't see any sign of it going away.

business world of cost-cutting, the need to quickly produce results,
less staff and less time for documentation , writing in Cobol (
especially using structured methodology) produces results which are
the nearest you can get to self-documentation (in the absence of any
formal documentation).
