Some points from that article:

A bonobo skull in a Belgian colonial museum (in the Belgian Congo?) was
originally thought to be a juvenile chimp, until a taxonomist decided in
1929 it was an adult, and probably a pygmy chimp subspecies of some sort, in
1929.  It was decided that it was a separate species sometime in the 1930's.
This is one of the last large mammal species to be found by science.

The behavioral differences were apparently not discussed in print until
1954, and it appears that the observers were so concerned about the shocking
nature of their findings that they wrote them *in Latin*.  *In 1954*.
Extensive field research on bonobos didn't really crank up until the
mid-1970's (but that's only about a decade after similar research on chimps
cranked up).

I can't say that bonobos are particularly *homosexually* inclined; from what
I gather, it's more that they're *pansexually* inclined.    They're not all
that inclined to form *pair bonds* in the first place (the strongest
long-term familial bond appears to be between a male bonobo and his mother).
There are other nonhuman species that don't preclude "homosexual
inclination" or even homosexual lifelong pair-bonding -- this is
particularly common among bird species; swans are well known for this -- but
bonobos don't happen to be among them.  Pair-bonding, in fact, isn't so much
part of the repertoire of chimps *or* bonobos to begin with.

Male chimps pretty much treat their mates as property, intimidate or beat
them into submission, and use them as objects to be exploited for their own
pleasure; I'm not convinced that just because this behavior exhibits itself
heterosexually this should be considered somehow more "civilized" than the
friendly behavior of the bonobo!
