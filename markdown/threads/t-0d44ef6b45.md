Frederico Fonseca is one of the more gracious posters here. And is never short
on time to assist others. So I feel rather sheepish about this, but I can't
resist.

I have suggested in other threads that object oriented design leads people to
over modularize.  So I rest my case, and would add only that when a selection,
for whatever reason has 500 possibilities we are nolonger dealing with a user
interface that is of the human kind.

Frederico does point out that for most situations "This will normally be 1 or
more columns, and will normally fit the screen."

Frederico is doing excellent work and is nice to share his interesting
challenge, while he awaits some support. But to me it does reveal an underlying
problem with the OOD strategy. We cannot contain cost if we are
over layering this much.  The desing of this particular OCX is not from the
very generous Frederico, but is something he is interested  in, and provides an
example, and I emphasize not the most common norm. But there is a tendency with
the new design technologies towards what looks to me like a fairly expensive
over elaboration.

Truthfully it is going to be the applications for which  COBOL has
traditionally been used that are going to bring this matter urgetly to the
forefront.  COBOL based systems tend to be extremely long list of data with a
bit of code attached to it. If we encapsulate absolutely each and every bit of
it at the data element level, then 
the object member sets will be large indeed.

We in the business data processing sector, more than anyone else, are going to
see this problem. 

I think we should note it as it comes on screen, and do hope that Frederico, in
his broad wisdom, can see that this is not meant to pick on him at all, but
just to exploit his post because it records a blip on the screen. We are in
this together.  COBOL apps deal with a wide bandwidth of data names.  As this
stuff hits the new development methodologies, we could well see some
elephantine results. 

I think that the contrary force is going to have to come from our end of the
industry. We do not really need to wrap everything. We need to develop a
science of when to elaborate and when not to.

There are cost issues beyond the development and the maintenance cost. When an
excellent technician, such as the original poster, is presented with a list of
500 items to review or even to select from. Isn't there a chance that he might
make a mistaken selection, and doesn't that chance of operator error increase
as the length of the list increases?

Best Wishes,
 and I hope you get good response from Fujitsu.
Bob Rayhawk
RKRayhawk@aol.com
