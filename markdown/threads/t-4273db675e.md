I am working on developing the ultimate efficiency in terms of the
program modification and CPU usage trade-off for some Enterprise COBOL
programs that initialize large data areas with many fields.  One of
the programs has a 2500 byte area that consists of 300+ fields of
various types and sizes.  This area is re-nitialized for every
maintenance transaction that is applied, and on a recent run, there
were 55,000+ maintenance transactions.  I looked at the assembler code
generated for the initialization, and it consists of 4+ pages, at
least one for every field.  I assumed, incorrectly, that changing the
program to initialize the area once and then move it to working-
storage, and then for subsequent transactions moving the working-
storage field back had to be faster.  In reality, the compiler
generates a MVLC(length) moving 1 character at a time, and that is
very slow.  Can I call C and move more bytes at once?  If I can, will
calling C, performing the move(s) required and then returning be
faster?  If anyone has experience with this situation, please let me
know!
Thanks,
Chas

Coding for micro efficiency is usually futile. In your example, moving 2500 
bytes, one byte at a time, and assuming 6 microseconds per move, yields a 
time of 1.5 milliseconds. The logging of the resulting transaction has to 
take ten times that.

Whatever technique you use is going to take SOME time, so the savings will 
be less than the 1.5 milliseconds mentioned.

Before you get all exercised, time the existing code. Loop a million times, 
once with the initialize code and once with just the loop. Time both. Divide 
the result by 1,000,000 then multiply by 55,000. The result represents the 
maximum time saving during the day. I'll wager it's less than a couple of 
minutes.

Sometimes micro efficiency is necessary - maybe even in your situation. In 
the cases where the transaction must be processed as quickly as possible 
because other transactions are stacking up behind it, you've got to the best 
you can. Another example involves communication - again there is an urgent 
stream.

Usually, even in these two cases, it's often cheaper to just buy a faster 
machine than it is to re-program some sub-level routines.

Here's a trick. Initialize the block. Write it to a scratch file. Every time 
you need the initialized block, read it back in. Of course a good operating 
system won't actually read the disk; the system will grab the block out of a 
system buffer. Point is, the OS move may be faster than the COBOL move.

It's not a bug, so no, it hasn't been fixed.  :)  I know in most 
environments in which I've worked, uninitialized variables basically get 
whatever was in memory the last time it was used.

Long answer - in COBOL 85, INITIALIZE has the effect of doing an 
elementary MOVE to every elementary item below the level initialized. 
Since FILLER fields have no name, they cannot be manipulated by 
INITIALIZE.  The 2002 standard addressed that issue by adding the "WITH 
FILLER" clause to the INITIALIZE statement.  It, as the words used would 
suggest, initializes the filler items as well.
