know

these days, resumes are scanned for keywords - another reason to put it
on
your resume.

What's missing in this disscussion is what is implied by "IBM COBOL
(whatever)." It implies that one knows the platform, operating
system, editing evironments, debuggers, syntax variations, add-ons,
performance optimizations, work culture, etc. that the buzzword means.
It tells the prospective employeer one is well-verse enough in the
environment to "hit the ground running" without the need for
additional training to know "the basics."

There were times during my job hunting when I applied for a System/3x
COBOL position I got turned down just because I didn't know the
platform - OCL, S/36 editor, etc. - and got turned down for a UNIX
COBOL position because I didn't know vi (now that I know I rather
stick with SPF). Even now I know I will have problems entering the
DB2 world without "the basics" even though what I need to learn is
just enough DB2 and SQL syntax to substitute for most of the
traditional input/output syntax in batch and CICS so to get my foot in
the door (I know, you DB2 purists will balk at this last suggestion;
but consister how much DB2 syntax you write is so routine you never
think about what you're writing).

My advice is get exposiure to as many computing environments as
possible while in school. If you got a class assignment that doesn't
involve platform-dependent data in a file, ask your professor for
permission and a student account to an alternate platform to get
exposure. If you code in "C" on a PC and/or UNIX platform, get some
exposiure on an IBM "C" platform and learn how to adjust to that
platform (esp. trigraph substitution and writing a pure batch or
TSO-interactive program). Mind, you need to ask later in the semister
after demostrating a solid understanding of the platform and language
you're on at present.

The idea is to truthfully pad your resume and applications the
necessary information needed to land the entry-level job and
demostrate that knowledge during the technicial interview. Above all
be willing to demostrate taking on and solving problems on different
platforms - that's the bottom line.

Just my 1.5 cents worth, adjusted for inflation
Boyce Williams
