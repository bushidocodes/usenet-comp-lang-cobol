```
So... my knowledge is a bit out of date and I'm not sure where I would 
begin to research such things; assistance would be greatly appreciated 
and, I'm sure, worth at least double what I'm offering to pay for it.

Years ago I was part of a Huge Team, composed of company employees, 
independent consultants/contractors/hired guns (of which I was one), 
consultants/advisors from a Swedish firm and bunches and bunches of 
consultants from Peat, Marwick... I *told* you this was 'years ago'.

It was the habit of the Big 8 Accounting Firms, back then, to send out 
teams.  Usually there were ten folks in the team, two Senior and eight 
Junior; as best I can tell the goal of the team was to generate as much 
paperwork and meetings as possible while the Junior members learned the 
client's business at the client's expense.  It was hellish, gruelling work 
for them and the Junior folks would drop like flies... but when the 
project was complete there was, usually, at least one who had learned 
something and stayed on... and who would become a Senior member for the 
next project.

This situation was quite profitable for the Accounting Firm; as I recall 
the Junior members were paid between US$45K - US$60K a year while the Firm 
billed the client between US$100K - US$120K a year.

I have no idea what the Senior members were billed at... and that is my 
question.  There is the possibility that my current client might not renew 
my contract past the end of next March and I've been contacted by someone 
I worked with, years ago, who is now in a position to sign my 
timesheets... he's on one these Huge Team projects and he wants to know if 
I am willing to come on as The Old COBOL Guy so that I might, in my 
familiar role as (echo chamber on) CCCAAPPPTTTAAAAIIIINNNN COBOL... 
OBOl... OBol... Obol, once again be set against my arch-nemesis, Meeting 
Man.

This is all tentative, of course... but right now is The Dead Season 
(little gets done between the end of November and the third week of 
January) so I figured I might use the time for getting a little 
information.  Does anyone know what the Big 8... errrr, 4 are billing 
their Senior folks at nowadays (or where I might be able to find this 
out)?

Thanks much!
```

```
What people "could be making" really has no basis in reality.  What
consulting companies charge a customer of a consultant's time is NOT what
the consultant COULD be making if s/he were on their own.

A consulting company must also pay the background people who are selling
the "wares", the person answering the phones, and sometimes the person in
between gigs.  It's these overhead charges that make a larger company more
attractive than a consultant going it alone even though the fee is less. 
The overhead indicates that there is a backbone to the company that can
cover the investment if the consultant gets hit by the proverbial bus (or
quits).

There is a lot to be said for the lone consultant but the overhead of a
company can also mitigate a buyers apprehension.

What a company charges for their consultants is not necessarily what would
be paid for a lone consultant!

BTW, I am NOT a consultant but was in a former life.  More like a high
paid temporary worker...didn't "consult" much at all!!
```

```
"Consultant" has IMNSHO become one of the most-abused terms in the IT
industry.

If you are not in a position, unable  or unwilling to tell a client A) This
is not a good way to do this;  or B) Suggest better ways yourself, please
don't market yourself as a 'consultant.'  If you are full of technical skill
but are clueless about the client's industry, its practices and the way your
software will be used by the operators and other users, please don't market
yourself as a 'consultant'.  If the client is not going to look to you for
opinions and ideas, please don't market yourself as a 'consultant.'  If you
can't take a project from 'business concept' to finished goods, please don't
market yourself as a 'consultant.'  If you can't communicate equally well
with end-users, managers and existing I/T staff, please don't market
yourself as a ''consultant."

There's nothing inherently evil or demeaning about being a 'contract
programmer/analyst.'  It's a noble profession. Heck, you can even be an
'expert' if you like.

Me, I'm a crummy contract progammer/analyst or technical expert... I'm not
terribly fast when coding, I have to refer to the 'syntax guide' and other
manuals quite often, and I don't really grasp very quickly what existing
code is doing unless it is well-commented in English.

But within my industries and fields of expertise, I'm a hell of a good
consultant.
```
