One of the issues that you need to "think about" when comparing
maintainability/readability of COBOL vs C (or C++) is what happens when you
(or someone else) reads a program written by ANOTHER programmer - not when
reading your own code (although even that can be hard to understand after a
while).  I believe (but can't provide references at the moment) that both
IBM and MERANT have some statistics (available to customers) supporting what
happens when "equally experienced" COBOL and C (C++) programmers are given
"equally well written" programs created by another programmer - and asked to
make a "minor" enhancement.

As stated elsewhere, it is certainly possible to write "bad" COBOL programs
and "good" C (C++) programs - but there is (I believe) some evidence that
the "self-documenting" constrains in COBOL *tend* to make equally
"qualified" COBOL programmers create "better" (as far as maintainability
goes) programs.  ON THE OTHER HAND,  I believe that there is *also* evidence
that these same "equally qualified" programmers tend to write "more
efficient" code in C (C++) than in COBOL (especially when "low-level"
machine interfaces are required - or you are talking about quick-running -
not long batch oriented programs).

Well as an example of obfuscated code, how about this (see at the

others did not find it obfuscated and I did was that I was
actually trying to work out what it did so I could use it.

To understand that code, you are reduced to finding every
reference to all the switches to try and reverse engineer what
they mean. This is in the test driver, not one of the test
programs themselves, so the excuse of doing a torture test of
COBOL is not relevant. 

I believe that COBOL has a number of major advantages over other
languages, in that you have good suupport for common business
functions like financial calculations on large numbers, big
sorts, precise control over numeric accuracy, etc, plus you have
standard ways to use indexed files, etc. That and the fact that a
lot of talented and experienced programmers only know COBOL well
makes it worth writing a free compiler for it. 

But I am not convinced about the claim that COBOL is more
readable. The claim is often made but seldom backed up by
evidence. Bill Klein referred to studies that Merant etc have and
I will follow that up. But my own experience was that I found
COBOL harder to learn than Fortran and C, which may be in part
why I am sceptical that it is more readable, once you are past
the learning curve. And you do need to get past the learning
curve to understand all the rules like truncation, scope of
performs, what the magic level numbers mean etc.

Also why did I say "add a to b giving c" and not "compute c  =
a+b"?

A good question, although I would suggest that the 'add'
statement is more in keeping with the spirit of COBOL and the
'compute' is more C-like in that it uses symbols rather than
words to describe the operation.

I don't use compute because early on in my programming career, I
found that the precision of compute statements was very
unpredictable. IBM do have a description of the intermediate
precision in the back of the manual but I found often the
intermediate precision was not what I wanted and it was hard to
get a clear idea in your head of what was going to happen. And
the results are compiler dependent. So, no compute for me unless
precision was not an issue eg when dealing with whole integers. I
belive this issue is addressed in the later IBM compilers where
they have increased the intermediate precision, and also COBOL
2002 has new way to specify a higher degree of precision in
intermediate results, so it may be time to have another look. 

Another programming habit I picked up a while ago is to always
have "-1" on the end of data names, to avoid clashing with
reserved words. How do other people avoid the ever growing list
of reserved words?

Tim Josling
