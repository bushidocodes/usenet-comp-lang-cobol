The problem with comparing languages is often one of expressibility
versus expresssivness.  COBOL, by design, is expressive of the kinds
of problems encountered in business data processing.  C++, while it
can be used to express solutions to such problems (expressibility)
is not inherently suited to expressiveness for such solutions. 
Object-oriented COBOL is a good choice where expressivness is more 
important than expressibility for financial and accounting software
solutions.

And for financial applications, it is still the only object-oriented
programming language, with the exception of Ada 95, that has direct
support for monetary data types and a large number of options for
formatting and correctly handling them.  C++ does not have this
capability, although one can design a class to emulate it.  This
is one of those cases where a built-in type is more appropriate 
than depending on a user-defined class.  

C++ is also excessively needy of pointer constructs.  Such constructs
are unnecessary for most business data processing problems.  On the
other hand, COBOL is a little weak in its support for the kind of
sophisticated data structures possible in C++.  For example, COBOL
programmers typically do not have much experience with Linked Lists,
Binary Search Trees, etc.  It is a matter of picking the language
that best expresses solutions to the kind of problems one is 
attempting to solve.  For business data processing, the best choice
would be either COBOL or Ada 95.
