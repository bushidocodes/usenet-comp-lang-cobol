```
I believe that the trade-off between execution efficiency and readability/maintainability should
*almost always* be made to favor the maintenance programmer and not the computer, for one
simple reason: numerous studies have repeatedly shown that a *very* large portion (e.g. 80%)
of the life cycle cost of any software is in maintenance, and only a small portion is in execution.

Therefore, unless truly enormous improvements in execution efficiency can be achieved (and by
that I mean at least two orders of magnitude), it is best to sacrifice execution efficiency for
ease of maintainability. This is, by the way, one good argument for using Cobol for business
applications instead of C, Ada, Fortran, etc. But I wouldn't post that to comp.lang.c !

Frequently, the majority of the time spent executing a program is consumed in only a few
modules, so code optimizations elsewhere in the program make little sense -- and no sense
at all if they come at the expense of clarity and maintainability.

Bear in mind also that in a multiuser environment, hundreds or thousands of copies of a given
program may be running simultaneously, and slight improvements in its execution efficiency
can produce tremendous gains.

Finally, if a piece of code is consuming sufficient system resources that it seems desirable to
optimize it even at the expense of maintainability, consider that perhaps the entire algorithm is
sub-optimal and should be replaced. For example, it doesn't make sense to optimize the
execution speed of a linear search if you can replace it with a binary search!
```
