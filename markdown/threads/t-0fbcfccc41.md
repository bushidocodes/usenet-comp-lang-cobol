Per the posts of "TPF"...he is missing several important facts:

1) He suggested converting all the report programs to something else
   (IIRC it was "Cristal"?)

Sure, it's a lot easier to use a report generator to put out a simple
report rather than coding all the headers, page breaks and the like by
hand in COBOL.  Indeed, many mainframe shops have been using generators
(ie Easytrieve, SAS, DYL280, FOCUS) for years to do just that. 

But the big problem is that each generator is unique and not
static.  Years ago our organization had to change its Easytrieve
to Easytrieve Plus.  Our sister organization had to go from DYL260
to DYL280.  COBOL programs could be left alone.  Now that our
organizations are merged, management would like to save on license
fees and use only one generator.  But that would mean either all
the DYL280 programs or all the Easytreive programs would have to
be converted; plus all the staff retrained.  Very expensive and
time consuming.


Another big problem is that Client Server oriented products are far
from static and upgrades require staff retraining and rollouts.
My organization was suffering through a hodgepodge of PCs running
DOS 3.3 to Windows 97.  They spent a fortune in overtime to upgrade
everyone to Windows 95/97 even though the older 286/386s were doing just
fine where they were.  (A lot of people used them merely as a dumb
3270 terminal with simple word processing and their old 286 worked
just fine.)  Indeed, just as some of us got used to Windows 3.1 they
stuck Win 95 and now their's Win 97.

Likewise, we got used to tools like Word 6.0 and now there's Word 97
(with its %%#@!+*&^!! dancing paper clip).

It must be remembered that techno-geeks ENJOY upgrading their systems
and learning new products.  The magazine writers make their living off
of doing this so naturally they encourage it.  But for those of us in
the real world, supporter our clients who our serving our customers,
it is a big expense of time and money and inconvenience.



2) Staff experience:  Experienced COBOL programmers have been
around long enough now that they know how to write clear, concise,
and efficient code, and quickly identify bugs when they occur.
Our COBOL based applications today are extremely reliable and
machine efficient, processing thousands of online transactions
and millions of batch transactions.

In contrast, Client Server is new to everybody.  Systems crash--
is it the individual's PC, network, "switch", server, or what?
People have no experience and are figuring it all out.  This is
extremely costly to an organization in terms of both customer
dissatisfaction and employee time.

Training a large organization is costly too.  Not only taking
people away from their regular jobs, but buying a course/instructor,
and most importantly, the learning curve.



Being the "first on your block to own the newest..." is a bad 
strategy in data processing.


This of course is not to say we should frozen in place.  Of course
not.  But new products should roll out slowly AFTER they've been proven.

Many companies seem to want to put their most critical business
application on Client Server, without enough experience first.
I know of one outfit that tried to scrap their RPG S/36 with
Sun/Oracle and the new system is nowhere near ready to go up.
Every test fails miserably.  They just keep finding new problems.
(Now consultant-client finger pointing has begun.)
Since the S/36 isn't Y2k compatible, they're now making plans
(at considerable cost and inconvenience) to fake out the S/36
so it can keep it running.


Years ago, when IBM introduced its System/360, it knew it had to
provide an easy upgrade for existing users of 1401 and 7090 gear
and built in hardware emulation.  Until a few years ago, I knew of
shops STILL using a few 1401 Autocoder programs!  (I wonder if any
still are?)  The point is emulation allowed users to convert at
THEIR OWN PACE.

System/360, while a tremendous improvement over 1401/7090, had its
share of growing pains in its early years and had a long learning
curve.  Some users wisely took their time, retaining older systems
they knew well and were reliable and gradually evolved into COBOL
and online processing.
