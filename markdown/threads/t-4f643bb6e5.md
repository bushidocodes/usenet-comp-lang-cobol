Sorry for being so late with my response, been busy these days. However, to
start with your ending, please do not be afraid of sounding silly. An honest
question never sounds silly. We all have to start one place, we all have
been at the same level once.

So, Interesting question. What should you do... There is a couple of things
that are important to remember in such a consideration. At least of my
humble opinion;

1. There is nothing that is free. By saying so, I mean you will not
necessarily have to spend a lot of money, actually, most could be free,
except for the computer. Well, you even get a computer for free these days.
What you will have to pay, is your attention,  dedication and frustrations
for a large number of hours ahead. When I sit here now, it feels like I have
been doing software programming for all of my life. But, you and I, we know
that this isn't true. I, as for others out there, has spent thousands of
hours behind a keyboard, to get where we are today. I am not saying that it
is demanded several years of studying and practise to become a software
developer, but you will no doubt have to spend a lot of time with it, and,
unfortunately, there will be times and tasks that are tedious and dull once
in a while. These moments are your crossroads, if you turn there you're
gone. If you keep moving, another barrier is broken. This of course applies
to any occupation one chooses.

2. The study, this may be the point where you would appreciate input from as
many as possible. I have one idea, my personal idea about this. Others
believe they have a better idea and that I am wrong, I leave to you to
choose. I started my career as a software developer learning myself basic on
a Dragon32, many, many years ago. There was PC's those days, but they were
insane expensive. Back then I was not really sure what I wanted to do. Later
on, I had some more basic in school, still without knowing that this was the
real thing. Finally, when I entered university I realized that software
development was my choice of occupation. The study I took was covering a lot
of topics, and a lot of languages. In the sense of being any expert back
then, I would say pascal was it. I had hardly seen a Cobol compiler then.
However, my first job after school was developing in Cobol, and I had no big
thoughts about that. Because, it is just another language. In my opinion,
learning a new language for software development is just as a new language
to speak. You learn the words, the grammar rules and there you go.

In my first email to you I said that a good programmer wouldn't care whether
he/she was using Ada, Cobol, C, Pascal, Basic, Fortran what ever. From my
point of view, the essential is to know the methods, methods, which should
be language insensitive.

I use to say that there are two kinds of programmers, those who just write;
They are rarely innovative, they do what the book says, if the book doesn't
cover the problem they have two options, either get another book or they
don't do it. I use to compare these kinds with myself needing a table. If I
need a table I would go to a warehouse selling such construction kits with
precut and all that stuff. If I cannot find such a shop, I would improvise,
but I would never start over like a carpenter and create a table from
scratch. The carpenter here would be the variant 2 of the developers; These
guys uses books as well, but for them methodology are superiour to the
language. They know what to do to calculate interests of a loan, they know
how to reuse code, they know how to improve performance and reliability.
They know to pay attention to the goal of their task in a wide perspective,
not isolated in the single problem ahead of them.

So, you have already had many suggestions about books that teach you Cobol
in 21 days. First, remember that the book is not going to teach you
anything, it is you that has to use dedication, attention and frustration
*with* the book. Second, you do not learn Cobol or any other language in 21
days, that's a commercial, it sounds better than "Teach yourself Cobol in
365 days". What you would do in 21 days is to get a more or less
understanding of the basics. From which you may extend if you like. I
haven't seen these books, but I believe in those people that has recommended
them for you.

It seems to me, that you are not 100% sure of what you want to do. I don't
know how old you are, if you are at school, working or what ever. As a
starter I think the approach of a book would be good. If you are a family
man, going back to school could be hard, but I think it would be a good
choice. It is hard to capture new stuff without a stimulating crowd of
people to do it together with. You do need to learn methods and techniques,
and have them explained.

If you go to school. Wants to become a developer. Choose generic courses.
Learn how computers are built. Study operating systems basics, learn your
language insensitive methods and you are ready for any language, be it Cobol
or assembly.

I am sort of afraid though, that you may believe that you could do a course
of 6 months or so and get a job with a salary of $50' - $100', I hate to
disappoint you, but those guys are very, very experienced.

Well, this turned out as something else than I thought of when I started the
writing. I hope you find anything useful in my answer.

Cheesle
