```
A couple of years ago I saw a routine posted in BASIC in a DOS or
Windows newsgroup.  Looked like it would probably work.

you to transmit the card number and let the system tell you if it's a
bad card.

believe the original intent was to detect digit transposition for
keypunched cardnumbers.

Discover or Amex and ask them.  They might tell you if you give them a
good enough reason.
```

```
taking orders via the telephone. The idea was to do basic card
validation at the order entry stage to guard against the operator
transposing/missing digits.

The answer from the card company (visa) was to phone the number
through for authorisation - not practical when you have a caller on
the line wanting to place an order, even with an electronic card
terminal - it's needs a line, which in the UK are expensive
compared to the USA.

The system is designed for the orders to be stored, passed in a batch
to a supervisor to get the authorisations and then release the
orders for picking/dispatch. Bigger systems do this automatically,
but this was written for a small company, just starting up - hence
the simple 'batch' approach.

I did manage to obtain the information (not for all cards) I
needed from a colleague who knew my reasons for wanting it, and
it has trapped a few operator errors saving callbacks to customers,
although not does not guarantee a valid card or that the
transaction will be approved. 

For security reasons, I am not willing to pass this on to anybody,
unless I know them, trust them and regard the reason as being valid
- so please no email requests they will be ignored.
```

```
The Canadian social insurance number (SIN) is a 9 digit number where the
first 8 digits are random and the last digit is the checksum. The checksum
is derived using a MOD10 algorithm, as follows:

1) Add up digits 1,3,5 and 7 giving subtotal-1
2) **this is the tricky part** Add the sum of the digits of the result of
doubling digits 2,4,6 and 8 giving subtotal-2.
3) Add subtotal-1 and subtotal-2
4) Divide the total by 10.
5) Subtract the remainder by 10 giving the checksum.

For example:
  Given 450680772

1)  4 + 0 + 8 + 7 = 19 (subtotal-1)
2)  5 + 5 = 10 --> 1 + 0 = 1
     6 + 6 = 12 --> 1 + 2 = 3
     0 + 0 = 0
     7 + 7 = 14 --> 1 + 4 = 5
     1 + 3 + 0 + 5 = 9 (subtotal-2)

3)  19 + 9 = 28
4)  28 / 10 = 2 r 8.
5) 10 - 8 = 2 (checksum)

>
```

```
There are several reasons for allowing the credit card company to
perform the validation, but the best one is so that they can change the
algorithm without you having to re-write your code.

Look at the phone company for example.
In the Denver area, we now have to enter area code for all local calls. 
Let's say you wrote a program which sees the area code and adds a 1
prefix to call, or sees the area code - recognizes it as the calling
area code and drops it.

Or the PBX which won't let you dial long distance calls except (800) and
has to be reprogrammed to accept 888.

Things change.  Where they can change, try to narrow down the
responsibility for making the changes.

Another thing - I often see people doing more editing than is
necessary.  If a key has to be numeric, sure a COBOL numeric check is
more efficient than calc'ing to the database.  But as I said, things
change.  When business needs say that key can now be alphanumeric, do
you want to change the code?  And when 99% of the time people enter the
key numerically - where's the efficiency in checking it 100% of the
time?

Maybe you can check it after it fails - only if you can give the user a
better hint on what's wrong by doing so.
```
