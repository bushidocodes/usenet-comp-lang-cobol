Hmm. 1986 to 2001, and assuming you were about 20, that makes you currently 35 ?
Reality check - bit of a problem making a 'new career' at 35. Not saying it can't
be done, but be aware of it. What has your career been since then - any
association with DP ?

Materials - best starting point, get Thane Hubbell's 'Teach Yourself COBOL in 24
hours', comes with a free version of Fujitsu on CD. Another alternative, if you
can qualify, is the Student version of Micro Focus Net Express - about $US 100.
(Even if you go the Net Express route - still get Thane's book for syntax
concepts).

Small PC firms may/may not be prepared to take on a 'student' once you show some
savvy. Mainframes -  given larger budgets, they may be able to show some
flexibility and 'train' partially qualified students - just don't know. Whichever
route you go, it wouldn't hurt to be able to take along a 'portfolio' of a
smallish application that you have written - say a Menu, Edit file programs,
Print programs, processing programs etc. Then they know you aren't just B/S-ing !

Not critical - but either Fujitsu or Micro Focus, see if you can get a handle on
Object Orientation. As backup to this, learn Java as a complementary language to
COBOL.

Good luck !

Jimmy, Calgary AB

You may be right as far as a "career" goes, but there is a "skills market"

provided your attitude is right and you are capable of communicating
effectively...)

The main thing is to change the mindset from "a job for life with sickness
benefit, paid holidays and the corporate pension fund" to "sell what I know
for the maximum benefit for all concerned, take responsibility for my own
pension fund, have fun, and take holidays when I want to and not when the
Company says I can.".

That pound or so of slush inside your head is the best survival tool you
will ever have. Nurture it, stimulate it, keep it sharp, and don't sell it
for less than it is worth.

Works for me...<G>
