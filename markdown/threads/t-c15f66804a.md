### Date routines -Zeller's congruence may not be lowest price

Addressed to: Distribution list (see below)

When upgrading a client's common date routine to use four digit years, I
reused some techniques from an earlier project for CPU efficiency in date
routines. Since this routine does date arithmetic (add or subtract days,
weeks, months, or years), I benchmarked it against using the COBOL functions
INTEGER-OF-DATE and DATE-OF-INTEGER to also do the date arithmetic when done
with days. The in-house routine converts the date from yyyymmdd to yyyyddd,
does the addition or subtraction of number of days, loops to adjust the year
if the resulting day isn't in the current year and converts it back to
yyyymmdd. The in-house routine proved faster for adjustment factors of less
than 20 years. This lead me to pursue on my own time the writing of a
routine to get the COBOL day number (day 1 is January 1, 1601). The result
was code that was 4 times as fast (took 1/4 the time). The results raise
various interesting questions. The compiler involved is IBM's COBOL for MVS
and VM release 1.2 running on OS390 release 1.3. From other sources, I
understand that the Language Environment Routine involved uses Zeller's
congruence. The inspiration for the title came from the claim by Zellers, a
Canadian discount department store chain that their law is to have the
lowest price. The following paragraphs deal with the technique I used to
obtain the day number, the reasons that I believe account for the
differences, and some of the management questions this poses. There will be
a separate posting with the topic "COBOL day number program" that contains a
test program and MVS JCL to execute it. The JCL has been modified to
eliminate site specific information. I will be interested in results from
other platforms.

The techniques that I used should be platform independent but do require
that CPU be able to do decimal arithmetic as opposed to converting all
numbers to binary for calculation. This requirement is for efficiency
rather than correctness of result. The routine multiples the current year
by twenty-five to obtain a seven digit leap year work field. The fields are
USAGE DISPLAY PIC 9(4) and PIC 9(7) respectively (character numeric). The
test for leap year is that the low order two digits of leap year work equal
zero and (the low order two digits of year are not equal zero or the low
order four digits of leap year work are equal zero). This requires only one
multiply and no divides. The day of current year day is obtained by adding
the day of month to the cumulative days in the year based on a 12 entry
table (1 per month), and 1 for leap year if the month is greater than 2. The
cumulative days range from 0 for January through 334 for December. One is
then subtracted from the year and 25 from the leap year work. The day
number is then calculated as the number of days = (year * 365) + the high
order 5 digits of the leap year work field - the century portion of the year
+ the high order 3 digits of the leap year work field - 584388 + the days
in the current year day. A similar algorithm was used in a
routine to obtain the COBOL day number from yyyyddd. The testing program is basically a loop that for all dates
within a given range does one of three things, just displays the date area
for selected years, calculates the COBOL day number using INTEGER-OF-DATE
and displays the date area for selected years or calculates the COBOL day
number using my algorithm and displays the date area for selected years.
This was so that I could both debug the routine and obtain relative timing
that would remove the CPU time for the loop. The control records I used
caused the routine to execute one of the three options for all dates in the
years 1900 - 2102 inclusive and display the date work area for years 1900 -
1904, 1989 - 2004, and 2092 - 2102. The CPU times were .64 - .65 seconds
for the do nothing loop 1.31 - 1.34 seconds for the internal routine loop
and 3.48 - 3.50 seconds for the INTEGER-OF-DATE loop.

While I contend that the majority of this difference is explained by the
choice of algorithm, approximately .50 seconds probably is due to
sub-routine call overhead. The COBOL for MVS compiler generates a call to
a common Language Environment subroutine for the calculation. The time
estimate is based on work with the clients common date routine which is
designed so to be used as either a separately called module or a nested
program. The routines that I have seen for calculating a day number require
multiple divides. Division is costly, especially when truncation is
involved. Multiplication also can be expensive. The reverse routine
DATE-OF-INTEGER to calculate yyyymmdd from COBOL day number is even more
expensive. I have cut the time needed for the conversion from ddd to
mmdd by using 11 IF statements in a modified binary search algorithm to
determine the number of days to subtract from the day number and the month
related to the day number.

The management questions are interesting. My algorithm for calculating day
number is demonstrably faster yet I am reluctant to recommend using it. To
be safely used it should be encapsulated in a callable routine (separate
module or nested program). The instructions for its use would have to be
written and stored in a manner that people can find them. At best it adds
a level of site specific obscurity for outsiders working on a program. Use
of INTEGER-OF-DATE is documented in the COBOL manual and is standard (other
than for IBM shops that elected to use the compiler option to generate
Lillian date where day 1 is November 15, 1582). The ratios of CPU time
between the two options are impressive but the difference per execution
is less than 300 micro-seconds on an IBM 9672 R42. Based on other testing
where there was a major effort to reduce the CPU time for a nightly batch
program, substantial reductions in the CPU time for date conversions will
probably have little effect even if 10 percent of the time is spent in the
date routines. On the other hand, it may be worth while to do a similar
exercise for your vendor platform and then pressure the vendor through user
groups and other means to improve the efficiency of the algorithm. It also
shows that choice of technique for solving a problem can dramatically affect
the relative resource cost. Depending on the dollar cost for CPU time,
memory or other resources in question, the effort to be efficient may be
cost effective.
