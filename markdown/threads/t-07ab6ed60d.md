Information Technology Association of America's Story

First, the bad news: The U.S. IT work force shrank by an aggregate of 528,496 workers in 2001, with companies hiring 2.1 million IT workers while laying off about 2.6 million, according to the Information Technology Association of America's http://www.itaa.org third annual IT work force study released Monday. Next, the hopeful news: IT hiring managers are forecasting happy days around the corner, according to the report, with aggregate demand for 1.1 million IT workers in 2002.

If hiring managers live up to the expectations expressed in the report-based on phone interviews with 532 hiring managers--it will mean the IT job market will have risen from the dead-or at least the comatose-with demand for IT professionals increasing 27 percent this year over last. But even that rebound wouldn't put the IT work force back where it was in 2000: It's only 71 percent of the number of IT jobs that needed to be filled just two years ago. 

While demand for IT workers may be on the upswing, hiring managers--as they have in the past--said a significant number of open positions will go unfilled because of a chronic gap between the skills required by hiring organizations and the skills possessed by most applications. In fact, half of those 1.1 million jobs that could be added will go unfilled due to the skills gap, the report said. 

The ITAA, an Arlington, Va., trade group representing IT product and service providers, has historically used this purported gap as ammunition in lobbying for increases in the quota of H-1B visas, which allow hiring organizations to use IT professionals from outside the United States. With the H-1B quota due for consideration before Congress again next year, the ITAA plans to ask for another increase in the quota despite the rough 2001 experienced by many laid-off IT workers. 

"There's lots of individual workers and companies showing pain," granted ITAA President Harris Miller. "That's what the data show. But we don't want to make a mistake and repeat what occurred 12 years ago when this country went through a recession. … The conclusion reached-and it was false-by education and by companies was that we didn't need people with technical skills." 

Besides planning to increase hiring, managers are also turning to other sources for their IT needs. Indeed, the report cited a 17 percent increase in outsourcing in 2001 compared to 2000. And, according to experts, increasingly that outsourcing is coming from offshore providers. According to a separate study by Meta Group Research Fellow Howard Rubin, the United States' share of the estimated 5 million global IT jobs is shrinking. (Meta's numbers don't jibe with the ITAA's because of varying definitions for what constitutes an IT worker. Narrow definitions used by the government, for example, have traditionally not counted positions such as technology support or Web development in counting IT workers, noted a spokeswoman for the ITAA.) 

This country now has about half of the worldwide IT work force, and that work force is growing at a rate of about 10 percent annually. Worldwide IT work force expansion rates, meanwhile, have been growing by up to 20 percent yearly, with countries such as Canada, India and the Philippines experiencing a growth rate two to four times that of the United States. 

As for the type of worker IT managers are looking for, technical support workers won't gain much ground. Companies are predicting they'll hire 281,406 help desk types in 2002. That's up from 218,238 last year, but less than half of the 616,055 that hiring managers said were needed in 2000. Network design and administration skills, meanwhile, will be in big demand with 199,348 hires forecast in the coming year. Programmers and software engineers are the next most sought-after position, with 161,487 anticipated hirings in 2002. 

The ITAA report was based on interviews with hiring managers at 155 IT firms and 377 non-IT firms between Feb. 11 and March 14. It also found that workers at IT firms have suffered disproportionately, with total IT jobs in that sector dropping by 15 percent. IT jobs at non-IT firms dropped by 4 percent. The South, which the largest percentage (3,406,519) of IT workers call home, also shed the largest percentage of workers, at 34 percent, or 181,928 workers.

Very interesting Statistics.

Sadly, this trend is being reflected around the World. 

It isn't just that cheap overseas labour is taking the jobs.

The fact is that the whole Industry is being re-shuffled.

More and more Companies are saying: "Why are we paying a fortune for in-house IT when we are NOT an IT Company?".

The days when the ONLY way to get Corporate IT systems was to write them, are gone.(OK, there are a few remaining outposts of fortress COBOL but, believe me, their days are numbered....)

Whether we, as IT workers, like it or not, the fact remains that most Companies will eventually vote with their feet and go for a "standard" solution like SAP, Chordiant, Siebel or whatever...

Some of the more astute Managers are starting to realise that it is cheaper to Re-Engineer the Business Processes (BPR) so as they conform to a standard solution, than it is to employ IT people to amend the package or write bespoke software to make it comply with the Company's existing processes.

A history of failed delivery and unmet expectations by IT departments has also not helped the perception of IT as a function that simply soaks up money.

The reason that "Web Enablement" solutions are still a lively area of the IT market is because there are no  current "standard" solutions that fit this area. (They are on the way, and it is only a matter of time...)

I would not personally recommend young people to get into IT at the moment. I know that sounds like sacrilege and the perceived wisdom is that IT is the future... it is, but NOT IT as we understand it. I would encourage youngsters to go the Business Administration or Accountancy route. (IT is a part of this, but it is not necessary to make a living from IT).

Management and Business will always be there (there cannot be a Market Place without it, and the Market Place is what drives everything...), IT specialists will be limited to the field of hardware, Networks, and configuring and tuning standard packages.

(Employees of IT Companies will still do a bit of software development, but there will be a very limited demand (compared to the historical demand where EVERYONE needed programmers) for these people, and the remuneration will therefore be lower than has been the case. Especially when they must compete with offshore sweat shops. (We are already seeing the emergence of this trend and it is one of the things that has prompted your post...))

It is ironic to me that I wrote in the last years of the last Century (1994 to be precise):

"History will show that Computer Programming was a phenomenon of the latter half of the 20th Century. By the early part of the 21st Century it was no longer required."

I didn't expect this to become reality before 2010 at the earliest and more likely 2020. And yet the trends are already in evidence and gathering momentum.

I believe a combination of demand from the Market Place, smarter Operating Systems, and more general computer literacy in the population, along with the development of standard businesss solutions (already well under way and growing exponentially every year) will bring this prophecy to fruition.

Write to your Congressman by all means. It won't make a jot of difference.

You cannot buck the Market.

The Market has decided it was paying too much for IT and is making an "adjustment".

It is reminiscent of the situation in 19th Century England when Lace Makers were replaced by machines clever enough to do it (the Lace Makers smugly thought it could never happen, as the knowledge they held was too complex for any machine to ever be able to implement it), or the situation with the Coal industry in 20th Century Britain where it just became uneconomic to mine it, and industry no longer had the same demands for it as had driven the Industrial Revolution. There are many examples...The Steel industry in the USA in the late 20th Century... Why should we ever think that IT is/was immune?

For 60 odd years now computer technology has been infiltrating and changing the way we live. For the most part, it has made our lives better. I long for the day when ANYBODY will be able to sit at a computer and make it do what they want it to do. If that means the end of the Technocracy (including my own career, which has been dedicated to IT for the past 37 years) then I shall figure we did a good job and fade out gracefully... probably still writing OO COBOL components and Java Beans because they fascinate me...<G>)

I arrived back in Europe 5 weeks ago. I have a track record of successful IT Project Management. And yet I found that every job I applied for there were at least 100 applicants. I have been a freelance for the past 25 years and have never been unemployed for more than 2 weeks when I didn't want to be (until now...<G>) The only way I can get work is by calling on people I know (which I have never done in the past...). I have NEVER seen the market so depressed. 

"USA IT workers a thing of the past"?  It ain't just the USA....<G>

Pete.

fields on hand for consultation in the component construction
companies.

familiar with the components from the component companies and will do
the connecting of various components together from their  knowledge of
the users requirements and their domain of expertise.

customer to domain based development for a component house. A shift of
programmers from programming to re-education in a domain so that they
can become component pluggers for customers.

Not too much we can do effectively, other than our government trying to
negotiate a fairer deal - but when the elephant sneezes or flatulates -
we get a big whiff  up here.

I think it was Pete who mentioned the coal industry in the UK - I have
very, very great sympathy for coal workers, who faced a very dangerous
existence - but you just can't keep a non-viable industry going just to
feed folks you feel sorry for. Similarly your own steel industry has
taken a nose dive.

Above may seem off topic but is inter-related. You can no more stop
progression in our field than you can the demise of the coal industry or
the decline of the US steel industry. I mentioned in an earlier message,
large Canadian company had outsourced IT to IBM, and the staff were
switched over to IBM. Follow that up with the big smooth marketing
outfits like SAP - they are going to erode the programming base. I'm
willing to bet you might have to shell out between $200,000 to possibly
$500,000 for their 'product' but as the recipient CEO, that makes more
sense to you than an internal IT department with an annual budge of say
$3 - $5 million, or perhaps more.

So where does that put us - you and me. To quote an old British labour
union expression "F.... You Jack. I'm all right !". I'm into my sunset
years, and although I will regret a decline in a profession I've loved,
it it not going to cripple my remaining existence on Mother Earth.

In your case a deep concern for your working future, and quite rightly.
But a projectionist mindset isn't going to get you anywhere.
International commerce is making a grab - there are many, but two
immediate examples Microsoft, (they've just bought some outfit in Canada
for around $? million), and Wallmart. The later has challenged the
status quo both here in Canada and the UK. (Surprisingly the concept was
not accepted in Germany, where they fell flat on their faces).

For youngsters Pete suggested Admin or Accounting. Certainly a B.Admin
seems to be an attractive route to go, giving you a generalist skill
that you can bend to different commercial needs. Accounting - hmmmm not
so sure. Spent some three years in general accounting before I got into
what we then called EDP, (even ADP !). Interesting but a bit of a drudge
with its monthly timelines to be repetitively met. It can only be
effective for you, the individual, if you become very, very much of a
specialist in a unique area of accounting - say Treasury, where you play
the market, with zillions of dollars as though it was your own version
of Monopoly.

The same pattern applies to IT. Given that you are of reasonable
intelligence, and with some forethought, you can become a proficient
COBOL programmer, (obviously with different levels of competency
depending upon you individual makeup). Working on a mainframe you could
be a whiz at setting up silky smooth JCL sets. In to-day's world, from
your personal angle, that just isn't good enough. Without being cocky we
would all be in agreement that COBOL is the clearest language in which
to fathom business rules. Even if you stick solely with COBOL, you shoud
have an objective of  specializing, getting into a very unique area.
(Example, my next door neighbour, trained as a lab technician, works
very successfully from home with a whole daffy of equipment writing in C
for a company head-quarterd in LA. Specialty graphics associated with
lab results. His HQ is constantly pestering  him to relocate to LA,
which he refuses to do. He's considered an important enough employee to
continue working from Calgary, although he goes down to LA roughly every
4 to 6 weeks, and they pick up the tab for travel and accommodation).

Sometimes specialties are difficult to find, some happily trip over them
by accident, and get into a unique world they would never have gotten
into, following the traditional Human Resources recruiting route. Bu you
can lean towards becoming a specialist - components.

Ahhh ! Says Joe down in Aussieland, more bullshit about OO !  With a
broad statement, "I can do it in structured COBOL". Well, I threw out a
challenge to him - doesn't really matter, but he never responded.
Without question, if you want to survive in IT, you are going to have to
become a specialist and components is a lucrative path to follow, so
that you become a survivalist.

Joe's comments, (paraphrased),  to Pete's latest, "So you buy umpteen
components off the shelf, and now you've got a 100 or a 150 people
customizing them - big deal.". Give him currently the benefit of the
doubt. I would wager, 80% WILL be off the shelf, with possibly some 20%
written in house. We are only at the BEGINNING of components and the 20%
while not totally disappearing, will reduce. Example - you produce
components which create an invoice- does all the good standard stuff,
addressing, product/service information, dollar extensions, totaling
etc. Even takes care of federal, 'local' sales taxes, providing you fit
to a pattern.

Ahhh ! says somebody in Canada, wont work for us, because we have (a)
Federal (b) Provincial and (c) in the case of the have-not provinces on
the East Coast - HST (Harmonized Sales Tax) - where the feds collect a
'total' tax and return a portion to the have-nots. Agreed - it wont work
- so somebody up in the Great White North writes a component module to
cover this specific instance. Now they sell it to other Canadian
companies.

A further aspect of componentizing is specialization to an industry -
this is *currently happening* in the Lower 48, where the various States
have similar but different vehicle licence/registration systems. They
are working on swapping components that fit all, where appropriate.
Carry that same concept through to airline reservation systems, hospital
administration, tutorial administration etc., to name a few.

Before you ask - I haven't a bloody clue as yet what componentizing is
about. I understand the abstract concept and where it can lead, but
being a meat and potatoes man, I just have to see it backed up by hard
solid code - and please, in COBOL for preference. I know I can reference
VB and Java texts about components, but I'm sure that much of it can be
done in COBOL - which is exactly what Pete Dashwood is doing with F/J
POWERCOBOL. Having triggered this as a topic elsewhere, I've had some
initial responses - they haven't helped me much. I really want to
doggedly take a crack at this one, starting out with a very clear
definition of what a component is. One person responded, and I'm being
facetious about his reply, "A component is whatever you want it to be,
providing it is loose".  Mr. Meat and Potatoes doesn't like that one - I
want to get down to the real nitty gritty.
