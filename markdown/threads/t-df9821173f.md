```
This won't work without at least one space between the full stop and the
pseudo-text delimiter, as in my previous followup.

In the copybook, STANDARD. is two text-words, STANDARD and a separator
period.  In the REPLACING phrase, STANDARD.== is only one text-word.  The
full stop is part of the character string STANDARD. because a space does
not follow it, so there will be no match.

However, STANDARD. == is two text-words, STANDARD and a separator period,
that can match the two text-words in the copybook.
```

```
I don't think it's possible for REPLACING to convert a statement to a
comment.  Certainly my compilers can't do it; I strip comments in the
tokenizer, long before REPLACING becomes active.

This is not actually a problem with COPY REPLACING; the real problem
comes earlier, namely, it's a misunderstanding of the COPY feature to put
an 01-level in a copybook.

CoBOL is the original database.  Copybooks are the DDL.  You should
describe the data exactly once, then copy it in at whatever level you
need it under whatever name you need it, whether it be in the FILE
SECTION, the WORKING-STORAGE SECTION, the LINKAGE SECTION, or whatever. 

Putting the name in the copybook is an example of binding too early,
which reduces your flexibility.  So the best way to solve this problem is
to take both the FD and the 01-level out of the copybooks.

If it's politically impossible to fix the copybooks, you can use the
REPLACING to convert the FD to a blank line instead of to a comment.  I
don't have access to your platform today, but I just tested this on VAX
CoBOL:

  COPY "FDLIB01.LIB" REPLACING ==FD Some-File-Name. == BY == ==.
```
