I must politely disagree. I don't think that you can infer that COBOL
has been encroached upon simply because there are a lot more languages
now than in the 1960's. Consider this..........

In the 60's many small to medium sized business didn't even own
computing equipment (other than adding machines). Also consider the
fact that having a computer in the home at that time was something
that you could only find on a science fiction novel.

There are a lot more computers running today than there were in the
1960's. More computers means more programs running. This increase in
the volume of machines running and software programs in use does not
suggest that the number of COBOL programs has decreased nor does it
suggest that COBOL has declined in usage.

The **only** thing that can be inferred from a larger number of
computers running is that the total market for software and hardware
products has increased, not that any given language has fallen from
favor. A much larger market for hardware and software products could
easily result in an increase in the variety of languages (solutions)
to help fill the huge increase in demand.

If COBOL has a smaller percentage of total market usage than it did in
the 60's, it's probably because the total market for software has
grown at an incredible rate over the past 30 years.

In other words, if in the 1960's, you maintained 10 COBOL programs and
only 100 programs are in use world wide, then you are maintaining 10%
of the world's programs.

If 30 years later, you maintain 20 COBOL programs, but their are 1000
programs in use worldwide, then you are maintaining 100% more than you
were 30 years earlier, but you are also maintaining only 2% of the
world's programs now.

Steve......the pie has gotten a lot bigger since the 1960's. The
industry has grown at an impressive rate. More buyers mean more
languages entering the marketplace, not the decline of any one
language. That's all we can really infer unless we go out and conduct
some sort of survey.

Sentry Market Research did conduct a survey in 1995/1996. They asked
programmers which languages they were using for client/server
development. Visual Basic edged COBOL by a whisker and came in first
with 23% of the respondents saying that they used VB. COBOL came in
second with 21%. Here's the rest of the pack:

C++, 3rd place (18%)
C, 4th place (15%)
4GL's, 5th place, (15%)
Smalltalk, 6th place (3%)

Although it is merely my personal speculation, I would venture to say
that COBOL has even gained in popularity over the past year or two.

Cobol has NEVER been popular. I've never claimed that it is or was
popular. I've only stated with facts, references, even dated
publication issues complete with page numbers that Cobol is the most
used language and is aggressively being used today in new applications
on all corporate used platforms, and why it is being used.

In 1960, teachers used carbon paper and typewriters to make up tests.
In the 1970, programmers used keypunch machines, and slide-rules. In
1980, I used a mainframe text-writer to do my word processing (very
messy, and no spellchecker!).

Computerization has proliferated at an amazing rate. Yes, Cobol has
been encroached by languages in distributed and de-centralized areas
because there was a need to move users closer to office desktop tools
and away from terminal based interfaces. And there is a heck of a lot
of programming being done in these areas. But Cobol's forte is
business data processing, and that hasn't changed. Behind these
distributed and de-centralized areas is still a focal point to
encompass most or all of this captured data. To manipulate it, to
process it, to turn that data into all kinds of information. And this
is where Cobol code can be found. Because of the proliferation of
computerization, and even the other languages, this has created more
application development for Cobol.

A perfect example of this is at an IS shop that is less than a mile
from my house. Ten years ago, this brokerage firm was probably 95%
Cobol. In the meantime, it aggressively moved to c/s and using C, and
is now Sun Microsystems 4th largest customer. But, Cobol still makes
up roughly 70% of that shops applications.
