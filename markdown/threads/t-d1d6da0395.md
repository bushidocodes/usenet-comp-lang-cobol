Stephen,
I have never seen any numbers and trends/etc but I'm sure some
company which markets the non-Cobol point of view has some numbers.
Most likely showing the downward trend in support of their product.

You do make a very good point though I don't know if you intended to
do so. The schools guessing at what to teach. 15 years ago I was
told Cobol was dead and Pascal was the wave of the future. I took two
semesters of Cobol on what I dare say was somewhat entertained as a
mainframe - IBM S/34 (monitors!) and a S/3 (used the 96 column punch
cards) at a small junior college. When I moved to the big university,
we had a PDP/1170 and eventually a Vax! Both schools taught Pascal as
our savior. I have yet to see a production Pascal program.

One important item we continually discussed at both schools - our
teachers were teaching us about a business in which they did not
currently work. My wife is taking an intro to DP course at a large
college and it's a joke....very similar to the one I had many years
ago. My biggest complaint with the schools, to date I use 10% or less
of what I learned in college. The best course I had was advanced
Cobol - taught at night by a local senior programmer which used it
daily in his job.

A final thought, after company XYZ dumps $$$ into upgrading their old
legacy Cobol systems for Y2K, do you think they'll drop it like a rock
and spend the dollars in converting to C++/client server immediately
after Jan 2000? I've been interviewing heavily lately for Cobol
positions and not one has been for Y2K. They have been scratch
development or enhancement projects.

I'd be interested in seeing other's responses.

Cheers,
Kevin

Programmer Dude:

It depends on what you mean by "Delphi works better than embedded SQL
statements in Cobol". If you mean it is easier because Delphi provides
an "SQL Painter", like a GUI painter for the forms/screens, this only
makes it easier--not better. Coding native SQL for many things is not
too terribly difficult. Perhaps some Microfocus folks/users out their
can tell us what Microfocus Cobol provides as an "SQL painter". A huge
misconception can be made about a product if you don't separate the
"development environment" from the "language". Delphi is a development
environment; Cobol and Pascal are not. But Delphi does provide a good
GUI development environment for example. But from the perspective of a
good business programming language, I do not believe Pascal is a good
replacement for Cobol. Cobol provides a variety of "development" tools,
too.

The only point I was trying to make was how the entire industry is
becoming fragmented with a proliferation of the latest "new and
improved". People use to do pilots on new products--too many today just
believe everything they hear or read in a magazine and take it for fact.
I don't know how many times I have heard about a gung-ho project going
bust in the middle or towards the end because the "latest and greatest"
couldn't do something. Another point I was also trying to make is that
this situation has contributed severly in delaying and addressing the
Y2k issue.

Delphi may be making in-roads into prodcution, but are they
mission-critical systems? Are they very large systems? According to a
survey of I saw from Computerworld, Delphi nor Pascal was listed as one
of the top 5 in demand for 1997. HTML, C++, C, Java and Cobol were.
The most disturbing thing I noticed was that Cobol was not listed #1.
With the Y2K problem, it only verifies mine, as well as many others,
that this problem is still being ignored. Only in the West North
Central Region was Cobol listed #2 as in most demand (skills).

I wasn't trying to insult Delphi. Borland produces fine products. But,
as also read at one time, a major effort to try to bring Borland back to
profitability is throught the Delphi product--thus my remark about
"re-packaged Pascal".

Mike Dodas

Luis:

Thanks for you thoughts on this. I'm sure Pascal has advanced since its
early days. Certainly the OO capabilities and the development
environment which has been wrapped around Pascal (the Delphi product) is
far different than its predecesors. But I still believe my original
comments about unseating established markets to gain in-roads to a
market. And while this new Pascal is different from earlier Pascal's,
it is still Pascal. I don't think too many would argue that Delphi is a
major product for Borland. It may also be a way for Borland to
introduce the Pascal language into mainstream business applications (in
a significant way) for the first time. I personally do not believe
Pascal is designed as a good business language replacement for Cobol,
however.

The interesting thing is that Cobol has established new standards for
object-orientation also. Some Cobol vendors have already implemented
them. But you know what, it's still Cobol and is not claiming to be
anything else. The '97 standard, if compared to say the '68 standard,
would look very, very different if you take into consideration all of
the features and changes over a 30-year period. Cobol has always been
the recognized programming language for business applications. It also
has evolved to protect the investment in older applications. I doubt
most other languages can reliably claim this.

This is my opinion and I am not inviting a debate. I am sure you may
feel completely different on this subject. I know very well how
sensitive this "language debate" issues are.

I first learned COBOL in 1967 (30 years ago) and also have taught it for
many years at a local college in the evening. I will both echo Kevin's
comments on the quality of many instructors and the lack of Pascal in the
"real world". But I would also like to add a few statistics about use of
COBOL which may be helpful. In the division of the company in which I
work, we have had excellent control over COBOL source code for over 25
years. As part of the year2000 assessment we are making an effort to
obsolete whatever is no longer running. The overall statistics are as
follows:
No longer running - 25%
Not impacted by year 2000 - 10-15%
Definitely impacted (has date LOGIC) - 10-15%
Possibly impacted - 50% (has dates, but no logic, therefore would be
impacted if we expanded to 4 digit years, but not otherwise)
On a base of over 2000 programs when only 500 have been obsoleted during
the past 25 years, it seems pretty obvious to me that it will take a long
time for the remaining 1500 to "disappear". In the meantime, while we may
not be doing much "new" development in COBOL, there is certainly enough
enhancement work to keep several people busy for many years to come.

I wouldn't throw away my COBOL manual or skills for a while yet!
