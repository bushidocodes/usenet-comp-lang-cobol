Not seeing all of this thread, I just wanted to make certain that everyone
knows

A) ANSI/ISO *has* enhanced ACCEPT FROM DAY/DATE by adding the (fully
compatible with previous COBOL Standards)
  -  YYYYMMDD
  -  YYYYDDD

keywords for requesting 4 rather than 2 digit year - while their omission
works as this statement always has.

B) This was a "quite stable" part of the 2002 Standard that many (I think
most - but not all) vendors adopted before the year 2000 - therefore, it has
been commonly available for close to 5 years now - depending upon the
compiler vendor.

C) The intrinsic function CURRENT-DATE became part of Standard COBOL in 1989
(which CERTAINLY should have been plenty of time for anyone wanting to use
"Standard COBOL" for doing Y2K remediation)

D) The rules for ACCEPT FROM DAY/DATE do (and as far as I know have always)
been defined as working "as if" a MOVE were done from a 6 or 5 (now possibly
8 or 7) digit numeric integer to a numeric receiving item.  This means that
they were WELL (and are still well) defined for cases where the receiving
item is *NOT* 6 or 5 (or 8 or 7) digits long.  This means that any "attempt"
at changing the meaning of this statement DEPENDENT upon the size of the
receiving item would CERTAINLY have broken programs that relied on well
defined ANSI/ISO conforming compiler behavior (where truncation and padding
worked "as expected" by knowledgeable programmers).

  ***

I hope that this thread doesn't turn out to be like  a previous thread where
one person was unable to understand how standard conforming COBOL works (and
has always worked) and got into a discussion of how he WANTED it to work -
thinking that this matters for existing COBOL programs - or language
definitions.
