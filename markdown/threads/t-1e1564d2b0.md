```
Seriously, the 19->20 rollover is a serious problem. One that

Report sorting is only one part of the problem, and a small one --
as it may affect the ordering of the output, but not it's basic

serious.

But there are many processing rules which will give unexpected
results. For example, your younger sister turns 16 on 02Jan2000
and trots down to DMV to get her driver's license. The program
checks, and --she doesn't qualify, because she's not old enough.
Subtract the DOB (84002) from today (00002) and it's not > 16!

What happens when that negative number is used to calculate the
interest due on your purchase at "Moe's house of rip-off electronics"?
There are already stories of 105 year old men being invited to
sign up for kindergarden.

A lot of the problems will be very subtle. For example, LOTS of
programmers know of the problem, and explicitly coded to work
around it. So there is code of the following form:

Make-YYMMDD-into-YYYYMMDD.

move YYMMDD to YYYYMMDD (3:6)
if YYMMDD(1:2) < "90"
move "20" to YYMMDD (1:2)
else
move "19" to YYMMDD (1:2)
end-if
.

Unfortunately, you chose 1990 as the switch point in this HRS system
you just wrote, while the payroll -- written in 1982 -- uses 1978.
Comes time to pay your 401(k) loan and the two systems don't
interpret the data the same way. Will yield interesting results...
```

```
I still think that grasshopper has a basically reasonable point. Right now
there is a lot of software that is using a 2-digit year. This two digit
year is used to represent years in the range 1900-1999. Now of course
there are problems even now (what about bonds maturing next century etc),
but in practice, it is workable for some range of applications to use a
limited year range.

If it is practical to use a limited year range representing a hundred year
span, then it is perfectly possible to use the same two digits to represent
a hundred year span that is some other hundred year span, say 1930-2029.
Basically what we have is a mod 100 representation of the year, and we have
to arbitrarily decide what principle value to take.

To "fix" any program with the date problem, we have to locate all accesses
to the date field and at least think about them.

Yes, we can change to 4 digits (or perhaps 3 digits, 4 digits really seems
like overkill to me), but we can also just shift the interpretation of the
2-digit field.

Yes, you can complain that there are some problems of not being able to
represent dates outside your chosen principle range, but these problems
are obviously from a mathematical point of view isomorphic to the problems
that exist in this century with the principle interpretation of 1900-1999.

There may be a number of situations in which this is the preferable way of
handling a given date field, because it may mean avoiding converting
existing data files (which can as we all know be much more of a nightmare
than converting programs).

Suppose for example we have a payroll program, where the dates are

year started working
year expected retirement

this is a perfect case where you can change your view of the existing data
to move the principle range, since it is unlikely you have any employees
who started working before 1900. Indeed, this is an example where you can
build into the program a rolling interpretation.

Fundamentally in this scheme we are storing years that represent the
date:

n * 100 + yy

where yy is the stored low order value, and n is implied. The program now
assumes that n is always 19, but what you need to ask is whether for a
particular field it is in fact possible to deduce n from other considerations.
For example, in the case of a date that represents the year that someone
started working, you choose the maximum value of n suh that the resultant
date is not later than the current year.
```

```
If you wnat to fix up arithmetic on dates, don't make the 50 year assumption,
that will just mean that your legacy code will blow up 50 years later.
Instead do arithmetic on such dates mod 100 when computing such things
as ages. You will still invite 105-year olds to Kindergarten, but that
is inevitable if you only store 2 decimal digits for a birth date!

This will make those arithmetic calculations last for ever.

Of course comparisons are another matter. However, in a particular
situation you may be able to similarly make the operations independent
of century. FOr example, if you are comparing the date of someones
first purchase of a house, and their date of marriage, again you know
that the difference cannot be more than 100 years, so the comparison
can be done in a general way that is independent of date.

What you need to do in general is to raise all these operations to a
higher level of abstraction. THe lack of user defined operators and
convenient user defined functions in COBOL makes this a little awkward
syntactically, but that'
s the proper approach in my view.
```

```
Our particular product uses CCYYMMDD format for benefits, it is a Human
Resources product, and YYMMDD for payroll. This presents a sticky problem
since most benefits are withheld during payroll. Since we cannot expand the
database, one of the problems with vendor supplied software, we have use one
of our in-house developed routines. This was derived after we attended a
COBOL/370 course. Try the following, in your initialization:
*** Note: This example assumes you use COBOL/II
ACCEPT ws-date from date.
ADD 20 to date-yy
giving cent-window-bottom.
* This gives you an 80 year window in which to work.
add 01 to cent-window-bottom
giving cent-window-top.

Then, in your date comparison routines, try this:

EVALUATE ip-date-yy
WHEN cent-window-bottom thru 99
move '19' to ip-date-cc
when 00 thru cent-window-top
move '20' to ip-date-cc
END-EVALUATE.

I believe this works in most cases. It has for us. As an example:

Today's date: 02/04/95
yy +20 = 15 = cent window-bottom
+1 = 16

ergo: 15 - 99 = CC19
00 - 16 = CC20
```

```
Another scheme for handling the century change without discombobulating
file formats is to encode. Suppose that you are using two packed digits
to represent the year, you can say:

00000000 (binary) = 1900
10011001 (binary) = 1999
11nnnnnn (binary) = 2000 + nn

this encoding scheme gives 63 additional years without changing file formats,
you just extend the set of data that can be in the file leaving the old data
as an accurate subset.

Of course all access to the dates must be abstracted and filtered through
routines that understand this coding scheme.
```
