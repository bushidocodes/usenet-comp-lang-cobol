This depends on how COBOL grows with new demands and technology. It 
should be THE Common Business-Oriented Language "by definition". So it 
should now have assimilated all the fourth- and fifth-generation 
principles required for business. This has happened in some areas, such 
as OO (though done at too technical and basic a level and without much 
thought for business) and in its fourth-generation data-oriented 
features. Unfortunately, there are some who seek to extend the language 
as though it were a purely third-generation language, so that many of the 
new features proposed are just a wish-list of what COBOL might have had 
in the 60s and 70s. For example, the new error recovery feature is 
unstructured even though structured techniques were prevalent long before 
the last standard.

```
gilbert@reaper.bean-net.com wrote:
> What will the future in Cobol be like after the Y2k problem is absorbed/solved?
> Will there still be jobs for Cobol programmers? In short, is the future bright
> for cobol or are we approaching the sunset days of Cobol?
> Gilbert

Here are a few guesses, just to get the discussion flowing:

Right now and for the short term, the demand for COBOL programmers is
declining, since all the Y2K projects have begun and many are wrapping
up. (And new COBOL projects are being postponed until after the crisis
has passed.)

There will be an uptick after the first of the year as postponed
projects begin, but much of the programmer supply will come from Y2K
projects wrapping up.

In the medium run, the demand will be slightly reduced from pre-Y2K days
due to the fact that Y2K precipitated the conversion of some COBOL
systems to other languages.


In the longer run:

--the demand for mainframe COBOL programmers will increase, albeit as
slowly as pre-Y2K, since the large mainframe systems that survived Y2K
aren't going away.

--demand for PC/Unix COBOL programmers may grow significantly. Many of
the Y2K conversions were from mainframe COBOL to PC-based COBOL. The
success of such projects may help to confirm the wisdom of using COBOL
on the newer platforms. 


Any significant growth in demand will come from the PC/Unix platforms.
Success here will depend on several factors:

--Vendor support. Micro-Focus seems to be holding its own and growing.
IBM seems to be hanging in there. The advent of the Fujitsu compiler is
a very good sign. Syncronix's PerCOBOL (COBOL/Java) is an even better
sign. Computer Associates seems to be the one vendor that is dropping
the ball (with their Realia products).

--Academic support for business systems. In the past, new programmers
have come from computer science departments, which tend to be adjuncts
of math departments. COBOL's word-based symbols don't look like math, so
academicians have tended to put it down. (Academicians tend to put down
that which they don't understand.) 

--Alternatives (i.e. competition from other languages). Complex business
systems development will gravitate toward a language that is
self-documenting, is highly structurable, and allows the easy hiding of
complexity. This is not C++. This is probably not Java. This could be
COBOL. Or a variant of COBOL. (If a good open-source COBOL ever gains
recognition, COBOL may rapidly mutate into variants.) Or it could be a
new language entirely. 

So what do *you* think?

--John Hicks
```
