I worked for companies for around 10 years, then contracted for 30.

For me, contracting was better.

Everybody's different and there is no right or wrong, just what you feel
comfortable with.

The famine and feast part is often up to the individual. In 20 years of
contracting I was never out of work for more than 10 days, unless I wanted
to be. After the computer boom period of the late 70s/early 80s things
slowed down a bit as client/server systems slowly replaced centralised
mainframes. As I was primarily working in mainframe environments, it did
take longer to get a job.  It still never took more than a few weeks. I
addressed this by learning new skills and moving away from purely technical
work into more management oriented work. (People management requires a
different skill set from technical management and there is a learning curve
involved.)

As for satisfaction, there is just as much satisfaction in seeing a 6 month
project completed successfully as there is in marking another year with the
corporation and getting a raise.

Contracting clears the mind. You are not concerned with the internicine
politics of career-building, just getting the job done.

I found that moving into many different environments was educational and
interesting. It opens up new vistas and prevents you ascribing to the belief
that there is only the 'IBM way' or the 'Univac way' or whatever...

Doing it in different cultures and countries was good for my personal
development and I have never regretted moving to a contract lifestyle.

The pitfalls that Robert and Doc have outlined are certainly real and there
can be problems with agents and clients. But solving them is good for you...
:-) You soon learn which ones can be trusted and which ones can't. I work
through no more than 3 agents in Europe and know the people concerned
personally. I also contract direct and this is often a better solution for
all concerned.

I guess the main reason it appeals to me is because it means I can work when
I WANT to instead of when I HAVE to. I have been able to spend the last
couple of years doing whatever I like and I could never have done that if I
was a corporate citizen.

I look back over my contract career with great satisfaction. It afforded me
a lifestyle that I could probably not have had easily from anything else.

But, it isn't for everybody. If you have kids and a mortgage or are not
mobile, it becomes more difficult (although I know people who still manage
it.)

There is little security for any of us apart from what is between our ears.
Companies go broke, people who have dedicated decades of service to the
corporation are shafted when it suits the company, (I have seen this happen
to good people), so there really isn't much of an argument for the
'security' of permanent employment.

Diversity is a good thing. Usually it seems that the people who SHOULD be
contractors, are, and the ones who shouldn't, aren't. It sorts itself out.

And it isn't wrong for either school.

It's not always a free choice in my neck of the woods (greater Toronto 
area).  Some clients now *require* contractors to work through an agency 
that is on their 'preferred suppliers list'.  The 'preferred suppliers' have 
pre-negotiated fixed rates with the client, which gives the client some cost 
certainty.  This applies even if the contractor is incorporated.

Aside from cost certainty, there are two other justifications cited for this 
rule:

1.  By interposing a third party between the client and the contractor, it 
reinforces the fact that the contractor is *not* an employee of the client.
2.  All of the client's financial dealings are with the third party, which 
is a 'trusted supplier'.  Trusted, that is, to collect the GST (Goods and 
Services Tax) required by Canadian law *and* to remit it to the Government.

Whatever the reasons, one thing that I have learned after 15 years 
contracting is that you have to play the game by their rules if you want to 
stay in the consulting business.

Sometimes the system works to your advantage.  I found the job I wanted, and 
then asked the client for a list of  their 'preferred vendors'.  I then 
worked my way through the list until I found the agency that could offer me 
the best deal.

'Best deal' for me, is a combination of rate and *service*.  I turned down 
one agency that offered a slightly higher rate because they couldn't 
guarantee that I would be paid on time.  "You get paid when we get paid" was 
their motto.  Their contractors are paid several weeks after the invoice 
date and they never know if the cheque is going to bounce or not.
