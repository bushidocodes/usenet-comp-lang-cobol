wrote:

>I suppose it inevitable that, every year or so, someone must clarify the
difference 
>between "structured programming" and "modular programming."

Any attempt to do structured programming without paying attention to
modularity
is likely to be a failure.    In COBOL, this fails because structured
programming
has been so dependent on Perform statements, traditionally implemented on 
modules that refer to global data.    

 >So Richard Riehle, Pete, Jimmy, and others, here is the clarification. 
>Structured programming refers to the arrangement of statements in a
procedure 
>and uses words like: sequence, choice, and iteration, to describe the
structure 
>of the procedure. 

I am quite familiar with this notion of fundamental structures.   In fact,
my original
argument was that these were inadequate prior to ANSI-85 because there was
no
support for elementary structures.   A sequence has a sequence terminator.
The period
in COBOL was primitive support that that.   A selection statement did not
have a 
terminator that permitted arrangement of well-scoped if statements.    An
iteration could
only be formed using a perform of a module.    

>Modular programming refers to the decomposition of systems 
>and uses words like: cohesion, coupling, encapsulation, and interface, to 
>describe the relationship between separate procedures. Procedural
decomposition (PD) 
>and object-orientation (OO) are types of modular programming. Both PD
modules 
>and OO methods may consist of "structured" code (or they may be spaghetti).


Sorry, must respectfully disagree.   You are certainly correct that the
original meaning of
structured programming was founded on the work of Jacopini and Bohm along
with the
famous paper by Dijkstra.   I have read the literature.   We simply have a
different 
interpretation of it.   The literature, and the theory of structured
programming, did not
stop with the pronouncements of the early writers.   

>There is no comparison or contrast between "structured programming" and
"object-oriented 
>programming". They occur at different levels. Pete, OOP will never replace
"structured"; 
>though it may replace PD. ;-) 

It is true that elementary structures are essential to well-formed programs.
An elementary 
structure has an entry point and a scope.    COBOL structures, prior to
ANSI-85, did not 
sufficiently satisfy this requirement.     In OOP, both elementary
structures and modularity
are essential.    The kind of modules (classes) are different, but they are
modules.

>Richard, what constitutes structured programming is well-defined and your
description 
>is so broad as to be incorrect. 

And yours is too narrow to be useful.  

>See, Youdon, "Techniques of Program Structure and 
>Design," Prentice-Hall, 1973, for more complete descriptions of both
modular programming 
>and structured programming. 

Structured programming requires modular programming.    They are so closely
intermixed, for
any kind of production code that only toy programs could be written without
considering them
both in the same way.    You may want to believe that one can do structured
programming without
well-formed modules, but that would not get you very far in writing
structured programs.   

>You might choose to re-read the chapters on "Modularity" in 
>"Structured Design" with the idea that it is a discussion of modular
programming; 
>not structured programming. ;-) 

There is a very good reason why this set of chapters is in the referenced
book.   Structured
Design requires modularity.     Even if you want to assert that structured
programming does
not require modularity,  it is difficult to imagine doing structured design
without it.   And
modularity requires some understanding of the scope, visibility, protection,
and interfaces
of modules.   

We have come a long way from the "two elementary formations" of Jacopini and
Bohm.   In fact,
a strict interpretation of their paper would suggest that  the early Yourdon
statements were too
extreme.   A technology does not stand still.     A definition of structured
programming that fails
to acknowledge the integral importance of modularity, is a definition that
falls short of its intention. 

Richard Riehle

the knowledge of that discipline. The definition of those words
and the meaning of those phrases are discussed and refined

the reason! ;-)
