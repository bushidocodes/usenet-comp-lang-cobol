My 2c worth:

My suggestion would be that you go for a structured language like
Cobol. Now this is a very emotional issue in the programming world.
There are many fellows who regard Cobol as outmoded. BUT, as a
language with which to make first contact with the programming world,
it has some very definite plus points. It has avery strict set of
rules and avery strict format for the whole program, which tends to
set good standards all round. Cobol will serve to teach you most of
the principles involved in programming.

You can convert easily from Cobol to any other language, but to
convert from any other language to any other language is not so easy.

OOP is a buzzword which has since proven to have been slightly
overrated.

Go and have a look at your local Computer Publications and look at the
job market. I am willing to bet that you will get few opportunities
for "OOP programmers" (C and C++). I personally think C++ will be
very difficult if you do not have a very good grounding in a language
like Cobol.

BUT beware, learning Cobol by itself can also be a very empty
experience. Programming does not consist of the language alone. I
cannot teach you Spannish and then expect you to be an award winning
and publishing Spannish poet. 70% of programming lies in sound
program design and the other 30% is merely fitting the design to the
language - translating the design into coding.

Try and find ex-South Africans in NZ and ask where they were trained:
if it was either Van Zyl & Pritcahrd or Luso, you can ask them to
help you a bit with program design and with further advice.

As to which 'one' of these languages you should take (and, therefore, which
one you should skip), there is an ongoing argument between learning the
Next-Big-Thing (e.g., Java), or learning the Dying-Even-As-We-Speak thing
(e.g., COBOL). The example of note is, COBOL has been dying for 20 years
(or maybe 30), and there is more of a shortage for COBOL coders (and hence
higher rates) than ever before. Java, and other OOL's, have some very
attractive features. The problem is that you don't know which one will
stay popular (I'll skip the list of dead bodies...). So, if employment
prospects are what you are looking for, you probably want COBOL.

I don't think either one is simpler, in general, to learn (some people like
one, some the other). I'm personally a strong believer that you probably
should learn at least three or four languages, including assembler, so you
can see what is universal, and what is syntax. That is a luxury most
people don't seem to have, now, but it's a common characteristic of all the
most skilled programmers, in any language, that I know.
