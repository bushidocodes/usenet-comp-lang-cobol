```
I'm not a big time user of COBOL direct file processing, but from a
study I made several years (and several COBOL compilers) ago, I have
long believed that using VSAM from a COBOL program was less efficient
than writing the I/O in Assembler.  I'm wondering if (1) this is true,
and (2) if any improvements in the COBOL to VSAM interfaces have
occurred in modern IBM COBOL implementations.

History:
We were trying to decide what types of files or data bases to use to
manage a large volume of financial records.  The records were keyed by
account number and other information.  A given record, once created,
would be in the system for a long time (government contracts).  Each
record would have a variable number of "elements of cost" such as labor,
material, reproduction costs, travel, etc. up to about 50 unique
categories.

We looked at IMS, at DB2, at VSAM, and at fixed and variable length
sequential data sets.  (Variable length sequential won.)

As I recall, we simulated an expected use of VSAM, where 90%+ of the
records being processed would already exist in the "master file", and
the transactions ran about 25% of the number of records in the VSAM
file.  This resulted in the application program reading a record,
updating several of the amounts in the array of "cost elements", and
occasionally adding a new "cost element".  The record would have to be
updated in the VSAM file, of course.  In COBOL, as I recall, each time
the record got updated, the block it was in got written to disk, and
read back in.  In Assembler, I could control this activity, and the
result was about a 90% reduction in EXCP's (I/O activity).

If the program updated 10 records in a block (Control Interval?), the
COBOL version would do 22 I/O's, and the Assembler version 2 I/O's.

I asked a contractor who was working at our place at the time, but who
had recently worked in a shop that did more VSAM, whether they used an
Assembler Language interface to access the VSAM data, and she said she
thought maybe they did.
=====
Any info you could provide would be appreciated!
```

```
Depends upon the hardware a good bit; if you are running on anything like modern hardware, the efficiency between COBOL and
Assembler is a lot less important than if you are running on a 10+ year old machine.

Given that, if you know your business processes very well, you can always write a much more efficient program in Assembler than in
COBOL, and it will be far faster to use VSAM than a database. The trade off is that the system will be more costly to maintain in
terms of human time, and less flexible than a system written in COBOL, and far less flexible than a system written using a database.

To wit, I know of (and work with) a 30 year old system written in assembler that does its job far faster - on a 20 year old Wang VS
machine - than comparable systems perform on a modern machine which is awash with spare MIPS. It runs a life insurance daily cycle
in minutes, and virtually all queries into the system are sub second. That means if you call the company, they will have your policy
on screen as fast as they can type in your policy number, with current loan values calculated and all that kind of good stuff. By
comparison, other companies often take minutes just to get that information on screen, and then they cannot tell you the current
values and balances on the policy. (*sigh*)

The tradeoff of course, is that the system is much harder to expand or modify; but that isn't a problem with the current system
because it does what it was designed to do and new capabilities are generally not required. It is an example of a very well designed
application for a mainframe system.

Basically, you should not even be worrying about the implementation details of your project until you have all the business details
worked out in fine detail. At that point, the implementation details often take care of themselves. (Yes, that sounds like a brush
off, but it is true. If the details turn out to be basically batch oriented, then a VSAM solution is almost a no brainer; if
primarily interactive, then a DB/2 solution might be a better choice. If it has to be implemented under UNIX or on a Mainframe or
within a certain period of time, all those factors play a big part.)

What you might want to do is find a good software engineer in your area and pick his or her brains for a couple weeks. Software
engineers (like yours implemented) are usually very good at doing exactly that kind of activity, both by training and by
temperament.

-Paul
```

```
I've just been involved in migrating a shop from VSE to z/OS
1.1. They had their system tuned for the way VSE implemented
VSAM and so they ran quite fast (in fact, they incurred
increased elapsed times for some batch jobs).

What we did to solve this was to use BLSR (you code this in
the JCL). We also installed a ISV product and gave it tuning
parameters. The end result was a lowered number of I/Os and
a certain amount of buffering that the COBOL programs would
not otherwise do.

Note that with variable length records and KSDS you will be
seeing lots of CA/CI splits unless you have built your files
with plenty of free space. Even then, you will have to do
re-orgs based on how often updates are done where records
expand in size -- if you need good response time.
```

```
    "1.10.7 Improving VSAM performance"
at

http://publibz.boulder.ibm.com/cgi-bin/bookmgr_OS390/BOOKS/igy3pg10/1.10.7

It certainly does give SOME guidance on "tuning" VSAM applications in COBOL.
As indicated, some of the "defaults" (like BUFNI) are *not* ideal for
performance with many applications.  From what I have heard, it is ALWAYS
possible to create some sort of Assembler "tuned" application that performs
better than a "simple" COBOL application.  On the other hand, current
optimization and hardware/software improvements makes me think that you
would find "sufficient" VSAM performance with "good tuning".

P.S.  I also suggest that you look at informational APAR II13288 - which
addresses a change in CI-size introduced with z/OS V1R3.  This may change
performance for MANY existing and new applications (COBOL or otherwise)
unless appropriate action is taken.
```
