program that will loop calculating COBOL day numbers (day 1 = January
1,
1601) for all days in a year range specified on a control record. On
an OS390 system using COBOL for MVS and VM the code below takes less
than half the time of FUNCTION INTEGER-OF-DATE.   The calculation in
the loop is either null for getting the timing for looping through the
range, FUNCTION INTEGER-OF-DATE or the following code which assumes
years divisible by 4000 are not leap years. The 100 and 400 year rules
are followed.  This calculation does only 3 multiplies including the
one for the subscript and no division.  The full program is available
on request.  
 

COMPUTE WS-WORK-DAY = WORK-GREG-DD OF DATEWORK
                       + WS-CUMULATIVE-DAYS (WORK-GREG-MM OF DATEWORK)
COMPUTE WS-LEAP-WORK-UNPACKED =
                  WORK-GREG-NUM-CCYY OF DATEWORK * 25
IF WORK-GREG-MM OF DATEWORK > '02'
        AND WS-LEAP-CHK = '00'
        AND (WORK-GREG-YY OF DATEWORK NOT = '00'
            OR WS-LEAP-CENTURY-CHK = '00')
     ADD 1 TO WS-WORK-DAY
END-IF
COMPUTE WS-YEAR-WORK = WORK-GREG-NUM-CCYY OF DATEWORK - 1
SUBTRACT 25 FROM WS-LEAP-WORK-UNPACKED
COMPUTE WORK-COBOL-DAY-NO OF DATEWORK =
                      (WS-YEAR-WORK * 365) + WS-LEAP-COUNT
                       - WS-CENTURY-WORK + WS-LEAP-CENTURY-CNT
                       + WS-WORK-DAY - 584388 - WS-MILLENIUM-NON-LEAP

Om mainframes, which usually have hardware packed decimal
support, packed decimal is not much slower than binary. On other
machines, which generally do not have packed decimal support in
hardware, it is around 10-100 times slower than binary.

However some PC implementations of COBOL do their binary
arithmetic in 'big-endian' format to more closely match mainframe
processing. That's slow too. 

Arithmetic using display format is slow anywhere.

This is sometimes referred to as using a "proleptic" Gregorian
calendar.  Thus we can say that A.D. 300 was not a leap year in
the Gregorian calendar, even though the Gregorian calendar was
not invented until centuries later.

Here's a bit of trivia that would further complicate things if
you tried to program for the calendar change.  Prior to the
adoption of the Gregorian calenar, New Year's Day did not fall
on January 1, but on March 25.  In Britain and the American
colonies, for example, the day following March 24, 1700, was
March 25, 1701.  George Washington was born on February 11, 1731,
according to the calendar that was in effect at that time.  After
the adoption of the Gregorian calendar in September, 1752, his
birthday became February 22, 1732.
