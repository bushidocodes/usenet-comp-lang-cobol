Okay:

- In the first place, a resume is not the place to explain hows and
wheres of why you changed employment; it's simply a list of
experience.

- On your resume, simply list the beginning and ending dates
(month/year all that's necessary), the position, and the employer, and
of course your accomplishments/responsibilities in that position, etc.
 Usual stuff.  DO NOT attempt to explain WHY you left one employer for
another.  Not relevant here.

- Remember: All a resume is is a tool to get you the INTERVIEW! 
Nothing more.

- Note that a potential NEW employer is NOT going to see your
application forms or personnel file from the previous.  So they will
not see that phrase there.

- If you get the interview, the first thing that's going to stand out,
and the most relevant issue is that you were with your former employer
for 13 YEARS!  That's pretty darn good.

- Question: Did you survive numerous downsizings and cutbacks?  Or is
this the first time the company has faced an adverse business climate?
 If the former, that's a good sign - the company kept you over the
other folks it deemed to let go on prior occasions.  That meant you
were considered more valuable.  (Usually, but my no means always, of
course).  If this is the first real cutback they've done, and you're
one of the first to go, that's the time to ask yourself (and your
former supervisor if you can) what you could have done better so
they'd have wanted to keep you over the others.

- As far as your question about the employer prior to that, if it were
to come up, you could HONESTLY say something like:

"Well, the company was going in a direction I was concerned about.  I
expressed my reservations about what I saw happening around me to my
supervisor, who - much to my surprise - took great exception to my
being so frank with him.  I was considering leaving anyway, so we
mutually agreed that I probably should do so."

This is true, right?  If you were that unhappy with your boss at the
time, I'm sure you WERE considering leaving.  I'm sure you mutually
agreed that that was a good thing.  If pressed on the issue of
resignation vs. firing, you can honestly say:

"Well, after the above discussion with my supervisor, I planned on
submitting my resignation, but wasn't given the chance.  They just
asked me to leave."

(i.e. "fired you" without actually saying those words.)  Question is:
Is this true?  If you had the kind of conversation with your boss that
I imagine you did, you probably WERE planning on leaving.

Anyway, that was 13 years ago.  Your ex-supervisor probably isn't
there any more himself, and your personnel file has probably long
since been archived in some small, dark, dusty room.  Doubtful that
due dilligence will extend back that far.

- Now, as one who has dealt with troublesome subordinates myself,
since I'm helping you get your next job, I would like to clarify:

No boss should be so insecure as to fire you for disagreeing with him.
 Depending on how you do it, of course.  Expressing reservations or
concerns is one thing.  Arguing at length, or in front of others is
another.  And yet another thing is because you disagree, to refuse to
do what you're instructed to do at all.

The first is good: Varied viewpoints are always welcome.  No one
should be surrounded by "yes men".
The second isn't good.  I'll put up with an argument a few times. 
Then it gets old.
The latter I won't put up with at all.  If this is what you did, I'd
have fired you too.

Anyway, good luck with your search.

Hey, I have nothing against 'employment at will' as long as it works both 
ways.  I've received contracts which contained the 'standard' clause along 
the lines of 'We can fire you in an instant but if you terminate without 
our permission you have to pay us for the remaining term of the contract'; 
I've notified folks that the clause is unacceptable and that if they want 
me they need to draw up a new contract with the clause equalised... either 
I get the same no-notice privelege or they have to pay me on early 
termination.  (In the World of Theatre this is known as a 'pay or play 
contract')

They always just strike out the clause - one *cannot* give Equal Status to 
a mere consultant! - and there have been times when I've had the need to 
dump someone while there was still work on the table but after all efforts 
to resolve a difficulty/dispute had failed.  Someone *always* says 'You 
can't do that, there's a clause in your contract!'... and when it gets 
pointed out that this clause isn't there someone murfles and blurfles 
about 'corporate loyalty' or 'professional behavior'.

Works both ways.
