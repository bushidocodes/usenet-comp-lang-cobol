I'll begin with a disclaimer. The opinions expressed here do not
necessarily reflect those of my employer.

I am a novice systems analyst who has recently completed a development
program at EDS. I am now working with several people who attended the same
36 week college program that (I suspect) Dan attended -DePaul University's
Computer Career Program. I have a couple thoughts: I feel the EDS training
is excellent. They cover a lot of ground quickly, but they also provide
hands on - on the job opportunities to apply what was learned. (I know many
of you are cringing, but please bear with me...)

I can only speak for the the team I am working with now, but of the 10 team
members, 8 are new - entry level people. Our team consists of 4 people from
the College program mentioned above and 4 people from the corporate
development program, like me. We have a pair of excellent mentors in our
project leader and technical leader. Their combined experience is
considerable. I have learned much from both of them, from a professional
and technical standpoint. Of course I have much to learn, but our project
has been very successful. We have met all of our deadlines and we are now
in system testing, and things have gone very smoothly.

I don't know where you leaders, managers and experienced people expect to
find good people with the job market the way it is. We know that fewer
people have been opting for college computer programs in recent years. The
people in these 36 week college programs and corporate development programs
have a very diverse background. (I studied voice performance and music
education.) To fill the positions, you are going to have to be creative and
look for people with alternative approaches. I believe that a mentorship
approach is very appropriate to bring new people in and get them up to
speed.

-Art

OK, I will say unequivocally that any team with eight entry levels, and two
'mentors' is poorly constituted to train while doing quality work. Try it
the other way around, with eight experienced people and two entry levels.
Better yet, make that nine to one.

Congratulations on meeting your deadlines. You will continue to be
employed. As to whether it went well, you will know better after
acceptance testing. Working for EDS you won't likely be around after the
users actually start using the product, which is when you could really find
out how well it worked.
›

I agree that it is hard to find good people. And not every one has to go
through a four year CS degree (which was not a recognized field, and hence
did not have a major, when I went through). My problem with large
consulting groups is when they pull someone out of school, sometimes before
they even finish their 36 week program, and send them out to a client
claiming they are experienced.

Examples:

When a vendor sends out a 'four year experienced MVS/CICS analyst' who does
not know what either acronym means, nor what 'a TSO' is, I have my doubts.


When the only background a 'system architect' for a multi-user CICS
transaction system has is in dBase III, and he is billing at $200/hour, the
customer might not be getting their money's worth.

When the project leader for a ten person development group is a 'senior'
employee at six months out of school, I am skeptical of the vendor's high
professional standards.

When the consulting vendor is finishing up a contract, and schedules their
on-site people to be 'briefed' on what the vendor has put on their resume
to bid them to the next client, which they should claim to have done, and
you KNOW that they are fabricating the experience and resume, I think it
should be called fraud. It is, of course, especially insulting when they
are doing this at the client site, demonstrating that once the bill is
paid, they don't even bother to hide it from their existing clients.

Now, your experience at EDS may be wonderful, but not everyone's has been.
And your development group may be delivering wonderful, high quality
product, despite the short experience of most involved, but that, too, is
atypical. I wish you the very best of luck in your tenure at EDS, and I
hope you are allowed to continue doing work of which you can be justifiably
proud. But if you do, please consider just how lucky you are.
