I can't speak for the company, only myself.

It doesn't matter. Whether you take 6 C++ courses or one, nothing you
learn in school except the basics is worth anything. A semester long
project of 300 lines is a weeks work in the "real world". It's become
the return of the curse of my 15 year old engineering degree: "All we
can teach you in 4 years is how to think about a problem and where to
lookup part of the answers". The subject is just too big.

So, I tend to like a well-rounded background, because it shows you can
grab anything I throw at you. I also like internships, even if not
computer related, because it means you've learned the little things:

Show up.
Work.
Call mommy/girlfriend/boyfriend after the work is done, not
before.

When I'm hiring NUGs (New University Graduates), I mostly look for
attitude, as that's all you can expect. Once in a while you get a bit
more, but it's rare.

Based on my experience (straight from HS to professional workplace)
having a wide range of experience is better. The general theory
probably runs something along the lines of "If you've got a good basic
knowledge of a language/topic you can always go back and add depth
later." In fact, to some extent this is what your professional career
is going to consist of - adding depth.

Also, consider the competition factor: if you narrow in on C or C++,
you're still going to have a lot of competitors for a job. If you have
some combination, say, COBOL, C++ and LISP, there will be fewer places
to look for work, but you'll have a lot less competition - keep this in
mind for the salary negotiation phase...
