```
Related to all the threads on "What is broken" with the Standards process,
getting enhancements into COBOL, cost of COBOL compilers, etc.

/* begin depression */

1) Think about the number of COBOL programmers in the world - Think about
what percentage of those programmers have web/Usenet access.
   NOW - tell me how many people actually follow (much less participate in)
this newsgroup - or any comparable "forum" related to COBOL (current, past,
OR future), (include "lurkers" if you want)
  Conclusion:
      Today's COBOL programmers (90% plus) do NOT care about COBOL - just
about "doing their job" and getting paid.

2) Think about the "history" of vendors trying to sell COBOL compilers (or
products) for the "individual" programmer.  Think about Micro Focus' $600
dollar product, think about IBM's $750 product, even think about Fujitsu's
PURCHASED $1000 product.
  Conclusion:
    The people who get involved with this NG care about an single user COBOL
development product - the market place does NOT make this profitable. (And
the only thing that MIGHT make it profitable for vendors - "run time license
fees" - gets attacked by those who claim to want such a product.)

3) What is your COBOL vendor of choice?  Do you know how to OFFICIALLY
request an enhancement to their product?  Have you ever done it?  Have you
TRACKED whether or not it was implemented (and if so when)?
  Conclusion:
     COBOL programmers think that "someone else" is taking care of the
language's future - and it will be "fine" - even though a few like
complaining about what they get (and when they get it).

4) Have you even looked at the "Substantive Change" section of the next COBOL
Standard?  Have you EVER sent an email requesting an enhancement, change of
direction, or "correction" to the Standards committees related to EITHER the
current or proposed Standard?  (If you got a "negative" reply from one note,
did you follow up and TELL them that you found the reply negative - and try
and get a more positive reply on some other issue?)
  Conclusion: (see above)
     Also, the Standards process is in the hands of a few "well meaning" but
little rewarded people who do the best that they can - but with little
appreciation, no feed-back, and virtually no "real world" user input. As bad
as things are for J4 (the ANSI-ish group) things are even worse when it comes
to getting COBOL people to communicate with their national bodies for ISO
issues.

5) Does your COBOL vendor have a local user group?  If not, have you tried to
form one?  Have you looked into sponsoring a local COBOL "interest group"
that is NOT associated with any particular vendor? Have you  done anything
with your local university, junior college, or even high school to ENCOURAGE
the use of COBOL? Have you even tried to get any of your COBOL
"acquaintances" to participate in this NG?
  Conclusion:
     COBOL is viewed as a necessary tool for many peoples' livelihood - but
it doesn't seem to be something that they want "their sister to marry".

/* end whatever */

OBVIOUSLY, many of the active participants in this NG actually do "further"
the future of COBOL - so I am ranting to the wrong crowd.  However, I did
think I should at least express my view of the fact that what is "broken" is
not the process or something "out there" - but the lack of participation FROM
WITHIN.
```

To question #2: What I'd really like to see is a compiler + editing
environment that works like, say, VB Control Creation Edition.  Compilation,
debugging, learning the product, but no final distributable executables; and
NOTHING else disabled, especially not the part about ODBC database access,
as learning this is one of the most desirable skills.  An inability to
create the final executable would pretty much disable any profitability for
one person shops, although I can see where a paranoid vendor would fear a
customer picking up one real version, a bunch of the free versions, and then
doing all final compilations through the one real version owned by the
company.  Still, this opens up a company to being reported for piracy by
disgruntled employees.


To question #3: IBM.  And no, I've never had cause to suggest any changes
directly to IBM.  The company I used to work for would probably not have
been receptive to an individual employee calling up a client's licensed
vendor and starting to make suggestions.

of it (sorry, but after a while of looking around, it seemed like it was
never going to come out).

newsgroup, even going so far as to teach them everything they needed to know
to get onto usenet and view newsgroups, but I must admit, AFAIK all they
ever did was lurk.


As much as there might be internal lack of participation in the Standards
Committee from the COBOL community at large, there is also plenty of
external suppression.  How many times have you ever seen some bigshot
well-known programming guru (at, say, Yourdon's level, at least before he
started going off on the trends and preditions book writing business) slam
cobol with phrases like, "The teaching of COBOL stunts the mind," or, "No
one should be using COBOL anymore," hmm (which is not to say Yourdon made
either of those statements in particular, but I've read stuff of his where
he backhands COBOL quite nastily)?  How often does that happen to C and C++?
This is just as much a battle for public opinion as a political race, and
there are probably politics involved in it, which (if I can hazard a guess)
is why I think some people stay away from it.

All  "official involvement" in the ANSI Standards activities are
SELF-financed.  (For example, I have to pay $600 a year to be a "member" and
pay for my transportation to and from meetings, and all hotel and meal
costs - for up to 6 meetings per year.)

This is why MOST of the participants have "company sponsorship" for their
standards work.  (Such members are also paid by their employers for the time
at the meetings and in preparation for meetings.)

NEEDLESS TO SAY - this is NOT cheap <G>

On the other hand, there is NO financial requirement on people who want to
review or submit papers to the COBOL (or other) working groups.

My assumption is that C (and C++ and SQL) have SLIGHTLY more active
membership because more companies are willing to spend the time (and money)
in such activities.  At its height (around the '85 Standard), I think that
the COBOL J4 (X3J4 then) committee had about 20+ members.  It is now around
13.

shops, programs, and programmers are still using '74 compilers - also seems
