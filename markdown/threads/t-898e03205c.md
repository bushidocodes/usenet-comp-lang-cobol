Well Steve, I'm not quite as impressed with the documentation as you
are. Someone else mentioned that Fujitsu should have provided it in
Acrobat format instead of Common Ground (which I thought was quite
good), but that's not what concerns me. Aside from the (perhaps more
numerous than normal) typos, the appearance of little notes attached
to several sentences throughout the 1032 pages of the COBOL85 Language
Manual and the 832 pages of the COBOL85 Users Guide that state such
things as 'This should really say...' and 'This figure would be better
titled....' tell me that this product's introduction may have been
driven by the marketing department rather than the development team.
Always a bad move.

In addition to typos and unremoved reviewer's notes, I'm a little
disturbed by inconsistencies between the documentation and product
usage. For example, in the COBOL85 Users Guide, the areas devoted to
walking through the included code samples don't always agree with the
directories and filenames established by the default installation.
Usually, it's not too hard to figure out what you're really supposed
to enter for the directory pointers and such, but occasionally it
requires some lengthy head-scratching and Hail Mary typing to get
something to work. And on at least one occasion, nothing you enter
will ever, ever, ever get the sample to execute.

Seems that Fujitsu decided that the software necessary to create
screen descriptor files was not something they wanted to put on the
"introductory" CD. I'm not going to fault them for not including on a
free CD everything needed to compile and execute the sample code they,
themselves provided even though the documentation refers you to the
relevant manual (which is also not provided), but it sure would have
been nice if they had mentioned 'batteries not included' in one of the
readme files. (Ran the COBOL85 debugger to try to figure out what was
wrong and crashed Windows95 every single time. [Their PowerCOBOL
debugger is conveniently and safely disabled.])

All of the above comments have been about Fujitsu's COBOL85 product,
which is the character-oriented compiler. The CD also contains their
windows-oriented PowerCOBOL product. It's like any other GUI compiler
as far as creating windows and attaching code to objects is concerned.
Perhaps not as full-featured as VB4 or any of the GUI C++ compilers.
So far, I haven't had much trouble with PowerCOBOL, but then, I
haven't done anything to really stress-test it either.

To be fair, Fujitsu only advertised their PowerCOBOL product as the
freebie item, but from my perspective, Fujitsu COBOL just isn't worth
further examination for doing what I wanted it to do; migrate Micro
Focus character-mode applications. The CD contained character-mode
COBOL85, so that's what I looked at most.

I was happy with Micro Focus to begin with. Now I'm even happier with
it because of the experience with Fujitsu's COBOL85.
