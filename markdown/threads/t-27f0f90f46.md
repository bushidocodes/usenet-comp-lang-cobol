I have been doing some research into IBM's Websphere product and am very
favourably impressed.

It seems fairly certain that IBM is going this way for OO programming.

I won't write a blurb here for Websphere (you can read IBM's own blurb on
their Web Site) but this is a useful way to deploy OO Components.

Sadly, the preferred environment is Java (they use J2EE and Enterprise Java
Beans) and I find no mention anywhere of OO COBOL. Their Visual Age Java and
Java environment (associated with WebSphere) seems excellent, although I
haven't used it personally.

 I say "sadly", not as a criticism of Java, but rather because it is pretty
apparent that IBM have no more faith in COBOL (apart from Fortress COBOL,
which has a limited lifetime as more and more Companies opt for ERP and
business solutions like SAP, Chordiant, and Siebel).

This seems to confirm what we have suspected for some time now; IBM are
dropping support for OO COBOL.
(The inevitable corollary to this is the demise of COBOL. The future is in
OO, for all programming languages.)

Nevertheless, Websphere is a good product and those of you on mainframe
sites who realise the need to expand your skill sets would be well advised
to grab some of the tutorials and get stuck into it.

The demand in Europe for people with this experience is increasing and, as
component technology takes over (I predicted this some years ago), this will
become a valuable (that means more money in your pocket) skill.

It is very apparent in looking at job requirements here that there is an
increasing demand for Component technologies and we are seeing projects
advertised with UML, Rational Tools and even RAD/DSDM being specified. All
of these function best in the OO environment.

I'd be interested to hear comments from people in this NG who are actually
using Websphere in the workplace. (Maybe the wrong forum, but hopefully
not...<G>).

What do you think of it?

Pete.

Well, don't count out COBOL yet. :)

Just one instance - in WebSphere environments, databases tend to be the
primary data source, especially (not coincidentally!) DB/2. Guess what legacy
language is the easiest for most legacy programmers to write stored procedures in?

(And why do you *want* stored procedures? Because you can encode business
logic in them that is completely insensitive to the tiers above it, and it makes
the best sense to keep your data processing as close to the data source as
possible. Not to mention they are trivially easy to write. :)

Yep - COBOL - on *all* supported DB/2 platforms, either from the IBM compiler or
from MicroFocus, PerCOBOL, AcuCOBOL, etc.
