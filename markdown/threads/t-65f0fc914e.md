It's a very well produced/written paper and well worth the read.

Looking at the COBOL implementations of things I do every day in C# with VS 
2008, I'm not sorry I made the transition. COBOL is clumsy by comparison.

BUT, if you buy this compiler and IDE it DOES give you managed code in 
COBOL. For some people that would be an important consideration.

There is a tentativeness in the writing. The author knows that .NET is about 
Classes and Objects, but he/she also knows that COBOLWorld is generally not 
hip to these things and there will be resistance. As a result we get a very 
oversimplified summary of OO COBOL, and then detailed examples that probably 
will terrify the people reading them.

Nevertheless, it is a good implementation, and, if you intend to cling to 
COBOL til your last dying breath, it has to be an option.

In the final analysis, whether you like it or not, if you move to .NET you 
will be utilizing an Object Oriented, component based environment. This 
paper is saying that using indexed files and procedural code is viable in 
this environment.

I disagree.

You CAN do it (I'm not arguing about it being technically feasible...), but 
you won't enjoy it.

It is one thing to say: "I can write Windows Forms or even WPF applications 
in COBOL..."

It is quite another to match your application against another one running in 
the same environment that utilises full OO and is fully tiered with layers 
of components that interact and are reusable.

So, you gradually start getting into OO COBOL. With some perseverance and a 
bit of practise you can do (almost) anything that the other languages can 
do.  Good stuff. As time goes by, you get more facile with it, grasp more 
concepts and feel more comfortable. MF have made it possible for you, with 
this very good implementation.

There are two ways you can view this scenario:

1. Clinging to COBOL was a good thing and made your transition into 21st 
century programming much less painful and abrupt. It brought you time to 
modernise existing applications and was not traumatic on your programming 
staff. However, you have made no real progress. Yes, you have a better user 
interface which may extend the life of your apps a bit longer, but you paid 
dearly for it.

2. You have spent a lot of money on a product that you are eventually, and 
inevitably going to discard.

If you are responsible for a medium size business that is used to spending 
serious money for development tools, viewpoint 1 is probably no big deal.

On the other hand if you are a small business where money does not come 
easily and there is no room for unnecessary expenditure, you probably won't 
want to spend money on something that could be acquired for free.

Sooner or later your applications will have to be migrated anyway, and 
moving to .NET is a fantastic opportunity to get OFF flat files into DB 
objects, and to refactor and encapsulate the essential parts of your 
existing applications in a way that lets them co-exist with modern 
languages, while your programmers pick up the deal on Objects.

If you take option 1 and very gradually ease yourself into the new 
environment, you will reach a point where OO COBOL is "doing it" for you and 
you feel quite comfortable with this approach. As your experience with it 
grows you start to understand the advantages of it. Pretty soon there is no 
way you would go back to procedural coding.

Then you realise that other languages do it better and quicker. COBOL just 
takes too long and is still tied into maintaining source code, and grounded 
in procedural approaches.

As you are now sold on OO and components, you decide to let go, and move to 
C# (or VB.NET or C++.NET).

Should you have grasped the nettle and made this move at the point where you 
first considered moving to .NET?

(You would have saved thousands of dollars, some of which might have been 
used for training in Object Oriented programming...)

Or, was moving to .NET COBOL the right choice?

(OK, it cost, but the change gradient was flattened and the resulting trauma 
was lessened. Your people got to learn about OO COBOL in the topmost OO 
environment.)

Obviously, it is a subjective decision that cannot be generalised.

The bottom line is that you don't NEED a .NET compiler (MF OR Fujitsu) to 
run your COBOL on .NET, if you are OK with it not being managed code. We 
showed with the COBOL structure tool that standard COBOL (once it is 
converted to an OO COBOL component) can run perfectly well in the .NET 
environment, as unmanaged code, using InterOP Services.

I have a number of applications that are doing this. They integrate very 
well with C# and it allows me to leverage Legacy COBOL for no charge and 
without remaining locked in to it.

I really like the MF .NET version of COBOL, but I still think it is an 
"unnecessary" product.

Much of the productivity gain is provided by VS 2008 which I can download 
for free from MicroSoft.

C# lets me do more, faster, than COBOL ever did. It can be downloaded for 
free from MicroSoft.

One man's opinion. :-)

Pete.

People interested in using COBOL with Visual studio might do well to look at 
some of these video tutorials:

http://www.microsoft.com/windowsserver/mainframe/training.mspx#cobol.net

This is a series of tutorials provided by MicroSoft, which demonstrates the 
use of  both Fujitsu (NeoKicks) and MicroFocus toolsets with Visual Studio 
as part of a migration away from the mainframe platform.

Unfortunately, the videos are not "engaging" (although the presenter is 
knowledgable) and there are some problems with being able to read screens.

This is a factual, fairly dry presentation which I found difficult to watch, 
but I hung in there because the quality of the information is good and the 
content overall is interesting.

The one I watched showed how to bring a mainframe CICS application into .NET 
using ASP.NET to scale the application to the Web.

(He also discusses, in a quite unassuming way, why you might want to do 
this...)

While the video may not be "entertaining" it does get the job done and I 
developed more warmth for the presenter as I got to know him better :-)

He quoted some impressive companies who have migrated from the mainframe 
into .NET but I liked the fact that he was not Evangelical in his approach.

I see the following two problems with this approach:

1. The tools (from both MicroFocus and Fujitsu) are expensive.
2. Moving COBOL to another platform does not address the underlying problem 
with procedural code, which is the paradigm itself.

I am persuaded that the best approach if you are considering migration is 
NOT to consider COBOL on .NET as an end point. (It may well be a good 
starting point, though.)

Succesful migration WITH A FUTURE means layers (tiers) and objects. (This 
means you are not "locked in" to COBOL, but you can still use it if you want 
to. You can create objects that utilise the full capabilities of the .NET 
Framework in modern languages (neither of the major COBOL for .NET 
implemenations can use the full facilities of the environment that are

Here's what I know about our COM support in native-mode COBOL:

- There are a bunch of COM demos included with the product, including
standalone clients and servers, ActiveX controls, etc. The ones I
looked at use OO COBOL syntax, and seem pretty clean.

- Net Express includes Class and Method Wizards for creating COM
clients and servers.

- Procedural COBOL programs can be COM clients as well, just by adding
a class-control paragraph with the COM class ProgID or CLSID, an
object-reference data item, and a couple of invoke statements.

- The Interface Mapping Toolkit supports mapping COBOL programs as COM
servers. The IMTK is a pretty nice way to wrap existing COBOL code as
a service; it finds entry points and parameters, and lets you define
the methods and messages that map to them.

- There's a whole bookshelf on COM programming in the Net Express
documentation, including a chapter on using COM with .NET.

For .NET COBOL, it appears COM support is pretty straightforward. For
a .NET COM server, you define your interface and implementation; for a
.NET COM client, you add a reference to your Visual Studio project,
invoke the constructor to get a proxy reference, and invoke proxy
methods to send messages to the server.
