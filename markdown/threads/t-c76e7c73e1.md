       perform a thru d
       stop run.

    a.
       display "hello".
    b.
       display " ".
    d.
       display "world".

   c.
      display "Disney".

Peter:

There is no single industry standard for identifying and performing
subroutines in COBOL. The best you can hope for, at present, is a "shop
standard". There are at least three different such standards which can
be reasonably defended. Here's a quick synopsis:

1. OUTLAW GO-TOs. Each subroutine is contained in a single paragraph and
performed with a simple PERFORM. GO-TOs are outlawed. 

(Advantage: Subroutines are clearly identified. Disadvantages:
Distracting devices (flags, excessive nesting) become necessary to avoid
GO-TOs. Breaks down easily (when a heretical programmer inserts an
exceptional GO-TO).

2. USE COBOL SECTIONS. Each subroutines is contained in a single COBOL
section and performed with a simple PERFORM of that section. GO-TOs, if
used, must be to paragraphs with the current section. 

(Advantages: Subroutines are clearly identified. Doesn't break down with
the use of GO-TOs. 
Disadvantage: Most COBOL programmers are afraid of using sections.)

3. OUTLAW SIMPLE PERFORMS. Each subroutine is contained in a range of
paragraphs and performed with a PERFORM-THRU of that range. The last
paragraph in the range must be named the same as the first with an
"-EXIT" tacked on.  GO-TOs may transfer control within a single
subroutine. (Optional to the standard:) GO-TOs may transfer out of a
subroutine only in exceptional (terminal error) conditions. 

(Advantage: Identifies subroutines, but not as clearly as 1 & 2. Doesn't
break with GO-TOs. Doesn't require sections. 
Disadvantages: Perform-thrus are less readable than simple performs.
Breaks easily when the naming convention is violated. You can't identify
a subroutine without first searching for an exit paragraph. PERFORM-THRU
allows for overlapping ranges, a very messy "structure".)

As you can see, the example you cite follows the third standard. Both
you and your co-workers are correct: It is hogwash. It is also the most
prevalent of the three standards!

There have been numerous threads on this subject in this newsgroup. It
is a religious subject (and we may be embarking on another long
thread...)

(There is consensus on one thing: You do not want to mix these standards
in a single program!)
