Books are primarily bought by people who _don't_know_ the language (or
the topic of the book) and want to learn it so that they _might_ get a
job.  You are basing your estimates of how many programmers there are
on the numbers _who_don't_know_ the language.
 
>    In job offers, knowledge of COBOL is rarely asked for, but Java  
> much more, and C++, too.

A job offer relates to an empty desk in an office.  You are basing the
numbers of programmers there are by counting the empty desks - ie the
number of programmers that aren't there.

In fact one may conclude that the people buying books are doing so in
the hope that it will enable them to get one of the jobs being
advertised.  If they do so then it may be discovered that all they
know is what they remember from reading the book and that job winds up
being re-advertised.

The fact is that there is no shortage of Cobol programmers, thus few
jobs get to be advertised.  This may be beacause it is a declining
market.  Java is certainly an increasing market and there is a
shortage.

The actual total numbers involved in each group cannot be determined
from the number of books sold or the shortfall.

For example, as related on 'Top Gear' by Jerremy Clarkson, there is a
queue of 2,500 wanting the new Ford GT40 (he is one of them).  I am
sure that more books on this car are sold than that of most other
cars.  Your argument would conclude that there are more GT40s than any
other car on the roads.
