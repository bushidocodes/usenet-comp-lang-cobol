One must be carefull when looking at statements like this. The earliest
System /370's iron - the model 145, 155, and 165 were intorduced in 1972
or so. It would not surprise me if the hardware clock embedded in these
beasties would work just finel after 2000. As defined the hardware clock
has a period of about 143 years. A base date of 1900 is recommended in
the Principles of Operation book - an open question is "does all software
use this base date?".

Bigger questions is does the unsupported 20 year old versions of software
tolerate even 1999 without strange results. Such code - which would
likely run on such antiques ( current s/w will not run since the old
machines do not have recently added instructions or features) used 1998
and 1999 for various special meanings. It is also likely that other date
related code in the O/S would fail.

IBM has publicly issued - available on WWW - statements as to what
software versions will support the date rollover. That statement includes
both OS and application code. I do not recall seeing iron in that
posting.

Will old system / 370's not work. Well maybe the combination of the last
available software and the old iron - but for that matter I seem to recall
that the IBM PC and PC/XT are likely in the same boat - especially if one
considers the BIOS as part of the iron.

Please look at IBM pub GC28-1251 "The Year 2000 and 2-Digit Dates: A Guide for
Planning and Implementation". You can reach a copy via the Web, starting from
"http://www.software.ibm.com" or (in several formats) via anonymous ftp from
"ftp://lscftp.kgn.ibm.com/pub/year2000/y2kpaper.ps.z" - this is just one of the
file names. Appendix A, page A-22, says under Hardware: "The hardware timers on
the IBM S/390, AS/400, RISC/6000, and PowerPC machines are not sensitive to
change of century." Under IBM Personal Computer, it says "All new models of IBM
PCs shipped in 1996 will automatically update the century byte [of the BIOS - my
addition]."

I read this to say that all other hardware is 'at risk', although this is only
fully discussed for PC's.

Another respondent referred to the need to update the Operating System and
Program Products which this publication also mentions as becoming Year 2000
compliant in 1996 releases. I interpret this as meaning that shops running
"old" (but not necessarily upsupported) releases of products like CICS to allow
them to continue to execute macro level transaction programs are facing a 'drop
dead' date if any of those transactions do anything date related. Those IT
organizations who have saved 'scads' of money by not staying up to date are
going to have to make a withdrawal from their piggy banks. Thankfully, IBM
may provide some relief thru its "OS/390" offering, which I interpret as a
Prepackaged, pregenerated, and pretested OS release, including at least 30
program products used by many or most customers.

I see our strategy as: designing a "standard MVS", implementing a development
and a production LPAR, and migrating our current applications as we examine (and
fix most of) them for Year 2000 problems from the current MVS to the new one.

Year 2000, a relatively simple problem technically, will be the biggest change
management and configuration management problem any given shop has ever
encountered, due to the need to look at all platforms, all systems on those
platforms, all interfaces between the systems and platforms, locate all pieces
of every system, schedule when changes to related systems must be / can be made,
etc. If you aren't up to date on Y2K, look at "http://www.year2000.com". It
will give you lots of information, useful to COBOL programmers, to managers,
etc. Be sure to consider subscribing to the e-mail discussion group (you need
only to send a note to "lis··.@hoo··p.net" with "subscribe year2000" in the
body (no title / subject is needed).
Colin Campbell
