In fact there seems to be so much confusion about what .NET is that

It seems to me that the main ideas behind .NET were formed from
considering how to have a post-dotcom and post-PC business model that
generated sufficient revenue.  That is how to ensure that MS customers
continued to buy new MS software on a regular basis instead of
considering that their Win98/W2k/XP machines were all they ever needed
_and_ how to change MS to a services based revenue stream rather than
sales based (for example see how licence 6 has done this).

Of course since the initial talk about what .NET would be, MS have had
to rather flatten the concept by allowing that other companies may
want to be servers charging for services.

The basis of .NET seems to be that, for example, software house would
no longer sell an application or a library to do a specific task, they
would provide it as a service function and charge per use. Programmers
would write in C# and instead of buying libraries and code to build
into their programs, would simply attach to services over the
internet.  Some of these services would be provided by MS and some by
independant companies and by partners.

The 'advantage' to the .NET user is that (supposedly) the application
service is always updated and centrally managed and thus provides the
latest functionality without needing to be updated and recompiled into
the client program.

The only purpose of the .NET languages is to a) not be Java, b)
connect to .NET services, c) be C# or C# with a different syntax.

Now, in order to pay for these services, some central control over
authentication and payment is required: passport and wallet. 
Originally only MS could be trusted to do this.

As a business model for MS it seems that it could be a winner.  They
would provide the central control (because they are a bunch of control
freaks) and provide some services, such as Office chargable on a
per-document or per-page basis.  Other would provide other services
and MS would take a percentage (something they tried to do before). 
Where a significant revenue was being generated they would notice this
and then could offer their own 'better' version of it, or simply
redirect users to it (as they have been known to do before).

But this business model does rely on users being convinced they need
to use it.  So far this part has failed.

I believe it isn't greed as such.

The reasoning goes like this...

"We need to recoup the cost of development, the R & D, and make a profit.

means we need to charge a bit under $4000 per seat. Guess that prices it

What they haven't realised is that COBOL is no longer viable in a Corporate
environment, for anything other than maintaining existing COBOL.

(I would have introduced it to the site I am managing if it were. Simply

That means it is even more difficult to sell...

And so on.

It isn't greed, it is necessity. I expect to see considerable reorganization
amongst COBOL vendors in the next 5 - 10 years. Some current players will
definitely leave the Market Place.

It is ironic, because the failing attraction of COBOL is not down to the
vendors. The major vendors have consistently supported the language and
implemented the facilities that it needed.

Personally, I believe there are four factors that are killing COBOL:

1. Lack of a proper adminstering Standards body, and the consequent lack of
credibility and involvement of the User Base. (This has now reached the
point where this body has zero credibility and nobody really cares what they
do. It is too late now to salvage COBOL.)

2. The refusal by most Fortress COBOL sites to pick up OO programming and
thereby ensure that COBOL could participate in the Object revolution which
is now taking place. Fortress COBOL sites represent the majority of the
potential COBOL user base. If they don't want something there is little
point in providing it. (This one really grieves me. I have been using OO
COBOL for nearly 4 years now and find the Fujitsu implementation to be
outstanding. I have COBOL Object Components that co-exist beautifully with
components written in other languages, but this is unlikely to become the
norm. I know of no Component developer who is using COBOL to produce
commercial components. (The ones I produce are for my own use; I don't
intend to sell them commercially.))

3. The historically superior attitude of COBOL programmers when dealing with
the User base. To be fair, I haven't seen much of this recently, but it was
prevalent in the 80s and into the 90s, and that is the time when today's
Managers were working in the Business...
(This has won COBOL no friends in the Business and led to Users doing things
for themselves rather than interface with the IT department. The logical
progression from spreadsheets, word-processors, and databases is user built
applications which do not require professional programmers; this is starting
to emerge in some application areas now. Wizards are provided which interact
with the User and eventually generate the required application code.)

4. The tenacious adherence by COBOL programmers to the COBOL file system.
The rest of the World went to open databases and a free exchange of data.
Many COBOL people remained convinced (some are still, to this day, and I'll
be surprised if this post brings no response in defence of the COBOL file
system) for far too long that Relational Databases are best avoided and
there is nothing like ISAM or VSAM/KSDS, conveniently ignoring that it
requires one-off programs to manipulate them...

If COBOL eventually disappears from the realms of serious professional
computer programming, it won't be through the greed of the vendors.

represent the data elements that occur in the Business and apply
procedural code to them, manipulating and modelling what happens in
real life. It is simply a question of emulating these rules in
procedural code.

Non-procedural systems can encapsulate these variables and cause them
to behave the same way they do in the real world when certain stimuli
are applied. However the process is more dynamic and flexible. Instead
of having to repeat these objects in every procedure that references
them, we can simply extend their behaviour to accommodate new or
modified processes. This is possible because they contain their own
behaviours.

smart system. (This will be useful, particularly in cases where the

understanding. At the moment we do this in Workshops but there's no
fundamental reason why a smart computer couldn't participate as just
another Workshop member.)

definition.
