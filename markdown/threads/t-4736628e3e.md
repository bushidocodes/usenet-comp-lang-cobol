objects and methods that VBA can take advantage of when a particular
external library is referred to in a project. (Even though VBA is not

are multple ways to accomplish the same thing, each of which seems to
require an obscure string of commands or parameters that are buried
somewhere in the vast library that is MSDN. (I'm being a *bit*

its wrappers like DAO or ADO. You can connect to a database using a
driver or ODBC. You can use SQL.

The other part of the equation is working through the recordset that
queries return.

Access is easily accessible from COBOL via either Fujitsu or Micro Focus. 
Fujitsu require you to use ODBC for embedded SQL (or there is a PowerCOBOL 
component for ADO, or you can interface to ADO via CALL), but ODBC is often 
a good solution anyway. (At least as far as embedded SQL (ESQL) goes... 
there are MUCH better solutions to accessing databases than ESQL, but that 
is what COBOL generally uses.) If you haven't done it or aren't sure about 
it, you can download the paper I wrote with samples in it from: 
http://www.primacomputing.co.nz/cobol21/SQL.aspx

Access 2003 is a good implementation of the SQL standard and uses a cut down 
version of SQL Server as the DB engine. However, it isn't good at concurrent 
access where more than 8 terminals are involved and it isn't really scalable 
easily. (I can say this with confidence because I have had to scale it). 
Generally, (particularly in a commercial organization), SQL Server is a 
better choice. (there are also some very good Open RDBMS like MySQL (now 
owned by ORACLE) and PostgreSQL, but the choice of RDBMS isn't the issue 
here and I digress... :-))

Obviously, if you have major problems with any of this, Charlie, just post 
here.

No, that is something you shouldn't be concerned about. Definitely report 
what you have found but try to keep it neutral and factual in tone. (don't 
be alarmist or let your own worries come through.). Just the facts, maybe 
backed up with some recommendations at the end, and with your own planned

Fourth (and last) piece of advice: NEVER worry about management's (or 
anybody else's...) expectations. They haven't done the investigation you 
have, so they have no right to an expectation. Besides, trying to meet 
unrealistic expectations is a certain road to ruin. Once you have done the 
investigation and taken inventory, everybody is in a position to share a 
realistic expectation, including yourself. (this is the expectation you try 
to exceed... :-))

Many years ago I did an investigation similar to the one you are doing and 
reported back to management. The particular manager was proud of his 
reputation as a "hard man" (he was actually a bully...)  He read my report 
then snarled: "That's all very well, but I want it by <whatever>!"

I looked him in the eye and said: "Well, I hope you can find someone who 
will deliver it by <whatever>. I'm positive that I can't, and I don't 
normally undertake tasks where the outcome is certain failure."

I went back to my cubicle and started collecting my stuff, expecting to be 
summarily dismissed. Instead, the phone rang and I was summoned to the 
presence. He asked if it would change things if certain requirements were 
removed (descoped) and more resources were applied. I agreed to reassess and 
we did it by the time he actually wanted it. Supposing I had just responded 
with: "Yes Sir!"... A team would have worked themselves to a frazzle, morale 
would have been non-existent (unrealistic management expectations are 
totally demotivating to the shop floor, who know they haven't got a hope in 
Hell of success), and when the whole thing finally went tits up, it would 
have been my responsibility...

The lesson I learned was to call it like I see it and not to worry about 
what they want to hear. There is no point in playing games no-one can win. 
If that is what your management are expecting, don't accommodate them.

Once you think about this you will realise that the worst that can happen is 
that you will be fired. In actuality, good people are hard enough to find 
that this is a very remote possibility, and the person whose expectations 
you didn't meet has to report to someone higher. Managers firing good 
employees does not reflect well on them and awkward questions get asked... 
Besides, when you think about it, there was always going to be a time when 
you would no longer work for them...

Getting fired from a job is seldom fatal and it can actually be a growth 
experience. I've been fired twice in my career and on both occasions it was 
personal and political. Hence my first piece of advice to you. I'm still 
here :-)

Well, I have given you four pieces and I hope they help (remember that 
advice which costs nothing, is usually worth the price :-) You can decide 
for yourself.).

Again:

1. Don't get involved in the politics. (In particular, don't denigrate or 
blame others, or agree with people who are denigrating or blaming others. 
Have no opinion about it, or, if you do have an opinion abut it, keep it to 
yourself. Assigning blame will not lessen the workload.)

2. Don't be intimidated by your own doubts or what management expect. (Facts 
are facts; just be honest and call it like you see it. Try to keep your 
emotions out of it. Don't be afraid to say you don't know, if that is the 
fact.)

3. NEVER undertake a task with broken or inappropriate tools. (The primary 
role of management (as supervisors) is to support and facilitate. If you 
don't have what you need they are failing in that role. Raise it, and if no 
action is taken, document the fact, without emotion.) Who paid for the books 
you are reading?

4. NEVER worry about management's (or anybody else's...) expectations. 
Especially when those expectations are based on inadequate investigation and 
wishes, rather than solid reality. On reflection, I see this is similar to 
number two, but it is worth saying twice... :-)

Good luck.

I'm under the impression that you are a seasoned COBOL programmer.
Other folks have said, "don't worry about learning these things, you can do
it".
If you are reasonably competent, I think they are right, that stuff is just
a matter of reading and learning.  Lots of people have done this.

My first response would have been, "What do I (Charlie) know about
COBOL parsing and fact extraction?"  Parsing is rather an exotic art
and few practice it to level of being experts (I'll immodestly say I'm one
of them).

If the goal is to acquire "some information" from the code and imprecision
isn't an issue, you might get away with ad hoc string hacking to try to
extract the data in question.

If the goal is acquire precise information (after all, there's no point
in misleading the analysts that are supposed to be using this data),
then you need precise parsers and program analysis tools.  As a simple
answer, if you find CALL ABC in COBOL program X, are
you supposed to document for X which of the other 10,000 programs might
get called?  (Note: the example isn't using a literal string)
[This answer has to be computed by what amounts to a global
points-to analysis across the entire system of code].

You didn't say if anybody thought the contractor was any good at
this.   If he wasn't and your organization was expecting precise answers,
then that might explain partly why the software was never really ready.
And in this case, it won't matter how much MS Access or VB you do,
you won't get to precise parsing and fact extraction because you'd
be building on rotten foundations.

If the contractor *is* good at precise parsing, then your problem will
be fixing the parser, and again knowing MS Access or VB will
be necessary, that won't help you, because the problem will be understanding
how to make parsers work, and how to make them work for IBM COBOL, JCL, ...
And that's a skill you might have, but I have guessed you do not.
If you know roughly what's in the Dragon book, you might be OK.
If you don't know that that is, you will be in deep parsing trouble.

If I were in your shoes, I'd ask management what the project requirements
were in terms of answer precision (and make sure they announce the answer
loudly and clearly).  If fuzziness is OK, then you should dive in with
enthusiasm
and play until you retired. It will likely be a lot of fun.
My guess is the remaining work will be endless,  as people will continually
ask you to improve the answers and you make incremental progress.

If fuzziness is *not* OK, I'd think really hard about whether you can
succeed.
In that case, your company should seriously consider getting a tool from
a source that can provide that kind of precise data, because a home-grown
solution won't be anywhere near as useful.
