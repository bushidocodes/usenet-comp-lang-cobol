This is not an easy task.

I did this for a JAVA class and we used Rule Based Design Methods to identify
all of the rules and then designed the code to cover every single rule.
Just be aware that there are special rules for Leap years and century years.

I recommend first ruling out the obvious.

1. A day of the month must be between 1 and 31.
2. A Month must be between 1 and 12.
3. A year mut be between (Use your own rule!).

In some instances you may want all known dates, but it doesn't seem likely.
Using 3 rules like this eliminates all of the obvious errors only the not so
obvious errors like 31 days in February can get through.  The hard part is the
difference in days of the month.  February requires special processing for leap
year and century years.

Another way to do this is to load a system dictionary with all known possible
dates for a given year.  That way you can look the possible date up without
having to test it, and load all possible dates into a table to test in memory.

Of course we went further than this.  We designed a way to find the next day,
the previous day, and then X days in the past or present, and then the number of
days between 2 dates.

There is probably some obvious way to test this.  Like saving the system date
and attempting to change it and looking for the error code in assembly as a
called routine.  Or converting a date to julian and looking for failure.  I
don't know what would be the result if you looked for the julian date of a given
date and it was invalid to start with. It may just return all zeroes.

There was once a COBOL programmer in the mid to late 1990s. For the
sake of this story, we'll call him Charlie.
 
After years of being treated as a technological dinosaur by all the UNIX
programmers and website developers, Charlie was finally getting some
respect. He'd become a private consultant specializing in Year 2000
conversions. He was working 70 and 80 and even 90 hour weeks, but it
was worth it.

Several years of this had taken its toll on Charlie. He had problems
sleeping and began having anxiety dreams about the Year 2000. All he
could think about was how he could avoid the year 2000 and all that
came with it.

Charlie made a deal to have himself frozen until March 15th, 2000. The
next thing he would know is he'd wake up in the year 2000; after the
New Year celebrations and computer debacles; after the leap day.

He was put into his cryogenic receptacle, the technicians set the revive
date, and that was that.

The next thing that Charlie saw was an enormous room filled with
people. They were all shouting "I can't believe it!" and "It's a
miracle" and "He's alive!".

Charlie couldn't contain his enthusiasm. "It is over?" he asked. "Is
2000 already here? Are all the millennial parties and promotions and
crises all over and done with?"

A spokesman explained that there had been a problem with the programming
of the timer on Charlie's cryogenic receptacle, it hadn't been year 2000
compliant. It was actually eight thousand years later, not the year 2000.
But the spokesman told Charlie that he shouldn't get excited; someone
important wanted to speak to him.

Suddenly a wall-sized 3D hologram displayed the image of the Prime
Minister of Earth.  He told Charlie not to be upset. That this was a
wonderful time to be alive. That there was world peace and no more
starvation, there were colonies on all the planets. That technology had
advanced to such a degree that everyone had virtual reality interfaces
which allowed them to contact anyone else in the solar system, or to
watch any entertainment, or to hear any music recorded anywhere.

"That sounds terrific," said Charlie. "But I'm curious. Why is everybody
so interested in me?"

"Well," said the Prime Minister. "The year 10,000 is just around the
corner, and it says in your files that you know COBOL".
