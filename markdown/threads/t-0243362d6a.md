IMHO, it is preferable to have a slowly-reacting standards process rather than
one which is responsive enough to adapt quickly to the theory of the moment.
You have been around for a while, I believe, so from your own experience you'll
be able to identify any number of bright ideas that took the IS community by
storm and then vanished without a trace.  Had the standards process incorporated
any of these it would now be loaded with utterly irrelevant clutter which would
render the standard unworkable.

As well, I'd be very leery of a popular vote on elements.  Vox populi, vox dei
is all very well in theory but mistaken enthusiasm and issue du jour are far
from being the best way to go.  For instance: according to the first principles
of structured programming, the EVALUATE verb is not necessary to the language.
Should it be voted out of existence, then?  Or the COMPUTE which some people
hate with a passion?  I think not!  Far better a standards process which defines
correctness of implementation rather than what is correct (dare I say
"politically correct"?).  Obsolete and unworkable constructs will eventually
fall by the wayside anyway (RIP ALTER).

I agree with this completely.  I would like to see the following changes:

1)  ALL users are allowed a voice.  Input should be provided in such a
format that it is easy to read for all users, regardless of experience.
Recognizing that fact that standards have to be fairly exact, perhaps a
bulleted summary could provided with the technical write up (IE - 2
documents - the complicated, complex, exact technical "legalese" and a
summary for the common user).

2)  A Congress will actually be responsible for changing the standard, based
upon user input.  This body should be responsible for explaining why
standard changes were accepted/rejected.

3)  Standard updates should be published on a schedule.  3 - 5 years sounds
right to me.

Just as a note, I think that standard "extensions" should be made available
for differing platforms (IE - extensions to the standard for that apply to
that platform/range of platforms only).  Programming environments are very
different from the PC to a mainframe or AS/400 (when was the last time you
needed to get a cartridge number on the PC, or update the registry on a
mainframe).  These are very basic functions within their respective
environments, and not allowing the standard to be flexible enough to
recognize these differences will only lead to less and less compilers using
the standards.

Before replying I've noted both Thane's and Pete's replies. Not surprisingly even developers
are not in agreement on this one - Pete's point that it wouldn't be a good thing to
acknowledge O/S. I'll confine myself to PCs (Windowing) because I know damn all about
mainframes or Unix/Linux - although I get the 'feeling' the latter two are 'close' to
Windowing.

I think examples may illustrate my points best :-

(a) Report Writer - after a LOT of musical chairs it is NOW entrenched in COBOL 2002. BUT -
you can't use it easily with Windowing. Either back when using dot matrix or now lasers,
which have been around now for a considerable time - you are confronted with how you get at
fonts, condensed, expanded etc. You can get yourself a nice fat tome on HP and look up the
control sequences to do the above.
Net result - PC users in the main look for an alternate, easier source - in Net Express we
have PC_PRINT, Flexus print routines (I believe) and others go another route - (oh dear,
forgotten name - but Pete once referred to it as being included with the F/J compiler ???).
Net result - R/W hasn't been commonly used with PCs and probably WONT be in the future. (Of
course - as Bill has pointed out - you can buy support 'extensions' for R/W - from - guess
who ?).

(b) File naming - for my specific application an absolute MUST - dealing with many
client/facility combinations. No problem on a mainframe - just load your appropriate JCL
cards. PC - inter-active, jumping from client A1, A2, B7, G3, A10 in one session. Both F/J
and M/F have provided their own extensions to do this - CBL_FILE routines :- create
filenames, check exists, rename, delete, copy etc. The alternative - delve into Windows
searching and testing for the appropriate APIs.

(c) Screen Section - now a part of COBOL 2002. It is a minimum of 5 YEARS since I last used
Micro Focus Screen Section, and that looks entirely different to the Screen Section I used
with RM/COBOL 74. I had a quick look at the FCD and I see it does include 'vendor
implementations'. However - truly used in a PC environment, Screen Section is as useful as
udders on a bull. To use the M/F Screen Section back in DOS mode, I needed both
CBL-SCREEN_ATTRIBUTE routines and CBL_MOUSE routines. Without these you are left with a very
vanilla-flavoured Screen Section. (I wonder how useful Screen Section is/will be on
mainframes if there are no CBL_routines).

Those are the three that immediately spring to mind. There may be others, but those are the
'majors'. Yes they are very O/S specific - but without them you are seriously lumbered when
attempting COBOL at the PC level.

Still on the 'sad' note. I've noted before that you you have written '..... you

COBOL and Assembler. Had somebody offered the challenge of, say, 'produce COBOL

others would probably have accepted the challenge, and come up with new COBOL
syntax to accommodate this. Regrettably it is now too late.

You've offered an alternative to R/W - using OLE to get at MS Word - and of
course that's limited to the OO world, currently F/J and M/F, possibly VisualAge
(?). (Interestingly the concept of 'previewing' was a suggestion made by one N/E

again, PC_PRINT is non-standard.

The alternative now is to look upon COBOL (albeit we LOVE it), as a mongrel
language. Turn to other languages or packages that do things COBOL is just
incapable of doing. There is a LEGITIMATE reason for doing fonts, previewing
etc. as you describe - just a great pity that this isn't possible within the R/W
module.

Ignoring COBOL file handling, for which there are still legitimate uses - COBOL
like other languages with ESQL is able to access DBs - so in that respect COBOL
is not lacking. Given the COBOL files module - it is one up on the other
languages which don't have a built in feature and need other file-handling
packages apart from DB usage.

Note that while now part of COBOL 2002 - Stephen is not exactly jumping up and
down about either R/W or Screen Section. And of course, he didn't comment on
file-naming etc. - it just isn't the same problem on a mainframe. Seeing that
file-naming etc. is a part of both F/J and M/F, you would logically think this
is a requirement for the others that Stephen referred to - CA, AcuCobol, RM etc.

interested party has access to such media.  Not every COBOL programmer is
allowed access to the internet at work, not every parent wants to have
internet capability accessible to his children in his home, not every
government allows its citizenry unlimited access to the internet or to
e-mail or other similar mechanisms.  I do not believe it's appropriate to
presume that anyone with input that might be valuable to the future of COBOL
has access to the mechanisms proposed here, and would contend that the
requirement would discriminate against those whose only available
communications routes are exemplified by "snail mail" and diplomatic
courier.  I consider this a *serious* problem with the proposal.

Second, the proposal calls for a "timeboxed" approach.  This ties into 1)
above; a significantly longer "timebox" than you seem to envision might
mitigate my concerns somewhat.   Also, I believe an *incomplete* design for
any feature may well turn out to be worse than not having the feature at

convinced that Rick Smith's study of the EXIT rules, which he claims has
been going on for a year, was time ill-spent.  His study resulted in closure

leading to an error in the standard.   I can envision this process working
very well for projects in which the participants (and their expertise) are
well-defined, but in combination with a fluid and ill-defined workforce of
uncertain expertise and dedication, I question whether it can be presumed
effective.   With a "captive" (and paid) workforce, the participants in a
project can be presumed to be working on it at times decreed by project
management.  The "timebox" approach requires a fixed workforce of known
capability, I believe, and this work force is both volunteer and ad-hoc on a
moment-by-moment basis.  I cannot help but doubt the applicability of
"timebox" project management with such a fluid workforce of indeterminate
talent and expertise.
