re the topic of this thread, personally, I'm not into bashing anybody.

As for this report, it just makes me laugh that people are outraged and 
shocked at something which has been an industry practice since the late 
1960s.

I remember being quite shocked  (in the late 1960s) when we upgraded our 
mainframe to find that the upgrade actually took 5 minutes (removing the 
brick that was slowing down the processor) and then 9 hours to run 
diagnostics and check everything was OK. It was done on a Saturday morning 
and I happened to be at work. I was always interested in what the engineers 
had to do and the engineer who performed the upgrade was a friend. He 
explained what the process actually entailed. When I expressed my 
disapproval at what seemed like a shoddy practice he explained it like this:

" Let's say you've spent large sums of money inventing a processor chip that 
has amazing capabilities. It is expensive to produce but if you do large 
scale production the price falls significantly. There is only a limited 
market for the top end capabilities of your chip but a considerable market 
for less powerful versions of it. You can't sell the full capability for the 
same price as the limited one, because people are not stupid and they will 
simply buy the cheap version and use it for top end applications You COULD 
have a number of different versions of your chip so that there is a 
marketing range, but that means you need separate production runs and the 
costs for everybody across the range would be higher. Instead, we mass 
produce a single chip. Then we slow it down to provide a marketing range. 
Say models 10, 20, 30, 40 etc. The chip in the 10 is exactly the same chip 
as is in the 40 but it has a circuit board ('brick' in the trade) which 
limits its capability. You want an upgrade? we replace the brick with a 
different one that gives you the new model. We haven't charged you for the 
full capability of the chip so it isn't really unfair, and by doing this, we 
can keep the cost of production down so that everybody who buys our 
equipment (from top to bottom of the range) gets a benefit."

Whether you are persuaded by his argument or not, the fact is that every 
major manufacturer of computer equipment has implemented this approach in 
some form or another.

Before you decide to take a pair of pliers and upgrade your mainfame to the 
maximum possible, you might like to read the fine print on your contract... 
any unauthorised "engineering" means the equipment can be re-possessed by 
the vendor immediately (if you are leasing it) and all warranties and 
service agreements are rendered null and void (if you own it.)

Furthermore, some of the upgrades require more than just brick removal. 
(addition of new buses, memory etc. to support the new capability.

Intel are just doing what everybody else does (but nobody talks about...)

Pete.

Pete, this is a perfect description and there is nothing wrong with
it. The simple fact, that it is cheaper to build in the full
capability of a product and release the parts that the customer is
paying for just makes sense.

I can recall when I was a hardware engineer in the early 70's Univac
had a 9200 and a 9300 model. As far as I can recall, the processor
side of the upgrade - some features where not available to the 9200
model - was a jumper to be wired into the backpanel. For test
purposes, there was a switch and when the hardware engineer did forget
to set it back, the system was twice as fast.

The same would apply for systems based on micro code, a different
microcode would allow a completly different system with a completly
different pricing structure based on competitive situations. This was
true for the Unisys model 7E running the OS/3 OS and the same hardware
running MCP with a different microcode.
