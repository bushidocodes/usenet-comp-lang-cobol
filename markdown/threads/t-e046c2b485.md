Mainframes are boxes that are frequently the size of a small dining room
and may be cooled by water jackets, etcetera. Typically, these machines
are kept in air-conditioned rooms with constant temperature and
humidity. Disk drives are often the size of an oil drum and external to
the main cpu box. Mainframes are served by operators and may have
robotic cartridge loaders and tape drives the size of small wardrobes. 

>I come from an NT, PC, or servers with
>UNIX/Pentium setup. I keep
>hearing the word "legacy" on all the COBOL portals and user groups. What
>is this?
Legacy refers to systems coded in 3GL languages pre-dating the sexy
Java/VB/etcetera languages of more recent developments. Legacy systems
are generally robust and reliable. They are usually planned and
controlled developments. They have lasted for as long as 30 years and
have been subject to frequent amendments and upgrades. As such they are
a valuable and often little understood resource which companies can ill
afford to ditch. Regrettably, companies often see the sexier languages
as worthy of investment whilst ignoring the legacy systems that have
cost them millions to develop. Consequently, they often undertake re-
writes with little or no pre-planning and control. Result: money down
the drain, fragile bug ridden systems, loss of accuracy/functionality/re
liability, promotion for the development manager and headaches for the
maintenance manager....

ps. Cobol is too easy. Perhaps that is where we went wrong?

Wrongo, or That turns out not to be the case.
Your average "MainFrame" these days is about the size of your refridgerator.
It can have an Internal Raid array as well as access to "Mainframe standard"
external Raid arrays.

The biggest difference between NT, Unix etc is in the area of I/O.
"Mainframes" are designed to move truly massive amounts of data around.
They typically have 4 high speed light fibre paths to external raid arrays.
Thats per Operating system image. "Mainframes" can host up to 8 operating
system images per box (thats 32 high speed fibre channels to IO).
"Mainframes" can have up to 10 (I think at last count)
CPU chips working on the same memory and IO (called tightly coupled).
You can Couple up to 10 (I think) "Mainframe" boxes in a loosely coupled
environment. This typlically requires 1 or 2 extra "Mainframe"s with battery
backed up memory to act as traffic cops for the others. This is called Parallel
Sysplex.

Mainframes can share Fibre Channels, Memory, CPU's amongst the various host
operating systems running on them. You can change the mix of memory, channels
and CPU's given to each operating system. For CPU's, one or more CPU's can
be shared by one or more operating systems. You can control the overall level
of CPU utilization by each operating system. So you can CAP OPSYS1 at 20% and
OPSYS2 at 80% even though 3 CPU's are assigned to both operating systems.

You can run different operating systems on the same hardware simultaneously,
OS/390 (was MVS), VM and VSE.

If you want to check out more see HTTP://WWW.IBM.COM/S390

Multiple OS images can run on any combination of physically separate
mainframes or within multiple LPARs on a single mainframe.  The logical
isolation of processors and memory usage among LPARS is so total that
there is no effective difference is terms of OS reliability from the
separate mainframe case.  These OS images can be completely independent
(as in a Y2K test platform with no shared DASD or devices), or with
OS/390 (MVS) may be closely joined as a "sysplex" - sharing a
sychronized microsecond time-of-day clock, sharing DASD, and other
devices, and processing from a common workload, with dynamic routing of
incoming transactions based on the image with the most available
resources. A multi-image sysplex has the advantage that with proper
planning you can dynamically add or remove images from the sysplex with
little or no disruption of service.  The goal is to be able to upgrade
hardware one mainframe at a time, or Operating System software one OS
image at a time, while still maintaining a functional sysplex that
continues to process incoming transactions during the transition.
