I predict that TV and computer will move into each other. You will see TVs 
that handle email, connect to social networks, run GOOGLE searches and, 
eventually, allow you to run remote apps (probably from the cloud) and 
provided by suppliers like GOOGLE and Microsoft, just for openers.

Large, flat screen, TVs with surface technology will become computer 
terminals without you buying add-ons. In effect, your TV will become a large 
iPad... :-)

I don't have cable so I'm not sure what services are available here on it or 
what they cost. I agree that, for the most part, the free-to-air movies are 
crap, but then it is really a matter of opinion...somebody must like them or 
there'd be no point in airing them.

Recently I bought a new digital SLR (Nikon 3100) largely to do an assignment 
for a forthcoming book connected with the Rugby World Cup, which is being 
written by a friend of mine and he asked me to do the photography. Saturday 
morning found me up bright and early (a very rare occurrence; I generally 
don't do mornings... :-)) photographng the nine-year-olds playing Rugby at a 
local park. It was delightful. Early morning Winter sunshine, really hot 
coffee and kids whose enthusiasm and sportsmanship was an example to adults.

(Before getting into computers at age 20, I was a Press photographer with 
the local newspaper here, and I have had a passion for photography all my 
life. Before the digital age, I took slides and processed them in the 
garage...digital is much less messy :-).) Anyway, the new camera takes HD 
movies (limited by what can be stored on the flash card; usually about 10 
minutes) as well as photographs. I couldn't help smiling at how easy it all 
is now. Remove the HDSC card from the camera, insert into laptop, copy to 
backup, clear card, edit and review photos/movies with various photo 
processing software (no, I don't have Adobe Photoshop but I have 3 other 
packages that do everything I need), then connect to large screen TV over 
HDMI interface. Not a single chemical or solution required, no need for a 
thermometer or darkroom and the whole process done in minutes with the 
results viewable on TV... :-)

I never would have thought that TV would figure in my photography, but it 
does.

Who knows what other roles our TV screens will play in the future?

Pete.

That should have been 'late 70s'. The Apple II was 1978.

In 1979 ICL released a departmental mini called the ME29. This had as
part of the system an application that drove a Prestel service.
Televisions were being made, in the UK at least, with Teletext
decoders built in. Services such as CeeFax broadcast information in
the TV flyback delay time which could be displayed on screen. In fact
this is still being done.

A Prestel service used the Ceefax decoder in the TV but drove it from
a keyboard and dialup 1200/75 modem unit to a computer service. This
allowed the system to be interactive by entering queries or data and
getting responses back to your TV. As part of the support job for the
ME29 I developed some simple interactive demonstrations.
