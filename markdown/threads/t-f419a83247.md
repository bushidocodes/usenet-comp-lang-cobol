Sounds funny at first.  (I am assuming they are not thinking of being
I/O bound)

But as I think more it is a good question - whether to those managers or
to ISS people.  A business has two primary measurements for its
decisions:

1.  True/False:  We are complying with the law and government
regulations.
2.  What are the monetary costs and benefits of this choice.

Sometimes the choices are to make very efficient code, efficient use of
hardware, efficient maintenance, efficient upgrading, flexibility in
upgrading, ability to find good people, ability to keep good people,
ability to get a working tool to the money makers quickly, etc.

Measure your choice to use CoBOL in $$$ with criteria such as those in
mind.  Measure upgrading your disks the same way.

Ummm ... As I indicated earlier, this class wasn't geared to a very high
level of expertise in either business management or in IS technologies.
This was way before the Personal Computer revolution, and these
businessfolks were attempting to begin the process of considering the
possibility of automating *some* part of their accounting mechanisms, and to
figure out "What's a computer?  What does RAM mean?  What's a utility (and
how does one buy stock in it)?  How does a printer work?" and so forth.

Back then, the Personal Computer hadn't really taken off.  If you were
beyond the pen-and-ink stage, you either used a service bureau or you had
mag-stripe ledger machines from Burroughs or Monroe Computometer or NCR.
The folks in question were migrating from the mag-stripe level to the
files-on-disk level of automation to gain better control of their business
and better access to cash flow information.

These were shops like a family-run wholesale novelty distributor (daddy
bought the computer at sonny's insistence), a local long-haul truck
distributor, a construction equipment rental firm (also family-run, being
dragged into the Automation Age by two sons of the owner), and so forth.
The larger ones among them might have had as many as twenty or thirty
employees total ...

But those were different times.   Today, a similar-size business could
probably do just as much (and probably a lot more) on a minimum-price PC
running something like QuickBooks than they were able to accomplish with
home-grown (or even store-bought) code on a smaller mainframe back then.
And a WHOLE lot less expensively.

       -Chuck Stevens
