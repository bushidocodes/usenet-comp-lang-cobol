The US Public Review period on the "draft" COBOL Standard (FCD) ends
tomorrow (April 9, 2001 - at the "end of business" day).

As of my posting this note, there have been 38 (count them 38) public review
comments submitted.

Of these 5 (only 5 !!!) have been from people who are NOT "members" of the
COBOL Standards committees - either as "official observers," J4 members, or
WG4 delegates.

Of those 5 comments, none seems to be from "real programmers" who have even
read any of the "summary" lists (of enhancements, incompatibilities, etc) in
the FCD - but instead they have done "joint" comments on the reaffirmation
of the current Standard and for the next one "basically" saying that "COBOL
is important to us - please keep working on it".

My guesses (and they are only guesses) of WHY there have been so few "real
programmers" providing ANY input (good, bad, mixed, whatever) are as follows
(roughly in the order that I think they apply - although I suspect that it
is actually a combination of reasons).

1) Those who care about a "next" COBOL Standard are SO concerned about how
delayed this revision is, that they would agree to ANYTHING - just to get it
approved and implemented as soon as possible.

2) Many COBOL programmers/sites don't know when/if they will ever see an
implementation with all (most? any?) of these new enhancements.  They still
have '74 Standard COBOL programs in production; their programmers don't
"use" any of the new stuff - even when available; and/or it was ONLY Y2K
that got their shop to actually upgrade to an '85 Standard compiler - so
they don't expect to see a 200x compiler until 2010 or so.

3) COBOL users think that the standards group are made up of experts who
"know what is best for them" - and they MUST be putting stuff in that the
users will want to use - and that they will be defined in the BEST possible
way.  Therefore, there is no use for a "mere" programmer to look at this
draft because it MUST be the best possible way for COBOL to be in the
"future".

4) People have been hearing about the enhancements in the draft Standard for
years now.  From what they have seen, it is the "best thing since sliced
bread" - and they wouldn't change a THING about it - even if they could.

5) "Total Apathy" about revisions to COBOL - especially what is in a
"Standard" instead of in THEIR "implementation".  (This may actually be the
most likely reason - but I always HOPE that it isn't - no matter how many
times I am proved wrong.)

  ***

I did an article in CobolReport on how to "review" the draft Standard - that
pointed out how to tell what was KNOWN to be incompatible with the current
Standard (and many implementations) - as well as to "review" what has been
added as "enhancements" in this draft.  The fact that "real users" who are
not on/involved with the standards committees have NOT actually submitted
*any* substantive comments disturbs me and surprises me (a little - not too
much).

One thing that did come thru in the MANY comments that have come in
concerning the "reaffirmation" of the current ANSI (not ISO) COBOL Standard
is how important portability is for COBOL programmers.  Therefore, let me
point out just a couple of things that add to my questioning WHY people
aren't commenting on the FCD.

A) In the draft Standard, there is the following statement (my ABSOLUTE
least favorite thing about the FCD),

 "The decision of whether to claim support for a processor-dependent
language element is within an implementor's discretion. Factors that may be
considered include, but are not limited to, hardware capability, software
capability, and market positioning of the processor."

Note Well:
   I don't know which I hate more relying on "market positioning" in a COBOL
Standard *or* the fact that implementors don't even need to tell WHY they
aren't supporting these things.

Note Well-2:
 J4 and WG4 have *explicitly* rejected the thought of calling this
"optional" as the '85 Standard does - and instead have "mixed" optional and
hardware/software dependent items and made them ALL "optional" in all but
name.

B) Some interesting "statistics" comparing:
   - The "base" '85 Standard (X3.23-1985)
  - The '85 Standard with both amendments (X3.23-1986 + X3.23a-1989 +
X3.23b-1993)
  - The current draft of the next COBOL Standard (FCD)

I -  Implementor-Defined Language Element
    a)  Base '85 Standard - 80 items
    b) '85 Standard with Amendments - now 87 items
    c)  FCD - 218 items      *>> note this number particularly !!!

II - Undefined Language Element List
    a) Base '85 Standard - 28 items
    b) '85 Standard with Amendments -  now 36 items
    c) - FCD - 65 items

III -  Hardware Dependent Language Element List  & Optional Modules ('85
Standard vs Processor Dependent (FCD)
    a) Base '85 Standard: Hardware dependent - 20 items; 4 optional modules
    b) '85 Standard with Amendments: Hardware dependent -(still 20 items),
now 5 optional modules
    c) FCD - 30 items (includes both "individual" features and "module-type"
items)

For those who think that almost doubling the "undefined" list and MORE than
doubling the "implementor defined" list is "reasonable" because the new
Standard adds so many more "new" goodies, look at the following two
comparisons.

IV - Substantive changes potentially affecting (incompatible changes from
prior revision)
    a) Base '85 Standard - 42 items
    b) '85 Standard with Amendments - now  66 items
    c) FCD - 41 items

V - Substantive changes not affecting (incompatible changes from prior
revision)
    a) Base '85 Standard -  107 items
    b) '85 Standard with Amendments - now 113 items
    c) FCD - 147 items

The only good news (IMHO) is that the number of "incompatible" NEW changes
in the FCD is about the same as those in the '85 Standard (remember what it
was like moving from an '74 Standard compiler to an '85 Standard one - at
least this time it won't be much worse).  However, the fact that there were
24 ADDITIONAL "incompatible" changes in the amendments to the '85 Standard
might give users something to worry about as the new Standard gets
implemented - much less enhanced.

    ***

In "short":

 - If you live/work outside the US, check with your national body to see
when Public Review comments are due there.  You may still have "plenty" of
time to get your input in (again - it can be "good" "bad" or "detailed")

- If you live/work inside the US, then you CAN still get any comments that
you have in by the end of the day, Monday, April 9.

- If you live/work inside the US and cannot get your comments in by April
9 - but do have some, you can still send them in (especially if they get
there as "soon" after April 9 as possible).  Although ANSI (J4) are not
REQUIRED to process "late" comments, they often do.  The next J4 meeting is
scheduled to start April 22, 2001.  My *guess* is that any "detailed"
comments (suggestions, corrections, deletions - but NOT requests for
additions) that are received before that will be "looked at" - and if a
"valid" problem will actually be submitted by the US to ISO.

   ***

Finally, "once upon a time and long ago," - I was the project leader for the
GUIDE (now defunct IBM mainframe user group) COBOL project.  I had a "real"
reputation for raising issues/problems that none of the other project
members "understood" or took action on when I raised them.  The reason was
that I kept (trying) to look at the "ramifications" for things that were
"planned" or "previewed" - while most of the project was dealing with how to
get "today's" or "yesterday's" programs and compilers to work.  (A
"work-around" was better than a fix - if the "work-around" worked today -
and the "fix" would be available in a few weeks/months/releases).

There was no way then (nor is there any way now) that I can "force" COBOL
programmers to "look ahead" at what will happen to them, their programs, and
their compilers based on what is in a "draft Standard".  However, I can
"beg" you to at least be aware now - and spend as many resources on
"prevention of possible problems" as you do in "complaining about
implementations that you must live with."

submission includes the changes to the Open Source or GNU style code).

Why?
Well look at the history of the Cobol group.
They missed adding verbs for non-gui screens until GUI arrived.
They missed adding verbs for gui windows etc until ... well they still
haven't done anything.
They missed adding verbs for HTML and WEB interactions.
They missed adding verbs for including Java and Javascript.
They missed (and this is a biggie) adding verbs for relational databases
EVEN THOUGH SQL is a SETTLED AND STANDARD database language.
They missed adding verbs for transaction and process oriented tasks, and
this is another biggie, even though roughly 80% of the cobol code is
involved in online money transactions.
They added OO - which is now being used as an excuse to not add other
verbs because of so called standard OO libraries of function. As if we
haven't already got non-OO standard libraries.
Need I continue?

The only solution to this is to allow the language to evolve via
something like what Linux and it's various tools and programs are going
through now. A Massive increase in function - but still done in a
standard way by dint of all those concerned agreeing on the approach and
interfaces. (which is what a standard is isn't it?)

This is a far cry from the current situation where several competing
companies produce compilers that they make EVERY attempt to be different
from each other so that they can lock their users into their product.

These basic infrastructure items will never fulfill the requirement of
write once run anywhere until the programming languages and the
operating systems are removed from the arena of comercial competition.
