Mentioning you are new, and asking for advice, is like mentioning you grow
roses to someone who raises horses -- most old programmers have lots, and
they'll give it to you for free if you back your truck up to the barn.

COBOL is wordy, but after the first program you don't have to write as
much; you just copy. In the standard business world, there are only four or
five basic structures.
Think of those books they sell in the supermarkets that have a dashing,
darkly handsome fellow on the cover. He's usually sweeping some lady in a
long dress. They only have one plot, but a million variations. If you
pulled one into WORD and changed "Derek" to "Arthur", "Samantha" to
"Louise", and "Castlecrest" to "Valley Home", then tried to publish it,
you'd be guilty of plagarism. If you do the same in a COBOL shop you've
adhered to standards, saved time, and proven you know how to code
efficiently.

Some of the better organized shops have a file of skeletons. If you want to
write a program that, for instance, reads a file and produces a report with
three levels of subtotals, you pull template #6 into your text editor,
change the file names and compile it. It takes about 30 - 40 minutes to
write, compile and test the program.

You can't use the same word twice for different things, but if you are
clever you can come close. For instance, if you have two files (master and
history, for instance) and one screen (from an on-line transaction), all
with name, rank and serial number, they Could be named

HST-FIRST-NAME
HST-LAST-NAME
HST-RANK
HST-SERIAL-NUM

MAS-FIRST-NAME
MAS-LAST-NAME
MAS-RANK
MAS-SERIAL-NUM

INP-FIRST-NAME . . .

You probably see a pattern. Typing is boring; if you have a decent editor,
you can copy faster than you can type. Use the same suffix for fields that
are the same thing in different places. It saves time and makes your code
easier to read.

Other random advice:

Make sure your years are four digits whenever possible.

Don't copy programs in school; it is considered unfair. Things change when
you get into the business world.

If you run into a programmer, ask to read some of his/her programs, just to
get a feel for what the code is like. He/she would probably be honored. If
your parents work in a large company, they may know one.

Good luck.

Ted··.@the··n.net
