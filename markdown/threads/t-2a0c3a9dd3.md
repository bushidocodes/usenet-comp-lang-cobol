I certainly do know what NFC is, and I would never use it to pay for anything. But then I rarely use a credit card and I sign for it and don't even have a PIN.

It is a matter of authority. My signature is the way that I choose to assert my authority, not some easily stolen number, and certainly not some automatic authorization merely by having a particular device, or a copy of it.

While it is a benefit to a retailer is is of zero advantage to me. 'Easy' is not a way that I want for spending my money, I want that to be as hard as possible, mainly to prevent others being able to do it (with _my_ money).

Retailers have no downside. Once a transaction is made with NFC then they are paid even if the device was used fraudulently. They, or many of them, also do their best to take payment with stolen credit cards - they want the transaction and to make their profit and care not about the card owner's problem with their bank.

However, NFC is useful beyond retail (and _without_ a wallet). The latest Panasonic cameras can connect to a phone or tablet by using NFC to establish a WiFi connection (with the camera as the host). This allows the phone/tablet to be used to control the camera, take photos, view immediately, upload to the internet or PC. Of course you can also connect without NFC by selecting the WiFi connection*.


* My camera does not have the NFC feature but I do control it from a phone.

I've been keeping an oculus borrowed from Polyphemus on these phenomena
and noticed several years ago how people in the local shopping-mall would
discuss going into stores, examining merchandise and then buying it for a
lower price online. (I learned of the term 'showrooming' from The
Economist and find it to be succinct but under-used)

There is, as yet, no way around this kind of payment minimisation of which
I am aware; it is the old habit of 'comparison shopping' where one set of
merchants has a price-advantage incurred by a lack of physical presence
but an incentive-disadvantage of being unable to have the article
physically examined (and sales-volk to apply subtle pressures such as
'upselling'). For books this trade-off is minimal; the latest penny
dreadful by Authorus Formulaicus is more-or-less the same whether you can
glance at the binding before buying but something as simple as a hammer
should be hand-tested to see if it fits one's swing.

Then there are the social aspects of 'going to market' or taking one's
colleague along for 'another set of eyes' that come into play... there are
both economic and psychological aspects, each more complex than the
superficial 'they just want the lowest price'. (See studies of end-cap
sales in supermarkets for an example).
