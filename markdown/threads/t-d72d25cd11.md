In 1983 Paul Allen gave a talk at COMDEC (I think it was) about 'the

When MS did add DOSKEY to MS-DOS 5 it was not installed and was barely

Windows 95/98 also had DOSKEY (I think the name was different though)
that was not even mentioned in the manual or help.

The aim seemed to be to make the command line the worst experience
possible. DOSKEY was not particularly good but it did give some basic
facilities such as recall and line editing. Without it if you made a

DR-DOS was always more useful. While MS-DOS 5 caught up to DR-DOS 5 it
did so nearly two years later. Then DR-DOS 6 was a year ahead of MS's
catch up 6. Even the little known DR-DOS 3.3 and 3.4 was better than
MS-DOS 3.3 (and 4.01) as it could support large disks while MS still
had the 32MByte barrier.

DRI's FDISK also had the ability to set different cluster sizes. FAT
is a particularly poor file system for random file access. So COBOL
indexed files were very slow. To access a specific block the system
had to start at the directory entry and follow the chain of FAT
entries to find the required clusters. By formatting the disk with
clusters that were 8Kb instead of 2Kb that MS-DOS did, random access
to largish (~1Mbyte) indexed files was 3 times faster.

Another complaint against poor features in MS-DOS (and Win9x) when
running COBOL systems was that record locking was done using SHARE. If
the program locked a record and then crashed or was killed the lock
stayed even though the process was no longer running. Start the
program again and it reported the record was locked. A reboot was
necessary.

The way that 'competition' worked was that MS signed up its OEMs to
the illegal (as determined later in court) Per-Box pricing scheme. In
order to get _any_ MS software the OEM had to pay MS for every box
sold regardless of what OS was installed (or none). So a DR-DOS
machine still had to pay for MS-DOS as well.

MS also illegally (as determined later in court) bundled MS-DOS and
Windows 3.11 as one package so killing any DR-DOS market.

You may call that 'competition' but then so is burning down your
competitor's premises.

It's sad that you stopped, Doc. I can understand and relate to it, though.

I went to see Andre Sovia and never touched a guitar again for over six 
months. (Normally I play at least 3 or 4 times a week.)

When you see true masters you realise you are probably never going to be 
that good.

But there's another important realization too: Music is NOT a contest.

I have a friend who runs a software house in Australia. He frequently visits 
New Zealand and usually stays at my house for a few days when he is in the 
area. He always brings a guitar (although I have several here for visitors) 
and we play together. It is a real delight and I enjoy hearing him and 
playing.

On one occasion he was struggling with a song and he stopped and said: 
"Bugger! I'll never be as good as you, Pete." It was in that moment that I 
realized it really isn't a contest. I said as much at the time and helped 
him with the rough patch and he now plays the song proficiently. The old 
school chum I mentioned (we used to play together when we were 13, and were 
both in rock and surfing bands by the time we were 16) has helped me with 
chord progressions and is a much more capable guitarist than I. We stilll 
have great fun when we play and I am not intimidated by his superior skill; 
I celebrate it, enjoy it, and learn from him. Most importantly, we both have 
fun when we play.

I recently wrote about this at some length in connection with the Tauranga 
Acoustic Music club (which I try to support.).

The following is long, and way off topic, but it is important to me and some 
of you will relate to it. Besides, life is about leisure as well as work...

================= skip this if you have no soul :-) 
=============================================

Music for me is a joy. Playing it is a satisfying release and expression. I 
taught myself ukulele when I was 10 and have been playing guitars ever 
since. It has been a comfort to me when I have been alone in strange lands 
and it has enabled me to communicate with people whose language I could not 
speak. Always, whether alone or with others, playing music is a joy. It 
should leave you buzzing and happy.

I see two parts to the playing of music:

1. The acquisition of skill. This is something that goes on throughout your 
life. These are the "hard yards". Hundreds (thousands ?) of hours of getting 
your fingers to be able to instantly form chord shapes and learning theory 
of music so you can extend what you already know.

2. The playing and exercise of what you acquired in 1 above. This is where 
you make the music your own. There is an exuberance and release in playing 
it that I can only define as "joy". I sit and play on a Sunday afternoon and 
sometimes three or four hours can go by and it seems like 20 minutes. (I 
have very long-suffering neighbours, but I respect them and don't play after 
10 at night. I told them all that if it bothers them, to let me know, and I 
can do down the beach and do it. the response was: "Pete, we love to hear 
you.") At the end of one of these sessions I am drained but delighted and 
happy. I usually need to go and take a shower and change my shirt because 
the nervous energy and excitement makes me sweat.

So, two parts. the "hard yards" and the "fun".

Now, sometimes the hard yards are really hard and we become intense and 
focused. It is very serious business as we translate dots and/or chords on 
paper into a melody or accompaniment. We work for hours learning the new 
song and playing it off the paper. It becomes easier with repetition and 
gradually we acquire the new piece and add it to the repertoire. For some 
people, once they have mastered it, that is enough. They pull out the piece 
of paper and play the song just like they practised it. It is pleasant, but 
it is mechanical. If the skill level is high it will sound accomplished, but 
it doesn't have fire or feeling in it and there is no real effort to make it 
their own.

For others, (and I am one), it isn't enough to be able to play the song; I 
want to own it. I want to feel what the person who wrote it felt and 
understand why they wrote it. I want it to be as comfortable and second 
nature to me as if I had written it.  I'll change the original and rearrange 
it until that is achieved, always respecting the author, but not being 
confined by what he/she wrote. I won't play it off a piece of paper because 
that would mean I can only play it one way (and besides, doing that is for 
practice at home, not for performance.). Probably, it will be slightly 
different every time I play it. It is mine; I can do what I like with it.

(Obviously, if you do this, it is more difficult for people to accompany 
you. It requires more skill. Part of learning to interpret your own 
repertoire, is learning to fit in with someone else's performance and that 
is where jamming comes in.)

So now we come to jamming.

Jamming is not a place to be doing the hard yards. It isn't a practice or 
rehearsal, it is a celebration of what you have achieved. (That doesn't mean 
showing off or "look at me."; rather it is about putting together what you 
know on the spur of the moment to enhance someone else's performance or 
simply to share in it.) There is no "rude" or "all about courtesy" in 
jamming. (courtesy should always be a given, in ANYTHING.)  It is simply 
fun. You should be able to do something on the spur of the moment, find it 
doesn't work, laugh about it, try something else. It ISN'T rude if someone 
plays a guitar solo while you are singing; if you think it is, then you are 
being too "precious" about your singing.  You are gathered together to play 
music and have fun. It ISN'T a "performance" with a possibly critical 
audience (who paid money to listen.) making judgements about it. The person 
playing the guitar solo is having fun and trying to see what works or doesn't. 
The person singing is having fun expressing the song and learning to focus 
and project so that unaccustomed accompaniments are not off-putting. (If you 
are focused and into the song, could someone's lead guitar background REALLY 
put you off? It possibly could if it was outstanding. I'd want to stop and 
listen to it. :-).  I remember as a teenager playing with Robbie Laven (his 
brother Artie and I were best mates at school). Even then, Robbie had a 
shining talent that used to make me gasp. He would play lead while I sang, 
and part of me just wanted to listen to what he was doing. . It was a very 
good discipline.)

So, in jamming everybody gets to contribute and in doing so they have fun 
and they also learn something, without doing the hard yards that real 
practice entails.

Try not to lose sight of the main purpose in jamming. It is about having 
fun. Incidental benefits are that learning also occurs. Some things work and 
some things don't. It really doesn't matter.

I have played music most of my life. I have performed on radio, appeared on 
TV, had people pay money to listen to me (that always amazed me. :-)), 
busked in the street for a living (and done very well out of it), played in 
the park for free on Sundays and attracted quite a large crowd (couple of 
hundred people) without any electronic assistance, I've played in bands, and 
by myself, played in London pubs and had regular customers who'd come back 
to hear me every week. it isn't because I have a great voice or am a great 
musician (I have no illusions about that. :-)); it is because I LOVE doing 
it and I care about what I play (even when it is trivial rubbish) and THAT 
strikes a chord in the people watching. They start to experience some of the 
same joy in the music that I am feeling.  I simply can't imagine NOT doing 
it, and ALWAYS it has been a delight.

There is joy in music. People who see it as something very serious and hard 
work and it has to be "right" are simply not getting it. (There IS hard work 
and it DOES need to be right if people are paying to hear it, but that isn't 
why we play it and that is the last thing that should be on anyone's mind in 
a jam session.)

Finally, I'd like to tell you about a couple of occasions when music really 
helped in my life.

1. I arrived in Thailand after a very tiring flight from Oz, at 1 in the 
morning. I declined the taxi driver's offer of finding me a woman (way too 
tired. :-)) and was amused when he then asked: "You want little boy?" All I 
wanted was sleep. The next morning I awoke feeling refreshed but a stranger 
in a strange land. I was going through a hard time emotionally and felt down 
and miserable. I had a 12 string Yamaha with me and decided some music might 
cheer me up. After about half an hour I was feeling much better and about 
ready to go sightseeing, when the maid who did the room knocked on the door 
and asked if her and her friend could listen, as they were finishing their 
shift. I was glad of the company, so they came in and I played a couple of 
songs. Then one of them asked if she could get some friends and the next 
thing there were about 8 people (mainly off-duty hotel staff) in my room 
eating refreshments they had brought and drinking fruit juice and beer and 
generally appreciating the music. They tried to sing along wherever they 
could but with their English not being great and the Asian ear for Western 
music, the results were pretty appalling :-). Never mind, we all had fun and 
by now I was buzzing. I decided to ask them if they had any requests and one 
of them said: "You pray 'Tell Rora I Rove her' " Wouldn't be my choice but 
these people had lifted my spirits and it was the least I could do. I duly 
started with the ominous, almost spoken, intro. "Tommy and Laura were 
lovers. He wanted to give her everything."  they were sitting on the bed and 
on the floor, their eyes gleaming, hanging on every word. :-) Then we came 
to the chorus. they put their heads back, closed their eyes and meowed in 
unison: TELL RORA I ROVE HER!... It was absolutely hysterical and it took me 
a great effort not to burst out laughing. We finished the impromptu party 
and I went sightseeing but I learned something about music that day. It isn't 
about how good you are; music is not a contest. It is about what you feel 
and about enjoying it to the utmost.

2. I was living in a little village (Nagarkot) about 8000 feet up in the 
Himalayan foothills. Almost every day I used to walk 15 KM down the winding 
mountain tracks (no roads) into the Kathmandu valley to the ancient town of 
Bhaktipur, to see if there was any mail. :-). I had dropped the Yamaha 
mentioned above and broken a machine head on it. A local artisan in 
Bhaktipur who dealt in musical instruments had offered to fix it and I left 
it with him for a few days while he waited for a new machine head to arrive. 
On this day, I was going to collect it and then check the mail. It was my 
custom to have lunch in the bazaar then get out of the midday heat by going 
to a Buddhist temple for a little quiet contemplation. I picked up the 
guitar, noted the man had done a terrific  job (it was like new) and paid 
him, then wended my way towards a nearby temple. On the steps a saffron clad 
monk was playing a musical instrument, unlike anything I had ever seen. It 
was played with a bow, like a violin but it was similar to a many stringed 
banjo or ukulele. (I found out later it was called a "sarangai" - 
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarangi ). He saw me approaching with a guitar 
case and grinned a welcome. I sat down on the steps and took out the 12 
string. He started playing something that sounded like an Irish jig crossed 
with Country & Western :-) (I know. but it is really hard to describe.) I 
played an accompaniment and then he played an accompaniment and tossed me 
the fairly simple little melody. People began to gather. Children and their 
mothers started dancing. The monk and I were "chatting" in music and this 
went on for over an hour. I have never forgotten the sheer exhilaration of 
that session. We ended up with around 60 people all laughing and dancing and 
sometimes clapping time. It made all the hours of "hard yards" worthwhile 
just to be able to participate in that one single event. At the end I was 
drained but sublimely happy. I put the guitar back in the case and moved to 
go but the monk indicated to wait. He disappeared and returned a few moments 
later with some of his brothers, one of whom spoke a little English. They 
invited me for tea and I gladly accepted. It was a fairy tale experience and 
I shall never forget it.

I saw how the joy of music transcends culture and language barriers. It is 
something innate in all of us. Technique and skill, are useful inasmuch as 
they broaden the boundaries in which we can express ourselves, but they are 
not the end point, or even the primary goal. A learner playing four chords 
on guitar can be just as enriched by the experience as a Master like 
Townsend, Clapton, Hendrix, or Segovia.

It is really quite wrong (at least, in my opinion) to get bogged down in the 
mechanics of music. Don't worry if it "isn't like the record" or the timing 
is different or there are new harmonies there; play it, experience it, own 
it,  ENJOY IT!

=========================  end of statement on "joy of music" 
============================

Although playing music has been an essential part of my life I realise that 
eventually arthritis will rob me of this simple pleasure (Not yet, but I get 
the occasional twinge in the fingers as a warning...) That was why I 
splashed out and bought the Taylor; might as well enjoy the time I have left 
to the utmost...

For millions of people, simply listening to music is pleasure enough. Most 
of us have favourite artists and genres and modern technology allows us to 
carry them with us.

Oddly enough, as I wrote that last sentence, I realised that when I'm 
programming, I prefer quiet. Never realised that before. I have worked with 
folks who would be writing code and considered their iPod to be a vital 
productivity aid. :-)

Not me.

Perhaps, at some level, I see music as being "not work" and programming as 
having its own creative rewards and satisfactions.

I don't know, but it is very quiet around here when I am coding. :-)

Pete.
