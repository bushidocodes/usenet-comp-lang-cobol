it's a problem because eons ago, system designers stored the date
carried by the OS (for all the applications that need it), in 2 digit
(YY) format to save the 16 bits that the other 2 digits (CC) would
occupy. this was true of the IBM mainframe, and every big business
app is written on that platform; COBOL being the dominant development
language.

And big business pays big.

For someone out of school, I wouldn't fathom a pay rate. Pass your
resume on to a headhunter. rates are different depending on where you
work/live. you'll have a better shot if you have some business
experience than if you're right out of school, with none. I started
in operations to get into systems, with a systems degree.

I can tell you don't bother looking in RI. I have 8 years of most
everything that is in demand, and still only bring up the bottom of
the scale you mentioned.

if you read ANY newspaper or magazine article about y2k, you'll get
the same story. they DO do a good job of explaining it.

Just a note: the trend I've seen from reading ComputerWorld and stuff,
and talking to mf people, is that mainframe shops are tending to hire
people who are already experienced mf COBOL developers, who have been
downsized or whatever and want jobs. They're not as interested in
hiring fresh-out-of-school youngsters. I think it would be extremely
hard now to "break in" unless you could hone your skills on a real
mainframe (at college or something), because a theoretical knowledge of
COBOL w/no real world expertise wouldn't get you very far. I'm sure
there are still entry level jobs, but I bet they're fewer, harder
to get, and pay less.

The advice I hear from mf people is to be like a utility infielder
and be able to handle all aspects of the development process: know
JCL, REXX, CLISTS, ALC, and other stuff and be well-rounded, not just
a COBOL expert.

Scott

PS: This does not seem to be a good time to begin a college education
to be a COBOL programmer. If you graduated in 2-4 years from right
now, the year 2000 stuff would peak, but after that the demand for
COBOL programmers would level off considerably and you'd probably
get laid off in 01 or 02. Once the conversion is done, the companies
with the 2000 problem will cut back to maintennance levels of
programmers!

Barbara, Its is not a stupid question, It all comes down to money.
Over the years, computer year dates (i.e.: 1995) have been stored as
95 saving disk space.
Many would say this has been short sighted on our part but it has not
been. Over the long run Millions of dollars have been saved in hardware
cost
in storing data this way. The millions it will cost to fix this will not
take away
from the millions already saved. The real problem is that corporate
America
( the bean counters in the corporation) do not like any thing that takes
big
bucks from the bottom line. Cost savings that occurred over years are so
easily
forgotten about.

Today the year 2000 problem is only a problem because corporate
America has waited so long to fix a problem. This problem will not go away
and we can not move the date to fix it.
Today, if you are just starting to fix this problem you have started to
late.
If you just started to fix this problem then the $1 per line of code might
be to low.
If you have already fixed the problem then the cost estimate may have been
to high.
However the answer to your question Barbara is that the year 2000 problem
cost is high.

Also exist problems in date calculations exist.
(i.e.: the year is 2001, a stored birth date is 01/01/00.
is that person 1 year old or 101 years old? without knowing the century how
would
you know?) Storage of the century in dates of systems created after 1994
has been foolish and short sighted, this has muddy the problem somewhat for
the
cost of fixing these systems, cost are so much higher than cost from a
system from
1984 (again the money thing).

If you like to know more, there is a newsgroup out there on the problem,
and several
web sites that can help.
