Hi:

Remember first that COBOL is a business language. It is not
intended for streaming media, network routing, or other
complicated stuff. It IS intended for payroll, accounting,
and every other type of ordinary business application.

COBOL by itself is crippled since it does not have a GUI.
However, there are many add-ons to COBOL such as my favorite,
Flexus SP2 which lets you do virtually anything allowed by
the Windows API including pictures and sound. There are
also things which let your COBOL programs be thin clients,
deal with the Windows Print Manager, and so on.

You can do virtually anything with COBOL and you can do it
easier and faster than with any other language.

Don't let anybody turn you off COBOL. You should check out
Flexus at Flexus.com.  You might also buy Thane Hubbell's book
'Teach yourself COBOL in 24 hours' - it comes with a Fujitsu
compiler and SP2. Thane is very active in the forum and can
help you a lot if you get stuck.

Thanks

In general, you can write anything with any general purpose language,
so COBOL isn't 'unique' in that respect.  But COBOL (COmmon Business
Oriented Language) was designed for the common bread and butter business
applications, such as payroll, general ledger, accounts payable, and
any application that handles money.  COBOL has powerful capabilities
in data formatting and printing.  Unlike most programming languages,
COBOL easily supports decimal numbers, including decimal fractions
(e.g. 123.456) exactly.  For what COBOL was designed for, nothing else
is quite as good.  There are COBOL programs still running after more
than 30 years.  COBOL is easy to learn and program, is simple rather
than complex and obscure, is efficient and reliable.  In other words,
COBOL is very cost effective, for what it is designed for, which is
why there is significantly more COBOL code in use today than in all
other computer languages put together.  But COBOL was never intended
for writing system programs, such as operating systems, compilers or
word processors.  Other languages are much better for those purposes.
But newer versions of COBOL can be used for GUI and web applications
very efficiently.

First, CoBOL is not a database, it is a language.

Second, with any language or project, taking extra time up front to plan isn't
necessarily a bad thing.  In fact studies have shown that the total amount of time
spent in analysis plus the total time spent in testing is a constant.

Third, the main advantage CoBOL has is that it is easy to read and to maintain.

pseudo-English syntax.

long time, and the last study I read (two years ago), showed that it was still the
most common language in new code.  I do not recommend using it to write device
drivers and the like.  It was designed for business applications, and that is what
it is used most for.
