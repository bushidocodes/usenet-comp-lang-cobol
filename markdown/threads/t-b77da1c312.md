I did this once in COBOL, and the general approach is to convert each
date into an absolute number of days since some base date, then subtract
the smaller number from the larger number. This is a little easier said
than done.

First of all, you need a routine to convert a gregorian date (day &
month) into a Julian date (days since beginning of year). Then you need
a routine to convert the year into a number of days since the selected
base date. Many routines assumed a base date of 01 JAN 1900, which will
cause you Year2000 problems.

It is probably better to assume a base date of 01/01/1601 A.D. Then you
need to subtract out or divide out the whole-year periods. The routine I
used assumed a base-date of 01/01/0001, but I was showing off.

The 400-year cycle beginning 01 JAN 1601 and ending 31 DEC 2000 has
146,097 days in it. Note that 146,097 is evenly divisible by 7! This
means that 01 JAN 1601 was a Monday, and 01 JAN 2001 will also
be a Monday. 01 JAN 2000 will be a Saturday, and everyone will be hung
over from New Year's even partying when all the computers break down on
the weekend.

If you have subtracted out all 400-year cycles, each 100-year cycle
remaining has 36,524 days. Once you have subtracted out those, then each
whole 4-year cycle has 1,461 days, then the remaining whole years have
365 days. Add your julian days to that total.

This was probably not a terribly elegant solution, but it worked. I
found it a little more taxing to convert absolute number of days back to
a julian or gregorian date. That routine will come in handy if you
need to generate a new date by adding a count to an existing date. Note
also that once you have converted a date to an absolute number of days
from a base date, it is fairly trivial to divide by seven, and take your
remainder to get the day of the week.

Perhaps someone will post a more elegant mathematical solution.

Good luck!
