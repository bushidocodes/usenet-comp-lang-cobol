paragraph from the agreement and stand firm.

First off : I hope you realise that the clause says that if the
company determines your work is unsatisfactory at any point
they can force you to return all of the money you've been paid.
Let's play a little wiht this job. Suppose you work for nine months,
then suddenly the Client says that only the first month was preformed
satisfactory you have to pay them back ( at least thats what the
clause says ) for the last eight months salary. This can even be used
as blackmail. Say you've been there for nine months, and you hit a
snag: as an example something that happened to a friend of mine, he
did VB work with some C dlls. They wanted him to redo the C in VB.
He kept insisting that in VB that part would be too slow. Eventually
they parted ways on this issue, if not friendly then at least
amicably. If this clause was part of that contract they could say that
since he was leaving  with the project unfinished, his work during
that period was unsatisfactory. Thus he would be forced to pay back
the money ( probably not legally, but they can make you sweat it).
Thus they can blackmail you into doing things against your ethics.

Another thing that can happen is that they fall very far behind, or
start to reneg on things in the contract (such as vactaion pay ). They
get so bad about this that you walk out. When you try to sue them for
back pay, they make the same claim-- work unfinished implies job
preformance unsatisfactory. Since they haven't paid you it will be
even harder to get their money.

No matter what the agency says, I would insist on something in writing
from the client concerning what is and is not satisfactory. If the
client is seriously worried, then I would suggest that you make some
kind of procedure for drawing up a timetable. Don't plan for more then
a month ahead ( and leave plenty of wiggle room ). Easiest is to just
have a meeting once a month where you have a schedual for the next
month and you both sign off on it. Remember also that you have to
allow for modification of the schedual for emergencies. Example
in my last job, I would often get a call about a bug. Sometimes it
turned out to be a bug of users of my stuff ( COM objects that used
files to communicate with embedded sopftware. I wrote the COM objects
), the embedded side often created corrupt files, which I had to
analyse, or my own. In any case they would often shoot a one/two day
hole in my schedual. I don't mind as long as management makes
allowances for these holes.

Finally. In your reply to the agent I would say that you spoke to a
lawyer ( you might actually want to do this ), and his reaction was
that it was not solid and fair for all parties. Furthermore even if it
was, it would not be acceptable to you which is all that counts.
Oh yes, and insist that he and the client both submit to you in
writing what they expect your rate, and the rate the client pays to
be. If he complains point out it is in the contract workers bill of
rights.
