To be truthful, Cobol programming is probably not a good choice, since it
is closely tied to the life of IBM mainframes, which is on a long, inevitable
decline (IMO). Also, even if the IBM mf were to continue indefinitely, it
requires far more than COBOL to work in this market. You have to deal
with ISPF, JCL, Panvalet/Endevor, DB2, IMS/CICS, Linkage Editors, etc.
Plus the "ladder" of positions is more established and time-consuming
to climb in this industry.

The PC community is on a track of rapid, sustained growth. It also has
very few people with any "depth" in software development. This doesn't
mean they are inferior, just younger and less experienced. Also, Microsoft
clearly "owns" this market and is only getting stronger every year. BASIC
is their chosen corporate-wide strategic language, VisualBasic is one of
the hottest products in the PC marketplace, and is also one of the easiest
environments to learn in software development.
Therefore, it is a good place to start (or stay) and to see how you like
developing. The downside to this market is that it is full of kids who also
are chomping at the bit to get in, it changes on a daily basis, and you have
to deal with all sorts of hardware-level stuff that a COBOL developer
doesn't.
You also need to decide on a more specific direction:
-business?
-multimedia?
-independent vendor?
-communications?
-etc?

That's my opinion, Harold

Programming is like all other fields: people with experience are most in
demand; the question is how do you get hired to get the experience? I
started school for programming at 39 and finished when 40. I was in
Hartford, CT, which was running a programmer deficiency. The insurance
companies were gobbling up programmers as fast as they could be trained.
I was lucky because age and inexperience can be a big handicap. But, I
also worked as a head-hunter for a while and can tell you that if you
have the right credentials and are persistent you will get the job. It
will be real tough, but after 2 or three years you are fully "trained"
and a valuable commodity. Best bet for a job: large utilities or other
large companies, especially those with federal or state contracts. They
have to be fair in their hiring - they wouldn't be otherwise - and you
should get your best chance there. As for advancement, that's up to you.
With hustle you can do it. I did. I was hired most recently by a large
utility at 50. My salary has risen 45-50% in the last four years. You
can do it, too, if you really want to.

Good luck!
