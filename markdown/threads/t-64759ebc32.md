```
It is interesting to hear blame for Yaer 2000 being
laid on programmers or designers of COBOL systems.

I have been programming in COBOL since 1982.

Here is a typical discussion from a couple of programmers in the
early 80's.

Brad.) Gee, if this program were running in the year 2000 it
probably wouldn't work you know.

Tony.) Yeah right, and your programs are about as likely to be around
in the year 2000 as I am to be flying. You write such crap code anyway
that the team leader probably won't even let it go into production.

Brad.) True. Anyway, what sort of crap organisation would keep a
program around for 20 years.

Brad 15 Years Later.) My God was that Tony I just saw flying past my house.

Bottom line is that most of us thought about it but dismissed the possibility
of our code lasting that long. We weren't concerned about space and speed,
2 digit years just seemed like a logical thing to do at the time. The
programmers who came after us and re-wrote the systems were supposed to
account for the year 2000. God knows most us would be managers by then
and would be well aware of the problem and could brief them on it.

Unfortunately a lot of those people didn't make it past the early 90's.
None of us counted on the recession of the 90's,
computing was invincible, our jobs were for life.
Hmmmm maybe no-one will be re-writing that code after all. Oh well I don't
care because now that I'm redundant I am going to open up an organic fruit shop
and I hope I never see another computer again.
```
