Per Jimmy's request, here is what I sent him.

" Let me say something about "fixpacks at time of release".  This comes first
from my mainframe experience - but also from Micro Focus internal.

The "freeze" date for software to go GA is usually about 2-4 months BEFORE
users get it.  (This is normally the last "beta build" but may be one final
one if that beta shows anything critical.)  This "relatively long" lead time
allows for building product, finalizing documentation (to get it to match the
final software) and a variety of other things.

During that 2-4 months, it is quite common (and in my opinion HIGHLY
desirable) that the original beta sites and the internal users continue to
use this "GA version" of the software.  Therefore, it is likely (and again
DESIRABLE - IMHO) that at the time of GA, a "fix-pack" is made available with
all known fixed - discovered after "software freeze".

In the IBM mainframe world, this is called the PSP bucket (don't ask me what
that stands for) - and used to take a "full tape" for major products - and
had SIGNIFICANT numbers of fixes for things like COBOL.

Bottom-Line:  I just don't see WHY people object to these "fix-packs" being
available upon the date of GA.  Furthermore, the fix-packs themselves have to
go thru their own QA.  Therefore, they usually have a freeze date a couple of
weeks before GA.  This means that the 2nd fix-pack (if the vendor is
providing adequate support) will usually be available between 4 and 8 weeks
after GA.

  ***

You (Jimmy) may not like this, but does this help you see why (at least for
some of us), this is actually a GOOD thing, and I would personally be REAL
unhappy with any vendor who didn't provide their first "fix-pack" for a few
months after GA."

Steve Maguire talks about this in debugging the development process. 
The basic issue is that the fixes actually destabilize the product and
you cannot be sure whether it will work in every instance (only a few
people identified it for example).  So you work up a fix, release the
fix on day one. If you as the client choose to install the fix, it is
your choice.

In the vendors next beta cycle it is released generally (or you find
everyone installs it so it must be OK).

You have to remember that most of us work in a relatively controlled
environment. I know the about 50% of you have killer PC's,  mine is a
clunker P100 waiting for the olympics computer second hand discards.  
This is the range the typical vendor must deal with.  At work we
typically have Win95 or win 98 or win NT to run on not all three
(except the person that says I do!).  It is reasonably easy to verify
the impact of change in these sites, A couple of machine tests and you
are done.

The final thing is that the CD's go out to press a long time before
they hit the street,  they hit the street in waves and the first wave
finds an 'undocumented feature', it gets fixed quickly.  Don't knock
them for this, congratulations are in order.
