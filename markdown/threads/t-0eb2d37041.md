The # of jobs will slowly decline, because schools don't teach it anymore.
Students are now versed in Visual Basic, Access, etc (are they really
programming languages?)  The trend will be toward that.  Also, more 'canned'
packages, bought off the shelf, will be used.  All the old time COBOLers
will retire and maintenance/support of the old systems will begin to fail.

With that said, in a shop like ours, the mainframers still command the data.
There is no better language to handle data in than COBOL.  Many of the PC
databases are merely daily copies of the real data.  There is still a real
need to keep business data on a mainframe with COBOL.

COBOL will not disappear suddenly.  It will be chronic.  Businesses will
require even more data to be stored and processed, countering efforts.  It
will take years and years to totally dismantle our data and processes.  In
our shop, the legacy is just too big and important.  Our company would have
to hire dozens and dozens of additional staff if they wanted to "just do
away with all the old stuff" in any reasonable amount of time.  Many new
systems use data from COBOL programs and as such, will still need the COBOL
programs to be maintained.

Our shop writes new COBOL programs everyday.  One of the great things about
COBOL is it's expandability and standardness.  Most COBOL programs look the
same.  Grab a program from here, cut this, grab some snippets from there,
add a little magic, and viola, it's done.  And most COBOL shops that are
worth anything have standards.  This is what makes the system long-lived.

I am in no fear of my job.  And if I was, I'd just have to learn the new
stuff.

Jeff
