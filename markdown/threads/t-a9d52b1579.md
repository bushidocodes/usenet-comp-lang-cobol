Hi,

I am a U.S. citizen; and for several years now I have been thinking
that I would really enjoy working in Europe, doing the same thing I
do here in the U.S. (i.e. programming). As it is, I have about 7 years
of COBOL IBM mainframe experience, and also some client-server skills
(i.e. PowerBuilder). And mixed in with the COBOL IBM mainframe is CICS,
IMS DL/I & DC, TSO/ISPF, VSAM, JCL, and quite a few other things too.
I have also worked a little with Microfocus COBOL (i.e. as a development
platform for mainframe applications).

Is there anybody over there on that side of the Atlantic that knows
of any opportunities ? What's the story on work permits ? I've heard
that companies pretty much have to prove they can't find anybody else
inside the nation's borders before they can hire a foreigner. Has the
shortage for COBOL programmers gotten to the point where someone like
me would have a chance ?

I do speak a little French; and I really enjoy learning foreign languages.

Thanks in advance,

David Mazeau

Yo Dave!

I don't have any answers to your questions but I do have additional issues for
you to ponder. I spoke with Peter de Jager a couple weeks ago. de Jager is the
Year 2000 guy. He says that Europe is in big, big trouble. In part because of
the Year 2000 but also because they're normalizing their currency into a common
unit. The currency conversion is a big job.

I'd be real careful about signing any long term contracts. de Jager and the
other Year 2000 people are predicting a rapid spin up of rates. I'm hearing
numbers like $100/hour around Washington DC and just I cross posted a report
from New York of $150-180/hour. Six months ago, $50-70/hour looked real good.
(50-70/hour still looks good!)

Some predict that rates will double every 6-12 months. Include that in your
plans. You don't want to be working a 18 month contract and find that the guy
next to you is earning 3 times what you are.

I'd suggest that 4-6 month contracts at a fixed rate should be the rule until
2002 or so. Oh yeah, and save your money because the jobs may dry up after the
currency conversion and the year 2000 conversion.

Cory Hamasaki
