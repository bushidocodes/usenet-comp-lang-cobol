Good question :-) Here in NZ the rising generation is completely metric, 
but, of course, my generation had to learn both.

We went to decimal currency in 1967 (on the 10th of July... I couldn't 
forget that date because they started brainwashing us in 1965, and 
intensified it during 1966.) Nevertheless, I still remember that 12 one and 
eightpences equal a pound and 16 one and threepences, not to mention 20 
shillings... :-)

That was all pretty easy and we were well prepared for it. Measurements are 
another matter...

I can tell you how many chains in a furlong and how many inches in a mile, 
plus all the permutations, but I don't know how many pixels to an em :-)

We still measure farms in acres, although all the "official" documents are 
in hectares, and when properties are for sale the area is quoted in 
hectares. Yet conversation in the local pub talks about so-and-so's 200 acre 
spread, or how many sheep or cattle are run to the acre, not the hectare. 
(This might be because it is typically the older generation who are 
discussing things down the pub, while their sons are doing the actual work 
and running the farm... :-))

If somebody says "7 inches" I can visualize it immediately, but if they say 
"4 centimetres" I have no idea how much that is... I have to stop and 
calculate it back into inches before it becomes "real"... Yet, oddly, I have 
no trouble visualizing 300 grammes of ham at the supermarket or a litre of 
milk... We are complex creatures... :-)

"Your mileage may vary" is not an expression that I have ever heard used in 
NZ, even when we measured distances in miles. I have heard "mileage" used in 
the sense of milking something (getting more mileage out of it).

I suspect it is something that stays after technology moves on.

I say this because I noticed the other day that I offered to "tape" a TV 
program for someone, even though I have been recording to DVD for nearly a 
year now... Somehow "I'll tape it for you" sounds more familiar than "I'll 
burn it to disk for you..." :-)
