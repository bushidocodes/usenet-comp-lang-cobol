Hi, Cobol world,

'Discrimination' is a strong word. But in economics 'Price
Discrimination' is a vender strategy to supply the same products among
different markets at different prices to maximize its profits. I don't
intend to debate on price dicrimination but since the internet makes the
whole cobol world united, I don't see any reason why venders still
differeniate their product prices among nations(markets). I don't
like to see the word 'for USA only' especially when the price difference
make it worthwhile to take an intercontinental trip to the US to buy the
software. Afterall it's not a smuggling.

Through internet plus credit card and UPS or Fed Express, I wonder why
venders cannot treat every cobol community equally?

jason Mo

As a vendor I can shed some light on variable pricing depending on location. At least for higher end products...

Lawyers (bottom feeders) are expensive - al most as much as Cory H's predictions for COBOL programmers. For our mainframe based products we need legal review of license terms for each country that we export our product to.

In many countries, even as nearby as Canada, we have tax headaches when billing and getting paid. Imagine billing $1000 and getting a check with a memo that here's your $900, since you did not have a revenue Canada account, we sent the other $100 to Revenue Canada as tribute. (Yup we got to claim it as a foreign tax credit 15 months later, but the lawyer bill was greater than the tax credit).

Costs of doing business overseas drives the high cost of licenses.

Of course.... if an off-shore enterprise happens to have a US office, we will be happy to do the contract with that office, deliver the product there, get paid in good old US dollars, all at the US price. The fact that the data center is somewhere else can usually be ignored - that's basically the customer's headache.

Rex Widmer
Builder of software archeology tools and other strange programs to help survive in a legacy based world.

Although I agree with the spirit of your post it is worth mentioning that
there are sometimes some very real restrictions. For example,
encryption software or software that does fast advanced arbitrary precision
math are currently restricted by the U.S. government. There may be
patent/copyright restrictions in certain countries. Finally, some markets
are plagued by widespread piracy, especially if pirating isn't against local
laws, leading manufacturers to charge more to cover losses (which may fuel
further pirating, of course).
