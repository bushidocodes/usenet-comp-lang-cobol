I think you quite wrong.  The discussion is about programming, and neither
bi-metallic strips nor light switches require software yet.

However, imbedded computers such as those used in cars DO require
programming, and certainly account for a sizable percentage of programmer
occupations. They are not simple logic chips, they are full-bore
stored-program Von Neuman machines, ... with storage devices and lots of
interfacing.  I've spent a sizable proportion of my carreer programming such
computers, and, in fact, got my first PC for precisely that purpose ... as a
software development platform.

Interestingly enough, one of the first of those computers (the mostek F8)
had an assembler that was written in Cobol.  It ran on a PDP-10, and
produced tapes for a PROM blaster.  I, personally, had about 10,000 of those
systems running, and could not have done it without Cobol.

As I see the future, a very large percentage of those computers are going to
behave as a very intelligent device, hooked into a larger program through an
OCX control and either an ethernet or USB/firewire style of interface.
Systems for controling things like building heat and lights will be built
from both hardware and software components that behave like "objects".

Cobol is one of the few fairly high level languages that is designed for
system integration.  To limit the discussion to computers that are only used
as destop, standalone machines is very short sighted.  In fact, that the
language runs on main frames through to micro's is the the most important
aspect of the language.  It is the ONLY language with a standardized mass
storage interface that encompasses such a large variety of hardware, and the
ONLY language with built in multi-language support.

Donald.

Now that brings on a chuckle.  It also brought back memories of a project I
did a long time ago that did close to the same thing. I do not remember the
date, but I would guess about 1982-3. Bill could probably verify that from
the Cobol compiler I used.

I was hired to build a one-up car computer for a prototype engine.  This was
not a prototype for a car computer, but lab test equipment going into a
vehicle.  I was handed a brand new mid sized american muscle car that had
been stripped down to bare metal, and told to put a computer in the back
seat.  I had 116 monitering points (discrete) and 64 analog sensors, each 12
bit.  I could do everything from reading torque sensors on each axle
(hydrolicly driven individual axles), to reading the air pressure at ten
points in the carburation, to using a gyroscope to sense vertical plane.
Real neat toy.

I bought two nineteen inch racks, and we cut them with a torch to fit, and
welded them in.  Then we mounted an S100 bus computer running MPM-86,
running with 640k. Under that was a 20 megabyte wincester drive.  That took
up the left rack.  The right rack took all our interfacing and metering.  By
the time I finished building the interfaces and the drivers, I could see the
data at the
S-100 bus level as a sequence of BCD digits, memory mapped.

We put an 72 character by 10 line gas plasma display in the dash on the
passeger side, and put a keyboard on a fold down tray-type hinge.  That was
the operators console. We put a second identical display on the dash for the
standard speed, odometer, etc, plus stuff like a ten second running average
of fuel usage.

All of the high speed stuff was done in 8086 assembler, and all of the
formating into various reports was done in Cobol.  It was Micro-Focus Cobol,
the same one that ran on the Altos Computer. I was able to set up the two
screens as ansi-terminals at the assembler level, and define them as such at
the Cobol level, then program them using screen sections.  Stuff like a
speedometer only has to be updated one or twice a second. The menus for the
monitoring stuff were written in Cobol, though the data monitoring was all
done in assembler.  The Cobol programs all saw their data magically appear
as memory disk. They would open a random file in input mode, and read record
zero.  I could sample all inputs at 400 hz, with everything being stored on
disk until it ran out.

That was twenty years ago, when computer power was expensive. My budget was
in the $300,000 range. The Cobol compiler was about $800.  Now they use
Windows in an actual vehicle, and I'll bet the computer costs less than
$100.  I see no reason why Cobol would not work on that computer, provided
each car buyer was willing to pay a $2500 royalty on the run-time system.

Times change, do they not?
Donald.

It's true that VB6 code will not slide into VB.net, but the
applications and the compiler will still work.  An interface has
also been provided to make use of the .net architecture from
these "legacy" apps.

Porting a VB6 app to vb.net is roughly the same task we're
facing in taking a mpb Visual COBOL app to Fujitsu or
MicroFocus.

It boils down to this--if you keep your compiler working off the
same specifications, you have stability and can achieve a high
level of code re-use.  If you're free to develop new models, you
can leverage emerging technologies.  In this case, the brass
ring being sought is web services.

Perhaps this is not a profitable goal, but it would have been
foolish to attempt it in VB3's day--the marketplace and
infrastructure was simply not there.
