If you have exposure to the IBM mainframes and any training at all on "...
VSAM, DB2 and JCL....", then you are much more valuable in the market place
than you may currently imagine.  Do not go for a helpdesk job. Go for a coders
job.

If one may be so bold as to interpret 'USF' as the Uuniversity of San
Francisco, and assume that that is you current location, then you are in luck
for sure.  Demand for your skill set is high.  Look to the banks or the large
consulting firms advertising in your paper.

Be aware that jobs relating to Y2K may have a short-term duration (perhaps 24
more months considering repairs and support early in year 2000), and that may
not be what you want, although the pay is good.

If you are starting your career, or making a career change, you may actually
want to select the job that has better long term prospects.  Y2K work is
drudgery, and unthinking maintenance.  For long term security you may do best
if you get involved as a junior programmer now in either a DEVELOPMENT project
or MAJOR ENHANCEMENT project, that will engage all of your skills in creating
and perfecting full programs.

Your should prioritize getting DB2 experience! Combining that with COBOL will
make you more valuable when you remarket your self later.  Knowledge of SQL
will also permit you to broaden your skill base to other language disciplines
easily.

Do not avoid COBOL+SQL opportunities on other platforms. In other words, do not
sell yourself short. You are right to think that the IBM mainframe exposure
gives you a leg up in that sector of the market, but desktops are hot and will
be hot.  There are many variations on this, IBM AIX COBOL DB2, for example.

Surely you should try to get top dollar now.
But bare in mind there is a possibility that many shops will disgorge a lot of
experienced programmers in 18 to 24 months as the Y2K thing settles down. The
market will then be seeking folks to do development work, and that is the thing
you will want experience with.

Companies also like people with application knowledge. Try to go where your
strenghts are. Go towards companies that are involved in areas where you have
prior experience with the substance of what they do, or where you have strong
personal interests. 

Try to find a company which will put you in a position to learn about there
business, not just programming, and commit your self to that business activity.
Such an arrangement will make you a bit more secure on the down slope of the
current employment demand bulge.










Robert Rayhawk
