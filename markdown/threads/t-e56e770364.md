Yukon Mama's post brought to mind a disturbing, but interesting, development in
corporate mindsets today - that experience has little, if any, value.

For example, a number of Y2K contract programmers (often receiving $50+/hour)
with 20-25 years experience at my last client were offered disturbingly lowball
permanent employee offers - about $50K per year - while those straight out of a
local 9 month "learn to program" school started at $44K, often with a several
thousand dollar signing bonus, and entry level employees of consulting firms
with no experience commanded billing rates of $60+/hour.

In fact, it's VERY rare indeed to find any permanent employee COBOL positions
that pay even twice entry level salary, even though a person with 10+ years of
good experience most likely will outperform an entry level person by 400%-2500%
(if the entry level person even contributes anything positive in the first year
or two!).

Any comments?

That's the way it is increasingly going in the UK.  I think it's generally
recognised - in the UK at any rate - that being an IT in an end-user company
is a career path to nowhere.  They'll always be a role for techies in
end-user companies but only if they reinvent themselves as 'business
facilitators' - which the best IT people always were, of course.  But now, I
think, that phrase it just code for putting up with reduced career
opportunities.

Also, if you work at an end-user company, you're seen as a revenue consumer
and treated accordingly.  If you work for an outsourcer or consultancy then
you're a revenue earner for your company, and have more chance of being
treated better.  I made the move from end-user company to consultancy two
years ago; those are certainly my experiences.
