I am writing a rudimentary COBOL parser: one that knows enough about the
language to be able to find each statement in a source file. I had planned
on using LEX and YACC to perform this task, but have become aware that
COBOL statements do not necessarily end with periods or any other kind of
delimiter. This says to me that I have to do a lot of lookahead processing
to determine when a new statement is begun. YACC does not appear a
convenient choice for this kind of lookahead parsing. Can someone please
tell me what technique is usually used to parse COBOL, or whether YACC is
even usable for this? Thanks.

No, you do not need lookahead of more than 1 to address this
particular problem. You just write a grammar in such a way that
all Cobol verbs are acting as statement terminators.

However, you need lookahead of more than 1 to address
some other issues on Cobol parser.

Case in point:
PERFORM A OF B TIMES MOVE C TO D END-PERFORM
vs
PERFORM A OF B THRU C OF D

In this example you know what kind of PERFORM
(PERFORM paragraph or PERFORM block) you have only when
you reach words TIMES or THRU. Before that you don't know,
unless you use semantic feedback from symbol table,
which may not even be available (you are parsing legacy code, right?).


Your comments make me think that you did not get far enough in your
parser project, and that therefore you may consider buying a
well-tested Cobol parser from Siber Systems. It solves problem that
you mentioned and many other problems that arise in Cobol parsing.

The parser information itself is available from
http://www.siber.com/sct/

In addition to parser you get abstract syntax tree, library
for tree manipulation and readable Cobol code generator.

The parser is written in BtYacc (Backtracking Yacc) that is
freely available from http://www.siber.com/btyacc/.

While parsers vary, the technique that often is used for the Procedure
Division is the recognition of verbs. Each statement begins with a
verb, but it does not necessarily end with a period. To separate
statements and sentences from paragraph and section names, the
traditional reliance has been on the Area-A and Area-B margins, as
well as on the concept of reserved words.

The other divisions are usually done differently, though the reserved
word concept (and recognition) are usually employed. Most
implementors treat the Identification Division entries as comments,
with the exception of the program name. In the Environment Division,
files are initially parsed by recognition of the SELECT verb;
similarly, look for FD and SD in the Data Division.

I hope this gets you started. Good luck with your parser.
