> The problem with such "free" agreements is disparity in bargaining power

Donno if I buy it, Barb.

A lot of the abuses were the result of poor business sense on both 
sides.   

I once worked for a beltway bandit.  They negotiated rates, terms,
conditions, and such with the client.  Their role is strangely similar 
to the role of the union.  

If the client got uppity, mouthed off to a worker, the beltway bandit 
would shuck and jive but in reality, would pull the worker, swap them to
another contract, and life would go on. 

Sadly, while I have contracted to women bosses, none of them have 
required "consensual" sex as a condition of continued employment.  I 
don't think it's fair either way though. 
  
> Collective bargaining tends to equalize the disparity.

Maybe, but when you're the best and you know it, and they know it, it 
doesn't matter.  The problem isn't, as Clint says, a man's gotta know 
his limitations.   For some of us, the message is, a man's gotta know 
when he has the upper hand.  Or.  You gotta know your value.

If you know in your heart of hearts what you're worth, ask for it.  If 
you know that you're priced appropriately for the market, that's the 
number and there's no negotiation.

You also have to know when they're playing games.

You people over there in a.c.c and c.l.c. Know this, the market rate for
a geezer who fixes small briggs and stratton engines is $49.95/hour, 
the Acura dealer charges $79/hour across the board for mostly bolt-off, 
bolt-on work.  

(Please, don't bring up the mechanic's tools and shop overhead.  I pay 
rent on an office and have a mainframe that runs MVS.)

Programming, software engineering, computer science, and general geekin'
is difficult, complex and takes aptitude, experience, academics and 
$100/hour is dirt cheap for a technical specialist. 

I believe the access clickers, Lan boys, and web-weenie's generally 
get a fair rate, somewhere around a hundred an hour.   The rest of us 
don't charge enough. 

I'd like to see journeyman programmers, COBOL types especially, continue
to get the Y2K consulting rates.
