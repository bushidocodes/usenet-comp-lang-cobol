```
I am about to begin a 14 week Cobol training program.  As an unemployed
individual this opportunity has been presented and I am excited about the
prospect.  I am A+ certified and have been on computers since IIe days.  But
have no experience in programming.  I consider myself a quick learner.
However, I am concerned about learning programming.  I suppose it is normal
to be concerned about learning something new.

A specific computer company is sponsoring this training (in fact they have
helped to design the training program) and will interview (no guaranteed
jobs) all of the students with a B or better grade at course completion.
The first class of this type in this area (limited to 15 students per class)
had placement of 90%.

I guess I am asking how this sounds to those of you who are Cobol
programmers?  Can you really learn enough in 14 weeks to actually perform
the job.  I am only looking for an entry level position from this (foot in
the door).  Any advice would be greatly appreciated.
```

```
I don't think that the company expects you to be able to
start programming on your own. They probably just want to make
sure that you have some basic programming skill before you start.
You'll need to know the specifics of their particular installation, and
that may be a lot tougher than learning how to program. Also, if
you get employed in a bank or so, you'll need to know a lot about
the business part. Noone in their right mind would just give you
a 14 week Cobol training program, and then expect you to perform
on your own. If they spend a lot of money on giving you basic
Cobol skills I'm sure they're going to follow up on it once you
start working.

Where I work (a Danish insurance company) even semiskilled
programmers are not expected to do much entirely on their own
for at least 6 months or so.

Hey, and we're all still learning, right?

Kennet
```

```
You can definately learn enough in 14 weeks to perform well in an entry level
position.  That's actually pretty much how I got into the business.  I was out
of work and applied for a bunch of different jobs thru the state civil service
commission.  One of the positions was "Programmer Analyst Trainee" and the
requirements were a college degree (any field) and high score on an aptitude
test.  Most of the agencies I interviewd were actually looking for some
experience and using the position to get someone at a low salary, but one
agency actually had an in-house training course, which was really nothing more
than supervised self-study.  For 3 months I did nothing but study cobol, jcl,
vsam, and cics, and when finished was ready to go to work.
```
