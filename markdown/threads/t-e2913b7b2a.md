Let me say this much:

Micro Focus 3.2 and Fujitsu 3 support for OO are both quite out-of-date when
it comes to what is in the draft Standard (as is IBM's OO support).  Each of
the 3 is incompatible with the others.  There is currently a session
scheduled at the next J4 meeting when they hope to revise (yet again) the OO
specification in response to their last internal review.

Given all of this,
  I think that any text that matches a current compiler is OK to learn OO
COBOL from.  All you need to know is that some features may be added, other
may be deleted, and still others modified - before there is ever a truly
"Standard" definition of OO COBOL.

If you haven't seen it, there is PDF "white paper" on the OO features in the
draft Standard (as of a couple of years ago) that you can get via the
Fujitsu web page (www.adtools.com).  Also, you can send a note to the chair
of J4 - and ask him for a copy of the "concepts" section on OO.  He can be
reached at
      doncobol@mediaone.net
I don't know if you will get it in hardcopy or email - and it is out of date
too.

I hope something in here helps at least a little.

The anti-spam stuff, sorry, I apologize.  Since leaving netcom with >350KB per day of spam, I
have rigged all  my client's copies of my return addresses and now have -0-KB per day of spam!!
That's great.

I appreciate the information you've given me on the current status.  I'm considering launching
into study of Java, C and C++ at this point--nearly through with the Cobol course.  That's all
they offer at my current institution, 3 sections of cobol and one of CICS.  The cics looks
interesting, in a get-a-job sort of way  :)  Visual basic, v.4, is all the rest of their
offering!  (an intro course at that...)  I liked vb a lot, but found it limiting in terms of
creating new routines and deployment on computers without the runtimes installed.  The final
product is a little topheavy, and only viable on wintel systems.  It is a good front end for
any code, practically, on wintel.  

It seemed as we finish this section, Cobol 3, and are introduced to OO concepts, in small part,
that my new familiarity with cobol would be of assistance in learning and employing object
cobol.  But then the limitations of the text and the standards extant being only applicable by
vendor (and realistically, I don't have one!) make it seem that there is little course to
follow in this enterprise unless I start work in a shop that employs OO....  It is by
definition a shame that standards, such as the non-existant COBOL-97 standard cannot be agreed
upon and fielded by vendors in a timely fashion.  Just putting it out, get it out there so
people can use it.  Let vendors upgrade products on the fly like the pc world, and issue
updates to their products (and for free as well.)  

OK, mission critical requirements and arguments against (the above) aside for the moment, look
at another standards issue:  Was it the IETC or IIIE or whoever putzed about with the v.90
modem standard for so long, with debate between vendors and foot dragging by the "Committee"
taking so long to issue the standard that by the time it was sent out, everyone had separate
work arounds and modem customers didn't realize any value from the standard, because in the
end, it was the damn phone lines and CO switching and node implementations (filters and
modulation/remodulation) that prevent it from working in the majority of cases.  Smart
consumers with need or desire have found alternatives:  cable modem (I have had it from the
first day it was available on my street) or xDSL, or satellite or a string and two cans (an
actual improvement on v.90 in most areas!!)

Well, it's the concepts that I'm interested in so I think I'm bound to look into a language
that employs OO natively, and thus its use is defined.  It's silly to try this in cobol, except
to pass our last exam:  there is no road to follow, what roads there are lead to cliffs! 

I've been looking over the links on Cetus, very interesting.      /ts

I concur with what Bill said regarding standards/oops,
but would add a bit.  I think if you are going to learn oops,

earlier "student/give-away" versions.

For both MF and Fujitsu the current versions of the software
are much closer to "a standard" ... Bill is quite correct in that
the standard is not there.  However, things have firmed up a
whole lot since the early versions came out, and current
versions are bound to be closer to the emerging standard. You
have to keep in mind that OOPS is a multi-language standard.

Whatever emerges has to conform with calling conventions
that have come into being through VB, OCX, C++, etc.  The
current set do.  That is not true of Version 3 Fujitsu, however.
The MAJOR difference between 3 an 4 is in the OOPS. The
standard Cobol's are virtually identical.

I am not sure if student versions of current stuff are there or
not.

Thanks for your reply.  I'm pretty sure that there are not any 'student' versions of OOcobol.
As we approached the topic at the end of our 3rd quarter of cobol, it looked very interesting.
Esp with the tie-in to the methodology of other languages.  But the text is hopelessly out of
date.  There is not really a cross-vendor standard.  There is no help from the Cobol (ahem,
'97) standards committee at this point in time.  It seems as futile to learn OO cobol unless
employed to use it as it would be to learn Microfocus screen extensions.  They are a dead end.

My intent in learning cobol in the first place was to be able to write utility and network
client/server programs for wintel, and secondarily, for other platforms.  There is still a lot
of room for this, just that perhaps cobol is not a very choice language for this application.
I considered that cobol would be like learning latin when applied to studying other, probably
more applicable languages--having familiarity with programming processes, logic and procedures
and with the reasoning dictated by use of such fancy abacuses.  Thus the study of OO Cobol
seemed a logical extension to this premise.  Guess not.  I'm pretty disappointed.
