Please allow me to explain to you what you are looking at when you use
those free historical sources.  You are looking at something which
periodically saved the CLS, then did a sort on it.  CLS is the
Consolidated Last Sale.  This is a weighted average over span of time
designed to both inform all of the stock exchanges and to smooth
market trading.  I don't remember the formula (if I ever knew it in
its entirety) but odd-lots and small-rounds rarely make it into the
CLS of a high volume issue.  For the longest time, the only sale which
could be printed to tape (sent to the primary stock exchange for
inclusion in the consolidated quote) was a trade which actually
occurred on the trading floor of a licensed stock exchange.  Orders
which were matched in a broker's office or on an ECN were not eligible
for inclusion.  Regulations have changed some over the years, but
those changes started in the late 1990s.

There used to be more stock exchanges than this in the U.S.
http://www.cftech.com/BrainBank/FINANCE/USStockExchs.html

The reporting lags caused by communications of each time period is
what gave rise to the CLS as a need for smoothing statistical
outliers.

The simple fact is, we don't have a central clearing agency in this
country.  While not all trades are not required to print to tape, all
trades are required to go through clearing.  The only way to really
know what the day's high and low were would be to gather information
about every trade from every clearing company in the world and sort
them.

Smoothing is required because the difference between the bid and the
ask at any given exchange (known as the spread) can become quite wide,
especially with automated trading rules.  When a market order comes
(MKT) it has to automatically execute against Top of Book (TOB).  If
the local CTOB (Consolidated TOB) is better than the local TOB (LTOB)
the MKT has to be executed against specialist's inventory at CTOB
price, or be laid off to that market...which...given communications
lag times opens another can of worms.

An Odd-lot, is any potential trade consisting of less than a round-lot
quantity.  Many issues have a 100 share round-lot, but some issues,
like the Berkshire stuff, have 10 share round-lots.  There could even
be some which have 10000 share rounds, I don't remember.  Odd-lot
traders are a completely different world.  I never understood the odd-
lot way of life.  I never understood how a specialist could kick your
25 share trade into that system, or split your 50045 share trade into
a 50000 share trade they handled and 45 which went through they odd-
lot way of life.  I do know that back then you could get dramatically
different prices for the round-lot and odd-lot portions of your trades
unless you paid extra money to put an AON qualifier on your order.
All-Or-None meant the specialist receiving the order had to fill the
entire order in one trade or reject the order.  This also had the
benefit of protecting you from multiple trade fees (not as big an
issue with 7.95 trades as it was with $35+ trading fees).  If you sent
in an order to sell 500,000 shares of some issue at a fixed price and
became the CTOB, your order could be matched against 100 orders of
5,000 shares each and each match be a separate trade/clearing fee in
the eyes of your broker.

other times it is BASIC, or FORTRAN on the same platform.

attempt to replace a Cash Cow system originally written in DEC BASIC
with yet another be-all-end-all-ERP-product-sold-by-Gartner-this-
week.  So far, all of those products have failed spectacularly.

So, if you are just starting out, you need to get the skills to get
your foot in the door.  After you have a few years working with one of
the languages on that platform supporting a Cash Cow, you can poke
around and find another company with a Cash Cow on the same platform,
if it happens to be a different language, not a problem, you've got a
book which will let you quickly jump.

Here is some free career advance:

If there is a "free training video" on-line, or you can plunk down $20
