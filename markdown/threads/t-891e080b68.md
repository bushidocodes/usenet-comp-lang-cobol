```
Hi All,

     We are a COBOL shop, and outsourced our web site. To make a long story
short, It was written in Java Script,  a foreign language to us, it cost alot
of money, it couldnt handle our business applications like we would of liked
and it wasnt deleivered for over a year.
     We had purchased Fujitsu 5.0 and Flexus SP2 for some in house stuff.  We
were going to learn Java script, but decieded to try Flexus Thin Client.
     After 2 weeks, we had a rather large program, 23 files, over a million
records (history for a year + ) , ready to try via the internet.  One word:
INCREDIBLE.  
     Using a 56K modem from home, I was able to access our server, and from the
password screen to the first record I wanted, was under 20 seconds.  Another
programmer that has a cable modem said it was up in like 5 seconds.
    SP2 is also windows based, so it looks like the client is running the
application right from thier desk top.
    If you are a COBOL shop, and need to put applications on the web, this is
definatly the way to go.  Flexus offers a demo version of SP2 and Thin Client. 
They also offered technical assistance (always got an email back within 1 to 2
hours).
     We have decieded to scrap the web site, and Internet enable any
applications our clients need.  It was also very cost effective (for right now,
we only have between 5 - 8 clients)  It cost less to purchase the software then
it did to be held hostage by our web company.
     I can not tell you how happy we are with the results.  I hate to sound
like a salesperson, and didnt want to come off as one, but you have to try this
software. The demos are free, and again Flexus will offer support.  Any
questions, feel free to send my way, but I know Bob Wolfe from Flexus will
point you in the right direction.  It really is a joy going to work now, and
being appreciated from the boys upstairs ( they think we are incredibly smart
now, little do they know....)
     I havent been exposed to much web development, but do believe COBOL is
still the way to go for business applications.  The web developers always took
so long to make a correct change.  
     If one person tries this and uses it, my goodwill to others would have
been accomplished.  Thats just my 2 cents, for what its worth...........

Have a good one,

John
```

```
I think that the issues here are:

1.  Yes, you can accomplish the job in COBOL (whether or not you use
the Flexus products).

2.  There IS more than one way to "internet enable" your program and
it DOESN'T have to display the screens in a web browser.

3.  Sometimes displaying the screens in a web browser is a BAD idea
for a business-to-business application which needs to run across a
TCP/IP network.
```

```
< Legacy propaganda snipped! >

How dare you suggest that a COBOL programmer can replace a staff of
people for an online business application. If this catches on, we could
have massive unemployment in headhunters, tools-people, java-people,
web-people, c/c++ people, and with smaller staffs-project managers  and
CTO's too.

Business programmers? None of the current internet-enabling staff in
most companies know anything about business, they are "engineers",
implementing the latest-and-greatest technologies, and don't have time
to concern themselves with business! 

Get with the current trends. Most companies now have a CTO to hide the
business side of the company from the internet developers, and to
out-source when staffs get too big, thus lowering budgets and getting a
bonus before he goes somewhere else to do it again. During an
application development, the rules and technologies change so fast it
causes a high turn-over rate in employees. This allows salaries to go
up, and delays to be  extended even longer. It also keeps headhunters
doing placements (at 25%-35%), and consultants on more payrolls to
explain why things are going so slowly. The engineers that cannot keep
up with the furious change rate will be added to the consultant ranks,
which provides a steady replacement for the consultants that move into
CTO positions. The consulting position is an OJT requirement for CTO
wannabees, because they have to gain the experience of hiding all
technical details and faults from the business people and management.

It has taken many years for us to arrange our "business" plan.  We first
had to start at Colleges and Universities, getting the Professors to
convince everyone that "legacy" systems had outlived their usefulness.
Then having them adopt the new wave of languages and technologies, which
not only gave them higher incomes, but brought in new students,
increasing classes, and therefore increasing the sizes of the CS
departments. We made every effort to get them to implement languages
that are not maintainable for years at a time. This helped to keep the
number of students up, because of the newer languages that had to be
taught, because they fixed the problems caused by the current languages. 

Because maintenance is impossible over a period of time, most
modifications are transformed into new development efforts, which again
involves increasing staff sizes. This really works! Look at the history
of just the last few years. COBOL had to be replaced by C,C by C++,C++
by JAVA/BEANS/JINI/JUNK, and so on. The rapid pace of change also
enables an "upgrade every two years" for commercial software developed
following our plans. 

Microsoft started as one of our early supporters by divorcing themselves
from "legacy" tools like COBOL, and has continued by forcing change upon
the industry. By modifying their API's (mainly to break the competitor's
software) they can not only sell new versions of their OS, but also
enabled them to reap a gold mine in new tools-sold to their competition!
We are hoping that Microsoft can complete their plan of destroying all
industry and ISO standards, and replacing them with theirs. We have
encouraged them to implement completely new languages, which would
insure that change is part of the industry, and that everything would
have to be developed from scratch every two years.

Gone are the days when people wrote BASIC programs because there was no
business reason (your term not ours) to develop it COBOL, or because
staff was too small. You brought it on yourself by using the term
"maintenance" when updating programs to reflect new laws, tax changes,
and business rules. Because you actually produced something and were
valuable to your company, you simply cost too much. (Remember,
management thinks that 80% of your just is fixed broken programs.) Now
they can pay many more people, many more dollars, to develop C/C++
programs which call JAVA (interpreted just like BASIC was...), and
format everything to be displayed on a web page-just because they can,
and it doesn't even have to work! Your out-moded concepts of business
programming, and requiring programmers to understand their company's
business, has no place in the current marketplace. 

If you continue promoting the idea of using existing staff to do the
work, using "legacy" tools with only a few add-ons, most of us will be
out of work, or even worse, *WE* may have to produce something! Today's
economy cannot support your new-fangled ideas.



Ok, I feel better now (:-)

Gary
```
