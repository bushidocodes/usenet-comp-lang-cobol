> To me the notion of "Testing Forth Code" is a sort of oxymoron, most of
> the time--I test as I go, so the code works when it's done. 

There is testing and testing. Naturally I would expect all programmers to 
test the functionality of their own code as they produce it. However, some
languages do not facilitate this as easily as Forth does. In my own book,
the inspection and testing that follows the submission of the code for 
inclusion in the product is where the trust in certification is built up.
Such testing also includes limits checks where a deliberate attempt at 
breaking the code is made. You should find the paper on the EuroForth 
site covers some of the basics and the form in there and in FD from a 
couple of years back will help keep track of the testing and certification 
effort.

> Even long
> assembler definitions can be done this way, as my paper in some future
…[7 more quoted lines elided]…
>         http://Landau1.phys.virginia.edu/classes/551/

My method works for Assembly within Forth as well. It could probably apply
to similar languages to Forth. Just one word of caution though. Ensure your
versioning and configuration management systems are well up to scratch so 
that you can be certain exactly what you have tested.
