I came to this shop in 1987 after being in a VM & VSE shop.  The first

> WHAT ABOUT AMODE 24?  Well, that's been a headache all along.  We have already migrated most of our amode 24 assembler

Assembler has been our biggest problem getting to ALL31(ON).  Compared
to ten years ago, there are hardly any applications programmers left
with any depth in assembler programming.  I've done some, but I am no
expert at assembler.  Converting a fifteen or twenty year old
assembler subprogram to AMODE(31),RMODE(ANY) can range from easy to
very tricky to downright impossible.  In some cases, the best that
could be done with certain subprograms was AMODE(31),RMODE(24).  

Sometime in the early to mid 1980's, before I got to this shop, they
converted from DOS/VSE and ISAM to MVS and VSAM.  They had an
assembler subprogram used to load COBOL subprograms dynamically, and a
general purpose subprogram to access VSAM files.  I didn't understand
it at the time, but everyone used these routines with OS/VS COBOL
programs even though there was no need to.  OS/VS COBOL was perfectly
capable of calling a parogram dynamically (even with the NODYNAM
compile option) by using a variable containing the name of the called
subprogram.  OS/VS COBOL was perfectly capable of accessing VSAM files
with native COBOL verbs.  But everyone continued to use the old
assembler subprograms, which were AMODE(24),RMODE(24) because they
were last assembled before MVS/XA existed.  The lesson here is that
bad practices can endure for a very long time, and can be very
difficult to eliminate. 


> 
> Keeping track of what can call what (amode and rmode wise) has been a huge effort, but it is paying off.  A few years
> ago we had relatively frequent, but sporadic abends for "insufficient below the line storage available."  Now, it's
> unheard of.  Additionally, a lot of programs are now getting more file buffers than before (improving both VSAM and QSAM

One of our prime motivators for getting to the LE ALL31(ON) option is
the shrinking amount of below-the-line storage available to COBOL
applications with each new release of MVS/OS390/zOS.  We had some
COBOL programs with Working-storage close to 8 megabytes due to large
internal tables.  Some of these programs would ceased running with
OS/390 2.10.

We also had one *very* large COBOL application that was rewritten in
such a way that it could *not* run or be tested in an LPAR with
ALL31(OFF).  Tech Services also told us that 31-bit COBOL programs
would run faster that 24-bit COBOL programs once we got to ALL31(ON)
in every LPAR.  I converted an OS/VS program to COBOL for OS/390 & VM
2.2 earlier this year, and TS is recommending we recompile all COBOL
II programs with the current compiler.  At least the COBOL II programs
should not need any source code changes for that.  I believe a large
part of the resistance to change was that our OS/VS COBOL programs
typically would not compile under COBOL II or higher.  Typical issues
were CURRENT-DATE (the old special register), EXAMINE/TRANSFORM,
EXHIBIT NAMED, and BEFORE/AFTER POSITIONING.

TRUNC(OPT) is our mandated default.  I have used it with CICS and, to
a lesser extend, DB2 (I don't do a lot of DB2 programming myself).  I
have never experienced a problem caused by using TRUNC(OPT) rather
than TRUNC(BIN).

> (snip)
> NUMPROC.  OS/VS COBOL didn't have this compile option either.  According to IBM, OS/VS COBOL always generated
> NUMPROC(NOPFD) code.  I haven't been able to verify whether that statement is 100% correct.  NUMPROC(MIG) generates more
> efficient code than NUMPROC(PFD)  Both MIG and PFD are ANSI compliant.  NUMPROC(NOPFD) generates the most efficient
> code, though it can produce non ANSI compatible results.  We are switching to MIG, but I wished we had converted with

My understanding is that NUMPROC(NOPFD) is less efficient than
NUMPROC(MIG), because it generates more sign fixup code.  Nevertheless
I prefer NUMPROC(NOPFD), which is also our default, because the output
is better.  I have had at least one COBOL II Program that abended with
S0C7 under NUMPROC(MIG), but completed successfully with
NUMPROC(NOPFD) and produced correct results.

Given the nature of our processing, we could not ensure that input
data had valid signs according to the PICTURE clause in all cases. 
Therefore, NUMPROC(PFD), which actually generates the most efficient
code would also generate more incorrect results, like -0 or unsigned
zero not beintg equal to +0.

And while I may appear to contradict myself here, I agree with you
that I would rather have the program abend with S0C7 than complete
successfully with "unexpected results".

Remember that no matter what NUMPROC compile option you use, the
TARGET field will always have a sign that matches the PICTURE clause. 
The problem is:  what kind of results will you get if your SOURCE
field has an invalid sign, or even non-numeric data?

While we're on compile options, some others that we use are NOSSRANGE
and OFFSET.  Batch programs are typically NORENT.

One thing we do with our batch programs is that every program displays
three lines at the start of execution, something like this:

WS921010-0000 UPDATE BILLING MASTER FILE
WS921010-0001 COMPILED 2000-03-15 AT 15:35:57
WS921010-0002 EXECUTED 2002-08-17 AT 01:00:03

If you're called in the middle of the night, you can look at the
SYSOUT and immediately tell if a program has been recently changed. 
On the other hand, we don't have programs that read or copy COBOL
SYSOUT.  I suppose you could DISPLAY a blank line, or some other
innocuous message, during program initialization.  That would at least
guarantee that your SYSOUT is allocated.
