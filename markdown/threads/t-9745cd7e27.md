### Who Innovated First? ISPF "Point-And-Shoot" vs Amazon.com "1-Click"

- **From:** theodp@aol.com (Theo DP)
- **Date:** 2001-03-24T19:31:10+00:00
- **Newsgroups:** comp.lang.cobol
- **Message-ID:** `<20010324143110.14944.00000664@ng-mk1.aol.com>`

```
Amazon.com received a rather dubious business process patent in Fall, 1997, for
their "1-Click" Ordering patent, which describes a process to browse, order,
bill, accumulate, and deliver printed material with a single mouse click that
causes the order to be fulfilled by retrieving prespecified name, shipping, and
chargeback information that resides on a server. 

Does this really differ significantly from the process used since the mid '70's
by IBM mainframe users to browse, order, bill, accumulate, and deliver printed
materials (reports, file listings, etc.) with a single mouse click (or press of
a function key or ENTER key or light pen in earlier days) that causes the order
to be fulfilled by retrieving prespecified name, shipping, and chargeback
information that resides on a server (mainframe)? 

Please let me know what you informed users think!

I think not, especially since the patent is not restricted to any specific
embodiment of the 'invention', and both the IBM and Amazon processes certainly
fit the definitions of '1-Click' and shopping ("To look for something with the
intention of acquiring it").

The folks at BountyQuest, whose investors now include Unix publishing tycoon
and bounty poster (and IBM basher) Tim O'Reilly and Amazon.com CEO and patent
holder (and former mainframe programmer) Jeff Bezos, disagreed and rejected the
1995 ISPF manual that was submitted as prior art in a search that was
supposedly intended to invalidate the Amazon patent, but (surprise!) found no
winners.

You can check it out (and the three far less worthy 'runner ups' that were
eventually awarded a $10,000 consolation 'non-prize' when O'Reilly was faced by
the Press) at http://www.bountyquest.com/infocenter/1click.htm.
```
