Hi Anthony,

from the mail I receive, it seems that COBOL is alive and well in South
America. I know that several of the "how to" type papers I have written have
appeared on South American web sites (some in Brazil).

It might help us if you explained exactly what your "COBOL monograph" is,
and what it is for.

Is this something you are doing as part of a University or Technical College
course, or what is it that is required?

If you are required to write something extolling the virtues of COBOL you
will need to be careful. Standard COBOL (procedural) is in its twilight; the
future belongs to objects and layers. OO COBOL may have a place, but my
personal opinion is that even that place is limited.(The competition is too
fierce and other OO languages do it better...)

As for being able to make a living writing COBOL (as Doc advised) I really
couldn't agree with that. Sure, it is possible for us old timers, but I
wouldn't (couldn't in all conscience) advise a young person to get into
COBOL as a way to make a living any more. It is useful to have knowledge of
it, but make sure you have other skills as well.

In the last 15 years, the market for COBOL skills has shrunk by over 85%
(figures based on JobServe, a major European online placement service.)
While this may not reflect the situation in the place where you live, it is
still worth being aware of. You need more useful skills than COBOL if you
intend to be a career programmer in this century.

If you want to find some unique factor for your monograph, I don't think
portability is a really good one. Migrating COBOL across platforms is far
from straightforward. (Ask anyone who's done it...) yes, at a core level
COBOL is portable, but, in reality, people use vendor extensions and these
make it less so. It does not have the portability that Java has, for
example.

Consider the strengths that COBOL DOES have... it is easy to maintain (when
written well) but this is countered by the requirement to maintain thousands
of lines of it. (The cost of labour intensive maintenance has been a major
factor in the decline of COBOL.)

It has EXCELLENT I/O and data manipulation facilities. This is one reason
why it is likely to remain the best option for batch processing for the
foreseeable future.

It is easy to learn (even for people without a computer science background)
and you can achieve results pretty quickly with it while you are learning.

Please tell us more about what you are needing/trying to do.

Pete.

I couldn't tell if HeyBub meant that COBOL was the first language with
all three of those attributes, or simply that it was the first
language "put into general use" regardless of points 2 and 3.

The former assertion is correct; the latter is not. FORTRAN went into
general use a couple of years before COBOL did, and LISP around the
same time as COBOL.

The first FORTRAN compiler appeared in 1958. The first LISP
interpreter was probably running in late 1958, and the language went
public in 1960. CODASYL released the first COBOL specification in
1960. But neither FORTRAN nor LISP was designed by a committee (at
least not formally; Backus and McCarthy didn't work alone), and
neither was developed under the aegis of the DoD.
