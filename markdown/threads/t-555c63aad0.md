Easy sources for kosher designation:
1. Ask a rabbi.
2. Look for the kosher mark on the wrapper (circle-U, circle-K, and others).
3. All raw vegetables (processing may introduce non-kosher ingredients).

Kosher refers to the food intrinsically as well as how it is prepared or 
slaughtered.

Then there's a whole other thing for "Kosher for Passover."

Interesting thing about birds and kosherness.

The Bible lists seven birds you cannot eat and is silent on the permitted 
ones. So the rabbis set out to discover the clue. Of these seven, some were 
scavengers, some not. Some had two toes forward and two back. Some didn't. 
Some flew at night, others didn't. Oh well.

The rabbis threw up their hands (Oh vey!) and decided that the birds 
sacrificed in the original temple were okay (doves, quail, etc.), and others 
thru tradition (ducks, geese).

A thousand years pass. Every Jew on the planet knows what birds are 
permitted.

Columbus sailed for the new world the day after the Jews were expelled from 
Spain; his navigtor was Jewish. Jews were early in the New World.

They find a critter called a "turkey." **

Letters go back and forth between the New World Jews and the sages of the 
generation:

"Is 'Turkey' kosher?"
"What is "turkey?"
"It's a big bird. Tail feathers like a peacock, but all brown."
"Does it eat carrion?"
"We don't know. It eats corn."
"What's corn?"
and so on.

Ultimately, the rabbis decide a turkey is more like a chicken than a vulture 
and turkey, the animal, is deemed kosher. Everybody is notified.

Another several hundred years go by.

Penguins.

The rabbis refuse to start that ball rolling again. I think there is a 
form-letter all rabbis use when this question comes up, saying, in short: 
"Get a life."

-----------
** There are several opinions as to the origin of the word "Turkey." One is 
it is related to the Hebrew word for peacock, "Tuki."
