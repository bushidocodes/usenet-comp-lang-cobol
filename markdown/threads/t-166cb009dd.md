There are almost always ways to "beat" generic code.  Such is the nature of
optimization.  I am not yet convinced that the labor costs associated with
developing a hand-crafted in-memory sort are  unconditionally justified by
improved performance over the "system sort as described".   I know of at
least one "system sort" implementation that is heavily used in system
software and *specifically* allows the specification of its commands and
parameters so that the sort is performed without any I/O operations at all.
Is that so bad?

I suspect your reaction to the use of the "system sort" may either be based
on an implementation in which significant optimization techniques are
unavailable in the software, or on insufficient information about the
available mechanisms for improving performance of that "system sort".

I believe, in the application with which I am most familiar, that the number
of cases in which a "hand-crafted" sort -- particularly one involving a
large table with a complicated key structure -- would *dramatically*
outperform a system sort with a couple of optimizations and the use of input
and output procedures, or a compiler-generated "table sort" based on that
mechanism -- would be so few as to cause me to recommend to our users
"unless you can prove it's broke, don't fix it."  The cost of development of
a "handmade" sort is a whole lot higher than having the SORT verb accomplish
the same thing.

    -Chuck Stevens
