```
the example he cites, I have a table holding the 50 state
abbreviations in alphabetical order, and the search is for "TX".

Here is his query:

Hi!
        I noticed something about the Search All clause that I wanted
to
        share with you. So, here goes -

    As you have rightly explained, the Search All clause performs a
binary
    search. Out of pure curiosity, I tried using the Varying
    <Numeric-Data-Item> clause with it, in order to find out how many
    steps it took to eventually find Texas. But to my dismay, I
    encountered a compiler error. I presume that the Varying clause
simply
    counts forward from an initial value, until the search terminates.
If
    this is so, then why doesn't it work in the Search All case?

    Now, suppose I wanted to know how many steps the Search All clause
    took, then what options do I have?
 - Another When Clause ? 
       I can't code another When clause that increments a counter
       everytime the search for Texas fails.
 - Even if I could, since the counter would not be a key, I couldn't
use
 it!

        I hope you find this interesting and worth a thought!

--------------
```

```
Thane,
  I guess my question is WHY (what "programming logic") reason would you ever
WANT (or need) to know how many elements were compared?  I think that the
ANSI/ISO Standard that "SEARCH ALL" is a non-serial (and not a binary) search
method (and the fact that you canNOT use the VARYING clause) means that the
number of comparisons required to reach the "WHEN" condition is variable, not
reliable, and not something with an "external" interface.  I could (vaguely)
imagine a compiler that semi-randomly varies the technique it uses to find
the condition.  Therefore, it could take 3 comparisons to reach "TX" one
time - and 5 the next (with the same table) and that this would be
conforming - and even (possibly) a good/optimized implementation.

Bottom-line:
   You canNOT know how many comparisons resulting in reaching your WHEN
condition (or even AT END).  If you have a programming need to know how many
comparisons were required, then you need to do a sequential SEARCH (or your
own PERFORM routine).
```

```
<<I think that the ANSI/ISO Standard that "SEARCH ALL" is a non-serial (and
not a binary) search  method (and the fact that you canNOT use the VARYING
clause) means that the number of comparisons required to reach the "WHEN"

Not exactly right; in particular, the word "is" in the phrase " ... is a
non-serial ... search method ..." doesn't exactly reflect the wording of the
standard.

ANSI X3.23-1985 states, on Page VI-126 in Paragraph 6.22.4(4), "If format 2
of the SEARCH statement is used, a nonserial type of search may take place
...".    ANSI X3.23-1974 has the same wording on  Page III-9 in Paragraph
3.3.4(3).

The word "may" in this context is permissive, not imperative.  It is up to
the compiler implementor to decide what to do with SEARCH ALL.  The
algorithms permitted by this wording include a binary search, a sequential
search (which means that SEARCH ALL is fundamentally no more efficient than
SEARCH without ALL), or anything in between.

Unisys A Series COBOL74 implements a *serial* search for SEARCH ALL, in
conformance with that wording, partly because our customers demanded the
same item be found by SEARCH ALL as would be found by SEARCH, even when the
table contained duplicates in violation of the specifications for the verb.
However counterintuitive this might be, a serial search algorithm for SEARCH
ALL, while perhaps arguably  *discouraged* by the intent of the standard, is
nevertheless *permitted* by the *wording* of the standard.

The Unisys A Series COBOL85 choice to use a binary search for SEARCH ALL
(thus risking different results with duplicate table entries) is probably in
better conformance with the *spirit* of the standard than the A Series
COBOL74 choice was.

But the bottom line according to the standard is that there is no obligation
on the part of the compiler implementor to use a fundamentally different
approach for SEARCH ALL than is used for SEARCH; the wording allows the
implementor to do so, but does not require it.

    -Chuck Stevens
```

```
Though rare, they do exist.  The only one I know in any depth is the
proportional search.  I'm not sure I can describe this.  Rather than
looking at the mid-point of the table, it takes the search key's
relative position in the key space.  For example:

The key is 3 digits.  There are 20 items in the table with these keys
## Key
 1 040
 2 050
 3 052
 4 088
 5 100
 6 200
 7 220
 8 222
 9 223
10 226
11 280
12 400
13 405
14 410
15 420
16 600
17 700
18 710
19 720
20 721 

We want to find key 220.  Record 1 is key 40, record 20 is 721, so if
the keys are distributed evenly, key 220 should be record #
(220-40)*(20-1)/(721-40) + 1 = 3420/681 + 1 = 6.022
Record #6 is actually key 200, so we calculate in the space above
(220-200)*(20-6)/(721-200) + 6 = 280/521 + 6 =6.537
This rounds up to 7 (even if it rounded down to 6 it would round up to
7), which is the record we're looking for.  We found the correct
record in 2 iterations where a binary search would take 4 or 5.

If the data is relatively uniform, this search will usually have fewer
iterations.  The man who introduced me to this search boldly claimed
it was never more iterations than a binary search, which I promptly
showed him was false.  (As an exercise, try searching for 280.)
```

```
Binary search algorithms are of order Log (2) N, where N is the number of
search elements.

While the typical implementation may vary (many use heuristics to get even
better performance) this pretty much means that an entry in a table of 32
elements can be found with no more than 5 comparisons (2**5=32); a table of
16,777,216 entries (2**24) can be searched with no more than 24 comparisons;
etc. 

These are worst case situations - your mileage will vary based upon the data
and implementation.
```
