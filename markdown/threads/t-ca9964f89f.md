Last month itbusiness.ca had an article about top management losing
confidence in their IT department.  This month there is a survey that
shows IT having little faith in top management.  And somewhere I read
that IT should be focusing on the bottom line and adding business
value.  In short many organizations remain dysfunctional.

My belief is that many companies get the IT that they manage for.  In
most organizations, if the IT department isn't functioning well, I
suspect that the relations among the rest of the department are not
good as well.  If an organization, doesn't believe in documentation and
providing help to its employees to do a job, the applications will
probably reflect that.  While I am basically interested in the technical
side and have little interest in setting the overall goals of an
organization, I want to use my technical skills to meet the needs of
people who are tasked with meeting those goals.  The following is what I
believe is needed for an organization to be successful.

The job of top management is to set the goals, define the culture, and
both live and communicate those things.  There must be the willingness
to hear and deal with bad news.  The story was told a couple of years
ago in the Wall Street Journal that Sam Walton of Walmart used to start
the day asking what went wrong so that the top management could work on
improving while the then head of K-mart didn't want to hear any bad news
believing the lower levels should correct the problem without his help. 
Note today which organization is in bankruptcy.  Note which organization
is noted for having effective IT.  Equally important is making certain
that common terminology is used.  There has to be agreement on the
definition of terms such as a sale, a customer, or a product.  I know of
one organization that is spending a good deal of money to accomplish
this.  There must be teamwork in both setting and achieving the goals. 
As an applications programmer/analyst, I did not know the intimate
details of the business.  I had to depend on those who were actually in
the area of concern such as accounts receivable to make sure that what I
did met the business need.  At higher levels, it is not up to IT to
determine whether it is more important to get inventory turnaround
higher than it is to improve the order entry process.  It is up to IT to
provide insight as to the tools and costs of the tools to achieve the
goals.  IT should also provide the vision as to how multiple goals can
be combined and the roles that other people will play in them.

A second thing that must be a top management decision is that
documentation is important and requires people who have writing skills.
Documentation is needed so that an organization knows what it is doing.
This includes help systems and anything else needed so that an
organization isn't dependent on the memory of old Joe.  It will be
expensive to do it if an organization hasn't been doing so.  The
benefits are that processes that are documented probably help qualify an
organization for ISO 9000 certification and there will be fewer
surprises in any process upgrade be it a computer system, a 
manufacturing process or the set of hiring guidelines.

The IT organization should truly believe in teamwork and review.  Like
many people I can spot everyone's mistakes but mine.  I have been
fortunate enough to work in places where people willingly share their
knowledge.  What seems to be rare is formal review and post-mortems on
projects.  If we don't keep track of both what went right and what went
wrong as well as the time taken, our estimates will continue to be bad
and we will both repeat mistakes and forget the good practices.  This is
not an exercise in shooting the wounded, it is a way to reduce the
wounds in future battles.

IT also has to realize that it is basically in the communication and
publishing business.  An order entry system exists to let people in the
organization know that a customer is buying goods or services to be sent
to a given place and paid for by some method.  It probably has a
component that keeps track of what goods an organization has and where
they are.  It will only function well when terms such as customer are
understood in the same way by all concerned.  Changes to that or any
other system may have side effects so testing and specification is a
team effort involving both those who understand the computer side and
those who know how the business should work.  

While computer systems, even Windows, aren't as bad as some detractors
portray them, reliability isn't what it could be.  Much of this is at
the level of the quirks that we put up with in our cars or our dwelling
places but some of the problems are more serious.  Greater emphasis must
be placed on testing and verification.  This means that an ongoing
cooperative effort must be made to have current validation suites.  It
is extremely easy to make a change that in itself works but randomly
breaks something else.  Here again, it is not just IT but the various
groups within an organization that must cooperate to achieve the goal of
systems that function well.
  
In short, if IT is to be successful, the implementation and modification
of systems must be a team effort where varying skills are recognized and
used.  Programmers should not be expected to be writers.  People with
political skills can help those who are not good at negotiation.  The
various units must be willing to do their part in specification, review,
and validation.  Mistakes must be acknowledged and recorded so that they
won't be repeated.  Both successes and failures must be acknowledged and
analyzed so that the knowledge from both can be used to implement future
projects better.

Clark Morris, CFM Technical Programming Services Inc.
