Can't imagine why your instructor would say that. Fact is, each
element of WS has to have SOMETHING in it. If you don't declare what
you want, the compiler will make a guess - and not all compilers guess
the same. Some (yours for example) filled the space with blanks
(spaces, X'20'). Other compilers may very will fill with low-values
(X'00'). So, by using a VALUE to initialize the field with what you
want (even if you agree with the default action of the compiler), you
don't take any chances, plus the code is easier to move to a different
compiler that may initialize differently.
```

I have heard reports similar to this before, but when I questioned people
about it, they could never provide examples of "incorrect" behavior.  The two
rules that SOME TIMES cause problems are:

1) It is always the "original" (i.e. REDEFINED) item that gets initialized
(unless you use a specific INITIALIZE of a redefinition)

2) INITIALIZE with an OCCURS DEPENDING ON may cause compile-time errors
(depending on "extension").  This will be fixed in the next Standard, but may
cause you to be unable to use it when you want to - but you should find this
out at compile-time, not run-time.
```
