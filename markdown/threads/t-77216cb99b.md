A couple of "bits of information"

1) High-Values and Low-Values are *not* defined as X'00" and X'FF".  They
are defined as the highest and lowest values in the computers collating
sequence.  This really does mean that it is entirely "conforming" to have
space as "low-value" - if that collatates lowest on your computer.  (Never
on an IBM mainframe - which seems to be where this discussion is targetted -
but definitely existing on some computers.)

2) Hivh-Values *really* varies a lot on many machines.  For a "true ASCII"
(not extended ASCII) system which is only 7-bit, High-Values is (usually)
X"7F'.

3) Moving High-Values (or low-values) to a numeric receiving fields is sorty
of "fuzzy" in the Standard - but does usually cause problems in the "real
world".  The Standard says that it is legal - if your high-values (or
low-values) contains a "numeric"  (possibly integer - I would have to look
this up) value.  In most cases, this is NOT true.  On the other hand, the
following is "clearly" legal (syntax - but not at run-time) - and bound to
cause problems:

   05  Alpha-Field   Pic  X(02).
   05  Num-Field     Pic  9(02)

   Move Hihg-Values to Alpha-Field
   Move Alpha-Field to Num-Field

4) Plug for the next COBOL Standard -
   The draft of the next Standard provides "HIGHEST-ALGEGRAIC" and
"LOWEST-ALGEBRAIC" features that *can* be moved to numeric fields - and will
get the "expected" (desired) results.

P.S.  Given how long it looks like it will be until the next Standard - if
you are interested in this "enhancement" - during your own life time - then
contact your vendor-of-choice and ask for this enhancement NOW as an
extension.

True, and we had another rule, which frequently overruled that one: Save 
every byte you can.  Some times we got to pick which rule we followed, 
and sometimes we were told.  When the save-evevy-byte rule was in effect, 
dates were almost always PIC 9(5) COMP-3, and 99365 was the end of time.

Later on when I got away from COBOL, we stored dates in the "more 
convenient" YYMMDD form, packed into three bytes.  When that shop added a 
COBOL compiler and brought in a few new COBOL programmers, they found 
those dates quite easy to sort and compare, but a tad tricky to format 
and print.
