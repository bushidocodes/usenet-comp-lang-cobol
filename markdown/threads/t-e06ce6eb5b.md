This is a matter of opinion, of course, but I don't agree. And this is
one of my areas of scholarship - I'm pursuing a degree in digital
rhetoric in my spare time, and I presented on the rhetorical use of
typography and color at a couple of academic conferences (Watson and
CCCC) in the last year. So this is a topic I have been studying in
some depth of late.

Something can only be improved in relation to a set of criteria for
success. Broadly speaking, the success criteria for written messages
have been grouped into three categories: communication (the domain of
technical communication scholars), persuasion (rhetoric), and
aesthetics (poetics).

Communication criteria consider the probability that the recipient's
interpretation is one intended by, or at least agreeable to, the
author; and also take up questions of efficiency, readability, etc.
Here textual effects can be used to good effect, but only with
significant caveats. The design has to be effective, and the
technology has to operate consistently. Neither of those are fulfilled
by the typical HTML email or Usenet post. Relatively few people have
any significant training in visual design, typography, readability,
etc. And because HTML user agents are not under the author's control,
significant care and expertise are required to ensure that the text
will render well in all, or even most, viewers. Most authors are
neither equipped for nor interested in satisfying those constraints.
Instead they use HTML text effects naively, in ways that add nothing
to or detract from the communicative effectiveness of their message.

The rhetorical functions of textual effects are not very well
understood (despite a fair amount of theory and empirical research on
the question). Authors are on even shakier ground trying to use
textual effects to make their messages more persuasive.

With email and Usenet, we can largely ignore questions of aesthetics.
There are few artists working in those media, and few readers
expecting to serve as that kind of audience.

All of these factors are aggravated when addressing a broad or unknown
audience - as is the case with Usenet. Many people still read Usenet
messages with text-only clients. Many people still read it over
low-bandwidth connections. Many people have visual requirements that
don't cope well with J Random Poster's lousy font choices - or just
don't have a compatible font installed. Many people simply don't want
to see HTML or MIME content. Those readers will be annoyed by attempts
to use textual effects. Already the author has alienated part of the
audience; it's unlikely that any communicative or rhetorical
advantages gained by textual effects will overcome that deficit.

> The world is moving on. I've been using HTML mail for years now and never 
> had a problem.

MIME proponents have been saying that since 1992. It's entirely beside
the point. A significant number of readers *do not want* MIME or HTML
in media where they add little or no value. We have not wanted them
since 1992. We are unlikely to spontaneously begin to want them in 2009.

Additional affordances do not automatically make a technology better.
Background music has recognized communicative, rhetorical, and
aesthetic value for television and film; but if I got an email with
embedded background music (assuming that content was processed by my
user agent, which is highly unlikely) I'd delete it unread. It'd be
just as annoying as those Hallmark cards that play music.

There are media where typographic and color effects can be used to
good purpose - though even then only with care. Email and Usenet are
rarely, if ever, among them.

Of course some will disagree. And they will post in HTML, and their
posts will be ignored by people who aren't using MIME-enabled user agents.

William M. Klein wrote:
>    For my own information, what do you think about the (HTML) capability of 
> mixing proportional and non-proportional fonts?

As with most typesetting questions, there's no simple answer.

Fixed-width fonts are usually better for code - as are for example
fonts which use clearly distinct glyphs for all the characters that
are significant in the source code (so eg numeral 1 does not look like
lowercase letter l). That's a special context, where there are
typesetting constraints that don't apply to normal prose.

The use of proportional fonts (or fonts that are otherwise unsuitable
for setting code) for body text is a much more complicated question.
Few people have training in typesetting or visual design, and most
seem to make poor font choices: witness the widespread use of
unfortunate fonts like Arial, Verdana, and the accursed Comic Sans;
apparently random decisions between serif and sans-serif fonts; and so
forth.

And most software does a lousy job of displaying non-monospace fonts -
even Word, for example, is pretty poor at kerning. That's if it even
finds a suitable font and scales it properly.

> For this group (com.lang.cobol), I do find that those posts that are able to use 
> non-proportional fonts for COBOL source code - especially when discussing 
> "column sensitive" issues is an advantage.  I find it particularly useful (to 
> me) when "discussion" text is proportional while source code is 
> non-proportional.

Individual users may find it easier or more pleasant to read body text
set in a proportional font. Given the problems above, and my own
experiences with styled-text email, I'm not interested in looking at
Usenet posters' font experiments.

> Obviously, I would also find it useful (NOT in comp.lang.cobol) to be able to 
> mix alphabets (codesets) when discussing (in English) text in another alphabet 
> (e.g. Cyrillic) much less text in Kanji - and this doesn't even get into the 
> left-to-right vs right-to-left issues.

There are provisions in Usenet for using character sets and encodings
other than ASCII. Doing so restricts your audience, of course, but the
mechanisms are there. This doesn't require HTML; and it's a rather
different question than whether Usenet posters should decorate their
messages with color and font changes and the like.

> Are there ways to use "mixed fonts" and/or mixed alphabets in non-HTML posts?  I 
> am not aware of them, but that doesn't mean they don't exist.

Usenet itself doesn't impose any restriction on what appears in the
message body, thanks to MIME and content-encodings.

In terms of what the typical Usenet user agent will render, plain text
and HTML are pretty much the only choices. Plain text usually means
ASCII, but you can specify other character encodings, including ones
that support character sets for more than one alphabet / writing
scheme (various Unicode encodings, various ISO encodings, CJK
encodings, etc).

Plain text gives the poster no control over fonts, though.

Texts are never independent of their representations and

too much to try to summarize. Empirical studies have shown that even

within the conventions of discourse communities and genres. You don't
read a letter from a child using the same assumptions and criteria

the same assumptions and criteria they apply to novels they consider
"literature" (ie, high as opposed to popular art).
