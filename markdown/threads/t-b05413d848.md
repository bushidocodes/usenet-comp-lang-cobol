There are no intrinsic functions for calculating the difference
between two times. Any such calculation is the responsibility
of the programmer. This means that the program, to be
accurate, must maintain a table of dates for whch leap
seconds have been added and will be added.

I agree that the proposed change is serious as it has the
potential to break every program that relies on
CURRENT-DATE having values of only 0 through 59 seconds.
For example, a program which verifies the time stamp on
a transaction will incorrectly reject a transaction with a valid
time of 23:59:60. It may also incorrectly accept it!

I think the change to WHEN-COMPILED is humorous; but
ultimately it is better to make everything correct as soon
as possible.

It is my understanding that leap seconds may be added
at the end of June 30th or Dec 31st; but not more than one
leap second will be added to either of those dates. There
is also a possibility that leap seconds may be added
to both dates in the same year.

The 1985 ANSI C standard allowed for two leap seconds.
The current draft of C contains changes to reduce that to
one leap second! Those COBOL language implementations
that use C or C++ for their runtimes or those running on
Unix already have some ability to accomodate this change.

COBOL is late in correcting for this problem; but better late
than never.

For the US, the correct current time, from the US Naval
Observatory (?), is broadcast at several frequencys in
both an oral and encoded format. There are receivers
that translate the encoded format and, with appropriate
software, update computer clocks automatically. Those
computers must be able to accommodate leap seconds
and, by extension, those programs (and languages) used
on those computers must accommodate leap seconds,
as well.

Many VCRs are already capable of receiving a translated
time signal though their connection to the local cable
operator. (Actually, I don't know if it is the cable operator or
TV stations; but my VCR is always accurate and shows the
correct time within seconds after a power failure.) Soon, we
are likely to have the ability to inexpensively synchronize all
computers to the correct time. This means that all programs,
sooner or later, must be able to accommodate leap seconds.
------------------
Rick Smith
