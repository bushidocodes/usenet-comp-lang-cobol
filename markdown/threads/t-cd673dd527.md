In your report you state:

"In general, I would like to ask the relatively simple question: What
difference will it make to you, to any existing COBOL programmer, or
COBOL vendor,  if, on June 24, 2001, there is no ANSI COBOL Standard?
(isn't it possible that it will actually be an improvement as vendors
and users will be forced into talking to each other rather than using
ANSI procedures as an excuse for not  resolving outstanding issues and
questions?) "

Consider this:  most government (and some private) contracts for
software and hardware require that a certain ANSI or ISO standard be
followed.  If there is no ANSI standard for Cobol, might that mean
that new software written for the government would have to be in a
language that is ANSI compliant?  

You stated that this reaffirmation does not effect ISO one way or the
other.  I would beg to differ.  If ANSI is dropped, how long before
ISO is also dropped?  

Concerning users and vendors talking to each other if there is no ANSI
standard:  I really doubt that is going to happen.  I think for those
vendors that want to stay in the compiler writing business, especially
on the PC side, they're going to, without a standard to follow, go off
and develop their own functions and maybe syntax.  I don't see IBM
doing any such thing since they are just about the only player in the
mainframe market anyway, but the PC market is suspect.

You also stated in the report:

"My final question is to anyone who actually believes that it is
critical that the current ANSI COBOL Standard (and amendment or
amendments) be reaffirmed: "

I'm starting to believe that it is critical to have a standard.  The
reasons you give for not affirming:

" does not seem to me to be any existing group (Technical
   Committee or otherwise) with both the resources and the priorities
to actively maintain this Standard - if it were reaffirmed."

makes no sense since the standard has existed for many years in just
the same atmosphere and there seems to be a willingness among vendors
to follow the standard because it is "official".  

Just because J4 can't get there act together shouldn't have a
detrimental effect on whether Cobol is ANSI / ISO certified or not.

FYI -
  US Government contracts usually require support for the FIPS *not* the
ANSI Standard.  These two are not identical (and haven't been for years).

  For example,
    The Intrinsic Function module is *required* in the HIGH level FIPS
Standard but is optional in the ANSI Standard.

Furthermore, as far as my research indicates *no* current COBOL vendors are
"validating" their current (new) releases of their COBOL compilers.

I do hope that the ISO Standard "falls" soon - but the information that I
have is that this is currently "highly unlikely".  However, once again, I
don't see that making much difference because vendors are NOT implementing
all of the current Standard.

Ask your vendor for support for the Correction Amendment requirement for
continuation after a divide-by-zero - with no ON SIZE ERROR phrase - for
example.  IBM documents that they don't support this.

Unisys documents that they don't handle ":" in Copy/Replacing according to
the current ANSI Standard.

The bottom-line - however, is that if you think the ANSI Standard is a "good
thing" - then I do encourage you to send in your comments BOTH on the
reaffirmation and the current Public Review period.

I have submitted my initial feedback to the article to which Bill Klein
refers at the site on which it appears.  That article opposes the
reaffirmation of the standards for the COBOL language that are currently in
force. I felt it appropriate to post similar comments in this forum as well.

Unfortunately, prior comittments over the next two weeks prevent me from
preparing an in-depth response to Bill's article in a timely fashion, but I
feel I am ethically obligated, not only on behalf of myself but my company
and the customers we serve, to register my disagreement with him on this
matter.   I hope to prepare a more in-depth response to his article within a
few weeks.

I have personally been involved with development and maintenance of several
COBOL compilers over the last sixteen years.  Based on that past *and
current* experience I strongly believe having a standard to which all
implementors are expected to adhere makes for a better and a more consistent
language for all users of that language.  I am strongly opposed to allowing
the current standards to lapse, and I am strongly in favor of the adoption
and refinement of the current Final Committee Draft as a new standard, as
expeditiously as possible.

Moreover, as the primary representative on NCITS/J4 for Unisys Corporation,
I believe I can state on behalf of my management that Unisys supports both
the reaffirmation of the current standard and the continued efforts to
produce its successor, believing that both positions are not only in the
best interests of Unisys but also in the best interest of its current and
future customers.

    -Charles C. Stevens
    -Unisys Corporation
    -NCITS/J4 Primary Representative

On 7 March 2001 at 7:21PM, William M. Klein wrote:

<<If you support the reaffirmation of the current ANSI COBOL Standard, can
you comment on your company's committtment to implementing the Correction
Amendment specification of how to handle ":" in "partial-word"
COPY/REPLACING statements?>>

My company is committed to correcting the MCP/AS COBOL85 bug that prevents
the use of partial words in COPY...REPLACING and REPLACE statements.   At no
point was it our intent that this compiler be noncompliant; the implementors
(and subsequently I) simply misunderstood the implications of the standard
(and more particularly of the differences from the '74 standard) in this
area.    The entire COBOL85 team as well as the field support staff involved
in languages has been made aware of this error in our collective
understanding.

We have been and remain committed to compliance with the standard in this,
and all, areas.   This deficiency in MCP/AS COBOL85 is being treated as a
bug fix and is queued for correction.

We encourage our MCP/AS COBOL85 users to submit trouble reports for any
discrepancies between our implementation and the applicable standard, and
the submission of trouble reports through the field support organization is
the most effective way to get such deficiencies resolved.

<<If you think the current Standard SHOULD be reaffirmed but do not actually
implement the explcit specifications in that Standard, can you explain what
you think "reaffirmation" means?>>

I'm having trouble with the logic flow of this question; it strikes me that
there is at least one presumption in it that's incorrect .  I will thus
address its component phrases:

<<If you think the current Standard SHOULD be reaffirmed>>

Yes, I and my management believe that doing so serves not just Unisys but
more importantly our customers.   As suppliers of compilers that we work
hard to ensure are indeed compliant, we would find the lapse of the current
standards a great loss to us and a disservice to our customers.

<< but do not actually implement the exact specifications in that Standard,
>>

To the best of our ability, and to the best of our understanding,we do
implement the exact specifications of that Standard. There may be lapses in
the first case and mistakes in the second, but both are simply errors and
they are recorded and corrected as such.   No human endeavor is without
flaw.  We correct flaws in our products that we discover and that are
reported to us.

<<can you explain what you think "reaffirmation" means?>>

Since we *do* support reaffirmation of the standard, and we *do* implement
to the standard to the very best of our ability, there is no point in
providing an answer based on the combination of premises raised, because
that combination (introduced by "IF") is false.

Nevertheless, I will state that I believe "reaffirmation" in this context is
a formal recognition that the standard should continue in force.

    -Chuck Stevens

Chuck (and any other vendors watching this thread),

I would SUGGEST that you go thru the "Substantive Change" section in the
Correction Amendment and run the tests that are "listed" there (i.e. by
example).  It is now 8 years since the example for COPY/REPLACING was placed
in an approved amendment.  If you had "tested" it when that Standard was
"out for review" - it would now be a decade since you would have known how
this was REQUIRED to work in an ANSI (or ISO or FIPS) conforming compiler.

Besides the COPY/REPLACING example, I strongly suggest that you test your
compiler for the example on page B-36 - in the entry titled,
  "(8) Size error condition without ON SIZE ERROR phrase (1 NUC)."

Again, like the COPY/REPLACING issue, this is clearly documented as required
by the "base" '85 Standard and was not introduced by any subsequent
amendment - but wasn't identified as a substantive change until 1993.

This is *not* intended to "single out" Unisys - which has indeed indicated
their "renewed" interest in J4 - after they let their prior membership
lapse.  It is, however, intended to point out how little attention is paid
(even today) to those COBOL Standards that *do* get approved by ANSI and/or
ISO.
