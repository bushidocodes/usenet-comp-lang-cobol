For the record, it wasn't just programs written using COBOL which had  to be 
upgraded. Many, many, many applications written in using many, many 
different languages had been written assuming "YYMMDD" would be always be 
sufficent to compare two dates or add two years to one.

To answer your next question, "CC" was not routinely included in dates 
because it consumed memory, both primary (chips) and secondary (disk). 
Memory was a lot more expensive then.

> I assume (perhaps incorrectly) that COBOL programs are primarily for
> business applications, unlike FORTRAN for science and math, and BASIC
> for basic programming.

I'll take issue with your limitation on the BASIC language, but the COBOL 
and FORTRAN generalizations are not unreasonable. After all, "COBOL" is an 
acronym for COmmon Business Oriented Language., and "FORTRAN"  for FORmula 
TRANslation.

("BASIC" is allegedly an acronym for Beginners All-Purpose Symbolic 
Instruction Code, but most historians now agree that "BASIC as an acronym" 
rather than as a meaningful word unto itself came well after the fact).

Lastly on choice of language: It's not the paintbrush, it's the artist.

 > Why were companies still using such ancient code in 1999?

It worked. And as DD pointed out, much of it still does.

Of course, well-written software should have a long useful life; which 
should tell you something vis-a-vis my opinion on today's quality versus the 
quality we put into our products then.  Today there is so much 
short-sightedness caused by demand for short-term profit that the investment 
in really good software is simply not made. (There will be an expansion on 
this topic when (if?) I get around to writing that book).

C programs written in the 1980s could have used a six digit date, but the vast majority
did not. Why did Cobol programmers continue to use six digits, as late as systems
developed during the early 1990s, when other programmers did not?

I postulate the answer is cultural. They were following established norms. As others have
pointed out, limited memory was a rational reason for abbreviating dates in the 1960s. The
hardware limitation was largely gone in the 1970s, and completely gone by the 1980s. But
Cobol programmers in the 1980s already had 20 years of experience, whereas C programmers
were new to the game. That experience was etched in their style. 

They rationalized their choice of six digits by saying 'We'll all be gone by 2000' and
'This system won't last ten years' without serious thought or substantiation. Their
decision was based on style rather than reason. 

The culture of Cobol, unlike other languages, is oriented toward ritualistic conformance
with community norms, formalized as Shop Standards. Each shop acts as though its Standards
were engraved in granite and handed down from heaven, even though they wildly disagree
with each other (sounds like religion, doesn't it?) Beyond the published Standards are
many de facto stylistic norms saying 'do it the same way we've always done it and you'll
be safe.' The six digit date was one of those norms. 

In short, the Cobol culture was about conformity. Y2K was caused by 1960s hardware limits
projected to 1998 by cultural inertia.

It seems to me that some of the discussion of 6-digit/8-digit dates (2 vs 4 
digit years) seems to be ignoring the issue of "dates in files" being a "who 
knows where a field is used problem".  In many large shops where dates had been 
stored with 2 digit years, the files were "databases" (in the most general sense 
of the word - and not related to whether they were "flat files" "indexed files" 
"hierarchical databases" "network databases" "relational databases" etc).  The 
problem was that many of these shops did not have good information on in which 
programs in which applications the fields were used.  This made it difficult to 
"fix" the storage of a field (change from 2-digit to 4-digit) years as "unknown 
side-effects" were feared.  Even when (after) it was determined which programs 
and applications would be impacted, it was often VERY difficult to change all 
the programs at the same time.

Obviously, this is what was required (in the end) but it was part of the reason 
for some shops delaying (postponing) such changes.

It also partially explained the rise in "repository" or "warehouse" gathering 
tools during the 90's.  (It also relates to the IBM - at least - introduction of 
tools to "recover" source code for lost programs.  This started with OS/VS COBOL 
to VS COBOL II conversions but became critical for some Y2K resolutions.)

Those of the latest generation who have never bothered to study the 
history of computing just have no concept of the limited resources that 
were available during the early days of business computing.  Those whose 
experience is limited to the post-MS Windows era have probably never 
even heard of a computer with less than 4 MiB of memory (make that 128

awake/working during the day find the concept of changing the date at 
noon singularly non-useful.  IBM simply overloaded "Julian" to have a 
different meaning in the context of computer dates.  Giving words 
different meanings and implications in different contexts and different 
fields of endeavor is a highly common practice.  There is no reason why 
the interpretation of "Julian" in "Julian day" should be sacrosanct -- 
any more than the use of "Julian Day" by astronomers be criticized for 
use of "Julian" with a totally different meaning from that in "Julian

hardware-specific counter.  This forces those conventional date formats

 Burroughs Medium Systems was a line of Cobol computers. Most Cobol statements compiled to
one machine instruction. Most machine instructions had two operands where each operand had
type, length and address. If you wanted to add a packed number to a display number, you
could do it with one instruction. It had two move and compare instructions: alpha and
numeric. When sending and receiving fields were of unequal length, the numeric ones added
or deleted leading zeros (and did type conversion), the alpha ones added or deleted
trailing spaces. 

Memory addresses were decimal. Every nybble was addressable. An unsigned packed one digit
variable had a length of one nybble. 

The Cobol compiler allowed inline assembly code, which could reference Cobol paragraph and
data names, including qualification. Assembler code was seldom required; you could do
almost everything in Cobol. Major chunks of the operating system (MCP) were written in
Cobol. 

Executable programs were typically one third the size of the same program compiled on IBM
or most any other computer. 

There were three instruction sets available, two of which could be resident
sumultaneously. The other two were FORTRAN, which had lots of floating point instructions,
and ALGOL, which was used principally by the OS kernel and compilers. The job scheduler
treated the instruction set as a required resource. It would not schedule a Cobol and
FORTRAN program to run at the same time. 

The most well-known models were the B2000, B3500 and B4700.

Development stopped when Burroughs was swollowed by Unisys in 1986.
