```
Most of us in the mainframe world still have to deal with line printer
reports, and one of the most time consuming tasks is keeping the column
heading lined up with the detail data, especially when someone looks at the
report, and says "It looks good, but could you change the order of the
columns to ....", or "could you put more space between the columns".

I have a solution that makes the task a bit easier by using the detail line
for the column headings.
Moving columns around is no big deal, because the group item (column
heading) contains the detail line data.

I'm posting this because I always see separate working storage layouts for
column headings, and it  needlessly complicates development, and
maintenance.

Here is some sample code:

019700 01 R2-DTL-DETAIL-LINE-1.
019800
019900*    NAMES ENDING IN COH ARE THE COLUMN HEADING
020000
020100     05 R2-DTL-CC                           PIC X      VALUE ' '.
020200     05                                                PIC X
VALUE  SPACES.
020300     05 R2-DTL-BIN-NBR-COH.
020400       10 R2-DTL-BIN-NBR              PIC ZZ9.
020500     05                                                PIC X
VALUE  SPACES.
020600     05 R2-DTL-ITM-CNT-COH.
020700       10 R2-DTL-ITM-CNT              PIC ZZ,ZZ9.
020800     05                                                PIC XX
VALUE  SPACES.
020900     05 R2-DTL-ITEM-ID-COH.
021000       10 R2-DTL-ITEM-ID              PIC X(16).
021100     05                                              PIC XX     VALUE
SPACES.
021200     05 R2-DTL-ITEM-DESC-COH.
021300       10 R2-DTL-ITEM-DESC            PIC X(30).
021400     05                                                  PIC XX
VALUE  SPACES.
021500     05 R2-DTL-ITEM-LOC-COH.
021600       10 R2-DTL-ITEM-LOC             PIC X(16).
021700

036500
036600 3000-01-01-PRT-RPT2-COL-HEAD.
036700
036800     MOVE  '0'                                    TO R2-DTL-CC.
036900     MOVE  'BIN'                               TO R2-DTL-BIN-NBR-COH.
037000     MOVE  '   QTY'                           TO R2-DTL-ITM-CNT-COH.
037100     MOVE  'ITEM ID'                        TO R2-DTL-ITEM-ID-COH.
037200     MOVE  'ITEM DESCRIPTION '  TO R2-DTL-ITEM-DESC-COH.
037300     MOVE  'ITEM LOCATION'        TO R2-DTL-ITEM-LOC-COH.
037400
037500     WRITE  RPT2-SETUP-REPORT-REC-OUT
037600            FROM  R2-DTL-DETAIL-LINE-1
037700
037800     MOVE  SPACES
037900           TO  R2-DTL-BIN-NBR-COH
038000                  R2-DTL-ITM-CNT-COH
038100                  R2-DTL-ITEM-ID-COH
038200                  R2-DTL-ITEM-DESC-COH
038300                  R2-DTL-ITEM-LOC-COH.
```

heh - I am.  I get shot down, but it's usually by management, not by 
other programmers.  They generally think this stuff is really cool.

Well, imagine you've been on COBOL 74 since 1979 - and in 2001, you were 
finally able to start writing in COBOL 85.  Now, there's this guy in the 
office who, in his off time, networks with other COBOL professionals and 
gleans tips on style, practices, and efficient ways of doing things, and 
shares them with his co-workers.  Wouldn't you be inclined to at least 
listen?  :)

Not to toot my own horn, but I brought our entire application into COBOL 
85.  I dug into the system-level workings and got them compliant, and 
after that, it was as easy as a recompile (as not much went away between 
74 and 85).  I did it for several reasons...

1.  Our programs were getting too big to compile without implementing 
segmentation.  The "extended mode" executables created by the Unisys 
COBOL 85 compiler will segment themselves automatically (if you tell 
them to).

2.  We needed to be able to call routines to copy files around. 
Program-Callable FURPUR (PCFP) allowed us to do that, but it only works 
for extended-mode programs.

3.  When all of us first get to our shop, we're given 3 weeks of 
training (beginner, intermediate, and advanced) in COBOL.  But, it's 
COBOL 85 - so many of the things we'd learn, such as Evaluate, or inline 
Performs, we couldn't use.  (I think most folks will agree that an 
Evaluate is more maintainable than a slew of nested If statements...) 
So, this point is really "increased maintainability".

Now, inspired a bit by PECD and his component methodologies he's been 
touting here, I'm working to bring us into the dynamically called 
component world for some of our more common processes.  There are many 
advantages to this, the biggest one being that we don't have to 
recompile every program because a copy proc changed.  :)

Now, I haven't succeeded at everything I've tried.  When I was 
converting the system-level stuff to COBOL 85, there was a basic-mode 
routine that our date/time proc used to get the date and return a 
century too.  (I guess no one figured out YYYYMMDD...)  I changed this 
one line to "Accept W315-Current-Date-S8 From Date YYYYMMDD" - no 
functional change at all - but because it was in a copy proc which was 
copied in every program in the system, I was told I couldn't change it. 
  My solution - copy the whole proc to another proc, increment the 
number by 100 (P315 to P415), leave the paragraphs and variables the 
same, and press on.

A year later, I was given opportunity to change P315 - I made the 
change, and the programs that were using P415 were changed back to the 
"standard" the next time they were worked.

I guess I say all that to say - I think I'll keep my optimism.  When I 
share what I learn, I've done my part - but, we've got a good bunch of 
programmers that are working there too, and they want to expand their 
skills too.  :)

It allows you to set up some headings (up to 10), vary the spacing on printlines, automatically detects the date (if present) and the Page Number  (if present),  and the time (if present), allows you to simultaneously print various documents (so you could run several reports off ONE pass of the data, at the same time), and automatically takes care of line counts and heading production, unless you manually override it.
