I retired from the computer programming business four years ago,
having spent 35 plus years in the data processing business. My work
was primarily for businesses, mainly in banks and financial
institutions, and in entertainment industry companies (movies, TV,
radio, and publishing). In all of those companies. I also did a bit
of contract programming in the so-called aeorspace industry.

In all of those companies, dates were extremely important, and in most
of them, the issue of what happens on the turn of the century was also
important, since their plans and business activities concerned events
far out in the future.

Back in the mid-60's the first home mortgages were written that
matured after 2000, and the first insurance policies were written that
expired beyond the turn of the century, and the first bonds were sold
with maturity dates beyond 2000. This is not a new problem, even
though many people seem to just be waking up to it.

Although I've been retired for a while, I keep my hand in via reading
and via participation in a number of activities centered around the
use of computers. Lately there seems to be a sudden jump in the
number of articles in both the industry and popular press, predicting
dire consequences just around the corner, where, ostensibly our entire
lifestyle is going to come to a sudden halt because the computers in
our government and businesses are going to begin screwing up royally.

>From my reading, it seems that most of these articles are written by
one of two classes of people. The first is consultants who stand to
bring in a lot of business by "solving" this problem for users, and
the second is niave computer-illiterate freelance writers who have
bought the bullshit lock stock and barrell.

Lissen up, folks. The problems were solved 30 years ago by the
programmers of my generation, and your bank, insurance company and
stock broker are going to survive the change in millenium just fine.
Your government may, as well, though that's less sure and we really
don't give a hoot about them anyway.

The whole issue caused me to rummage through my files, though, and I
found that I still have the source code to a date
translation/calculation module which I wrote and sold widely within
the industry in the 70's and 80's. It's a program called FASTDATE and
at one time, I had versions of it in COBOL, BASIC (Microsoft, DEC and
probably another flavor or two), dBASE and maybe another language or
two. FASTDATE was written based on the Gregorian calendar, and is set
up to either be complied as a stand-alone module to be called by other
programs, or to be incorporated directly within a host program. The
latter method is rarely used, as it makes a lot more sense to have one
Black Box which everyone uses and only takes up space once on a disk

The way FASTDATE works is that you call it supplying a date in one of
a number of forms, and a code telling you which form you're passing,
the choices of which are
1) Calendar (6 or 8 digit)
2) Ordinal (year and day of year)
3) Gregorian (number of days since the beginning of the
Gregorian calendar).

FASTDATE returns you a number of useful pieces of information about
the date you input.

First, it tells you if it's a valid date in the first place, and if
valid, what day of the week it is.

Second, it tells you if it is a working date, based not only on the
traditional Monday through Friday, but taking into account national
holidays AND a table of your own company holidays (which you can
maintain quite simply). This is very important for companies which
must plan and schedule things only on valid working days, for
instance.

Third, it gives you back the equivalent of your input date in the
other forms, and in addition, gives you a spelled-out date that you
can use in report headers or the like, i. e., April 25, 1996.

>From the information FASTDATE provides you, you can easily calculate
the number of days between two days, the number of weeks and days
between two days, whether or not a week from Thursday falls on a
holiday or any other relationships you might wish to derive.
FASTDATE has been independently validated to be accurate from Octobrer
15, 1582 (day 1 of the Gregorian calendar) out to something like
December 29th 2625 (I can't really remember how far it goes out, but
it's surely beyond the lifetime or business deal of anyone around
now).

FASTDATE source code is about 3 - 4k in ASCII form.

I used to think that every programmer worth his pocket protector could
figure out how to write a similar program, but after selling it for a
while and seeing some of the questions that have arisen about dates
over the years, I realize I was wrong and that while many prople
probably could duplicate the program, most are too busy doing
something else.

So...

If anyone is intrerested, I can supply the source code and
documentation for FASTDATE, in plain vanilla ASCII form, in either of
two languages; COBOL or MS BASIC (the old version, not VB of QB).
While that mignt not be just exactly what you want, the code is
clearly enough written that you should be able to use it a pseudocode
to write a duplicate in any language you can think of.

I think a fair charge would be $20 for a zip file that contains all
the information and code you need, e-mailed or snail mailed directly
to your door (computer).

Interested parties may e-mail me at the address below. I don't
subscribe to this newsgroup, just popped in to make my pitch, so if
you want to comment, please do so via e-mail directly.

Many thanks for your indulgence...
