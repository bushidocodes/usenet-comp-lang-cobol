```
I'm sharing this experience in the hope you can profit from it.

A few weeks ago a main development machine suffered a hard drive crash.
This machine had over 12 million files on it...
To be fair, it was over 7 years old, and hard drives have an MTBF of
around 20,000 hours, so it shouldn't have been unexpected.

My first thought was that over 30 years worth of COBOL code was gone,
along with about 10 years worth of C# and some critical source for PRIMA
products. I knew there were backups (and later found that they were very
current for all the critical code) but it is like being hit with a
hammer and you are in a kind of shock that makes it hard to think straight.

I was devastated and completely de-motivated by it. (I actually
considered closing the Company and went into a depression for a couple
of days... :-))

Fortunately, the response, understanding, and support from clients
helped get me out of it. I decided to replace the machine and see if we
could get back to being productive.

You'd think it would really be pretty simple: Get the new machine, get
it configured and working, load everything from backup and away you go.

Such is not the case. There are SO many things that you take for granted
every day and it is not until disaster strikes that you realize you need
them.

The LAN has to accommodate and recognize the new machine and the other
machines on the LAN have to play nicely with it. The web server has to
be reachable and connected through FTP (ours is in Houston, Texas...we
are in Tauranga, NZ...), There are new versions of tools that you may
want to use (our main development used VS 2012, and SQL Server 2008;
could this be an opportunity to modernize?). What about the virtual
machines we use? (Fortunately, most of these are on external hard drives
so they weren't affected, but we still have to get them recognized and
working with the new server...)

What about mail and Office software? (Mail was the FIRST thing that had
to be restored and recovered so I could contact clients and let them
know what's happening.) Again, the disaster became an opportunity and I
got a really good deal on Office 2019 (don't like the subscription model
of Office 365, and the core code is exactly the same...). Outlook 2019
imported the mail accounts pretty well except that there were some
passwords I had forgotten (some of these accounts were very old...). It
took less time to hack the passwords than it would have to set the
accounts up again from scratch, but a lesson was learned and all the
passwords are safely stored now.

In the end, after 3 weeks full on, I am able to say that we can once
again support our products and provide proper cover to clients.

The transitions from VS 2012 to VS 2017 and from SQL Server 2008 to SQL
Server 2017 were pretty painless (but a lot of actual work).

All of the COBOL has been recovered. (A huge weight off my mind.)

I have used the restoration process to review and remove a lot of stuff
that we really don't use any more, and it has enabled me to target more
stuff that we still use but should have replaced by now.

The new machine has enabled me to come to grips with new features in
Windows (I like seeing a boot of Win 10 from cold take 12 seconds (SSD
for the OS) instead of 5 minutes, and being able to login biometrically
with a touch of my fingerprint... The new quad core, up to 4 GHZ
processor, is blazingly fast and, for example, a large Fujitsu NetCOBOL
for Windows compile that used to take around 20 seconds on a virtual
COBOL server, now takes 4 seconds on the same VM... The VM is just
getting much more power from the host.

But the main points I want to convey here are these:

1. Backup critical code EVERY DAY. (You know intellectually that you
should, but it becomes real when disaster strikes...)

2. Take a full image of the hard drive in critical servers at least
every 3 months. (I had such an image that was 6 weeks old and it was a
major help in the recovery.)

3. Keep calm and don't despair. (I wasted considerable time feeling
sorry for myself and it was only the support I was offered that kicked
me out of it...)

Recovery is still proceeding (it will go on for months as a background
task) but all of the critical services are up and running and in the
process of being base lined and having standard test plans run on them.

PRIMA will be officially back on the air as from Monday, 25th of March.

Cheers,

Pete.
```

I'd have to agree with Doc.

Have the system take care of it, but AT LEAST build several layers of safety. To mitigate mechanical issues: minimally setup RAID 1
(I use RAID10 because of the extra speed). With RAID1 there is always a current version of the drive available. If you're paranoid
duplicate that. My experience: Not needed, modern hardware will warn in time but heed the warnings instantly!

Use something like Syncovery to duplicate relevant folders to an extra disk (storage is cheap, especially compared to time spend and
frustration when having to recover. Syncovery can copy files on-change and will maintain versions for a set number of days so your
manual control should be moot - except "knowing what-when-where". Syncovery can sync to cloud services or other local servers. NB:
Think about data security...

Use Acronis for backups. I use 11.5 in GFS (Grandfather-Father-Son) mode. Daily incrementals, Weekly differentials, Monthly full
backup. Dailys are saved 9 days or until next differential, differentials 7 weeks, monthly until manual cleanup. Recovery is easy
& not time consuming BUT do validate every so often. Acronis will send you mails on succes or failure - if so desired. Restoring
individual files from an available point in time is easy.

THINK about what to backup when, to where and how. Out of your 14M files I cannot imagine all files changing every month, in fact I
would pose 10M of them have not changed in the past year... Those unchanged files should be imaged to multiple drives, seperated
from the working set, & not necessarily be backuped with specific software as a backup might be software version sensitive (ie: not
be readable in the future). Save your old backupsoftware, the serials and have an idea of what you've setup and why..

Finally: Consider an automatic off-site backup such as BackBlaze or similar but think about security issues (Patriot Act). I don't
use it but if I were to, I would only send encrypted files even though they store the data highly encrypted. Reason: Your key will
be transmitted to them to find & restore specific files when needed. That is my understanding, preencrypting files and decrypting
locally will pre-empt that risk.

As someone who's been in de MF bussiness for many years I've tried to implement good practises locally even though I have only a
couple of M files. However, with at least 10 HD crashes i have to say - knock on wood - I have never lost more than a few hours
worth of work. The biggest problem then is to redo the thought process, not the work.....

Truly: Not giving this issue proper thought, especially as a bussiness, is plain stupid. IF you don't take precautions and
hardware problems pop up you deserve to go under! As an IT guy you should know and REALISE the importance of a proper backup
mechanism.

This from someone who usually just lurks & infrequently at that.....

There's a difference between what a full backup of all files (I've
been using the word "conventional," but that of course could mean
different things to different people) and a sector-by-sector clone.
Either one of those should let you recover all files on the disk, but
the "conventional" backup should be a lot faster unless your disk is
mostly full. (And if your disk is almost full then you've got other
problems, including crappy performance as new files or parts of file
get fragmented. But you knew that.)

Rather than doing a full backup every day, you can do a full backup
once a week and incremental or differential backups every day. A
differential backup will save all files that have changed since the
last full backup, and an incremental backup will save files that have
changed since the last backup, whether it was full or incremental.

A weekly full backup and daily differential backups will let you
recover everything from at most two backup files. A weekly full backup
and daily incremental backups will let you recover everything from at
most seven files (one full backup and up to six incrementals).

If you have the idle time and the resources -- 4 TB disks are
affordable -- I can't see any reason not to do a full onsite backup
every day, saving the three or so most recent backup files. I would
also do a full encrypted backup to store offsite once in a while.

If you want to do a clone once a month or so as well as everything
else, go for it, but remember that a file backup that gets run is
better than a clone that doesn't.
