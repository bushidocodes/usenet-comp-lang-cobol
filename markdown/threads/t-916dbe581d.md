I sympathize with your dilemma, having been there myself.

The following is arguable and represents opinion. It is not necessarily fact
and many here will disagree. However, it is what I honestly believe...

The Situation:

1. There is little place for  traditional COBOL in the "New Technology".
2. There was a chance for COBOL to claim some of this territory by the
provision of OO COBOL. This was late arriving, due largely to a monolithic
Standards process, was rejected by the user base, who didn't really
understand the Network anyway and weren't prepared to learn about it,  and
the result is that the ship has sailed and COBOL is still on the quay.
(Despite the glee in some quarters over the .COM collapse, many businesses
are now starting to realise that the baby was almost thrown out with the
bath water, and that the Internet is not only here to stay, it is an
ESSENTIAL part of doing business. I predict an upsurge in Web Based business
and increased use of the Internet by nonWeb Based businesses. While COBOL
COULD play a part in this, it is unlikely to do so to any great degree.)
3. The future of COBOL is in batch processing. CICS will keep online legacy
systems running until they are converted, and there will be some limited
development in the future, but its once huge market share will be
continuously eroded by faster, cheaper, transaction processing alternatives
based on OO languages, and packages.
4. There is a strong market demand for less complexity and less need for
procedural programming. This is being met by a number of technologies,
including component based design, and it is likely we will see fundamental
changes in the way that corporations develop computer systems (if they
continue to do so at all...outsourcing is a very attractive alternative to
many businesses), with NO COBOL involved at all.
5. There is a new breed of IT professionals coming out of Acadaemia. They
are NOT "one language wonders" but have a broad understanding of the New
Technology and the tools and languageS available. They do not see solutions
in terms of "How would I program that in COBOL?". These will be the Managers
of tomorrow.
6. Business users and the World in general are becoming more "computer
literate" and capable of developing their own local business solutions with
standard software (spreadsheets, databases, etc.). The traditional role of
"computer programmer" is becoming like the role of "chauffeur"; once the
interface was simplified to the point where "anyone can do it", people opted
(for the most part) to drive themselves.
7. Emerging technologies within the next 15 years will place undreamed of
power in the hands of business users. It is likely that people will interact
iteratively with "smart" software, in their own natural language, to define
business processes.

A bleak picture for COBOL? Maybe. I use it (OO COBOL) to build business
components and find it excellent for that, but I accept that even this use
has a limited lifetime (about as long as my own...<G>).

What to do about it:

1. Expand your skill set and your IT horizons. You are, and will be, as
employable as the skills you have. Learn about standard software, be
familiar with spreadsheets, Relational databases, and Word Processors.

Make sure the following are in your "armoury":

1. At least one OO Language. (I recommend Java, but C++ is probably OK.  You
can teach yourself Java in 4 weeks easily if you apply yourself a couple of
hours a day...what is 4 weeks against the remainder of your career?)
2. HTML, XML, and knowledge about the Web and how to use it.(Again, easily
learned at home.)
3. Develop at least one area of "specialist expertise"; maybe an area of the
business...be the "General Ledger/Inventory
Control/SalesAnalysis/whatever..." Guru. This is saleable.
4. Optionally, study part time for an Accounting or Legal (Corporate Law)
qualification.

Look on your COBOL knowledge as valuable experience that got you into
commercial data processing and gave you insight into the use of computers in
business. You didn't waste your time studying it; it has given you
understanding of many fundamental concepts, but realise that there are also
other possibilities, and the world is changing. Don't expect every new
solution to be like COBOL, and try not to measure appples against oranges.
(Keep your mind open and realise there is more than ONE solution).

2. Consider moving from Technology into Applications. Business Process (Re)
Engineering is an interesting and useful area. So is Auditing (ISO9000
compliance is particularly lucrative...). "Computer programming" is not
everything... and it is becoming less and less...

3.  Do not fear the future. You learned COBOL; you can learn other stuff.
Never lose the "yearning for learning" and your future will take care of
itself.

Good Luck!

Pete.
