```
I can't resist revisiting this a bit.

Programmers who don't like or don't use GOTO's are fond of pointing out the
pitfalls of using the labels that are required: ALTERING a label that is
PERFORMED; falling through a PERFORMed label, etc. etc.; the general thesis
being that since there are several verbs that involve labels programmers who
use GOTO's may well use several verbs on a given label.  Just out of
curiosity, and in a wholly unscientific manner, since no accurate statistics
are possible unless every active Cobol programmer responds, I'd like to get
some ideas on the prevalence of these bad habits.

Note that I am ONLY interested in whether or how often these things are
done.  I have NO interest in commenting on them.

Therefore, I have several questions for those who use GOTO's::

1a - do you ever use ALTERs?
1b - if so, do you ever ALTER a PERFORMed label?
1c - do you ever fall through a label that can be ALTERed?
1d - how often do you come across ALTERs in programs you have to maintain?

2a - do you ever GOTO and PERFORM the same label?
2b - do you ever fall through a PERFORMed label?

3a - do you use both SECTIONS and paragraph labels in the same program?
(Exception: SORT INPUT/OUTPUT procedures don't need to be mentioned).
3b - do you always use a terminating label (i.e., 999-X. EXIT.) to indicate
the end of a PERFORMed paragraph, or a SECTION?  Or sometimes, or never?
3c - do you always, or sometimes, or never, use PERFORM ...THRU i.e., as
opposed to just PERFORM?

4a - do you ever use GOTO DEPENDING ON?
4b - if so, do you ever ALTER any of the target labels?
4c - are any of the target labels also PERFORMed?
4d - are any of the target labels also the destination of an unconditional
GOTO?

My own answers are:

1a - no; 1b,c - n/a; 1d - not for a long time

2a, b - never

3a - sort i/o procedures only.
3b, c- always.

4a- occasionally
4b, c - never
4d - in my youth I did.  Not for a long time, though.

I may come up with other questions.

Again, the object of the exewrcise is just to determine what practices
exist, and therefore (hopefully) to keep discussion of the topic to what IS
rather than what MIGHT BE.

Thanks
PL
```

```
If a set of programs arrives on my desk to be dealt with then it is
not relevant that your survey finds 92% of programmers do not use

days worth of checking that I do not need to do.

I may well have a note with the code that states that all the code has
been written perfectly and there are no incorrect labels or mistaken
go tos, but, cynic that I am, I want the compiler or similar to tell

Then you miss the point completely.

When I have a new pile of source code it is irrelevant what IS on your
desk, or Peter's, the issue is entirely what MIGHT BE within the code
in that pile.

I am saying that Cobol is treated as an archaic language because with
typical styles as described by Peter there is no mechanism that can
answer your questions automatically by examining the code. Certainly,
the style introduces 'signals' such as an end-of-section-exit-
paragraph is a signal that the next label _MUST_ have a SECTION
keyword. But these are all visual and must be laboriously checked
manually to ensure compliance.

Perhaps some checks could be automated but that would probably require
that naming conventions had been used - and then you need some way of
assuring that those had been.

As it happens I have been given a couple of hundred programs where I
have discovered some errors. The program have been developed over a
quarter century and many were originally '74.  The programmer did not
fill out your questionaire in respect of each program so I will have
to determine those answers myself, laboriously.

So the real question is: if I received one of your programs how would
I determine the answers to your questions without having to read every
line and follow every path ?

If I knew that it was you that had written it, and no one else since,
and the peer review procedures and standards of your site I could take
it on trust and would only need to check those bits that needed
changing and keep to your ways.

But if it arrives on my desk and I don't know the author I can only
judge from the code itself, and with Cobol (for reasons explained)
that requires lots of hard work.
```
