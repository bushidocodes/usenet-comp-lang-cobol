```
Seeing as the religious wars are dying out
and the Jensen wars are tapering off into
tired mutterings, I decided that we needed
something new to argue about.

My pet peeve is having to page through 100
lines of quoted material to find a "right on"
at the end.

So. Keeping it on topic, I wrote a Cobol
program last night. It read the last fifteen
days of posts (what I had in my buffer), and
prepared a report.

The report details all posters that posted
more than 5 posts in the last fifteen days.

The statistics garnered include total posts,
total lines posted, total lines quoted, original
lines of message, blank lines, and finally
my patented SPAM RATIO. Bits of code:

COMPUTE SPAM-RATIO = ORIGINAL-LINES / TOTAL-LINES * 100.

CHECK-OUT-POSTED-LINE.
ADD 1 TO TOTAL-LINES.
IF NEWS-LINE IS EQUAL TO SPACE
ADD 1 TO BLANK-LINES
ELSE
IF (FIRST-CHARACTER OF NEWS-LINE IS EQUAL TO ">"
OR
FIRST-CHARACTER OF NEWS-LINE IS EQUAL TO "%"
OR
FIRST-CHARACTER OF NEWS-LINE IS EQUAL TO "#"
OR
FIRST-CHARACTER OF NEWS-LINE IS EQUAL TO ":")
ADD 1 TO LINES-QUOTED
ELSE
ADD 1 TO ORIGINAL-LINES.

results next post.
```

```
You are quite right, putting the quoted material at the end
avoids the problem of having to page through it. In terms
of utility, though, it defeats the purpose.

As I see it, the purpose of quoting material is to give the
reader an idea of where the poster is coming from. By
putting it at the end, one is forced into the methodology
of paging back and forth.

I used to take part in an echo where one regular had a
"thing" about quoting. He made it a policy to never, under
any circumstances, quote. He claimed that one could
always use the traditional writing devices ... standard
methods like "with regard to ...", etc. After reading his
posts for about six months, I found they were always the
best written, and the most interesting.

I tried it for a few months, and found my writing style improved
immensely. Unfortunately, it made posting hard work. (Well,
at least a lot harder). So I went back to using quotations
but attempting to keep it down to four or five lines maximum,
more of an intro than the crux of the post.

One of the things that quoting does do is assure that one
responds in a point by point fashion. Personally, though, I
do not care much for that. I think it makes for boring reading
of the "I said", "you said", "I said" variety. I much prefer to
read the entire post, and if a particular point raises my
interest, respond to that.
```

```
An interesting post. Several years back, I read a book that claimed
the real mistake was not in writing code too soon, but in allowing
the code to stand. In other words, as long as you have ego invested
in the code and refuse to throw away poor work, then you have to
spend a lot of time on up front analysis.

My approach has always been to write something quick and dirty,
but then toss it and do it right. I find that all the analysis in the world
is often worthless when it gets down to the practicalities of actually
doing the work. A fast attempt at a proto that actually has to do
something, however, brings any weakness in basic thinking patterns
glaringly to the fore. Often it is only after attempting to solve the
problem that you realize what the problem actually is.

Programming is, after all, only systems analysis done in enough
detail and accurately enough that even a computer can understand
it.
```
