Very valid point that Thane makes. The small (one or two man shop) just can't
support the concept of trainees - money. (And remember this doesn't just apply to
programming, same goes for the one-man business, electrician, plumber etc.).

Howard mentioned elsewhere, 'train in-house' - and it is a role which must be
supported by both medium-sized shops (I offer no definition of how many
employees) and large shops.

There is also another factor abut small shops. The youngster will only be exposed
to the programming style of one or two people. Howard may care to respond - in a
big shop, even given that it has a fairly rigid standard as to how things are to
be put together, no doubt there is also room for individualistic style.

I can only quote vaguely from memory, but back in early seventies, my Chief
Programmer, with so many potential new employees already versed in COBOL,
(regardless of mainframe they had been working on), suggested we recruit young
'uns from a local technical college. (This was Taunton, Somerset, dreamy
delightful countryside and not a thriving buzz centre, say like London or
locally, even Bristol).

We got roughly 12 interested. Can't recall how, but the Chief Programmer
contacted a 25-30 year-old who had expertise in Structured methodology, who was
prepared to give the recruits lectures. Today we might find his style a little
amusing and the simplest program contained considerably more comment statements
than the actual code. Nevertheless it worked, and the comment code lead them down
a natural path to Structured.

With attrition etc., we eventually finished up with four of them as employees -
that was the situation before I left for Canada. Initially of course, they were
employed on the 'simple' stuff, program a print-out, and there were VOLUMES of
those comment statements in their source - but their programs did work.

One recruit was given an update to do. The Ops. Manager queried with me why such
a simple exercise was taking 3 hours to run. The Chief Programmer gave it to one
of his experienced programmers to look at - because of that stylized Structured
approach, he had no problem on zeroing-in on the defect in less than  20
minutes.; fine-tuned the program with some three or four lines of code - and we
were off to the races.

Now back to the 'small shop' teaching learners. In a Structured environment the
principal or principals have arrived at a style, based on what they have picked
up from shops in their own career, before going it alone.

Now here's a interesting one - OO - which is a 'new thing'. I know there are
probably many (???) Fujitsu and Net Express users who code in OO, but I'm
thinking of specifically four,  Thane, Don Tees, Pete and myself.. We are working
individually, each arriving at our own particular style. In my case I code solely
in OO classes, (purely for a common coding style). Certainly Thane and Pete don't
and justify mixing both Structured and OO Class structures, when appropriate.
Thane and Don use SP2, Pete advocates components using POWERCOBOL, and because it
was all that was initially available with VISOC (forerunner to Net Express), I
use GUI classes.

Not one of those OO approaches is wrong, they all work. But sit a kid alongside
any one of us, and like it or not he's bound to be persuaded that 'our'
(individual) style is Nirvana. Put this into a medium or large shop environment -
and he will be exposed to different points of view.

"Style" is a difficult thing to pinpoint and it is certainly true that most
teaching institutions don't teach it. (This is not a criticism; there is
little time and much to learn, so it is right and proper that the
"mechanics" of the language should have priority...)

I have students who I tutor via the Net and always start with some points on
style for structured COBOL.  I stress that style is a matter of opinion, and
they should form their own opinions as they acquire experience, so the stuff
I give them is simply a suggested start point. But it helps. (I have had
positive feedback from many students who see their listings become more
readable and easily debuggable just by implementing a few very simple
"rules" which rapidly become second nature. (Anyone reading this who would
like a copy, mail me and I'll send it by return.))

We have seen discussions here about things like "YES" "NO" flags, use of 88
levels, PERFORM of sections, etc., ad Nauseum and nothing is really achieved
by it because there is no "right" or "wrong", just opinion (sometimes
fervently felt to the point of Evangelism...)

Most experienced programmers can pick up a source listing (in any language
they are competent in, not just COBOL) and gauge very quickly the level of
expertise of the person who wrote it. There are "ways of doing things" which
are generally accepted and not accepted and most of us have developed our
own approaches to recurrent problems over the years.

When it comes to OO, the picture is slightly different. Jimmy mentioned four
people who he knows are using OO. But the toolsets are different and this
has an impact on the WAY in which OO is used. I don't think this is
important as I consider myself a pragmatist and use whatever I find useful,
but it DOES have a bearing on people's perception of OO.

I strongly believe (but not to the point of Evangelism...<G>) that COBOL
people should learn Java in order to pick up OO concepts, before they
attempt to use OO COBOL. (Java is also a very useful tool to have in your
toolbox...)

If you try and teach yourself OO COBOL you are presented with a plethora of
new program Headers... (Factories, Methods, Classes, different storage
sections,  Repositories, Objects, etc) which tends to obfuscate the
underlying simplicity and elegance. It is enough to get your head around the
"new" concepts of Polymorphism, Inheritance, Encapsulation, and the new
jargon that expresses it (see this paragraph...<G>) without having to deal
with a whole new raft of unfamiliar COBOL constructs at the same time. If
you then layer "style" on top of this you get a situation where the majority
of people just throw up their hands and stick to what they know...

Learning OO via Java is painless and simple. The language is designed to be
OO, and OO is intrinsic to it. (Where OO COBOL had it bolted on as an
afterthought. That is not to say that the new standard (Grrr!) hasn't done a
good job on it, but it suffers from excess verbosity (usually an advantage
in COBOL, but not in this instance).

There is no doubt in my mind that OO is the key to the future and eventually
programming (in whatever Language) will be done this way. It is just too
good to be ignored and besides, it is essential for the development of
Components, and Components are the Keys to the Kingdom...<G>
(Go and look at http://www.flashline.com and
http://www.componentsource.com )

Jimmy mentioned that some of us are using OO in different ways. He is quite
right and it would be very difficult to formulate an overriding "style" of
OO. The toolsets being used also have a big influence on it. I am quite
happy to let PowerCOBOL deal with all the headers and the message loop and
"baggage" and focus purely on my own code, but not everybody will be. Having
said that, I have deliberately forced myself to write OO code in COBOL 97
just to ensure that I understand what is needed and how it works.(RAN back
to PowerCOBOL as soon as I got it working...<G>)

So, if you sat someone down beside any of us, they would see a totally
different emphasis and picture of what "OO COBOL" is.  None of these
pictures are "wrong".

Hence, I advocate getting the concepts first (by teaching yourself Java -
recommend "Java made simple"  (will get you writing Web Applets in an hour
or so...) and "Sam's Teach Yourself Java in 24 hours" which has a delightful
vein of humour right through it, and is actually an entertaining "read"...)

Finally, as Thane and Jimmy have both pointed out, the chances of youngsters
getting an "Internship" are remote. These days, if you want to further your
career, you need to teach yourself. That means investing much time (and a
little money for books...) in your future.

"YOU are responsible for the condition YOU are in." (Sign in "Fat Jacks" on
Ventura...it doesn't just apply to making hamburgers...<G>)

Pete.
