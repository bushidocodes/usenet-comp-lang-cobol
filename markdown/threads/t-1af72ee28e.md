This is not true. The "millennium bug" only effects large mainframe
systems designed in the 60s and early 70s. To conserve space, these
machines' internal clock could store only two digits for the year.
In the year 2000, these two digits will become 00 and be interpreted
by the software as the year 1900. Any piece of software that makes
use of the date will no longer work correctly (e.g. calculation of
interest on deposits and loans). Thus, every piece of software that
make use of the date on these machines has to be modified. The only
ones owning these older systems are some large corporations and the
federal government (corporations are on track to have the problem
solved with time to spare but the federal government is getting
seriously behind).

Newer machines and PCs in particular do not have this problem.
Ignore the scare tactics, your PC software will work just as well
in the year 2000 as it did before.

Steve wrote:
›
› I'm a relative beginner at programming especially in the windows environment
› so excuse the question, but what is a sliding window. I've not come across
› this term before.


Let me give you a real example which I coded just last week for a client.
Assume that the 2 digit year is within the century beginning 79 years ago
and ending 20 years in the future. You could make any other assumption you
like about the target century, so long as the span is 100 years, but this was
appropriate for our data. You can easily resolve (interpret) the date to
be within that period by using the computer date and a little logic, like
this:

01 DATE-WORK-AREA.
03 DW-TODAYS-CCYY PIC 9(04).
03 DW-TODAYS-CCYY-R REDEFINES DW-TODAYS-CCYY.
05 DW-TODAYS-CC PIC 9(02).
05 DW-TODAYS-DD PIC 9(02).

03 DW-CCYY PIC 9(04).
03 DW-CCYY-R REDEFINES DW-CCYY.
05 DW-CC PIC 9(02).
05 DW-YY PIC 9(02).

Populate DW-TODAYS-CCYY from computer date at program BOJ. Then, when
you get ready to 'window' a 2 digit year, do this:

MOVE DW-TODAYS-CC TO DW-CC.
MOVE <2 digit year> TO DW-YY.
IF (DW-CCYY < DW-TODAYS-CCYY - 79)
ADD 100 TO DW-CCYY
ELSE
IF (DW-CCYY > DW-TODAYS-CCYY + 20)
SUBTRACT 100 FROM DW-CCYY.

Note that if the actual date is February 29, and DW-YY = zero, you will
need to examine DW-CC to see if it is a leap year. If DW-CC MOD 4 = 0,
then the century year is a leap year, otherwise not. If you don't take
this step, February 29, 2100, and 2200, 2300, 2500, etc., will be accepted
as valid, although 2100, 2200, 2300, 2500, etc. will not be leap years. ;-)

Because this method assumes the input date falls within a specified
'window', relative to the current date, you never have a problem as time
progresses, because the computer date progresses. Dates such as birth
dates usually cannot be resolved this way, because in early 2000, there
will be birth dates spanning three centuries (18xx, 19xx, 20xx), even
for living people. Only if you can assume that the dates will always
fall with some window <= 100 years, relative to some known date, such as
computer date, can this method be used.

Windowing is very useful to permit users to continue entering 6 digit
dates indefinitely, for transaction dates or other dates which will
always fall within the moving window.

Both these "facts" are incorrect.

PC's will not die on Dec 31, 1999, but it is possible that some PC
hardware may report incorrect dates and some PC applications (and not
necessarily ancient ones either) may either fail or record/report dates
incorrectly. There were still a significant percentage of PC
motherboards sold as late as 1996 and maybe even some today that will
not handle the rollover from 2359 12/31/1999 to 0000 01/01/2000 with
complete correctness; but for most instances a one-time manual setting
of the date and/or reboot of the hardware should be sufficient to
correct this problem. For those isolated cases, where either the number
of PC's involved or the need to have a PC server functional 24x7
precludes as a viable solution reboot and manual intervention, you need
to verify that the PC hardware is fully compliant to avoid outages. To
reduce your exposure on the PC software side, you should seek to verify
that any software you regard as critical, including your Operating
System, is at the appropriate maintenance level to perform those
critical applications. As examples of recent PC software with
Y2K-related bugs, there are Y2K-related patches to both Windows 95 and
to a relatively recent version of Quicken--whether these affect features
important to you depend on your usage of the product. If you depend on
spreadsheet applications which contain dates, or any other application
that works with dates, you could have a problem. Whether you verify
functionality in advance or adopt a fix/replace on failure approach
depends on whether this is a critical application you can't live
without.

As as already been pointed out, the Y2K (not millennium) "bug" is NOT a
mainframe systems problem, but potentially affects all software; nor is
it related to internal hardware clock limitations on mainframes, which
have been capable of supporting 4-digit years since the 60's (pardon me,
1960's). Mainframe Operating Systems have included direct support in
one form or another for year 2000 and beyond for over a decade, and even
prior to that it was perfectly feasible (though not always reasonable)
to implement software in a way that could use either 2-digit or 4-digit
years in a manner that would be consistent with dates after year 2000.
The presence of dates in a mainframe application does not necessarily
mean that the application will fail after 12/31/1999, only that failure
is a possibility. Even the presence of a 4-digit year does not imply
correctness (maybe 19xx is forced). These same possibilities for
failure are present in both mainframe and PC software, no matter how
recently written; although, hopefully with growing awareness of Y2K the
odds are lower in more recent code. Because of the sheer volume of
mainframe code, its longer life time, and the mission-critical nature of
much of this code, mainframe Y2K problems have received greater
attention and rightly so; but this is a software problem which can
adversely affect software on any hardware platform.

Y2K failures will most likely not manifest themselves as application or
system "crashes" but rather in the form of incorrect results: year 2000
dates reported as year 1900 dates; 02/28/2000 erroneously followed by
03/01/2000 instead of 02/29/2000 (Yes, Virginia, 2000 IS a leap year);
incorrect day-of-week calculations; incorrect date comparisons (which
can affect payroll, benefits, interest, etc.); incorrect sort by date.
Some of these failures may be of no major consequence; others may be
show-stoppers, and, if they result in corrupted data in a large
database, may be much more expensive to fix after the failure has
occurred.
