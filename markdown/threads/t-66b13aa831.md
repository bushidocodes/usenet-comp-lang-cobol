The definition by itself will not justify, of course.  But a procedure
statement can.

If you move a field shorter than Field-x into Field-x then the content of
the shorter field will be right-justified in Field-x.  Note that spaces in
the source field will be moved just as other characters.  If the source
field is longer than Field-x then it's content will be truncated on the
left to fit into Field-x.  If both fields have the same length, the content
will be moved unaltered.

So it is a kind of right-justification, but trailing spaces in the source
field will not be removed.

If you are sure there are no embedded spaces, then you can accomplish
right-justification by 

IF Field-x(1:1) NOT = SPACE
   UNSTRING Field-x DELIMITED BY SPACE INTO Field-x.

Gunnar.

"The JUSTIFIED clause is used to override the normal positioning  of
alphabetic or alphanumeric data when it is moved to a larger area.  If
an item is moved to a location that is larger than itself, it may be
necessary to specify the postion that the data is to occupy in the new
area."

"If the JUSTIFIED clause is used, it affects the positioning of the
receiving area, as follows:

1.  If the data being sent is larger than the receiving area, the
leftmost characters will be truncated.

2.  If the data being sent is smaller than the receving area, the

If the posters intent is to remove all the spaces to the right of the
sending field, then other instructions and techniques must be used as

We have almost monthly discussions about "JUSTIFY RIGHT".  The following is
a "summary" of information on both the rules and what programmers often
want.

1) JUSTIFY RIGHT will move a smaller field to a larger field and space pad
to the left in the receiving field.  If the original sending field ended in
spaces before the move, so will the receiving field after the move. If the
two fields are the same size (or the receiving field is smaller than the
sending field, then this phrase does NOTHING).

2) If you can figure out how many trailing spaces are in the original
sending field, then you can use reference modification to send ONLY the
"real data" to the receiving field, e.g.

  Move Send-Field (1:calculated-length) to Just-Right-Receiving-Field

3) When you are trying to calculate where the "real data" ends in your
sending field, make certain that you check from the end - not from the
beginning as the real data MAY include imbedded spaces.

4) On some (most? all?) compilers, it is probably more efficient to code a
"table move - character by character" than it is to use INSPECT, UNSTRING,
etc - however, such "efficient" code may or may not be worth the
"maintenance" overhead.  (This depends entirely on the compiler, the
application, and what your maintenance programmers are used to.) Several
coding examples of this technique can be seen in DejaNews discussions of
this topic.

5) The phrase "JUSTIFIED LEFT" does *NOT* exist in Standard COBOL (or in any
implementation that I know).  However, what most programmers would think
that such a phrase would mean - is the default for alphanumeric moves.  In
fact the COBOL word "RIGHT" is optional in the "JUST/JUSTIFIED RIGHT"
phrase.

***

I hope this helps.
