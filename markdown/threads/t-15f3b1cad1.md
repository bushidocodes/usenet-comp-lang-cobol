After 8 years in the trenches with EDS (I quit in June '96), my best advice
would be to use 'em then lose 'em.

If you go through the Systems Engineer Development (SED) program, you will get
great training but not necessarily in what you want. One of EDS' favorite
phrases is "where/what is the business need". This means they will train you
in whatever and send you wherever they want. If you don't like it -- they'll
show you the door.

And yes, there is a high possibility you'll work some very long hours. During
the toughest week of our SED training, my classmates and I put in a 100 hour
workweek. One of my first assignments, before I went through tech training,
was to manage the programmer's time cards. Several were in the testing phase
of a large project and were logging 80 - 100 hr workweeks.

The salary is not necessarily better at EDS. They will pay better than a small
company no doubt. But EDS is "pay for performance" which means no guaranteed
raises. No cost of living raises. I knew programmers who went two years
without a raise. You've got to continually improve your skills and accept more
responsibility to get raises. Furthermore, salaries are inflated in the
market right now due to the year 2000 crunch so don't be too impressed with
your friend's money. I received raises every year at EDS but was still able to
leverage a 30% salary increase from my new company due to the tight labor
market.

The uptight dress code still exists. And it's even more resrtictive in the
Plano HQ complex. Men can have beards now and women can wear pants now but the
rest is still the same. Suits, ties, no loafers and only long sleeved shirts
for men. Your suit jacket must be worn when you leave your desk and that
includes when you eat lunch in the company cafeteria (this rule is in effect at
Plano HQ only). The dress code will vary slightly at other accounts.

These are the categories you will be rated on in your 6mo/annual performance
appraisal (these may have changed since '96):
Job Specific Skills, Relationship Management, Process and Performance
Innovation, Industry Insight, Managing Complexity, Customer Focus and Impact,
Leadership (subcategories include Strong Personal Convictions, Visionary,
Establish Emotional Bonds, Inspirational, Team Oriented, Risk-Taking and Drive
to Excel)

During my time at EDS I saw bad things happen to good people. I saw four
people fired for non-performance. I've seen 13 people laid off, although they
formally fire you, during years of record revenue and profit (the company-wide
total was approx 10,000). I saw two managers that were popular with employees
and customers lose their jobs to office politics. I've seen two people
forcibly transferred to places they didn't want to go. I've seen some really
bad managers - were talking petty, vindictive, no people skills, and showing
blatant favoritism -which results in high staff turnover rates. On the last
account that I worked on, our manager was so bad that out of 12 people 2
transferred and 7 quit in a time period of less than 1 year. We each had 5 -
10 years experience.

Despite all I've just said remember this one thing above all else: Everyone's
experience at EDS is different. Some love it, some hate it, sometimes it's a
mixed bag (I include myself in this group).

Any other questions, just ask.
