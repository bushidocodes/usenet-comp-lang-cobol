We have some young (early to mid-twenties) consultants working on
helping us develop some in-house, browser based apps for our company
intranet, using the MS Interdev tools.  Most of the data is in Oracle
databases on the servers and the rest is in a DB2 database on the
mainframe.  I was musing out loud that perhaps, since we were
increasingly going to the DB2 databases, perhaps we should just scrap
the Oracle and put all the data back on DB2.  The young expert looked
at me in amazement and said (in all seriousness)  "do you think the
mainframe could handle the load?"  (BTW, the total load from these
apps probably won't go more than a few hundred - certainly well below
a thousand -- per minute.)  I tried to explain to him that we could
drive our servers (the fattest of which contains 4 x 400mhz Pentium
Xeons) to their knees well before the OS-390 even registered a blip.


I don't think he believed me ....

Henry Ford was having problems with one of the electrical generators that
was required to keep his assembly line running. The local serviceman was
unable to resolve the problem so Ford, feeling a little stressed, because

upgrade our current system, most of my cohorts were espousing the virtues of
COM-based distributed apps and that we should go in that direction

system).  I was fairly alone in supporting GUI-based COBOL like NetExpress
or AcuCOBOL.  In the end, however, it was my side's demonstration of lower
TCO and flatter development curve that one the day.  We are now developing a
new system using our old COBOL business logic with a fresh, Windows
front-end.

Don't paint with a broad brush when labelling the young.  There are some of
us who have our "heads on straight" and can see past the "Oooo, ain't it
cool?!" mentality of the new technologies to focus on what can get the job
done the most efficiently, effectively, and with the fastest ROI.
