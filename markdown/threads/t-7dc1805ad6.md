1) Some speculations on Eternity

Afterlife and eternity form a very important part of the theory of many
religions.  I have some thoughts to share!

If you'll agree that "eternity" = "an infinite number of years" then
consider:

A googol = 10**100 = is a pretty big number.  In fact some cosmological
theories conclude that it will be only 10**131 years until all the
protons in the universe decay, so it's a respectable number as well.

But I've invented a bigger number: a TRILLIGOL = 10**2 trillion = 10**
(2 * 10**12) (or even bigger if you prefer the British system of
notation).  The 2 is to make it more interesting to play around with;
the size of the number will make my conclusion more forcibly.

Let's do some manipulation.  10 ** (2 trillion** (10** 2 trillion)) =
                                            10 ** ((2 * 10**12) ** (2 *
10**12)) =
                                            10 ** ((2 ** 10 ** 12) * (10
**24))  I think!  I haven't yet satisified myself that I've got it
right.

Now let's repeat the process a (trilligol - 1) times: if somebody wants
to work it out please do and let me know.

Now let's take that many centuries.

We've arrived at a truly monstrous period of time: difficult to express
and utterly incomprehensible.  But (and you mathematically inclined
readers know what's coming) it is absolutely ZERO as compared to
infinity.  We've agreed that eternity = an infinite number of years. 
ANY number that we can construct, no matter how huge, no matter how many
trilligol centuries it would take to write it down even in powers of
trilligols - is zero as compared to infinity.

Therefore, it makes no sense at all to speak of eternity as something
that can be passed through by any sort of sentient being.  Any
subterfuge that can be inagined is useless - let's say that some
entities experience time 10** -600 as fast as others; no problem; in
10*600 of our years those entities would have experienced one of their
years, and eventually they would run up against infinity - to be sure it
would take them 10** 600 times as long as entities with our time rate,
but in terms of infinity that's no difference at all.

This demonstration doesn't, of course, absolutely rule out things on the
time scale of eternity.  Cantor proved that there are three orders of
infinity; and there was an article recently in the "Scientific American"
speculating that there might be an infinite number of orders of
infinity.  Perhaps "god" and the afterlife exist in one of the other
orders. But WE, living in at least the third order of infinity (since we
can conceive of it) can make no factual statements about the passage of
time or the state of sentient entities in higher orders.  Any such is
really meaningless since it is no statement of duration at all.

(Eternity extends in both directions, of course.  There was necessarily
an eternity before the Big Bang happened.  All that can be said in
rebuttal amounts to "there is no way we can measure it since before then
there was nothing to measure". But since ONE Big Bang happened, why
shouldn't there have been an infinite number already, before ours?  I
don't see how this can be ruled out; that being the case, even though
there's (probably) no way we can communicate with other universes to
find out, it can't be gainsaid that time was passing there BEFORE our
time began.  If there have been previous Big Bangs, then they all exist
in a meta-universe which is eternal in time and infinite in extent).

2) 2 + 2 = 5

For a top-rate mathematician, of the caliber of Reimann or Lobachevski
or Andrew Wiles (in our day), it would not be difficult to devise a
consistent arithmetic (A2) in which addition worked differently than in
ours (A1): such that 
in A1: x + y = z
in A2: x + y = z (as in A1) + K (any constant).

I'm not up to the task, but some consequences occur to me, such as:

- if in A2 2 + 2 = 5000, then would 5000 / 2 = 2?  How about 5000 / 3? 
Would 1 + 3 = 5000?  
- there would have to be a different definition for the K's since
obviously they aren't numbers in the same sense as the x & y; they would
be like chemical catalysts, which take part in reactions but come out of
them untouched

... and so on.

In fact, once you've defined A2, then A1 is a subset of it with K = 0.

Once this was done, it doesn't seem likely that it would represent a
reality (apart from K = 0) or that it would be otherwise be useful - but
who knows?  Square roots of negative numbers - impossible according to
the rules for square roots - are exceedingly important in all sorts of
everyday matters.

(Has this, by any chance, been done???)


3) More than 3 dimensions

Two of the sets of Grand Unified Theories require more than 3
dimensions; one needs 10, the other needs 26.  And I've read somewhere
that 16 dimensions are needed to describe an average atom (can't
remember where so don't press me on this one).

We can perceive directly 3 dimensions, and can make drawings which at
least hint at how things look in four.  This only proves that we can
perceive three dimensions.  There may in fact be an infinite number! 
Not to worry; mathematics can be developed that allow us to manipulate
such things and get an idea how hey must work; that's not the same as
perceiving them directly but we can at least claim some sort of
understanding.

(Given our ability to draw 4th-dimensional things, I find it difficult
to acept "time" as the fourth dimension).

    Certainly, you could come up with such a consistent arithmetic. You can 
come up with just about anything, really. The real question is whether this 
new arithmetic helps you reason about a problem or not. I'm assuming for now 
that you're using the symbol '=' with its normal meaning of equality, and 
that you're working with integers. Your integers form a set of values which, 
by themselves are meaningless. You can only gain meaning from values based 
on how they relate to each other. The value that we traditionally represent 
as '0', for example, has special properties with the operators we usually 
denote as '+' and '*'. With '+', '0' acts as the identity value, because '0' 
'+' anything else gives back the same value. If zero did not have this 
property, then it would suddenly become less interest. The value 87 for 
example, does not have this property, and generally isn't considered 
"special" with respect to addition. With respect to the '*' operator, '0' is 
a nullifier, because '0' '*' anything returns '0'. '1' is special because it 
acts as the identity function for '*'.

    Operators themselves are simply functions which map a pair of integers 
to an integer. You can define them in essentially any way you want. You 
could define a '?' operator, for example, which returns '0' if the sum of 
its two arguments is an even number, or '1' if the sum of its two arguments 
is an odd number (this assumes that you have previously defined the concepts 
of 'sum', 'even' and 'odd').

    So many different arithmetic systems exists that there exists names to 
describe the various categories of them. They include "rings", "groups", 
"magma", etc. You can read up about them at 
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure

> 3) More than 3 dimensions
>
> Two of the sets of Grand Unified Theories require more than 3
> dimensions; one needs 10, the other needs 26.

    Note that these are not "merely" dimensions, but spacial dimensions (as 
in "related to space", not as in "special"). The GUTs which predict more 
than 3 spacial dimensions claim that the dimensions other than the 3 that we 
can readily perceive are curled up upon themselves so tightly that we hardly 
notice them.

    Consider a sheet of paper. If that paper is flat, then it appears to 
consist of 2 spacial dimensions. However, if you take that paper, and then 
roll it up extremely tightly, it will appear to consist of only 1 dimension 
(i.e. a line instead of a surface).

> And I've read somewhere
> that 16 dimensions are needed to describe an average atom (can't
> remember where so don't press me on this one).

    This might be a reference to Hamiltonian state-space, where the state of 
the thing we're investigating (in this case, an atom), is represented by a 
point in a high-dimensional field of vectors. For example, to represent the 
state of an atom, you might need 3 dimensions to represent its x, y and z 
coordinates; then 3 more dimensions to represent its momentum along these 3 
axis, and then 3 more dimensions to represent its angular momentum along 
these 3 axis, and so on, to take up 16 dimensions, even though the atom 
we're modelling only uses up 3 spatial dimensions. At each point in this 16 
dimensional graph is a vector which points to the next state the atom will 
be in in the next moment in time.

    In other words, it is purely a "visualization aid", and it does not 
indicate that the atom takes up 16 spatial dimensions; not any more than a 
graph you might see in an executive boardroom meeting charting profit 
against time, indicates that profit takes up 2 spatial dimensions.

> (Given our ability to draw 4th-dimensional things, I find it difficult
> to acept "time" as the fourth dimension).

    "Dimensions" are an abstract concept. It may be useful, for example, to 
plot the magnetic charge of an object against time. In this case, 
electro-magnetic charge has "become" a dimension, with time being the other 
dimension. By definition, time is not a "spatial-dimension", but that 
doesn't mean it is never useful to treat time as a dimension for certain 
visualization aids.
