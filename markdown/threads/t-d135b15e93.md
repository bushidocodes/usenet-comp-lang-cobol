I have received a copy of RW's original post by private e-mail.

During the course of a year I am often asked to review drafts before
publication, sometimes for books (not always computer related), sometimes
for magazines.

As I usually make a charge for this, I had no intention of reviewing
Robert's lengthy document  in detail, for nothing.

However, I have done a first scan on it <G>.

If I understand correctly, he posted this document to CLC in order to get
feedback from a group of knowledgeable people.

That seems quite sensible to me (it's certainly cheaper than having it
professionally reviewed...<G>)

What follows is one person's opinion, based on a single cursory view of the
text.

My overall verdict: "Good in parts..." (like the Curate's egg <G>).

There is much in the tone and style that will alienate readers (as well as
CLC <G>) and if it is published as it stands, it will almost certainly
attract bad reviews (It definitely will if I get to review it...<G>). But
all of this can be relatively easily remedied. (A disclaimer right at the
start, stating that it represents one person's opinion, would help to defuse
some of it...).

Some of the "information" is just plain wrong, some of it is opinion stated
as fact (very much like what we've seen in CLC) but it would be very wrong
to throw the baby out with the bath water.

The directness of the style is not necessarily a bad thing. There may well
be a place for a book on COBOL that cuts to the chase... If you look at Wil
Price's "Elements of Object Oriented COBOL" (which is one of the best books
on COBOL I have ever read) or Fritz D MacCameron's "COBOL Logic and
Programming" (which is another COBOL book I really rate, despite it having
been published 30 years ago...), a large part of the attraction is the
directness of the style, which communicates effectively. I see no reason why
an author shouldn't produce a COBOL book that DOESN'T document every
construct, but deals rather with the ones the author has found to be most
useful, PROVIDED it is made very clear to the reader that there is "more"
and an Appendix or detailed Bibliography shows where to do further reading
if required.

I have to say that if I had misbehaved, and was given an imposition where I
must choose between reading 12 pages of RW's document, or reading 12 pages
of the COBOL standard, it would be a no-brainer...<G>

In summary, my take on this is that it is a draft of what is likely to be a
poor book on COBOL (as it stands).

Would I go to war or leave the Newsgroup over it?

Draw your own conclusions.

Pete.
