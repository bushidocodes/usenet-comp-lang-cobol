Reading all those stories about shortage of mainframe programmers and the
sky-high salaries? Are you worried, as a businessman, that you might not
be able to get enough qualified people to do Year 2000 conversion for your
company? Then, read my logical explanation about the situation.

What we have in labor market for programmers is a mismatch of demand and
supply. The American companies are burdened with high domestic wages. (By
this I mean- American wages are higher than most low wage countries. This
is a simple observation, not an invitation for debate about subjects such
as merits of socialism/capitalism, fairness of executive compensation,
income inequality, feminism, racism, ebonics, cloning, abortion, etc.)
American companies would like to tap foreign labor markets, but many of
them just don't have the expertise or the patience to deal with hassles of
doing business in a third world country. This view is reinforced by the
"technologists" and "consultants" who have a vested interest in shielding
their fiefdoms from foreign competition. (Forget the free market theory.
Competition is just not good.) They will tell you all sorts of horror
stories and problems related to off-shore work. If you haven't heard those
stories, stay tuned. As soon as post this ad to a newsgroup, several
people will regale you with their most amusing stories. (God, I love
newsgroups.)

If you are thinking about starting an off-shore software center in India,
then we can help. There are pros and cons related to doing your work in
India. You should know them before you contact us. Doing work off-shore
is a hassle. (How is that for stating the obvious?) But you want to do it
because it saves you money. (We all understand $$$$, don't we.) There is
no free lunch. It is that simple.

Send you e-mails to vik··.@pa··m.com but first visit our web site at
http://www.param.com

Vikram,

Your argument would make sense if you were making widgets, but you are not.
The "factory" concept doesn't apply to computer systems. Our organization has
found that off-site consultants don't produce a satisfactory product. The
real issue is effective communication, without which any major software
project is doomed to fail, and that doesn't occur at 10 miles, much less
10,000. We prefer to have consultants on-site even if the costs are higher.

In the long run, the costs are probably lower because we have fewer problems.

Alan_Browning wrote:

I'd go along with this. In a previous contract (ie NOT at BT) the client
got a Bangalore company to do all the coding for a new sub-system, with
a couple of UK analysts having designed the system on paper. It didn't
work & the client had to put several of their own people to rewrite the
code, in background as it were. I believe the problem was that you just
can't design & spec on paper without knowing the kind of things that
experienced programmers know, at least people aren't really used to
doing that & so it falls apart when you put all the modules together.
Yes I know it shouldn't happen but that's the reality.

The 'analyst/programmer' who can look both ways is the mainstay of
commercial DP & we're a long way from being able to split the
disciplines. For eg I've yet to meet an analyst that actually wrote
specs - they're all program designs in pseudo-code. If you ask them to
write down what the function is supposed to achieve in abstact terms you
get a blank look most of the time. Once I had a spec that was half GO
TOs, and several sections with nothing but GO TO somewhere else. And
that was the spec!

It has just occurred to me that we might make some progress if we split
program design from coding as separate skills. So the analyst splits up
the business function into logically distinct functions/data
structures/whatever, the system designer turns each entity into
something that's physically implementable & the coder translates the
psuedo-code into COBOL. Anyone seen this for real?
