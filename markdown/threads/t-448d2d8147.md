Response from Dashwood:>>>

Well, COBOL may be dead in Utah but it is alive and well in much of the USA
(and the rest of the World)

Y2K was NOT the death knell for COBOL.

Many projects which were shelved while people tackled Y2K (and much time and
resource was wasted running around in circles...) will require
implementation in the new Millennium. I have seen Business Studies which
estimate an average of at least 4 years work waiting to be done. (Of course,
these studies are notoriously inaccurate and I have seen no estimates of
average "man years" per organisation, but it is a fair bet that COBOL
development will continue well into the new century. I am talking here
"Mainframe COBOL" (mainly batch).

If we look at the situation with Client/Server or PC Networking, the picture
is not so encouraging as other tools are often preferred over COBOL.

In my experience this is often due to COBOL's "bad" image and a lack of
Management understanding about the relative merits of various languages. VB
and C guys are consistently amazed when I produce GUI based modules written
in COBOL and the usual reaction is "I didn't know you could do that in
COBOL...!!!"

ActiveX  (also known as .OCX) controls are particularly appropriate as they
can be plugged
directly onto forms (for both Windows or the Web) and the language they are
written in doesn't matter as, due to encapsulation, the only parts which are
visible are the properties
(variables) and methods (procedures).

I find Fujitsu COBOL extremely useful for doing this as they have a fine
environment for custom controls, but MicroFocus is also very good. Being
able to independently produce re-usable controls (and gradually build
libraries of them) has saved several projects
I have managed, from slipping. It is like having an extra resource (in this
case, me...<grin>) up your sleeve and this is the stuff that Project
Managers' dreams are made of....

I have written elsewhere in this NG about my belief that COBOL's days are
numbered. It wasn't ONLY COBOL (I include all procedural languages in this;
programming as we understand it will/must undergo a sea change), and the
number is STILL a fairly large
one...I reckon 15 years.

There is still a huge market for COBOL skills. The only difference is that,
whereas 30 years ago this market seemed interminable, it now appears to be
finite. (I agree it is a matter of opinion and before you all flame me or
suggest templates at dawn, let me stress again, that this is my OPINION. I
like to think it is an informed opinion but that is also subject to debate
<grin>)

Part of the future growth path for COBOL has to be in moving legacy code to
newer platforms. My company has already done a fair bit of this and I see no
diminishing of the requirement. We usually insist on setting up a pilot
project using the client's own COBOL programmers and converting them (both
evangelically and practically :-) ) to the merits of using the "new" COBOL
(GUI). This makes sound dollars and "sense". It re-motivates the COBOL team
who are often very worried about their futures, and it leverages their
existing skill into state of the art technology. The company (our client) is
happy, the programmers are happy, and I can afford to take long vacations
and go away and just think about stuff that I otherwise wouldn't get time
to...everybody wins, the best kind of game.

To Frank: I've never been to Utah. If it is REALLY as bad as you say, why
are you living there? (I know, 6 wives and 18 kids need a lot of looking
after...and the desert is beautiful in the Summer...;-) )

To Patrick: Your experience is still valuable. As for your age, I am much
older than you and still working (I insist on keeping my hand in at
programming and never let my other duties prevent me from doing so...Also
take a keen interest in technical problems encountered by the team and enjoy
the challenge of getting things working.) In fact, I'm planning stuff beyond
the next 15 years... If you can't find an employer who can see your value,
screw 'em! Go freelance. You can't be any worse off and you may find you
enjoy it. (I did for over 20 years).

It is interesting that a recent survey here in the U.K. found that older

So, if we are counting votes, my vote says that COBOL is far from dead.
(IMHO, "computer programming" by specialists (i.e. career programmers) is an
activity with a limited lifetime, but that's another story...).

Having said that, it DOES need a bit of assistance. I would expect COBOL
programmers on every corporate site to be honing their skills and looking at
opportunities to convert legacy systems to modern User-Friendly front ends
(let's get rid of "green screens" forever...). At the same time, Management
should be informed that the juicy new development they are about to
outsource for programming in C, could easily be done using in-house people
and GUI COBOL for much less money, with consequent positive benefits on
morale, maintainability, and project control. If they don't believe you,
knock out some GUI screens and show 'em...(I did this once and a certain
company lost a major contract....but a lot of other people kept their
jobs...and acquired some new skills which made them more valuable). If you
intend to keep working as a COBOL programmer, support the language. Talk to
VB guys (not in an argumentative or competitive way, but with genuine
interest in what they are doing and how they are doing it...you could learn
something). Find out what they have trouble with, then see if you can do it
in COBOL. (Offer them an ActiveX control to animate the Company Logo on a
Form, for example...that one worked for me).

The bottom line is that unless the corporate image of COBOL is changed, it
WILL die much sooner than need otherwise be the case. This is NOT an
impossible task. There are some fabulous COBOL tools now available and they
won't break the bank. (Certainly not the corporate bank.) COBOL had a slow
start on PCs because COBOL compilers have traditionally been 5 or 6 times
the cost of a VB or C compiler and that did not encourage students of
computer science and young people wanting to get into programming to
purchase them. That is no longer the case.

OK, this has been a long post but we care about the language, right? (see
"Who Cares?" elsewhere in this NG).

If so, then DO something about it!

Pete.
