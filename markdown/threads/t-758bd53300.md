I think the conflict involves "fair use" of copyrighted material.

I see no problem with the ANSI and ISO copyrights; provided that both
organizations accept that "fair use" is defined by the acknowledgement.
That is, "... authorization extends to the reproduction and use of COBOL
specifications in programming manuals or similar publications."

Hence, reproduction of the standard, as a standard and in competition
with ANSI or ISO, is infringement of their respective copyrights and,
therefore, requires permission and may be subject to royalty payments.

Reproduction, of those parts of the standard that define COBOL, when
used in a programming manual, does not infringe, does not require
permission, and is not subject to royalty payments.

Jimmy,

I certainly did not read Chuck's comments as condescending. I felt he 
was just stating facts. In the large world of COBOL, we need people 
doing various kinds of tasks -- and this requires various kinds of 
people. For the most part, people are doing the kinds of tasks they 
prefer, and have a strong lack of interest in many of the other tasks.

For example, some of the tasks are:

-- Writing new COBOL programs for company-specific applications
-- Maintaining and modifying those COBOL programs
-- Discussing applications written in COBOL
-- Discussing the process of writing COBOL
-- Writing general-purpose software
-- Writing program generators
-- Writing compilers
-- Extending the language
-- Standarizing the language

These tasks require a variety of skills and personal preferences, and 
you just don't find the skills and preferences for all tasks in any one 
person. The person is is both happy and competent at developing 
applications that support company operations often has little or no 
interest in discussing the process, or in hypothesizing how the process 
might be better, etc. This takes nothing away from that person's 
abilities -- it's just like saying that a bus driver is a different 
person from a farmer. But we very much need all these different tasks, 
and the different kinds of people who orient to these tasks.

If you graphed the number of people engaged in each of these tasks, 
you'd find (very roughly!) rapidly decreasing numbers from the top of 
the list to the end.

Now, I don't think these distinctions have very much to do with who has 
time. If someone who works at writing new custom COBOL programs wants 
to spend a few hours a week working in standardizing the language, 
he/she can certainly do so. Most programmers have many hours per week 
of personal time. Yes, it's hard to get paid for such work, but there 
aren't a lot of people interested either -- not compared with the 
number interested in doing basic programming. As it turns out, the 
numbers of people needed for the various tasks roughly (very roughly) 
corresponds to the number interested. And which came first, the chicken 
or the egg?

The controversies arise with the last two categories in my list -- 
those who extend the language and those who standardize the language -- 
because their work has a profound effect on the others. They bear much 
more of a responsibility for other parts of the COBOL community than do 
people acting in the other capacities listed. It's thus important that 
the people empowered to do these tasks take responsibility for their 
broad effect, and show this in a variety of ways, including good 
communication (both in and out!). When people in such positions become 
solipsistic or try to feather their own nests, they abrogate their 
responsibility.

I do not intend the above discussion to imply any comments about 
current instantiations of any of the categories, but only to help 
provide a bit of framework for discussing the broad variety of tasks 
and people.

something. I always throw a large and expensive party at the END of a
project when it has been delivered. During the project we have working
lunches but these are NOT boondoggles because we actually achieve stuff
during these sessions. If the Company will not pay for this, I do it out of
