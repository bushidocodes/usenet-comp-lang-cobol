```
For information on HOW to do "Arithmetic with Roman Numerals" there are a
number of web pages.  One that I (personally) found interesting was:

     http://www.jimloy.com/arith/roman.htm

I found the statements,

"So, in Egyptian, numbers are longer than they are in Roman numerals. But,
it turns out that arithmetic is simpler. There are fewer carries and no
borrows (when adding)."

which may (or may not) have some relevance to "progression" of numeric
systems.

    ***

This subject is, however, TOTALLY "off-topic" (in the COBOL newsgroup) as

1)  The use of "numerals" in both English and COBOL is quite "stable" and
well understood by even beginning "users" of either.

2) All posters that I respect in this NG, agree that the choice of "symbols"
as opposed to "words" in COBOL (in cases where both are allowed) *is* a
matter of STYLE not "substance" (with issues of maintainability, inadvertent
errors, print chains, etc - being QUITE subjective).

3) COBOL's 40 year old history of being "English-LIKE" is neither a current
"design" criterion nor of significant import to MOST programmers.

4) COBOL has a "variety" of items for which *only* SYMBOLS are allowed
(e.g. the "::" inline OO invocator or the traditional "==" pseudo-text
delimiter)
```

No official American or International standard for COBOL has in any way
denigrated the use of "non-English-like" constructs like "IF A > B".

The concept that the best COBOL is the most English-like COBOL *directly*
conflicts with the goals of J4, WG4, ISO, the non-English-speaking world,
and the international standards community.  "IF A > B" is arguably more
understandable to someone who does not speak or read English than "IF A IS
GREATER THAN B".

You may want to insist that COBOL programmers need to think just like good
ol' 'Murkins,  but such a goal strikes me, and I believe strikes many
outside the United States, as jingoistic at best.
