### Update on what happens to data to the right of the right margin

- **From:** "William M. Klein" <wmklein@nospam.netcom.com>

```
A while ago, I put out a note about the draft Standard seeming to be a
"problem" for IBM mainframe programmers and others who put data between the
R-margin and the end of the physical source line (i.e., columns 72-80 on IBM
mainframes - and many other punched-card image implementations),  I just
thought I would pass along the PROPOSED change that is happening (that in my
opinion) is a GREAT improvement.

The '85 Standard says,
   "Margin R is immediately to the right of the rightmost character position
of a line."

The (still in proposal state) draft says,
  "Margin R is a fixed number defined by the implementor that is immediately
to the right of the rightmost character position of the program-text area."

I think the "new wording" FINALLY officially allows implementors to define
the "real source portion of a line" (what the draft Standard calls
"program-text area") as ending BEFORE the physical end of a source line.

I should also note, that in other proposal(s), they are getting rid of
almost any "requirements" about what a listing needs to include - which have
always been pretty minimal anyway.  This means that implementors will be
able to do "whatever they think best" with the data from the "old" sequence
columns and from after the R-margin.  I think that this too will bring the
draft Standard into line with what implementors ACTUALLY DO (and what
programmers expect).
```

That is the way that the R-margin has *ALWAYS* worked in Standard COBOL.
Remember that "traditional" COBOL has ONLY supported "Fixed length" source
lines (none of this "fancy" line sequential or variable stuff).  On the
other hand, the draft Standard *does* introduce a NEW format that is
variable - and does mean that the compiler uses WHATEVER "source line" is
passed to it (with a few minor restriction).

Assuming today, you are using a PC or UNIX compiler today that defines the
R-margin as column 72 (or 80), and you pass it two source lines like,

      05    Long-Data-Item   Pic  X(100)    Value "123
 -            "456".
         ....
         Display Long-Data-Item

You will (or should) see that you get "lots" of spaces between the "3" and
the "4" - even if your "CRLF" is right after the "3".  This is becuae the
COBOL Standard *requires* that the alphanumeric literal be "filled with
spaces" out to the (virtual) R-margin - in fixed format (traditional) source
code.

length records anyway - but even these are being replaced by database
structures.

maintenance.  (hiding some complexity behind commercial database
internals).  This relates to the thread on subscripts vs indices, as
well as to some of our structured coding discussions.

I also see fewer real large programs.  A coworker had to migrate a
complex change to a program with 4 sorts and 5 output files - after he
analyzed it, he found that he could make several programs with simple
match merges and external sorts.
