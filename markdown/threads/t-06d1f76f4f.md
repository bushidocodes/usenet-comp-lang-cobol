```
This is a guess, but I expect the demand for COBOL programmers to peak fall
or winter of 1999, and begin to taper off sometime around summer or fall of
2000. Anything not fixed by the beginning of 2001 will probably be replaced.
However, many new projects will have been placed on hold for Y2k, so there
will be a real backlog of new systems needed. I expect many of them to be
done in COBOL, but time will tell. It will be a good idea to know something
other than COBOL by then, to cover your bases. But I don't see most
mainframe apps being written in anything but COBOL for quite a while.

Write a report program with complex formatting and control breaks in VB
sometime. Yuck! VB is *crippled* when it comes to print formatting. Oh,
you can format the data any way you want, of course; it just takes scads of
code to do it. The language designers of today obviously have no clue how
many reports businesses use. And doing business arithmetic without scaled
decimal data types is nuts. Nobody needs pointers to write a payroll or
general ledger program. Doing that stuff in C++ is also nuts.

Businesses have been complaining for years that applications were taking
longer and longer to develop, and consequently were becoming more and more
expensive to develop. Why on earth are we moving to operating systems and
languages which are more difficult to learn and use, and which require longer
and longer development times?

One thing we can say for sure. If COBOL does go, then it's going to take a
whole lot more programmers to do the same jobs with these languages which are
so poorly designed and clumsy for business programming. The demand for
programmers is going to increase overall, throughout the foreseeable future.
```
