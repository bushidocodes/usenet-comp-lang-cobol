You are never going to get this to work in  a procedural language/OS
combination such as MS-DOS or Linux COBOL. The closest you can get is to
update the on-screen clock "often" by updating the clock once per "main"
loop. Pseudo-code might look like...


000-MAIN.
   PERFORM 100-MAINLOOP  UNTIL DONE.

100-MAINLOOP.

         PERFORM TIME-ROUTINE
         PERFORM ONE-UNIT-OF-REAL-WORK.

TIME-ROUTINE.

         ACCEPT WS-TIME FROM TIME
         SUBTRACT WS-LAST-TIME FROM WS-TIME GIVING TIME-DIFFERENCE
         IF TIME-DIFFERENCE > ONE-SECOND-OR-WHATEVER-YOU-WANT
             (Update the screeen)
             MOVE WS-TIME  TO LAST-WS-TIME.


You can perform TIME-ROUTINE whenever you feel like it, when there is a
'break in the action'.

If you keep the user busy enough, they won't notice when it's two seconds
between updates instead of one second. (They also won't care).

If you are writing for Windows, of course, you can use a timer. In 32-bit
Windows the OS will interrupt your other processes for you and do the screen
update. In 16-bit Windows, you'll have to release control to Windows
periodically, just as you need to perform TIME-ROUTINE yourself as above.

MCM

Sure.  This is the kind of detail which students often want to put into
programs.  I have to admit though if I were teaching a course in a business
language such as COBOL, I would lower the grade of the program which pulled a
stunt like this.

Goals which I want to teach:
1.  Meet the users requirements.
2.  Make it easy for users to use.
3.  Make maintenance easy.  (KISS)
4.  Don't re-invent the wheel (If there is a clock on their computer's GUI,
don't clutter things up by putting another one).
5.  Look for alternative business solutions (put a clock on the wall)
6.  If you have done all of the above, make your program efficient.

Now where in the above would an on-screen clock be appropriate?  Where would it
be a detraction?  This is business, not playing.
