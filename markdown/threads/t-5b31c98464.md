This is not a COBOL tech question. If putting the topic in this NG is
disagreeable to the group, let's stop the thread. But I thought I'd ask
the pro's and avoid the charlatans and profit-motivated psuedo-doctors.
I've read a bunch of sites on the web and seen the varied opinions and
sales pitches. Also have colleagues who've had the operation(s) on the
wrists. None satisfactory that I know of (at least all seem to have some
side effects such as permanent weakness, etc.).

So...

Do any of you have any REAL solution to this problem?
I've got the ergonomic workstation, chair, etc. I've tried to use proper
posture, wrist supports, stretching, work-breaks, pills, braces, gloves,
etc.
Management here is very supportive of efforts to handle this problem.

But there is still the tingling at work and the night fall-asleep problem.
Have had symptoms for about 15 years and have sort of leveled off at
somewhere between nagging annoyance and slight difficulty in typing.

Any help appreciated.

Thanks
John

I had bilateral CTS about 10 years ago; had the CTR operation on the
right hand only. The left hand cured itself spontaneously.

At the time I had a good desk, a good keyboard, no mouse, a good chair,
good posture, and 30-40wpm typing skills. On the other hand I'd been in
the same job with the same gear for several years. I think the advice
about (a) taking breaks and (b) using different keyboards is good; I
also endorse the suggestion to learn ALL they keyboard accelerators and
use them wherever they exist. I don't agree that using a lower chair or
an easy chair is good advice. Look at how a good typist works (if you
can find one any more): good chair set high, backside right into the
chair, back arched, shoulders back. Mainly, SIT UP. Just imagine your
piano teacher is about to rap you over the knuckles again.

The CTR operation I had needed about 10-12 stitches and several weeks of
non-use of the hand. I believe there is now a laser procedure which is
much less invasive.

More recently I got tennis elbow from overuse of a mouse in a poor chair
and makeshift desk. I've since (a) scrapped the desk and chair and (b)
acquired a Goldtouch mouse (http://www.goldtouch.com) which I recommend.
It's genuinely ergonomic. They also make or will be making a keyboard.
