1999 has its own numeric problem
Thursday, December 31, 1998

By MICHAEL E. RUANE
The Washington Post

WASHINGTON - With anxious eyes fixed on the approaching millennium,
many members of the public seem to have missed a serious problem
facing them this very night.

Sneaking up for numerous centuries - ever since the ancient Romans
left things so fuzzy - the dilemma is as follows:

Does Friday begin the year MCMXCIX?

Or MDCCCCLXXXXVIIII?

Or MCMXCVIII?

Or MIM?

The sticky issue, how to render 1999 in Roman numerals, has been
raised by the National Institute of Standards and Technology - the
old Bureau of Standards - which, among other things, is the arbiter
of cosmic queries: How long is a second? When does the next century
begin? Etc.

As this dire moment approached, the institute began receiving
questions about what it calls "the Year 1999 Problem," and its
researchers began scratching their heads.

What should be the answer?

And who the heck cares about Roman numerals?

More people, it turns out, than you might think.

Architects. Movie producers. Kings. Popes. Officials of the Olympics,
certain newspapers and the Super Bowl. Families in which males pass
their names to their sons.

The main trouble with the Roman numeral system was that it made for
very long numbers, no doubt bloodying stone carvers of its day. Thus,
shortcuts, or what some experts call contractions, came into use.

The most difficult of these is the so-called subtraction principle,
in which a theoretically long Roman numeral is shortened to manageable
breadth.

The number 9, for example, which technically might be VIIII V for 5,
plus HU for 4 - is shortened to IX - 10, or X, minus I.

And therein lies the source of the Year 1999 Problem.

The Romans, and many other ancients, were not consistent in how they
used the subtraction principle.

Most observers believe the correct 1999 rendering is MCMXCIX: M stands
for 1000; C for 100, subtracted from the second M, equaling 900; X for
10, subtracted from the second C, equaling 90; and I for 1, subtracted
from the second X, equaling 9.

Total purists, though, might opt for MDCCCCLXXXXVIIII, which uses no
subtraction: M equals 1000; D equals 500; CCCC equals 400; L equals 50;
XXXX equals 40; V equals 5; and IIII equals 4. Total: 1999.

Partial purists can go for MCMXCVIIII, a combination of the two.

And for the ultimate subtractors, there's the racy MIM: M for 1000;
the I subtracting I from the second M, for 1999.

The institute says that MCMXCIX is "favored," but the rules are not
written in stone, so to speak. Said spokesman Michael E. Newman: "It's
one of those quirky things."

However it turns out, this trouble will be short-lived.

The year 2000 in Roman numerals is blessedly simple:

MM.

Although the modern system of notation called "Roman" numerals is not the
one that the Romans used, it is nevertheless well-defined.  It is a
right-recursive system with three symbols per level of recursion.  This
time I hope to make it a little clearer by providing constructive
definitions rather than the usual mathematical formulae.

NOTE: A dot after a numeral indicates an ordinal.  For example,
"the I. cycle" is pronounced "the first cycle".

The I. cycle traditionally uses the symbols "I" (one), "V" (five), and
"X" (ten).

    I. CYCLE (I, V, X): I II III IV V VI VII VIII IX

The II. cycle is derived from the I. cycle by shifting two new symbols in
from the right, "L" (fifty) and "C" (one hundred).

    II. CYCLE (X, L, C): X XX XXX XL L LX LXX LXXX XC

After each entry in the II. cycle, the _entire_ I. cycle is repeated,
recurring on the right.  For example:

    XI XII XIII XIV XV XVI XVII XVIII XIX

The III. cycle is derived from the II. cycle in the same manner.  The two
new symbols are traditionally "D" (five hundred) and "M" (one thousand).

    III. CYCLE (C, D, M): C CC CCC CD D DC DCC DCCC CM

After each entry in the III. cycle, the _entire_ II. cycle is repeated,
recurring on the right.  For example:

    CX CXX CXXX CXL CL CLX CLXX CLXXX CXC

After each entry in the repeated II. cycle, the _entire_ I. cycle is
repeated, always recurring on the right.  For example:

    CXI CXII CXIII CXIV CXV CXVI CXVII CXVIII CXIX

The two new symbols in the IV. cycle are traditionally overbar-V (five
thousand) and overbar-X (ten thousand).  This character set doesn't have
overbars, so I can't exhibit the IV. cycle, but you know that it starts
with "M", "MM", and "MMM".

The two new symbols in the V. cycle are overbar-L (fifty thousand)
and overbar-C (one hundred thousand).

The two new symbols in the VI. cycle are overbar-D (five hundred
thousand) and overbar-M (one thousand thousand).

The Romans used various symbols from time to time for five thousand
thousand and ten thousand thousand, but none of them made it into the
"Roman" numerals.

"MIM" is not a "Roman" numerals because an "I" (from the I. cycle) is to
the _left_ of an "M" (from the III. cycle).  By definition, earlier
cycles appear to the _right_ of later cycles.

Notice that each different cycle is one-based and contains nine entries. 
"Arabic" numerals have only one cycle, which is zero-based and contains
ten entries.


Here is a grammar of the printable "Roman" numerals in Backus-Naur form:

    RN = { 4 | 4A | A }
    A  = { 3 | 3B | B }
    B  = { 2 | 21 | 1 }

    1  = { I | II | III | IV | V | VI | VII | VIII | IX }
    2  = { X | XX | XXX | XL | L | LX | LXX | LXXX | XC }
    3  = { C | CC | CCC | CD | D | DC | DCC | DCCC | CM }
    4  = { M | MM | MMM                                 }


Last but least, here is a syntrax diagram (pun intended) of the printable
"Roman" numerals:

>>-------------------------------------------------------------------><
      |-- M ----|    |-- C -----|    |-- X -----|    |-- I -----|
