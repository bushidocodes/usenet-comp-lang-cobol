I am currently looking for anyone's take on the pros and cons of hourly vs.
salaried program consulting.
I currently program in a salaried capacity for a consulting firm in Tampa
Bay. I have one year mainframe experience w/ COBOL/DB2.

My firm claims that the some of pros of salaried work are the following:
1) Paid bench time
2) Vested 401k
3) Benefits package

The way I see it:
1) Bench time doesn't seem to be a problem in today's market
2) I can invest on my own (I'm not fully vested until after 5 years anyway)
3) Their benefits are decent (but I must pay tantamount to $2/hr for them
anyway!)

Bottom line:
At what point does one leave the security of salaried employment to embark
on an hourly position. In other words, how much more would one have to earn
per hour as an hourly employee to make the risk worthwhile? (considering
one must then pay for their own insurance, invest for their retirement, and
budget for possible time off in between contracts)

Note: If I appear somewhat fearful it's not due to my confidence/ability as
a programmer, it is because I am being offered an $8/hr higher bill rate
from another firm, have a 2 month old son to think about and don't want to
make any hasty decisions.

Thanks in advance for any and all replies,
Tazzer

Only you can really make the determination of what rate is enough. With
an infant, I would have to make sure of what medical plans would be
available and how much they would cost before deciding to strike out on
your own. I've worked both salaried and hourly through consulting firms
and there is nothing magical about either way. For each perceived
advantage they tout, you can be sure there is some disadvantage to
offset it. $8 x 2000 hours/year = $16,000. Will the extra money be
enough to cover paying your own insurance plans, cover child care if
needed, and in general make up for the hassles of hourly work? I'm not
sure it will.

I'm a big believer in work an hour, get paid an hour. I had done the
salary bit for a while, and too often found that the promise of comp
time to make up for unpaid overtime and long weekends went unfulfilled.
I have been consulting more or less on an independent basis for about 7
years, and have never had any down time! I have been lucky enough to
read between the lines on poorly managed projects so that I got out
before the "announcement". And my network has worked well enough so that
I could always locate another assignment when I thought it was time to
move on.

On the downside, you have to contend with your own insurance, extra
taxes and at least some paperwork that you did not have to deal with
before and the Federal and state governments. On the upside you get
access to monetary alternatives and lifestyle flexibility you don't have
as an employee. You can stow quite a bit of PRE-TAX cash in a SEP, a lot
more than a 401K. I think the limit is just a bit more than $24,000 for
1997 or 15% of your income (Don't know 1998 yet ... I'm obviously NOT an
accountant) This makes a big difference toward retiring at a reasonable
age. You can take extra time off to spend with your family. Of course
you won't get paid, but sometimes the time is more important.

If you do your own business, you WILL need an accountant. Most
accountants will be happy to provide a free initial consultation.
Interview a few accountants in your area. Tell them up-front when you
schedule the appointment that you are thinking of starting your own firm
and would like help on Corporation type (C or S) and expense issues, tax
issues, retirement planning... etc. This is a good way to get a lot of
info from a knowledgable source.

The only other advice that I can offer is that IF you decide to do this,
you should concentrate very hard on building what I call a F*CK-YOU
account. (Please excuse the language). This account will contain enough
cash to cover ALL of your personal living expenses for AT LEAST a six
month period. I know this may sound like a lot of money, but it is a
very good feeling to know that you always have this account to fall back
on just in case you do get benched or get forced into quitting.

Good Luck
