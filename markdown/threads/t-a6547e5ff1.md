- **From:** "Charles Hottel" <chottel@earthlink.net>

```
Some recent post have siad  "oh it is just GUI but it does not do anything". 
I think this is an incorrect view as most people are strongly visually 
oriented and do care about how their data is presented.

I once worked at The Library of Congress which uses MARC records to store 
information about books.  They had an inhouse file management system that 
programmers used to access information in the MARC records.  It was called 
Logic Library.  The library management wanted to replace Logic Library (LL) 
because they associated it with the "green screen" displays of CICS.  Of 
course the GUI was completely separate from (LL) and there was no off the 
shelf equivalent for LL.  The IT department created an web style GUI and 
interfaced it to LL and management and users were quite happy with it and 
the IT department was able to keep LL and continue to enhance it.

Both the GUI and the processing supporting it are important.
```

There are GUIs and there are GUIs !

GUIs often, but not always make scripting difficult.

They can also be sluggish when not given adequate oomph and
frustrating to experienced users.

On the other hand they can make the learning curve much easier, up to
the point where they have to be maintained.

They are very good for occasional users, who never want or need to
have to get to grips with the more complex features.  On the other
hand sometimes, more complex features are more easily accessible via a
GUI.

I suppose it all depends on how well they are implemented and whether
they provide comparable or better functionality to what went before.
