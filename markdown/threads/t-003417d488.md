LOL! Good one, Rufio!

Instances like this make me think that the current European trend of billing
for a "professional day" is not such a bad idea.

(Personally, I like to work this way because it is more "goal oriented" than
the hourly rate. We all know that there are a certain percentage of hours
which are worked which don't necessarily achieve much (or even anything) yet
they are billed for nevertheless...)

The "professional day" was started by companies who figured they could get 9
or 10 hours for the price of 8...

However, it cuts both ways... My "professional" day ends when I have
achieved the detailed goals I set each morning...  This means my day is
anything between 6 and 8 hours...On very rare occasions it might go to 10,
or even 12, the point is that it is goal driven. I do whatever it takes and
encourage teams to do likewise. This is a different mind set from hourly
work. If you are motivated, it is actually fun to achieve the goals you set
and the odd "hard day" is no problem.

I don't know if this approach is being adopted in the USA, but I would
advise embracing it if it is <G> (Be sure that you deliver, though...)

When assessing workloads, I work on the basis of 5.5 PRODUCTIVE hours per
person per day.

The reasons behind this are too involved to go into here, but it works.

Pete.

This raises a very good point, Doc.

I completely agree with your reasoning, however, there HAVE been occasions
when I have worked considerable hours without billing for it...

Your post made me think about this, and I have come up with the following
"rules" as to when extra unbillable hours would be acceptable FOR ME (I
accept that this is a VERY personal thing and some people will NEVER work
unbilled hours; I don't think they are wrong...)

1. If the Client ASKS me to work unbilled hours (as in your example) I would
NOT do it.  (This is demeaning and insulting. In effect they are saying: "We
know you can't get a job anywhere else so if you wish to continue enjoying
working for us, then scratch our backs...". I would politely but firmly
decline and if they decided to terminate the contract I would consider it a
lucky escape...<G>)

Such a client is unlikely to have my services in the future, UNLESS they
offered some other form of incentive (like, say a fully paid holiday in some
exotic location - I have had two instances (in twenty five years) where this
was done - the reasoning was that they could raid the "travel" budget more
easily than they could get more "contract resource" cash....).

2. If I have responsibility for goals and targets and I undercall it, I see
that as MY failure, not theirs, so I will gladly put in extra unbillable
hours to make it right.

3. If I accept THEIR time estimates and deadlines then point 2 above also
applies. Once it is agreed, then it is just the same as if I had done it. If
I have reservations about the timeframe I will explain why, where I see
pitfalls, and negotiate...

As contractors we have all been given unreasonable estimates from Managers
who are seeking to further their careers and don't necessarily understand
the effort involved. Here is an actual scenario, almost verbatim, that
happened in my career many years ago...

Manager: "This has to be done by Thursday."

(I take the spec. and read it)

Me: "Good luck. I hope you find someone who can do it. I can't."

(Look of shock from Manager...)
Manager: "What do you mean?! It has to be done by Thursday."

Me: "I can do it by Monday if I work some time over the weekend, drop what
I'm doing, and give this top priority. It will need exclusive access to the
system and that can only be obtained at the weekend anyway, it is predicated
on certain modules being available; I happen to know that at least one of
these is still being written, and there is a large amount of coding
required, which takes time."

In the end, he went away, consulted his Boss, and came back the next day.

Manager: "OK, Monday."

Me:"Nope, sorry, now it'll be Tuesday."

Manager: "But you said Monday!".

Me: "I did indeed. But that was yesterday and 24 hours have now been
wasted."

It was delivered on Tuesday morning...

Now the question arises, should I bill for the extra time I put in? Most
reasonable people would say "Yes", and especially as weekend work was
involved.

(As it happened, this was in a strange country (well, strange to me that
is...<G>) and I had nothing to do over the weekend anyway. In fact, I was
quite happy to get some peace and quiet to do the job. Being of a naturally
generous disposition, I decided NOT to bill them for the weekend work (it
was about 10 hours), but write it off to "Goodwill" instead...

Bad idea.

Later, when I needed some time for a personal reason, my request was
declined. I gently reminded them that there had been numerous occasions (the
above was not the only one...) when I had worked above and beyond what was
required and could they please cut me some slack. No dice. If they did that
for me they would have to do it for everybody. (Like, that is SUCH a bad
idea <G>?).

When the contract came up for renewal they asked me to extend. I declined.
They offered more money. I told them it wasn't about money, I just needed to
move on...

I have never worked there since and wouldn't, under the same conditions of
employment. (I would manage projects for them, because this is a different
deal. I have a large degree of autonomy and am paid on results.)

3. If a company I have had a good relationship with has an "emergency" I
will work whatever it takes and worry about how much of it gets billed,
later.

This is simple Marketing. I sell my service and sometimes, as in any
Marketing operation, it is necessary to look after the "regulars". However,
if they "expect" this or (as in Doc's example), actually ASK for it, then my
natural generosity disappears like frost in Summer...

SUMMARISING:

It is good to be reasonable. Be generous where you can. But don't let people
take advantage of your good nature...<G>

Pete.

PS. I endorse wholeheartedly Doc's point that he wouldn't bill for hours not
worked. I would have no truck with any contractor who did this.
