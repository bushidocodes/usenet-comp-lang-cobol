Conversely, the nonsensical practice of always putting periods and
commas inside adjacent closing quotation marks, regardless of where
they logically belong, is mandated by most US usage guides (that take
any position on the subject).

Thus we have, for example:

   Howard dislikes placing the period "outside of the quote."

required by most copy editors and the like.  Ridiculous.

I have read (but not verified) that this is an archaic holdover from
the days of movable lead type: small glyphs like periods and commas
were particularly susceptible to breakage when positioned next to
whitespace, so they were moved inside the quotation when possible.

Personally, I disagree with Howard, in the case where the end of
the quoted sentence is also the end of a sentence that incorporates
it, as in:

   As Howard wrote, "Programmers know where the logic needs to be".

There the period must terminate two scopes: that of the quoted sentence
and that of the enclosing sentence.  Unless you're going to employ two
periods - which, frankly, I think looks awful - the logical place to
do that is in the outer scope, with the inner scope implicitly closed.

typeface changes and the like.  CE insisted on removing a lot of

unnecessarily constrain expression, without providing inexperienced
authors with the understanding of prose style they'd need to know
when to disregard them.  Richard Ohmann wrote a fine piece in
_College English_ back in 1979 arguing against S&W's "Use definite,
specific, concrete language", and it's just as relevant today.

like the ones against "split infinitives", or sentence fragments,

English should behave like Latin, or were simply small-minded.  S&W
isn't so bad in this regard, but I still believe it puts writers on
the wrong path.  And I say this as someone who taught composition
at the college level, if only for a couple of years, and had to
plow through the mangled prose of undergraduates on a regular basis.

verbatim the wisdom of the muses.[1]  Fowler, in _Modern English

Inverted commas aren't used in conventional US typography.  So-called
"single quotes", which are graphically the same, are, but not for the
purpose you suggest (distinguishing terminological quotation from
direct quotation).  In conventional US typography, "double quotes" are
used for all quotation purposes - direct quotation, referring to a
word or phrase as such, indicating figurative or questionable usage
("scare quotes") - except one: quotation within quotation.  There, US
usage bizarrely employs single quote marks for the embedded quotation:

   "Richard wrote 'that's not correct'", I wrote.

Presumably this convention developed in parallel to the UK one of
employing double quotes for embedded quotations (with the outer
quotation set off with inverted commas).

With more than two levels of quotation, US copy editors seem to lean
toward alternating double and single quotes.  It quickly becomes a
mess, but then quotations with embedded quotations with embedded
quotations is already a mess.
