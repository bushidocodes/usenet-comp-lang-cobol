It would

task in which the user generally is not looking at the screen.

One other thing that I'd mention to the prospect whining about no GUI.
What is it
about GUIs that they would like to have? It's not enough to say I prefer
a GUI.
Why? Is there something missing from your 24x80 that they want? Perhaps
it could
be added without going to a GUI. Finally, is there sufficient return on
the investment
to go to a GUI?

The bottom line of all this is that HCI cannot be reduced to rules of
thumb like
"always use a GUI". It's just not that simple.

Ron Peterson wrote:

› Paul Oldham wrote:

…[4 more quoted lines elided]…
›› relation to repetive tasks?
 
› Your customer may be right. A GUI may be easier to learn and more
› efficient....There is no reason that you can't have both interfaces. 
› The terminal offers portability to more devices than a GUI.

Your arguments ought to include cost of training in terms of the
turnover experienced by that company, for staff in that role. That may
disadvantage the sale, though. And although a terminal may be ideal for
a particular type of application, there may only be a small subset of
those which are optimal in a 20x80 screen.

There is evidence that a standard "GUI" - in terms of particular
architecture and ways of interacting - may not be the most efficient
interface for repetitive, transactional tasks. This makes logical
sense, because the GUI was designed to support naive and occasional
users best. There is also some support for "experts", but even so,
these are experts in a different sense than those trained to a
particular all-day task at a terminal.

I've been looking at ways to use GUI toolkits to create an interface
optimised for transactional tasks (such as customer handling), because
the company has invested in the hardware to support Windows. Having
expected (but not received) efficiency gains by moving to GUI, we're
looking further into it. It will be a matter of a different sort of
architecture than standard GUI, but still "graphical".

There is a particular task in which we've found that drag&drop reduces
the cognitive load *so* dramatically that reduced training time alone
saves lots of money, and the operators are able to interact more with
the caller. The time saved turns out to be minimal. This obviously
could not be done on a character-based workstation.

However, if the option to have the more traditional sort of workstation
exists, it should not be dismissed out of hand - although I would
question whether a 20x80 terminal is the best way to do it. There is so
little space for information, that an application which is *about*
information surely must be handicapped by so little space.

There is one study involving GUI vs terminal, done with NYNEX, called
"Validating a GOMS Analysis", Gray, John & Atwood, HCI 1993. The result
of that study was to not move over to GUI for that particular
application.

There is also general awareness in the HCI community that GUI is not the
whole answer, but I don't know of much else which is published.
