Well, I have finally finished the course I've been developing. Many people had
some great suggestions for a title. My favorite was "Hot Catholic High School
Girls in Bondage". 

But I guess I'll chicken out and go with a more conventional title:

Inter-Language Communication in OS/390

I'm trying to be sensitive to issues of bandwidth and promotion, so let me just
point to our website for folks who might be interested (go to
www.trainersfriend.com; follow the links from the home page to Topics -> LE ->
Inter-Language Communication in OS/390; this is a course description; at the
bottom of the page is a link to the course objectives; at the bottom of this
page is a link to a detailed topical outline).

At one point, someone expressed incredulity (sp?): "What? A course on Calling?
That's like having a course on breathing!" I thought I could whip out a two day
course in a couple of weeks. Instead, it's taken me nearly six months!

A quick teaser then I'll sign off: Assembler, COBOL, PL/I, C, argument list
construction (variable numbers of args, omitted args, pass by reference, pass
by value, functions), parameter list retrieval, working with null-terminated
strings, static calls, dynamic calls, alternate entry points, shared external
data, object formats (OBJ, XOBJ, GOFF), executables (load modules, program
objects, DLLs); coding DLLs for the mainframe.

Thanks for your time and forebearance.

Good point.   I would love it if school kids learned to use slide
rules.  They wouldn't stand for it, but a slide rule is one of the best
tools for all the math that 95% of the  population ever needs to learn -
which most of those 95% do NOT learn:

1.  Proportion.  A is to B as C is to D...   Use this to calculate
mileage, etc.  Also used for non mathematical logic (boy is to man as
girl is to woman).
1a.  Inverse proportion.

2.  Estimation.  Since slide rules don't show the decimal points, slide
rule users have to have some idea what their answer should be.  With
this skill, people should develop the common sense to know when their
assumptions and calculations are wrong.  Even if the numbers come from a
computer!!

Here's a Java slide rule: 
http://www.comcen.com.au/~adavie/javaslide/javaslide.html

3.  Significant figures.  Look at a hole in the ground which is about 1
yard across.  It must be 3.1415926589 yards around, right?  Hey, pi to
one significant digit is 3.
