I have been programming for thirty-three years now.  I have a full-time
salesman to hand out my resumes and get me work. I have two assistants to
help me write code and make enough money to live by.

You come along, and suggest that I spend a month or two writing a demo
program for you to show you how clever I am, then spend a day or two writing
up thirty-three years of my work to asauge your curiosity, and  wonder why I
am not enamoured of the idea.

Probably the most common phrase I have heard from amateurs over the last
thirty years starts with "You should write ...

My response is canned.  "Good idea!" I exclaim.  Why don't you go out, raise
half a million in capital to cover the first two years and give us some
marketing money.  I'll write the code. That usually cools their jets
somewhat.

I rather suspect that the majority of posters in this NG feel somewhat the
same. We are professionals, not students. I am a lot more concerned about
*what* to write than *how* to write it. Once I know what I want to write,
the rest is just grunt work.  I can find a million people that will give me
a project to write for free, simply by snapping my fingers (or posting one
message on Usenet). If you really want a contest, come up with something
interesting ... don't try to convince me to write you an Isam update
program.  I've done that before.

So.

1.  If you want my resume, contact my salesman/partner.  I doubt that he
will be bothered, as he also does it for a living.  He seldom puts sales
packages together for the joy of it, as he has a private life to satisfy as
well.  However, he does have the data.  I don't even have a copy, as I have
not used it in ten years.

2. If you want me to work for you, tell me how much you pay, or write a
spec, and I will bid the job.  If I want to program for free, I have a
lifetime of projects to play with ... I will choose my own, thank you.
There are dozens of projects that I'd love to write if I had time.  Some of
them, I am busy writing, just for fun. Trying to write the worlds best
customer update module is not one of them, as I have already done it too
many times to count.  I'd rather go out and get laid, or perhaps write
something interesting.

And last but not least:

3. The last contest I entered was by Fujitsu.  In spite of the fact that
they named me a winner (second), and used my name in a press release, they
stiffed my for the prize (a T shirt).  That was fifteen years back.  They
announced a contest last year with a portable PC as the prize.  I did not
bother with a submission for that one. At least on the first they allowed
the entrant to decide what they wanted to program, and I managed to sell a
few thousand copies.  Contests are for beginners to make their bones.

------------****------------

BTW, I ran into a PC application that broke the ISAM methodology this week.
If the application had been queued client/server, there would have been no
problem.

I have an installation that is totally automatic, running on five PC's. The
data base is Isam, sitting on a central server and using file/level locking,
though that could be converted to record level locking quite easily.  The
new extension that is required has to run on the dashboard of a front-end
loader, via packet switched radio/Ethernet. (Front-end loader as in one of
those big construction machines, not as in a program).

Unfortunately, with a mobile platform, the network connection can make and
break constantly as the PC moves around.  Radio fades in and out, sometimes
when the file is locked.  Locking you central database for five minute while
the PC is out of site has rather disturbing consequences. I decided to write
the data needed by the loader out to a small sequential file and have the
loader computer read that file, sort it, and display it.  Transactions back
are handled as sort of a mini-batch run.  By doing that, they can be
deferred until the connection is back.

Thought you might be interested in why Isam does not always work.
