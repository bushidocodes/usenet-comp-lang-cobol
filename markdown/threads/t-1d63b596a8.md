```
This is a serious question.

Does anyone know of any vendor (or vendors):

A) Whose product(s) are related to either a single or multiple programming
language (but NOTHING else, no "games", "operating systems", hardware,
whatever)

    and

B) Do *not* have any run-time license fees (and/or EXORBITANT maintenance
fees)?

    ***

Certainly, this doesn't apply to Fujitsu or CA (for whom COBOL and other
compilers are a MINISCULE part of their business) or AcuCORP, Micro Focus,
or Liant (who have run-time license fees).  Equally true, it doesn't apply
to IBM, Unisys, HP, etc for whom the OS is their "major" product and
compilers "come along for the ride".

But what I don't know is whether any of the C++, Pascal, Ada, etc vendors
meet both of these criteria. I looked at the Borland site, and couldn't
figure out if they did or didn't meet them.  It seems to me that (at least
historically) most of the "C" vendors did *not* have a C product as their
"primary focus".  (Obviously, SUN and Java, does NOT meet both of these).
Similarly, any Microsoft compiler is eliminated as meeting BOTH of these.

Unless I am missing something, it seems that the compiler, and even
compiler-plus-tools, "business" does NOT make a profit *unless* some sort of
"run-time" profit is made by the original vendor.  On the other hand, it is
certainly true, that unless the "developer" (using the compiler) can still
make a profit, they won't buy (continue to use) that compiler.
```

I will second that.  I have been writing software for micro's since well
before the PC, and still have a copy of DOS 1.0.   I cannot think of a
single piece of software that charges both for the actual software package,
then everytime you use it for its prime purpose ... in this case to write
software for other computers.

In fact, the output of the program is completely, 100% useless without a
second software package snuck in after the first one has been paid for.
What would you think of a financial package, sold to accountants, that
required a percentage of all accounting fees? Think it would be worth 2-3000
per accountant?  How about a word processor that required a percentage of
all authors royalties for anything written using it?   How long do you thing
the banks would use Cobol, if the language vendor required a percentage of
every deposit?

When the only way to make a profit is to charge your customers, then require
them to charge their customers for you, then require them to charge THEIR
customers in addition, you are getting ridiculous.  I sell my software
through scale companies and cash register companies.  How the hell do I
explain that I am selling something I do not own, and after they buy it,
they will be required to pay some weird company somewhere extra money, and
sign a contract with them, before they can use what I am selling (and claim
to own).

Then after they have done that, they have to rent a telephone line for every
piece of equipment, hook it up to the internet, and call Britain to get
permission to start their cash register.  Pardon the language, but the
response I get is "You have to be out of your fucking mind".  Small vars
like myself cannot make a living using a language crippled like that.

Bill,

You've answered your own questions in your last statement. I feel concern for
the vendors just making money from software alone - but that doesn't extend to
my being charitable enough to stick with their money generating schemes.

Reality check - February 1981, prices are Canadian :-

64K Radio Shack (Model II)                                   $5,645
1-Drive expansion (gave total of  4 x 8 " floppies)     $1,699

-------

$7,344

Radio Shack - RM/COBOL compiler                       $  399

Having produced the compiler, and presumably the LRM, which came shrink-wrapped
in a Radio Shack cover, no more involvement by RM(Liant). There was no Tech
support, nor upgrades, plus no examples. (Well, Tandy did introduce *some* tech
support, including COBOL - first time I 'phoned asking for 'COBOL Support' -
"Yew all, we don't support other folks software !".)

My experience of tech support generally, Radio Shack, MS COBOL and to a lesser
extent MF COBOL, not worth the money you are being asked to shell out for
perhaps a once a year query. The plus side is the maintenance cost gives you
access to latest Fixpacks/upgrades - assuming you currently need them

In an effort to generate income over and above basic product price, our software
only vendors have turned to other approaches. (Who sez it was Arthur Andersen
that introduced the concept of  'inventive accounting' ?).

Prices are theoretical, because I don't know current status - Would you buy a
new compiler every four years ? Base price $3,600, now add 4 years maintenance
at $900 ???.  Add to that runtime fees.

There has to be a compromise between what vendors need to survive and what the
small guy is prepared to pay to stay in business.

COBOL is shooting itself in the foot, and it's a combination of following, in no
particular order :-

- Just like the larger populace doesn't bother to vote, most developers just sit
looking in and puzzled. The mainframe crowd, "Not our problem".

- COBOL Standards - too damn long a process. Plus the intended Standard
includes whole chunks that wont be implemented by some vendors, plus even if
implemented, probably wont be used by the majority of developers.

- Tools are available for SQL/DB, GUIs and  Printing that diminish COBOL's role
-
( viz. COBOL Files, Screen Section, Report Writer).

- Vendor pricing structures
