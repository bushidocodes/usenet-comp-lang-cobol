Alright, gang... time for a Tale from the Trenches, you young'uns pay
special attention... this is how it works in parts of the Real World:

I'd been given a job to modify to Y2K compliance... what I usually do in
such situations is kinda simple: I make a copy of the job, proc and
code, modify the job and proc to point to copies of the files which I've
made from Prod (Thou Shalt Not Unnecessarily Touch Prod!), modify the
code... and then I add a bit of Olde-Tymer's Laziness:

//JOBLIB DD DISP=SHR,DSN=SYS1.COB2LIB
//* DD DISP=SHR,DSN-MY.TEST.LOADLIB
// DD DISP=SHR,DSN=PROD.LOADLIB
... (buncha stuff)
//OUTFILE01 DD DSN=NODE.NODE.NODE.PRDOUT,DISP=(,CATLG,CATLG), etc.

(note for the More Proficient: STEP01 of the job is always an IEFBR14
to blow off any file with a CATLG so's to avoid NOTCAT 2s and they are
,,CATLG so if the sucker blows up I can sniff around whatever came out;
I realise that there are other methods out there but this'n works for
me)

What this setup does is allow me to wing off the job using the PROD load
modules; I then uncomment the second JOBLIB card... errrrr, line, do a
global replace on PRDOUT to TSTOUT and send the job off again... hey,
presto, runs using the same data, from test and Prod, ready for result
comparison. (This will not be on the blue-book exam but it might be
helpful to remember).

Anyhow... I set this particular job up for the first run from PROD...
and the sucker blows up, S0C4. This is... amusing, to say the least; I
browse the Prod loadlib and find out that the last time this program was
compiled was... July, 1987. No prob... but, just to be sure, I check
out the source... all SELECTs match up with the DD attributes, yup, it
got TRANSFORMs and WRITE... AFTER POSITIONING... but then I notice the
static-linked subroutines.

The code contains a combination of hard- and soft-linked subroutines...
but just to be sure (notice how frequently that phrase is used?) I check
the compile-dates of the subroutines... and *there* the trouble begins.
Two of the hard-linked subroutines show compile-dates of 1988 and
1993... since I do not have access to the subroutine source as of the
driver's compile-date I *cannot* reproduce the module for debugging.
What needs to be done, I conclude, is a Production Recompile; I put
together a memo to this effect, bounce it off to the Lead Consultant...
and he comes over to my cube, asking me to justify the request. I
explain DP102 to him and he seems *very* loath to pass on the request;
after all, asking for a Prod Recomp is asking someone to do *work*, and
Rule #0 is Nobody Wants To Do Any Work. I finally summarize my position
thusly:

'Look, the subroutines are hardlinked so their load-modules are
physically included into the driver's load module. The subroutines were
recompiled in Prod; this means they were changed. The changes were
either trivial or significant. If the changes were significant than the
prod driver should be recompiled to reflect these significant changes.
If the changes were trivial then the subroutines' being recompiled means
that Prod recompiles can be based on trivial changes... so what's the
problem?'

He didn't like that but he went away... and came back the next day
saying that Old Joe (an employee with a bit of familiarity with the
system we are working on) was looking into the matter and would get back
to us on Monday.

Came Friday, 1430 hrs, and our Weekly Meeting was held... the Lead
Consultant was out but the employee who signs our timesheets, and *his*
boss, were both there. A small amount of quacking, a bit of woof, and
then they broke out a birthday cake for the celebration of a Fellow
Geeks nativity. After folks had consumed a bit of carbohydrates I
addressed the group, thusly -

'Well, now that I got some more heads in a room with me I'd like to toss
out a... problem... yes, I have *problems*, not 'challenges'... '

... and I more-or-less outlined what I've set forth here. The
Time-Sheet Signer immediately replied 'Oh yes, the Lead Consultant
mentioned that... Old Joe is looking into that and will get back to us
on Monday.' This is a classic Put It Off manuever, a sort of 'No need
to look closely, we're working on it and should have results Any Day
Now'... the only problem with that is it assumes a moveable deadline,
which 01 Jan 2000 most certainly is *not*.

Anyhow, he smiled at me with a sort of 'Well, not only do I have that
under control but we don't have to do Any Work now' (see Rule #0)
grin... and I said 'Well, today is Friday... Monday's a ways off...

... fine, over the weekend Old Joe gets hit by a bus. (shocked laughter
from the group) Since we've got everyone here now I'd appreciate some
of insights possible only from a group.'

After a few questions and suggestions ('Did you change the proc?' 'No'
'Do the SELECTs match the DDs?' 'Yup') the consensu started to work
towards Prod Recomp... and then the Time-Sheet Signer's Boss piped up
with 'How long have you been wrestling with this?' 'It was given to me
yesterday afternoon', I says. 'Well,' he says, trying to play Old Wise
Man, 'sometimes it helps just to step back, rest a bit and let another
set of eyes see the problem.' ... notice again, as with the Signer
himself, how this 'solution' results in Nobody Doing Any Work.

'Well,' says I, 'that's kind of what I was doing here... asking the
group, getting a little four-eye debugging done... but, y'see, *I'm* the
guy who was asked to step in on the problem... what you are suggesting
is what was done when it was given to *me*.'

General laughter... and a hurried 'Well, I take back that suggestion,
then... wait for Old Joe on Monday.'

... and Nobody Does Any Work... and 01 Jan 2000 is another weekend
closer.

DD
