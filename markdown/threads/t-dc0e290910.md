Most employers prefer to hire people with experience in the job for which
they are being hired. However, the second choice is to hire people with
experience in working at all (e.g., showing up for work on time, etc.) In
that respect, taking the job as a store manager is far better than waiting
around for an entry level COBOL.

Also, managing a furniture store will probably be useful on the resume, if
you ever do get a programming job, when you have enough experience to start
leading a group (providing you have an interest in this). Most shops
expect their better programmers to demonstrate the ability to lead a group
of people when necessary, as part of the progression to Analyst, Team
Leader, etc. Amazing as it may seem, you will need many of the same skills
to do that as to manage a store, and these are skills lacking in many
straight technicians.

Lastly, it is amazing how many oportunities can come up in a small business
location to use whatever computer skills you have. Your store (should you
take that job) will probably have some sort of computers, and you can
probably learn something about being on the user side (another area sorely
lacking in some experienced technicians). All the best analysts I know
(IMNSHO) are people who have had to use the darn things to do a 'user' job,
and have seen both good and bad implementations. You can learn a lot by
seeing some of the stuff on the market, and thinking through how it works
(or how it should...)

in my honest opinion, it would ruin your chances forever of getting a
programming job, without somehow updating your education first. You sort
of would have to 'reset' your career.

This does not mean you should not pursue the manager position! I would
find it fascinating to experience being a manager of a shop; and
wouldn't you sleep badly at night knowing you let such a chance go?

Actually, many people tire of programming when they reach the 5th or
sixth year of their professional programming career. Others, of course,
thrive in coding for their entire life.

Consider who you yourself are :)
