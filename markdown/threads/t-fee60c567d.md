I know that there have been several (many?) past post that have explicitly
PRAISED Fujitsu for its excellent "support" (and compared this, among
others, to the cost and "responsiveness" of Micro Focus' support).  In case
you are an existing Fujitsu customer (paid up on maintenance) *OR* are
considering new/additional Fujitsu purchases, I thought that I would post to
this forum the information now available on the ServiceWise "home" page for
existing customers.

"Support Announcements

Changes in Support
We'd like to take this opportunity to let you know about a few changes the
NetCOBOL team is making to the way that we provide support.

In order to provide the best possible service to all of our customers, all
technical support incidents must be initiated through this Web support tool.
We will no longer be providing call in support. When the situation warrants,
members of our support staff will call you to help resolve issues, but our
primary method of communication will be through the Web support tool. Using
the tool ensures that we have a log of all activity related to your incident
and can bring the right resources to bear in giving you a timely response.

For incidents that fall outside the scope of our maintenance agreement,
we're introducing the option to purchase assistance for a fee of $125 per
incident. This includes How-to questions, requests for samples, and
information about interfaces to third party tools. In the past, our support
team has been quite liberal about answering such questions. Moving forward,
however, it is necessary for us to recover at least some of the cost
associated with this very costly activity.

For more detailed information about our support policies and procedures and
information on how to purchase paid incidents, please visit our Web site at
http://www.netcobol.com/support/ .

Thank you,
The Fujitsu NetCOBOL Support Team"

    ***

If you "read into" this (as I do - but possibly NOT as intended) this may
provide some "valuable" information to those who question whether or not
marketing COBOL to "individuals" (as opposed to enterprises) is profitable -
and whether or not providing "free run-times" is a good BUSINESS decision
for the vendor.

I stopped paying for maintenance after the Service Wise system was
introduced as I found it cumbersome and not responsive...useless.
Fortunately, I seldom need support anyway. Since Version 6 of COBOL 97 I
have found no bugs despite extensive use of most of the facilities
(including OO and component building).

As for NetCOBOL, well, I simply can't afford it, so it is academic. I build
Web Services with COBOL97/PowerCOBOL v6 and SOAP. I don't get the benefit of
the CLR but it works for me.

I think the point about interfacing to third party products is a fair one.

product that people may want to address with Fujitsu COBOL.

Putting it into perspective... the software is stable and works as specified
PROVIDED you don't try doing outlandish things with it. If you decide you
want to interface to a Bendix washing machine and drive the spin cycle with
COBOL, you can hardly expect Fujitsu to provide expertise and experience,
for free; it is your decision.

of what was once a MicroFocus market. When it comes to NetCOBOL, the runtime
is academic, because my understanding is that it generates the MS Common
Language Runtime (CLR). (I could be wrong about this; I don't claim any
expertise with NetCOBOL...)

In general, I don't think it is unfair or unreasonable for Fujitsu to
rationalise their maintenance arrangements for a new product that has cost a
fortune to develop, has a place at the cutting edge of the technology, and
is likely to require more support for that very reason. The fact that this
places it beyond the reach of "small" developers or individuals like myself,
is just a sad fact of doing business. If I could justify the cost of buying
it, I'd do so like a shot. That is not Fujitsu's problem.

For the standard Fujitsu products (COBOL 97/ PowerCOBOL) people can get help

As one of the support people fired by Fujitsu recently I guess I should
weigh in on this topic.

Please excuse the following brief personal history - there is a point to it.

For those who don't know me, I've been an COBOL advocate for 30+ years.
I've been a programmer,  trainer, manager, tech writer - I've designed
debuggers and programming tools - I've worked for three COBOL compiler
vendors.  But the job that has given me the most satisfaction over my career
have been the times when I worked the tech support hot lines.

In the early 80's I was part of the initial XPEDITER development team.  The
product was and I suppose still is a technological marvel.  It was also, in
the early years, buggy as hell.  Sales support had to cross their fingers
when demo-ing the product for fear of crashing TSO or IMS or CICS or the
whole mainframe.   The only reason we got past the early years was the
crackerjack support team back in San Jose to put out the fires.

In the late 80's I went to MicroFocus just as they started their big growth
spurt.  Again a good company with lots of tools an innovative products.  And
again the product was complicated and BUGGY. And again again it was the
great support team in Palo Alto there to back up sales.  I might also add
that the MicroFocus support team consisted a many of the same people who
worked on XPEDITER (including our own WMK).

In both of the jobs I had great satisfaction.  I was working with products,
although imperfect, that could be of tremendous benefit to my fellow COBOL
programmers.  I admit to getting a buzz upon seeing the eyes light up when a
programmer suddenly sees a better, less tedious way of doing their job.  I'm
also a compulsive problem solver - I like finding the bugs that others
overlook.  As both companies got larger, suddenly support was an expense to
be minimized.

I left in both cases.  I think it was to both companies detriment.  No, not
my leaving, but going that extra mile with personal one on one tech support
(when its needed).  Wasn't that what made IBM great when we were young?

In the late 90's I ended at Fujitsu.  Here was the ultimate company and job
I thought.  The product was virtually bug free and leading edge technology
(for Windows anyway).  COBOL at last had arrived and had a vendor to
champion it.  The documentation is very poor, and yet the technology so up
to date, and with virtually no other resources for COBOL programmers, I
thought support was essential to keeping the product line afloat.  Support
at Fujitsu was primarily a two person affair.  A guy named Alex handled most
of the routine stuff and I concentrated on the bleeding edge stuff and
legacy stuff.

Then on tax day in April we were rather uncerimoniously told our services
were no longer required.  We were told at the time it was temporary and for
budget reasons but we subsequently learned that to be horse sh*t.   We were
not let go because of money - it turns out we just weren't needed anymore.

I've done much soul searching in the last month.  I feel lost, betrayed.
Its the first time in my career I'm not needed.  I have no COBOL any more.
Is it time to switch languages for good?

I have just been reading Chris Richardson's new COBOL book.  It is
excellent.  I find a lot of ideas parallel my own right now.

OK, so I'm a little bitter.  I no longer see a reason to keep COBOL on my
resume.  Unfortunately, given my work history, I don't know how to hide it.

Lee Unterreiner    www.flu-ent.com
