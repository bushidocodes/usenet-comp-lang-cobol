```
I'm part of a team studying the implications of our customers remaining
on OS/VS COBOL.  The study started from one CICS shop that read the CICS
TS V2.3 announcement, which essentially says you'll no longer have a
separate COBOL Translator for CICS at that release.  (The translator is
integrated into the IBM COBOL compiler, so you'd need to be using the
current compiler.)  This would allow OS/VS COBOL programs to run, but
would keep them from being changed.

DB2 V7 seems to say similar things; there will be an integrated
pre-compiler in COBOL for OS/390 & VM V2R2, although I don't know for
sure that the integrated pre-compiler for SQL will be the ONLY way to
pre-compile.

There doesn't seem to be any imminent deadline for executing load
modules compiled with OS/VS COBOL.  CICS made noises in this direction,
but have since withdrawn those statements, I believe.  The latest we've
been ale to get from IBM says that Language Environment will continue to
support OS/VS COBOL into the foreseeable future.  However, our customer
is running CICS with LE, but their batch is using the OS/VS COBOL run
time library.  IBM understandably won't say anything "supportive" about
that!

Some people we've talked to have said they had assumed the world had
already finished converting to "new COBOL", because they had done so.
Some IBM folks we've talked to have expressed surprise (maybe even
shock) that the customer hasn't really even begun to convert.  After
all, conversion could have begun as long as 18 years ago when VS COBOL
II was announced.

Since looking at the first shop, the customer has told us that at least
two more of their divisions are in similar shape.  (The first shop is
roughly 80% OS/VS COBOL, most of the rest is VS COBOL II, with a very
few COBOL for MVS & VM load modules.)  They are starting to look at this
as an enterprise wide problem.  The shop where I do most of my work is
only about 40 - 50% converted, though we're going at it pretty hard
now.  This shop supports another large customer, and I recently learned
that another application group has hardly begun to convert.  In fact, I
helped the programmer there get his first run unit to execute just last
week.

My company, an IT services provider, is starting to listen to those of
us who've been crying wolf.  They may tackle the issue as something they
MUST do for their customers, to keep their mainframe applications
viable.

I'm curious.  Those of you who can share the information, what does your
shop look like in terms of load modules compiled (and executing) with
OS/VS COBOL vs. VS COBOL II vs. "new COBOL" (COBOL/370, COBOL for MVS &
VM, COBOL for OS/390 & VM, etc.)?

Are you starting to worry about your exposure if you still have OS/VS
COBOL running?

Thanks for the information!
```

```
"Translator support for the earlier (pre-Language Environment) compilers
```

```
My shop probably only has about 4000 COBOL programs in production. 
Very few of them are still in OS/VS COBOL.  Probably more than 50% of
our non-obsolete programs were last compiled with COBOL for MVS & VM
or COBOL for OS/390 & VM 2.2.0.  My guess is that less than 5% of our
non-obsolete programs were last compiled with OS/VS COBOL.  During our
Y2K runup we were requested to recompile all programs with COBOL II. 
Naturally, we missed a few, but it didn't cause us any problems then.

We replaced the OS/VS COBOL runtime library with the COBOL II runtime
library around 1990.  A few early adopters began writing COBOL II
programs around 1992, and we were very fortunate in that none of our
COBOL II programs ever had the "compatibility with Release 2" option. 
We converted to LE/MVS runtime in 1998, again for Y2K reasons.

We converted all of our CICS regions (about 90 of them) from CICS 4.1
to Transaction Server 1.3 last year.  We currently run OS/390 2.8 but
some test LPARS are already on 2.10.  We expect production LPARS to be
upgraded to 2.10 by mid-March at the latest.

Last year we had a big project to convert all COBOL programs to
AMODE(31),RMODE(ANY).  Naturally we missed a few.  Some could not be
converted because they called pre-XA assembler subprograms (usually
for tape access) that could not be changed for various reasons.  One
problem is the lack of skilled assembler programs in applications.

Another development group recently replaced a very large financial
application with a new version written entirely in LE-enabled COBOL. 
This application must run in an LPAR with ALL31(ON).  It turns out
they can't even test their programs in an LPAR with ALL31(OFF).  Our
sysprogs have been trying to convert other LPARS to ALL31(ON) and now
the problems are really biting us.

Of course, you could add an ALL31(OFF) parameter to the execute
statement, but that doesn't work for programs that have an assembler
main calling a COBOL subprogram.  We have a lot of work to do.  

> 
> Are you starting to worry about your exposure if you still have OS/VS
> COBOL running?
> 
> Thanks for the information!

If it's a batch OS/VS COBOL program, running as main, I wouldn't worry
too much.  You can always add ALL31(OFF) to the JCL.  

I don't believe we have any CICS programs still running that were last
compiled with OS/VS COBOL.  But then again, it's always possible to
miss one.
```

```
OK ,
  Let look at what happens when you concatenate run-time libraries (either
in Steplib or Linklist)

1) If the LE library is FIRST, then you will *not* have any run-time
problems. Why?  Because, you simply will NEVER use any of the routines from
the "OldBOL"  (OS/VS COBOL, VS COBOL II, DOS/VS COBOL, whatever) library.
EVERY routine that exists in the OldBOL library has an IDENTICALLY named
(but NOT necessarily identical object code) routine in the LE library.
  This means that your "concatenation" PROBABLY won't hurt you - it simply
does NOTHING - and you never will use any of the routines from the 2nd
library in the concatenation.

2) If the "OldBOL" library is FIRST in the concatenation, then you won't
have any problems *IF AND ONLY IF* the only application object code that is
executed - comes from the OldBOL compiler.  In other words, if you NEVER use
any routines from the LE library.  Again, this is a USELESS concatenation,
but shouldn't have "too many" problems.

So where do the problems come in?

If you have the OldBOL library FIRST *and* if even ONE program in the
application uses even ONE routine from the LE library.  Why?
  LE does a LOT of set-up when the first program in the run-unit is
"loaded".  This includes setting up of an "internal" table of where to
"find" all the COBOL (ILBO, ILBD, IGZ, whatever) routines.  Therefore, if
the first COBOL program in your run-unit is OS/VS COBOL (or VS COBOL II or
DOS/VS COBOL), this table is NOT set up, but instead the OLD (pre-LE) method
of finding run-time routines is "started".  Then when there is an
application need to "get to" one of the new routines occurs, it simply isn't
there BUT this isn't a main program, so
   RESULTS ARE UNPREDICTABLE.

Similarly,  If the COBOL main program is an IBM COBOL for this-and-that
program, and the OldBOL library is first in the concatenation, the "new"
(LE-ish) table *is* correctly set up and points to where to get all the
correct COBOL routines *FROM* the LE library - this includes BOTH IGZ *and*
ILBO routines (OldBOL and NewBOL).  Therefore, if even one OldBOL
application program is called *or* a NewBOL program "needs" an OldBOL
routine, the table of support routines points to the LE library *BUT* the
concatenation points to the OldBOL library. Again
  RESULTS ARE UNPREDICTABLE.

BOTTOM LINE:
  If you concatenate  LE before OldBOL libraries, this won't hurt you - it
just is USELESS
  If you concatenate OldBOL before LE, then things are OK
    BUT only as long as you never use any routines from the LE library.
    If you *do* use some routines from one library and other routines from
the other library then the BEST you can hope for is  "RESULTS ARE
UNPREDICTABLE".  (This doesn't mean that every program will "die" - it just
means that you are NOT supported and that someday, some modification to your
application is going to "die" and THEN you will find out why you never
should have done this in the first place.)
```
