> the DASD cost outweighs the cost of making me wait, even today.

I've worked on sites where this sort of retrieval can take anything up
to four hours because of the demand on systems, especially on the first
day back to work after something like a public holiday when a couple of
hundred programmers all want their datasets back at the same time. 
Surely it's got to cost a bit of money when this number of well-paid
people are sitting around staring at the ceiling for half a day; two
hundred of them makes one hundred lost man-days.

This kind of swings into the difference between test and live
environments these days.  At one time, when DASD was new and tapes were
still holding production data, we had to fit testing into the gaps
because live programs would run for a few hours at a time.  Of course,
we could use this time to write our code on coding sheets, and desk
check it and punch corrections, etc., etc.

In the modern environment, where we rely on being online and having
instant access to datasets of various sorts, I notice that many sites
still have the old-fashioned attitude to test and live, where anything
that a programmer needs has to take second place to everything else. 
This results in sitting around with nothing to do, when a simple
attitude adjustment in operations areas would allow us to get on with
the job that we are being paid to do, and cut the need for working into
the evening as a matter of course.

We all must have experiences of this sort of thing, and mine and Thane's
examples are only minor illustrations, but I was wondering if the old
attitudes do still exist at most sites and if it wasn't just my
imaginmation working overtime.

My installation had a similar experience about 2 years ago. Someone in
hardware planning decided we didn't need a planned CPU upgrade, so it
was postponed for a couple of months. So all us developers sat around
*waiting* for everything (especially during prime shift) and got little
done on a daily basis, while someone in the Ops area probably got a
bonus for saving the Bank the $1,000,000 for 2 months.

The adjustment is needed at the management level (higher than computer
ops). Someone needs to make the case that what is generally thought of
as the "test" system is the programmers'/developers' *production*
system. I would hope it'd be easy to justify additional hardware in a
setup like the one you described (and that I snipped) in which literally
everyone sits around for several hours every Monday. It not only costs
the company to pay those people (and the old response of "just work on
documentation" doesn't apply anymore, the doc is done online, too), but
these periods of waiting impact project schedules, too.
