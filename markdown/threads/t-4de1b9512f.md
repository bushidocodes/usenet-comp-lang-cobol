Of course, we all know that COBOL is adequate tool. Since many
companies are using COBOL, it *is* adequate. But I doubt that
it is adequate tool because of its good qualities. It is adequate
because everybody uses it.

In fact, we should first of all consider economic factors,
not the quality of the language. Peter is right saying that
COBOL programmers may be mediocre and therefore cheap.
Indeed, they are cheaper than C++/Powerbuilder programmers.

Then COBOL has been around since time of dinosaurs, so quite a
lot of applications was written in it. All these factors make
people continue using COBOL.

However it does not mean that COBOL is the best business app
language from the 'scientific' point of view. 85 ANSI stanard
improved it substatially, but it still hardly encourages
a good 'structured' style of programming.


Vadim Maslov


P.S. I don't have anything personal against COBOL. In fact,
the abundance of COBOL applications and people who want to 
move them to something less COBOLish is what is paying my salary.
And COBOL has good sides too -- for example, it has a definiton
that can be understood and using which, it's possible to write
a compiler. These cannot be said about other languages used
in data processing: IBM AS language comes to my mind as an example.
The semantic rules of this language (as well as how it can be
parsed into tokens) are not written down anywhere. AT least, not
in IBM AS reference manual.
