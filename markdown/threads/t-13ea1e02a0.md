I work for a major insurance processing firm here in Oklahoma City.
We do both life and health insurance processing for several companies around
America.

Basically the way our operation works is our customers are tied into our
Unisys machines via
a FEP or Front End Processor via dedicated lines to our customer sites.

And the code we are working with is COBOL running on a Unisys V series
computer.
In-fact we have banks of these machines, each performing processing for
multiple companies.

The Unisys V series uses StoQues and Core to Core memory transfers as it's
major communication
system.  It uses this process to pass data to and from programs as well as
to and from the FEP.

And we are re-writing this software on a test NT server, specially equipped
to talk to the FEP,
with STOQ and CRCR access, and our COBOL development platform is Net
Express.

We started out using OS/2 as our platform and Micro Focus OS/2 based Cobol
compilers.
The system was about 1/3 the way developed when the decision was made to
drop OS/2
in favor of NT.  I noticed that Micro Focus has also given notice of their
desire to leave the
OS/2 market in the comming months.  So I'm glad we made this decision back
in January.

So the platform to use was to be Net Express Version 2.0.

And we had some problems with Net Express Version 2.0 which virtually
stopped our project.
We were calling the support department in Micro Focus multiple times a week
with new bugs
we'd found.

And because of this fact, they elected to offer us the chance to be BETA
testers for Version 3.0.

I'm glad they did.  It saved our butts.

Let me discuss what we were up against here so you can have a better feeling
for our situation.

In our system there can be 300 programs in the MIX at any given time.  Some
take only seconds to
run, while others may take as long as 20 minutes yet others just reside in
there all day long.
We are 3/4 of the way through with 900 some odd programs left to convert.
By PHYSICAL COUNT, around 95% of the functionality of the Micro Focus Net
Express
product is in use.  It took that to emulate the V series which is a very
complicated and demanding
environment.   The PC we are running on is a very BEEF'ED up server running
NT.

Unisys COMP is like Micro Focus COMP-6.  We used this everywhere.  COMP-6 is
something
I'll bet nobody else uses.  But's it's a near perfect emulation of Unisys
Digits based COMP.
We had to interface with highly customized, NEW software and hardware to
communicate with
our FEP.  Our system not only has the potential of having 300 programs in
the MIX but behind
these .exe's there typically are multiple .dll programs being called and
dropped.
And entirely brand new sub OS was written under NT using Net Express to
simulate
several tasks our firm needed to accomplish our goals.  Custom drivers
galore!

We are NOT a lan based networking system.  Our Server does all the
processing and passes
data buffers to and from terminals and printers located all over the nation.
Our system is incredibly
fast.  It's faster than any LAN based system I've ever seen.

We have automatic LOGS on our system which record run times of software,
failures, ect...
We can detect failure in the operation of software with the flick of a wrist
on a console.
I can tell you the run times of every one of those packages and how they
related from
Version 2.0 to Version 3.0.

We have communicated extensively with the Beta Development people at Micro
Focus
in the last few months.

So it should be obvious to the causal reader that we do 'NO' Internet stuff.
We do 'NO' GUI stuff.  We just do the COBOL .exe/.dll stuff,,, the meat of
what COBOL has
to offer.  File I-O, processing, multi company data communication insurance
processing.

We have beat the hell out of this product.

This product is a much improved product over Version 2.0.
A much improved product.

A much improved product for an industry which demands a perfect cobol
product.
A mission critical product.  Net Express is both these for us.

I can not testify to it's internet capabilities, nor it's GUI capablilites,
nor alot of it's third party
data base capabilities.

But what I can tell you is the MEAT of it's COBOL services has been
thoroughly tested here.
That engine behind all those interfaces, the one which is the heart of your
product.
And in all that software, all working as advertised, the product has a very
consistent heartbeat.

Lately we have not been submitting FATAL errors to Micro Focus.  We haven't
had any for quite
a while.   We have been submitting wish list items which we feel make the
product easier to use and
more efficient.

I'm very glad they picked us as a beta site.  We had to be a BETA site.

COBOL is a very powerful language for which it is difficult to write a
decent compiler for.
It's not like C.  C has very little built in power.  C coders write their
own proceedures
and library them in via OOP.

COBOL has natural power built right into the language.  And because of that,
it's much
more difficult to maintain than C when refering to compilers.  But you will
never find more
maintainable source code than COBOL source code.  The more complex the
compiler,
the more work it does for the programmer, the more the programmer can
accomplish
in an 8 hour day.

I've been using Micro Focus products for around 10 years.  My background is
in insurance
processing.

The people I work with all have in excess of 20 years working in COBOL in
the insurance
industry.  A couple of whom have 30+ years.

COBOL standards in such a shop must be maintained.  And I think for the most
part
our crowd is satisfied.

I've worked for another major U.S. insurance processing company in the past.
They chose to drop COBOL in favor or Visual Basic for their next platform.

Standards just don't exist in other worlds.  Going from version 2.1 to 2.3
can be
a collossal catastophy in other platforms, C included.

But in Micro Focus's world of COBOL development I will tell you from
experience that
moving from one version to the next represented little challenge to us in
the past.
Occassionally you might have to re-code something, but it was rare.

I've recompiled hundreds and hundreds of cobol .exe's and .dll's using Net
express.
I've done it multiple times, each time we go to a new version in the BETA
since we
started the program.  Not one time was I forced to RE-code anything.  Not
one time.

I have a pentium II 450 mhz NT workstation.  I recompiled everything in our
shop, installed
the new runtime and was back up in just 6 hours.  That's a pile of running
software folks.

So I feel confident when I say that Net Express 3.0 is a good running
system.
I feel confident stating that Net Express is a platform which has MISSION
CRITICAL written
all over it.  How many of you have tried converting 35 years of Unisys V
code????
I mean we are using everything Net Express BASIC COBOL has to offer.
Virtually all
of their function calls.  Call's to strange third party pc software.  Call's
to data bases.
Math using every number system they support.  File I-O like you wouldn't
believe, locked or not.

But after all of this, I won't lie to you.  I too have a handfull of issues
they are still working on.
They are not fatal.  And I'm confident that Micro Focus will prevail in them
soon.

You must consider we have sent them dozens and dozens of issues, many fatal
in nature for
our situation.  Micro Focus has fixed them all.  We have NO fatals.

Net Express Version 3.0 is very solid.  I've very happy with it over all.
And the beta team in the U.K. has worked hard to address all the concerns of
all their beta clients from
around the world.

Now as you can see, most cobol programmers would probably have never used
the functionality I've
described in this message in their programs.  They would have no need to.
But I did and I'll say it all works.
And I want to point out that Net Express Version 2.0 worked well for most
"programmers".  It was their
comments I read when we opted to buy Net Express over Workbench; when we
moved from OS/2 to NT.
I am not implying that Net Express 2.0 is unusable to the general public in
any fashion what so ever.
What I am saying is in MY situation, Net Express 2.0 had some problems I
simply could not code around.
We tried but we simply couldn't do it.  And Micro Focus saw to it we were
included in their beta program
because they cared about our situation.  They realized we were an ideal beta
site to test the core of
the compiler and the run time.  And my heart pours out to them for their
months of support to me on this
project.

I have to give credit where credit is due.  Their support has been ""THERE""
with me since I started this
conversion some 2 years ago.  I've made alot of freinds along the way in
Micro Focus.  Support is critical
for developers.

I'm not trying to convince you to NOT buy Net Express version 2.0.  I
believe that 2.0 offeres
everything most shops would need.  If you take away the conversion
proprietary considerations
I'm mentioning, 2.0 was stable for use.  I seriously doubt most shops would
do the things we had to do.

I bought Net Express Version 2.0.  I also made sure we bought the
maintenance contract which
is offered with it.

Rather, I wanted to put something public to see if I can clear the air over
mainframe conversions
of COBOL code and their migrations to server platforms.  For us, 3.0 is
everything 2.0 was not.

Gentlemen!  It really CAN be done.
You really can take 35 year old COBOL code which has been running on a host
of mainframes for decades
and bring it to a NT server platform.

Net Express has the tools to make this possible.  Net Express is in my
opinion a 5 star compiler/run time system.

Their beta team has been responsive to my whinning!  After we got over the
fatals, the whinning from
the group started.  They have been making changes which are more framiliar
and acceptable to
older generations of COBOL developers and these suddle suggestions are
making my life brighter also.

If you NEED a very powerful and capable cobol compiler and run time then you
need Net Express.
I know I've never seen this much code running under a server at one time
sucessfully ever....

It's really very impressive.

It gives you a great respect for those who worked on Mainframes in the past.
And it gives you some hope for the future when you realize that you don't
have to throw thousands
of dollars of COBOL code away in favor of another language just to move to a
P.C.

I wanted to make this message a strong one in support of Net Express.  I
wanted to make this message
in such a fashion that people would believe in what I say.  I didn't want to
make it sound like a marketing
rep wrote it.  I hate marketing reps.

So many people put out messages which are negative based on their negative
experiences.
I felt it necessary to put out a positive message.  And in order to do that
you have to break a few eggs.

I think other's have been here with me in the past and know what I'm
refering to here.

My firm has made a positive impact on Net Express by filing their beta
reports and support reports thru
Micro Focus.  Micro Focus has molded these comments into working code.

And everybody will benefit in this.

I'm proud to be involved with the Net Express Version 3.0 beta and the Micro
Focus team.
And I will also bet you that if you read the comments of other BETA testers
working on various
beta's from around the country, no one else would have written as much about
their beta as I have.

And I will be happy when Version 3.0 is finally released.

I'm very pleased with the progress which has been made in this product over
the last few months.
We've come so far.  And the future for COBOL based systems looks very bright
from here.

Thank you Beta Team and by all means, buy the Net Express product.
If you need a mission critical product which is capable enough to handle the
tasks of mainframe compilers
then you really need this product.  If you intent to write sub OS systems,
do FEP communications,
talk to various strange sub products, do massive file I-O, and do it all at
a speed which is 50 times faster
than our old V, then buy this product.  If your organization has over 3000
packages which are 30 years
old in nature, spanning several ANSI COBOL generations in nature, HA HA
HA,,,
Do I need to ramble on here!!!!

Get your butt out there and try this thing.

I think they still offer a free 30 day test drive CD.
That's how I got hooked.

CU guys.

Charlie
