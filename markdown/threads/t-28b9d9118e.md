On May 14th in two different notes, Amir stated,

"My task is to explore the Language capabilities and report it.
I am testing different capabilities of the language to make my report."

and

"We are going to plan for migrate to IBM Mainframe environment.
So, We have to know the language to estimate better, I think."

  ***

As I have previously indicated, it is hard for me to believe that an 
organization that is large enough to already have an IBM mainframe (with a COBOL 
compiler) would assign such a task to someone with Amir's training and skill set 
(or lack therefore).  Of course it is possible that this assignment was made 
explicitly to PROVE that such a migration would be a "failure".)  It is also 
surprising (but not totally impossible) that an organization that already has an 
IBM mainframe with a COBOL compiler would be doing a NEW migration to such a 
platform.

HOWEVER,  I am willing to give him the benefit of the doubt as he has "stuck to 
his story" that this is what he is doing (and that he really has no one in the 
organization giving him this task to help him with COBOL or ISPF issues).

Therefore, this is a summary of what I think that someone with his skill-set 
should report back:

1) IBM mainframe COBOL has many if not all the capabilities of all the other 
languages that are available on that platform.  Therefore, if a migration to an 
IBM mainframe environment is expected, then the COBOL language is a reasonable 
choice.  If there is a question about migrating TO the IBM mainframe environment 
at all, then this does seem an "odd" (unusual) thing to be doing in this day and 
age.  One would need to know WHY this is being considered to give a reasonable 
answer as to whether it is a good idea or not.

2) Using IBM COBOL on the mainframe requires a LARGE number of skills.  These 
cannot be gained successfully in a piecemeal manner.  Either you need a full set 
of training or you need a large number of experienced programmers to assist 
"newbies". The skills needed include:
 - COBOL
 - JCL
 - Debugging (interactive and static) tool training

In addition, if development is to be done ON the mainframe itself, you should 
also have
 - ISPF
 - IBM utilities
 - SORT (either SyncSort or DFSORT training)
 - Additionally, you need to know what interactive and static debugging tool you 
will be using and have training for it (for example, IBM Debug Tool, Xpediter, 
Abend-Aid, DumpMaster, etc)
 - Many mainframe applications also use 3rd party tools such as SAS, EasyTrieve, 
etc

If the development of the mainframe targeted applications will be done on a PC, 
then you need to acquire current versions of appropriate (often expensive) tools 
such as
 - Micro Focus Mainframe Express
     or
 - IBM Rational Developer for zOS

In addition, most IBM mainframe applications use some or all  of the following 
which require additional training
 - CICS
 - IMS
 - DB2
 - MQSeries
Depending upon which of these are used, if development is planned to be done on 
the PC, then additional PC tools are needed.

If one wants to determine the CURRENT capabilities of IBM mainframe COBOL, you 
need a CURRENT IBM mainframe COBOL compilers.  Just some of the (not so recent 
but after IBM COBOL for OS/390 & VM) enhancements that are available include
 - new OO support
 - native XML support
 - COMP-5 (or native binary) support

   ***

I am certain that others in this group could (and probably will) add to the list 
of skills needed to successfully employ COBOL in an IBM zOS environment.  The 
real questions remain as to why this is being investigated and why one with NONE 
of the required existing skills has been assigned the task of doing the 
investigation.

I remain suspicious of the claims of the reason for Amir's posts and questions 
(and "investigation").  However, if he really was assigned this task with 
minimal or no internal support, then the major thing he should report back is 
that it DOES take training, knowledge, etc to successfully migrate to an IBM 
mainframe COBOL environment.

I've watched this thread with passing interest and seen the frustration with 
Amir growing.

I'm not sure it is entirely fair to call someone an "idiot" when English is 
not their first language, and they are trying to grapple with something that 
is completely new and foreign to them, but it is also frustrating to try and 
help someone and then see they don't take your advice. (I personally believe 
this was because of lack of understanding and miscommunication, not 
intransigence on Amir's part...) Having said that, good advice was provided,

tools that are "taken as read" by people on mainframe sites.

Just because someone HAS a copy of ISPF doesn't mean they are adept with 
it...

Just because a reference is provided to an IBM text, doesn't mean a 
non-English speaker will waltz through it and enjoy the experience. (I speak 
English as a first language and I HATE reading computer reference 
manuals...IBM are not the worst, but they are still terminally boring.)

For people who have a completely different background, the world of 
mainframe COBOL is bewildering and foreign.

If you have used tools like Eclipse and Visual Studio, for example, ISPF 
seems completely unintuitive. I can see why he had problems with things 
starting in certain columns.

Most people here take the 80-column punched card image that COBOL was built 
upon, for granted. It isn't reasonable to expect other people from a more 
modern background to do so. Certainly, they will have to if they intend to 
use mainframe COBOL, and you quite properly pointed out that training is 
required for this (as for any...) environment.
