There was a time here a while back that someone - can't recall who, it
might have been Mr Dilworth or Mr Wagner - expressed disdain for banks
because they were 'addicted to float' on checks.

Well... I'm trying to move into the 20th century. This entails setting
aside the goosefeather quill, eyeshade and arm-garters and dealing with an
online checking account. Something appeared that piqued my interest.

(those of parochial mind could need notifying that the following details
are about the United States of America)

When dealing with physical instruments (negotiated orders of withdrawal,
checks (what the Brits like to call 'cheques') and others) there was a law
passed mandating clearing-times across the country. A local-to-local
transaction was one day, a larger area, three and across the country was a
maximum of five days... or something like that.

This newfangled system does something else. I haven't yet been inclined
to trace transactions but it seems that even for a local transaction there
is a two-day lag. For example: I log on to the system on Tuesday at 10:am
and make a payment to a local firm. My bank posts a Transaction Date of
Tuesday and a Completion Date of Thursday.

In the Oldene Dayse were I to have stopped by a local firm and handed them
a check they could have deposited it before 3:pm and had the funds
available on the next business day ('overnight clearing').

This... is Progress!

DD

There are two things in play here Doc when it comes to "float". First
there are the Fed regulations concerning how long before a foreign
(not on us) check deposit must be made available to the customer.
Secondly, there is bank policy concerning the availability of those
funds and what they can be used for. With "float" the maximum number
of days a bank can withhold the availability of those funds is 7 days.
Generally speaking, most banks only go to 3 days. However, they can
also define certain foreign (again, no on us) deposits as Uncollected
Funds and can with hold availability up to 12 days. Add to that the
fact that a teller can put a "teller hold" on a deposit for as many
days as they wish and those funds will not be available.

Without getting into a whole discourse on banking and available funds,
know that banks will have one formula for funds available for
withdrawal, funds used to calculate interest should they pay interest
on the checking account; balances used in calculating OD or NSF fees
and, in some cases, uncollected funds fees, i.e., trying to draw
against uncollected funds - once commonly called kiting.

You may now see via the Internet your balances and most banks show
available balance. This is what you can draw against, so they tell
you. Present balance is your balance with everything cleared. Most
banks memo post ACH transactions twice a day, morning and late
evening. In most banks those transactions will show as "pending" and
their transaction amount, in the case of a credit, is added into
available. However, I also know that if you have a paycheck, e.g.,
post via ACH in the morning and you try and withdraw a significant
part of it in cash or certified check, the bank will not make those
funds available until it has cleared their nightly processing.

Hope that answers your questions.
