The policy of 'one license=one user only' makes sense for end-user software,
but not for software which itself is used to develop other software.

My favorite - and one about which I have written the publisher (on real
paper!) - is the Mercator Software mapping product.

Mercator is a very fine product, but at $10K up front and $2K/year
maintenance for a PC product, it is beyond comprehension that (if you follow
the letter of the license) you may not have a development machine and a
production machine simultaneously running the software, even if the
'production' machine uses only the run-time support module to execute the
maps you compile on the development machine.

I am an unabashed fan of the Mercator product, but trying to develop new or
modified Mercator components USING THE PRODUCTION SYSTEM is about the
dumbest thing one could possibly do, what with PC prices what they are; not
to mention the term 'production machine' suggests a Real User might actually
be using the equipment and software during business hours.

Seems to me any publisher marketing development software utilizing a
runtime - which includes many COBOL and other language compilers - would be
well-advised to adopt an 'out of the box' policy of one development license
= one license for the development part of the product plus two runtimes: one
for the developer and one for a non-developer (production) user. Second
license for a second developer? No problem.  Extra 'run-time only' licenses
for additional 'run-time only' users? No problem.

I've often expressed concern that developers of compilers and other tools
live in Fantasyland, and really do not have a clue what we applications
developers - their customers - face out here in The Real World. These
tunnel-vision marketing policies do nothing to disabuse me of that notion.

so tough it is a case of "charge whatever the traffic will bear" for some
vendors.

really a corporation (or even a small business) they groan because they
believe (rightly or wrongly...) that they could've extracted more... On the
other hand if they give you a price which is too high they will lose your
business. Have you tried making an offer? Like, "I don't want to pay more

the price is. (They must know because your offer was unacceptable...).

times and achieved a good discount. "Well, I guess I could a get a copy from

can do?"

This is obviously one of my hot buttons, and since I think I am beginning
(again) to ramble, I'll stop now.
