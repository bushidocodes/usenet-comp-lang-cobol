Steve, I'm not sure if you ever saw this letter that I sent to the ANSI
and
ISO COBOL distribution about 3 weeks ago. It stirred up a lot of debate
and I
expect some animosity. I meant no disrespect for any of the people who
give
so much of their time and efforts to produce a fine COBOL Standard.
After
all, that is the one thing all of us share an interest in - the
betterment of
the COBOL language. We all fine our own ways to work towards that goal.
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~


"...what goes around, comes around."
Fourteen years ago, at an International COBOL committee meeting in
Vienna,
(ISO WG4, formerly the COBOL Experts Group) work was being finalized on
what
was to become COBOL 85. Although work on this new revision began in
1979 (the
cut-off date of new features to be considered), the new COBOL standard
was
delayed, eventually released as COBOL 85. The delay was caused by a
public
outrage over (incorrectly identified) COBOL Standard incompatibilities
with
the previous COBOL standard. This outpouring of public comments to the
X3J4
committee was led by Joseph Brophey of Travelers Insurance. This
"people's
revolt" and the technical debate that followed caused the standard to be
delayed - by as much some believe as 4 years . At the Vienna COBOL
meeting, I
expressed the frustration shared by many over the slow pace of COBOL
standardization. It was noted then that the "official" ANSI and ISO
procedures needed to be changed dramatically to be more responsive to
our
industry's fast changing pace and our constituents' needs. To not do so
would
eventually cause the COBOL language to simply become irrelevant in the
application development community. Randall Bart and Tim Oxler, in their
recent correspondence, articulately express a similar sentiment. At
that
Vienna meeting in 1984, I proposed a way to deal with this problem -
the
"Addendum Process" (later called the "amendment process"). The purpose
of
the Addendum Process was (is) to introduce new features (current
state-of-the-
art, not 10-year-old-state-of-the-art) into all of our language
standards,
COBOL et al. much more frequently than we were (and still are) doing.
It was
hoped that significant new features would be added to the COBOL language
perhaps every 2 - 3 years. A full revision of COBOL (including
corrections)
would be published every 10 years perhaps. The American delegation
(ANSI
X3J4) whose mission would be to define and execute these new procedures
was
the least supportive of adopting the new process. While the
International
COBOL Committee did indeed adopt the Addendum process, it did so
reluctantly.
After many arguments and criticisms within the X3J4 committee during the
following months (years), the Addenda process, as defined by ANSI and
ISO
SC22, failed to bring about the faster development of COBOL as hoped;
instead
it caused the standardization process to become even more cumbersome and
more
bureaucratic. In retrospect, the problem was that the Addendum
process was
left to be defined and implemented by the ANSI and ISO executive
committees.
These were the very same people who were responsible for the
then-current
standardization procedures. They eventually made the "new" procedures
simply
an extenstion (a disguised variation) of the "old" procedures. The
concept
and objective of the Addendum process were eventually abandoned, along
with
the Addendum process itself (...the baby along with the bath water) .

Has anyone ever considered that just maybe, the slow pace that the COBOL
language evolves under current ISO and ANSI procedures, adds greatly to
its
oft-sited public image (misconception) of being "stogey, old fashion and
irrelevant." Those of us who know the current (and about-to-be) COBOL
standard are aware that COBOL is as modern and as functional and as
relevant
to modern application development (mainframe and PC alike) as any other
application development language; in most cases it is more relevant .
The
bureaucratic procedures under which we operate today to create new
"official"
versions of the COBOL standard, are antiquated, irrelevant and simply do
not
serve the public's (or COBOL's) needs any longer. It is instead causing
its
demise. The problem friends is the "PROCESS." Surely, COBOL cannot be
the
only application development language suffering this fate. I expect
that most
of the ANSI X3J committees (and perhaps many of the ISO SC22 Language
committees as well) are experiencing similar frustrations. It causes
many to
conclude that language "standards" are best developed outside of
"official
circles." Those of us who are an intrinsic part of "the process" know
the
consequences of having no standards or multiple "standards" or even
vendor-
controlled standards. I suspect that that is why most of us care
enough to
participate in standardization. But, when the process becomes
"perverted," -
when it begins to harm the development of the technology that it was
created
to serve, promote and nourish, thereby harming those who rely on the
technology - than, to paraphrase an earlier document dealing with
similar
circumstances, it is time to alter and abolish the bureaucracy
overseeing
this process, along with its rules and procedures, and institute a new
system
of standardization, being ever mindful of the lessons learned under the
old
system.
The dilemma we face today is that the very same bureaucracy that
oversees the
current standardization system has neither the incentive nor the courage
to
make the necessary changes.

Respectfully,

Jerome Garfunkel

Let's look at the communications technology then.

1968 to 1974 six years:
Snail mail the backbone of communications. Very expensive LD phone
calls. Transcontinental LD phone cumbersome and extremely expensive.
Air travel very expensive. Age of the typewriter and carbon paper.
New fangled "factsimile" technology emerging.
Sumation: Replies and answers could take weeks.

1974 to 1985 eleven years:
Overnight mail/package delivery, much cheaper air travel,
transcontinental LD phone still extremely expensive, but not
cumbersome. The fax machine gaining acceptance. The age of word
processing, starting with Wang office automation, and then on to the
PC.
Sumation: Replies and answers could take days

1985 to 2001 16 years:
Dirt cheap LD phone calls. Much cheaper transcontinental LD phone.
Email, internet, intranet, client/servers connecting office desktops
to all computer platforms, and internet, and office intranet. OCR
technology, world wide web, etc.
Sumation: Replies and answers could take minutes.

with people who can get some actual work done!!

I would like to add my name to those in favor of a change in the way the
COBOL SPEC is derived.

Working on new material, and publishing a standard do not compute.

COBOL enhancement has always come through the road of implementation of
a spec with interpretations by vendors, and reviews by their customers,
and other vendors customers. When the developed spec reaches
a state of "agreement" among the users, and vendors, a new standard can
be proposed. That proposal should in My Opinion NOT be subject to X3J4
further tinkering except in smoothing out the agreements already made.

Grabbed new features that appeal to members of X3J4 or others in the
standardization stream should NOT occur unless user groups have been
polled, and the need is accepted by them first. User groups meet (or did
when I was a pup) every year. User groups in the past were willing to
put them selves out to participate in things that meant something to
them.

It seems to me that the current combination of vendors, and others who
can afford to spend the time in the standardization process do not
represent the users as much as vendors of hardware and certain software
products.

When it was decided to go for the standardization process the government
testing system was in place, and contracts provided encouragement to
vendors to pass these tests. With that in mind the standardization
process was thought of as like the standardization of light bulbs. Many
vendors made bulbs that fit a given purpose and fixture. They got
together, and said to the world, if you want to make light bulbs, they
must look like this if you want them to fit the problem these standards
are meant to solve.

At first the adoption of minimum, and other levels of COBOL were
supposed to provide different "sizes" for different needs, but that did
not translate. Programing needs expand to fit the available hardware,
and imagination of systems designs.

So, we need to recognize this, and publish a spec, then standardize
again. We need to find some dedicated organizations who will assume the
responsibility. Such an organization could look at the current Y2000
spec, and say to the world, that's the new spec. In a few years, it will
be standardized using the feedback we get.

Warren Simmons
P.S. Maybe someone could put together a petition, circulate it among
users world wide, and send it to ANSI, and ISO, etc.

I hope that most of the readers of this thread accept the fact that nothing
we/you do is going to have an immediate impact. I do, however, have a
suggestion that MIGHT have a slightly faster impact.

Joining the J4 Standards committee takes time, money, and effort. HOWEVER,
the J4 meetings are always fully open. This means that anyone can come -
either to the full meeting or to days/parts of a meeting. Furthermore,
although there are some "theoretical restrictions," in reality, J4 is
usually pretty interested in the input of any guest who is there
participating to the extent that the guest wants to (comments, suggestions,
etc). There are, almost at all meetings, at least 1 or more "guest" and
when votes are taken, the votes are done twice - once with everyone in the
room and once with just the members (and both votes are recorded in the
minutes). Furthermore, the J4 meetings (about every other month) move around
the country so there are probably meetings scheduled for relatively close to
any reader (US only - sorry about that) some time in the next 6 months.

Therefore, if you are interested in getting input to the committee (and/or
in seeing how things are progressed/delayed), you should think of visiting a
meeting near you.

To get a current schedule, you can always contact the chair at
d.··.@mic··s.com.
