Gotodepending uses, as you know, a numeric index. Few, if any, problems 
*naturally* generate a numeric quantity suitable as such an index. In all 
other cases, you have to transform a series of Boolean expressions into 
appropriate index values. Thus, when you use gotodepending you have 
introduced a conceptual level of masking and indirection that is 
unnecessary. Moreover, you may have spatially separated the boolean 
conditions from their use. And if the gotodepending variable accidentally 
gets used for some other purpose, you are going to have a very nasty bug to 
resolve, one that can express itself in several ways.

It is far simpler and far easier to understand a program that directly 
references the boolean expressions in its conditional branching.

Class exercise: think of three distinct situations in which numeric 
quantities suitable for use in a gotodepending sentence arise naturally.

For you and for me, and for bunches of people.  But not for every one.  If you
abstract a text editor to the enth degree you can render the entire thing into
an array of procedure addresses that are indexed by the input key.

((Pardon my simplistic expression of that fact, I gloss a tad there, actually,

Such an abstracted text editor could then be infinity configurable by the user

Under this circumstance the mainline can not possibly name the procedures to be

And so the distinction I am after is a difference in level of abstraction.
Mathematical people tend to abstract more deeply.  Mathematical people tend to
abstract a larger portion of their world.

The way this relates to modern data processing is that the net represents a
truely massive explosion of available information that can be processed for a
gain.  If our procedures attempt to enumerate all references linguistically,
the text refering to that information explosion will be atleast as large as the
explosion itself, which seem to be a formula for unprofitable processing.

By profit I mean more than just costs in relation to revenues, which is very
important.  I emphasize that profit involves minimizing risk.

So, as magnificent a rabit as it might seem to come from such an unpresupposing
hat, a PERFORM DEPENDING ON construct could be a major new avenue of growth in
applicable problem domain for COBOL.

PERFORM DEPENDING ON in simple syntax could be harnessed to an integer.  To
help people learn what method selection is really all about in the first
instance.  But we could certainly harness it to more interesting engines.

Obviously it would be reasonable to consider strapping this syntax onto a table
of procedure names listed in the data division, which table could, of course be
altered, (and I used that word on purpose).  

We could also wire this construct onto a two dimmensional table, the first
element could be text for those who like it litteral, and the second element
the name of the procedure to PERFORM when the text is found.
(We could have a fun attribute to tell the compiler to worry or not about
case).

We could tack the syntax onto a table that had 88 levels in column one, and
procedure names in column two.

COBOLers can definitely understand tables.  The weakness in GOTO DEPENDING ON
is the lack of structure.  PERFORM DEPENDING ON would fit modern style readily.

The truth is CICS uses the HANDLE CONDITION table of vectors to do what the
postor prefers. For when a coder decides to  "...  let the program abend  ..."

back to us then out.

The postor is exactly right that the defaults are appropriate in many cases.
But that doesn't avoid computed GOTO logic at all. 

More and more people are coming to agree with Frank Swarbrick  that, if you do
override by means of EXEC CICS statement, it is preferable to do NOHANDLE and
handle it directly (that is don't mix GOTO logic into your innocent structured
COBOL program).

I apologize to those who are not into CICS, some of the above is almost

But vectoring to procedures is not unstructured in any way at all.

The CICS HANDLE logic does use a table of vectors.  When the condition happens
it jumps (hope you have your parachute) directly to the address that your COBOL
or ASSEMBLER program has set for that condition that last time it was set. 

Handles remain set.  Invoking a call successfully does not reset a handle.
Setting any other handles also does not reset a handle that is not mentioned.
So sometimes a down stream call will encounter an unexpected condition and CICS
will vecotr to some previous handle address; from there who knows what will
happen.

At the very least you get a confusing error message that sends you off
analyzing code that was successful and possibly long past.


The vendor now discourages use of HANDLE technology. Although the technique
works quite reliably, it is very powerful and can snow even an experienced
programmer. (Direct management of RESP and RESP2 are recommended now,
strongly).

In the modern era, it is certainly true that the HANDLE CONDITION table is a
GOTO syntax that mixes poorly with perform syntax.

It is, however technically, wrong to characterize vector tables as
unstructured.

The computed goto (in COBOL GO TO DEPENDING ON), is a miniature facility that
delivers an easily indexed array of program vectors to the coder of a procedure
division.

In languages like C you have robust facilities for this: multi-dimensional
matrices of function pointers.  The key to using this kind of technology is to
think in terms of indexes. Most COBOLers do not think that way.

There was even a recent post in this newsgroup _complaining_ about a programmer
who had asperations for the legal profession AND liked indexes. (Well of all
things!) The sentiment expressed there (even though mild) records a simple
fact. People who map their program environment mathematically do not mix well
with people who map the environment linguistically.

And, yes, it would be very difficult to nail down those terms. But I think most
readers will know what I am talking about. Proportionately speaking there are
fewer geeks into COBOL than say UNIX C/C++ kernel writing.

Linguistic people fit COBOL business world requirements well. And many of them
can not really fathom what the HANDLE CONDITION vector table is. (Not all!
Don't get upset with that opinion. For example, the 
original poster had it exactly).

It is just that we chase the egg-heads out, and still our COBOL world functions
smoothly.  It must be that the problem domain just does not demand dispatcher
logic coding structures very often. Atleast not in the past.

All the same, I feel confident that if we just had a computed _perform_,
everyone and her uncle would be out thar' codin' 'em.  As in, ...

PERFORM 
  DEPENDING ON 
      OBJECT-MANAGER-INDEX
          PAGE-UP-PROC
          PAGE-DOWN-PROC
          PF4-OUT-A-HERE-PROC
          ENTRY-VALIDATION-PROC
          CLS-PROC
    OTHER
          MAJOR-CONCERN-PROC
END-PERFORM.

Off hand, I see no reason why such syntax would preclude alternation between
'procedure-name' and 'procedure-name thru proc-exit', for those that are hooked
on screen consumption. 

Control vectors are the quintessence of object oriented programming. If the
COBOL language and its practitioners do not make room for it, then it's going
to continue to be coded in other languages.

NOTE: this IS a recommendation to use this structure, to keep it in the
language and to grow it.

Because Robert waxed so well on this subject, a little history may
be useful.

Most of the people who first put the spec of COBOL on paper where
UNIVAC I programmers. This means they were used to having only
1000 twelve character words of memory for I/O buffers, and program.
Overlay (segmentation) was common.  Programming was done i machine
language based upon the letters of the alphabet. A = add, etc.
Addresses ranged from 000 to 999.  R00100U00120 represented a perform.
A Go to was 00000U00nnn.  We were taught to modify instructions to
achieve our goals. It was easy to plug-in a number in the nnn portion of
the skip U instruction or any other instruction followed by a U
instruction, the number of the address one needed to go to. Remember
go to had not been invented.

When COBOL was invented, the Go to Depending on was added to allow
the generation of this kind of code.

Now, the part I consider funny about the whole thing is that Univac
's first compiler decided that no one would want to go to more than 26
paragraphs so that was a given limitation. Initial usage by USERS
showed that address modification needed to be more than 26.  I remember
beating on someone's desk to make this point.

Now, I agree with Robert that those who are not used to it may find this
a difficult concept. Yet, I read that "pointers" are among the features
of the working new standard. With pointers, one could code IDS if there
is a good randomizer. I may be off the deep
end here, but I wanted to share this history to place it into
perspective.

Warren Simmons

If you find yourself writing access functions for all of an object's
internal fields, then at least one of two things is probably 
happening:

1. You are abusing the class by violating its encapsulation;

2. You are trying to apply the OO paradigm to an area where it is
   not a good fit.

Much of the point of wrapping something up in an object is to hide
its internals -- first so that the client code doesn't have to be
concerned with the internals, and secondly so that you can change
the implementation of the internals without changing the client code.

If you don't have anything to hide, then you might as well use an
ordinary group item, leaving its component parts defined in a copybook
and open to the public.  You can still put procedural code in a 
subprogram, using different entry points to take the place of methods.

This approach some of the advantages of true object orientation but is 
more flexible than IBM's version of OO COBOL (which I think is what Ken 
Foskey is using.)  In particular, it lets you declare per-class data
as well as per-instance data without breaking encapsulation.

On the other hand: if you want to use inheritance and polymorphism,
there's no very easy way to fake it.  Define your base class, swallow
hard, and write a bunch of access routines.

Donald,

I had an expert that told me that the road to C++ is by a series of
steps.

1.  Better C (C++ has stronger type checking and finds bugs)

2.  Extended C, C++ has some output functions called streams that are
really easy to use and extending them is a breeze.

3.  Basic C++, some minor object defined, say currency class.

...

You can see that this is basically the path that many people take into
object oriented.  Why take the huge risk when a small one will suffice.

This leads into a thought I had today of the different level of reuse.

Code reuse no 1:

300-validate-date
	if jan
	   if days > 31
		error
	if feb
	   if days > 28
		error

400-validate-date2
	if jan
	   if days > 31
		error
	if feb
	   if days > 28 

* The leap year error must be corrected in as many places as it has been
copied.
* major changes to date logic requires major changes with this type of
logic (welcome to Y2K)
* multiple copies of the code are compiled into the program

Code reuse No 2:

300-validate-date
	copy validate replace ==datevar== with ==checkthis==.

400-validate-date2
	copy validate replace ==datevar== with ==checkalso==.

* The leap year error must be corrected once.
* lends itself to very simple code not complex business logic.
* multiple copies of the code are compiled into the program

Code reuse no 3:

300-validate-date
	move checkthis to validate-datein.
	call 'validate' with ws-validate.
	move validate-message to display-message.
	if return code not = zero
		error

400-   ditto above...

* lends itself to complex business logic.
* one copy of the code are compiled into the program.
* moves for every call creates an move overhead and extra working
storage.

Code reuse no 4:

300-validate-date

	call 'validate' with checkthis, display-message.
	if return code not = zero
		error

400-   ditto above...

* lends itself to complex business logic.
* one copy of the code are compiled into the program.

Code reuse no 5:

OK folks now dates are to be handled in reverse order yyyy-mm-dd.  The
code buried in the program assumes the date format.  We correct this
with objects.  I don't know the syntax so I will borrow from C++;

create date-field( day, month, year ).

display date-field->error.

The concept of a date is carefully encapsulated.

Lets not take date as an example but the Student number of University. 
A university had a quote of 1.5 Million to change it's number from 8
digits to 9 digits.

If all these fields were stored as objects the representations of these
would only need adjustment where it caused a problem (more than 80 bytes
on a screen after the increase for example).

This means that in order to work properly you would say:

	create a screen.
	attach fields to screen.
	display screen.
	validate cross reference between fields.
	update data to ??.

Each field would know what it was and know how to ask the screen to get
it.

This is utopia!

Can you imagine writing a generic date routine that updated on the
screen correctly whether it was run in MS windows or IBM CICS.  Only the
screen logic should know about this, what really happens is the screen
stuff gets imbedded into the actual class causing a very blurred model.

Yes you can use inheritance here to hide some of this implementation but
fundamentally it is still really a problem you are not writing a clean
interface between the screen and the field.

------------

I am surprised that reuse 3 is favored over reuse 4 in a lot shops. 
There are some very good reasons why 4 is better.  The reasoning I
generally get reuse 3 is that it is easier to understand.  Opinions
differ :->

Just trying to set some benchmarks of 'reuse' use for our discussions,
any corrections or extensions.

Anyway, encapsulation is an idea that is great, but it is like a hammer; you
can over do it with an implement like that.

Encapsulation is similar to modularization, somewhat.

Modularization is usually explained as breaking things into pieces.  But the
science of modularization is the theory of cohesion, good and bad cohesion.

Encapsulation is usually explained as putting everything so tightly together
that it becomes a blob of a beauty that of course successfully hides everything
from the outside world, and for ever hold your pieces in there.

Actually, encapsulation is a notion advocated by those who would have us place
our functions and our data inside of something.  It has got to be a mysterious
something or folks would not get paid to cobble these wonderments together so
we have all conspired to call them objects. How very impressive :})  (that is a
bewiskered smiley incase ya don't recognize it, it connotes wisdom ya kno').
(It is pronounced Hmm?!)

An encapsulated collection of stuff is exactly what a program has always been. 
Functions, data, logical views of data, all 'hidden' under one name. That name
can be triggered by messages, and offers responses. But don't call this a
program anymore, its more lucrative to call it an object.

Modularization was a great idea when it came along.  Programs used to be much
larger than they are now. The basic idea of separating input from process from
output, can't be wrong-headed; in leads to much more clear expressions.

But when a _FIELD_ is modularized, then someone is stealing (atleast most of
the time). If moving into a field, moving out of a field, adding to a field,
indeed every kind of reference to a field is a user written function, then the
code has mushroomed.  It is inconceivable that excessive application of object
oriented methods to program elements can produce more reliable systems.

Object Orientation is a good way to design, especially at a high level. But the
argument of reusability of code I find a dubious portion of the Object Oriented
religion.

Real production systems are huge, and after the major development cycle the
ones knowledgable about the details leave the work area. Reusability depends on
comprehension of the components. In mature appplications that comprehension
literally is nowhere to be found.

Object Orientaion can organize peoples thinking, but there is a need to
moderate its application prior to the detail level. With current industry
staffing practices the breaks are rarely applied, because there are no contrary
views to balance the predilections of the encapsulators.

The monthly processing created multiple tapes containing print
lines for several reports. The records were all 133 characters.
The first position contained a character for spacing of the print
lines. The remaining 132 characters contained the print line
itself.

The output tapes were sent to remote locatons for printing.
Each tape was formatted with 256 character blocks. During
the processing, the printer spacing character from the input
tape was converted to control characters for the printer used
at the remote locations.

The original program processed the input one character at a
time. First processing the spacing character then copying the
print line to the output block until the block was filled. It would
then write the block then start on a new block. Each output
tape was limited to no more than 10 000 blocks because the
first tape to each location was sent using a modem.

The program I wrote used a 512 character work area. The
work area was redefined into 256 elementary items of 132
characters each, with an offset of one character for each
redefines This allowed me to copy the print lines into the work
area with one move rather than character by character. To get
to the proper move statement, I used a GO TO DEPENDING
on the destination in the work area. This starting location was
between 1 and 256. Each paragraph that was a destination for
the GO contained the paragraph-name-xxx, a MOVE print-line
TO work-area-xxx, and a GO to a common paragraph which
added 132 to the output location, wrote the output record, etc.

small COBOL program to punch all of the cards in the redefines
and in the body of the loop replacing xxx with the correct
number. I then inserted the cards for the rest of the program.

The previous program created one tape every 45 minutes.
The replacement program created one tape every 4 minutes
but the rewind time explains the "only" 9:1 improvement.

I later rewrote the program in Assembler F using FEOV
to use two output drives. The time dropped to 3:45 per tape
with no rewind delay. The times were wall clock times. The
machine was a 360/40 DOS 26.2.

The Fortran IF statement existed because some old computer (IBM 1401 comes
to mind) had an operator which did the same thing.

Also, in the 1950 programmers used to generate branch tables by adding a
variable to an address.  It was feared that higher level languages would
be too inefficient if programmers lost the branch table, so it was
implemented in Fortran and Cobol.

IIRC, the Fortran statement looked like this:

    GOTO (10, 20, 50), K

Omission of the last comma was a very common source of syntax errors.

I wouldn't use GO TO DEPENDING in a new code, but I recall one that would
have been hard to remove.  The Burroughs Thrift System was designed for a
service bureau serving multiple banks.  The dumb terminals sent a very
short transaction, which contained an account type, a transaction type,
and a modifier.  For each bank, there was a table indexed by these three
variables, which yielded a two digit number.  There was then a large GO TO
DEPENDING which directed the transaction to the correct routine.  Thus
account type 2, tran type 1, modifier 1 might map to routine 12 for bank
#1 yet map to routine 15 for bank #2.  Then when bank #2 decides they also
want to run routine 12 we might define account type 2, tran type 1,
modifier 5 to routine 12.

This would likely be implemented completely differently today, but in it's
time GO TO DEPENDING was a sensible way to do this.
