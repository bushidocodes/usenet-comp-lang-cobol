### Fortress COBOL - some conclusions

First up, I'd like to thank everyone who posted to the thread. (Especially
those who managed to remain on topic for more than 50 milliseconds <G>).

I believe it is possible to at least get some insight into what causes
"Fortress COBOL", why it manifests itself, and why it is continuing to do
so. The posts to the thread and to CLC in general have been helpful in this
regard.

Here's what I see...(If you feel that these generalizations do not apply to
you, that's good.)

1. The general morale of mainframe people is low.

They feel they are under attack and watch in dismay as their jobs are moved
off-shore. Less and less development work is being placed on the mainframe,
the people who work in this area are perceived as "dinosaurs", and much of
the real value of mainframe implementation is either ignored or dismissed as
"mainframe bashing". There is little or no acknowledgement of the value of
mainframes and mainframers, by management, or colleagues who work in
Client/Server.

(I was surprised by the defensiveness exhibited. I made a tongue-in-cheek
comment about response times under TSO and it prompted a number of serious
posts defending something that didn't need to be defended...It was a joke,
but it seems people are losing their sense of humour...)

2. Management are perceived to be not interested in furthering the careers
of mainframe people.

Training is not being implemented for mainframers. But the same seems not to
be true for Client/Server people who mostly get what they need. This is part
of the management perception that they need this technology only until such
time as it can be replaced.

3. There is little new or "sexy" happening in the mainframe arena. (Note:
moves to Linux and Java, although technically mainframe activities, are
viewed with suspicion by many.) Nobody knows what Websphere is
about...<G>.(We haven't had a single thread on this in CLC...)

4. Maybe as a result of the general low morale, there is a high degree of
inertia being exhibited by many mainframers. Motivation to expand the skill
set has never been more necessary than at the moment, yet the majority are
doing nothing about increasing their value. Some see this as the
responsibility of their Management (it certainly was in the "old" days),
others feel that the job ends at 5pm (as long as they still have a job...).
On the other hand, there are some who are doing everything they can, even
though they may be past the mid point of their careers, to acquire new
skills and try to improve their value. Sadly, they are a minority.

5. Legacy Management is perceived as a "dead end" management role and the
bright young managers don't want to be there. The ones who are there tend to
be doing a holding action. Keep the costs right down, keep the machines
running until we can replace them. Investment in this area cannot be
justified, but don't tell the staff that.... (We have seen many examples of
short-sighted management action posted in CLC...usually from the point of
view of the people affected by it and not specifically as an example of bad
management, but easily interpolated to be such.)

Given all of the above, it is hardly surprising that people dig themselves
into entrenched positions. The result is the "Fortress", where change is
fiercely resisted and anything that challenges the Status Quo is attacked
overtly and covertly.

So what is the outlook? Can the war be won? Can the Fortress withstand the
tides of change sweeping through the industry?

In a word: No.

The computer boom is over. (It started ending in the 1980s). The days when
experts were required to get computers to do anything are finished. The
population is becoming more and more computer literate. Kids are growing up
with computer technology that they take for granted just like all the other
technology of the 21st century. The veils of mystery have been ripped away
and what was once a secret arcane art is now the preserve of spotty
adolescents.

There is no longer a secure job for life in being possessed of some
specialist knowledge (like, how to write COBOL).

(If it's any consolation, there isn't in knowing how to write any procedural
language. There might not be in anything computer related...or maybe the
days of secure jobs for life are just gone forever, no matter what you do
(maybe brain or heart surgery, or medicine in general has a future, but
computing certainly doesn't have the future it had in the 1960s and 70s...))

But that doesn't mean that we should all chuck it in <G>.

It simply means an adjustment to our expectations.

The factors that nurture the Fortress mentality are going to be with us for
some time (about 12 years, I reckon <G>) so individuals may want to think
about this and see what they can personally do to improve their own
situation. Get trained, get more strings to your bow, and get out if you are
going nowhere or the company you are with is simply "waiting to migrate".

Personally, I'm considering a career change...looked at Bank Robbing but I
can't run fast enough and I never liked firearms even when I was in the
army... (noisy, dangerous, things...).

I'm a bit too old for "Would you like fries with that?" so I am coming to
the conclusion that the only safe option for me is a rich widow or spinster.

Anyone got a sister on the shelf with a trust fund?

Pete.

I think this IS happening, but the readers of this forum are more focussed
on COBOL.

Let's face it, COBOL is a very easy skill to acquire and is ideally suited
for anyone of average intelligence, who speaks English, and is capable of
logical thought.

I have trained many COBOL programmers in my career, usually run a two week
class that gets them off the ground then get them working with an
experienced programmer for 6 months and they're away... The majority had
never considered "computer programming" as a career.

I think the more modern skills are not so immediately intuitive as COBOL and
it will therefore take a bit longer before this filters through to the
sub-continent. Nevertheless, the academic institutions there will be
teaching computer science as we speak, and I'm sure there are many C++, VB,
Java, and Web-skilled people just waiting for contracts.

The other important point here is that most of these languages never went
through the COBOL boom  up to the 80s. Expectations are not so high. (Young
programmers in the West, just out of college, are not expecting to make $60
an hour, even using the latest tools and languages.)

Companies in the West are also going to be more carfeul about letting
perceived "cutting edge" technology go off-shore. (This is really stupid but
it comes down to perception at management level, not necessarily technical
reality.) Until the in-house IT shops stop doing development (and I mean
writing applications from the ground up, as has been traditionally done),
this fear will remain.

As "development" comes to mean package configuration, component juggling,
and managing out-sourced requirements, there will be less aversion to it
being done off-shore or anywhere else. As the "development" responsibility
and capability slowly devolves back to the individual User Departments,
there will be less need to go outside the Company, let alone off-shore.

The "Asian Programming Boom" may not be of long duration. It can only last
as long as the need for "programming" lasts.

Nope. I don't get any more emotional about numbers than I do about
Programming Languages.

The 12 levels in my code go back to the days of punch cards. If cards got
minced in the reader (and they did quite often) the girls would have to
rebuild the source deck from a listing.  Lineflo was not always clear, and
it certainly gave no indication of what columns things were in. It helped
them if the level number showed what column they should start the line in.
Hence, start in column 12 and indent 3 columns from there, so 15, 18, 21,
etc. They used to punch up a card with "tabs" on it so they could tab to the
various columns for a COBOL listing. The fact was that punching COBOL was
not part of their normal jobs and so they wouldn't have a standard "tab temp
late" for it.  They all knew that 01 levels were in column 8 because we
wrote that on the listing.  Over the years, I found that this numbering
scheme is a very useful one for COBOL, and now I do it as a kind of respect
to the people I worked with then who taught me so much, and also from force
of habit. There is no deep significance to it <G>.
I have seen several other people use this scheme as well. I figure they are
old-timers like me...

As for the twelve years ... that would bring us to 2015. I am on record as
predicting that by then COBOL will not be a generally used programming
language and "computer programming" as we understood it for the decades from
1960 to 2000 will not be around. This is based on trends which started
emerging in the early-mid 90s. I am also expecting at least one major change
to the way we develop commercial software within the next 10 years.
Actually, the closing down of "in-house" COBOL computer programming is
running pretty close to this schedule. But it is not life and death. If I
get it wrong I'll be embarrassed (and deserve to be)...

So there is nothing special about "12" (at least, not in my universe; the
number has various mystical associations in other universes, which I won't
go into here...) and two swallows do not a summer make <G>.

Reading what Daniel has posted in this thread simply spells the classic

inevitable failure. The users will get a system "something like" what they

underacknowledged, having "sweated blood" to deliver, then had a lukewarm
reception and the old: "Thank you. It's very nice but can we just have this
one small change..."

Now, you can say what you like about the merits of the Waterfall and the
advantages of sign offs, but I KNOW that the above scenario happens more on
Waterfall projects than it does when DSDM (Dynamic System Design
Methodology) type approaches are implemented. The keyword here is "dynamic".
Translate that as "responsive to change".

Without digressing into a summarised lecture on DSDM, consider just the
following:

1. Change is a natural part of the Business environment.

2. IT can deal with it in one of two ways:

(a).  Control the flow of change into the project by requiring signoff of
clear deliverables. (This is what usually happens and is the basis of the
Waterfall.)

(b). Work WITH the Business to identify and prioritise change. Use technical
approaches that accommodate change. All development is timeboxed. Timeboxes
cannot slip or be extended. When the Business identify a new (not previously
recognised) requirement, it is prioritised and accommodated just like all
the other requirements. BUT, all parties know that when the timebox is up,
that's it. You get what was prioritised. No attempt is made to deliver
EVERYTHING unless this can be done easily, within the allocated time. Within
a given timebox priorities are iterated and the requirements re-visited to
ensure that what was top priority is still top priority, and changes have
been properly assimilated and prioritised, as development proceeds.
Priorities are driven by the Business, not by IT.

Under option (a), a system is delivered that matches the Requirements
Gathering all those months ago. It may or may not be useful.

Under option (b), the current priorites are delivered. The system may not
have EVERY requirement implemented, but what it does have will be what the
Business wants NOW, not what they thought they might want 9 months ago.
Useful functionality will have been trickled out of the timeboxes as
development proceeded, rather than a Big Bang "delivery" at the end of the
project.

Having worked together with IT throughout the development (mainly in JDWs)
the Business have "buy in" and accptance, even if the result is not perfect
functionally. It is seen as OUR system, not another system being thrust upon
them by IT...

Having spent 20 years doing it both ways, I am persuaded that option (b) is
better.

But the chance of getting approval for a project using this approach on a
Fortress site is about the same as finding evidence for
gravitons...(extremely remote, difficult to set up the right conditions, but
not impossible).

Even if you do get approval for a pilot project, it takes a skilled Project
Manager to hold all the threads (Business and IT) together. The selection of
the project team is critical to the success of this approach. Neverthelees,
I have managed and implemented around a dozen such projects and every one
was a success.

What amazes me is that we see the same failed approaches being repeated over
and over, with the same symptoms emerging, and we have no answer, other than
"Well, we delivered what they signed off...".

Personally, success on paper is not good enough for me. If the systems I
develop don't make a difference, I'm wasting my own and everybody else's
time, and I'd rather be doing something else.
