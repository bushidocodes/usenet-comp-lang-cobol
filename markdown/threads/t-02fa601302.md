hard Cobolers and has certainly been a bug-bear for me over the
years.

DOCUMENTATION.


Some years back I was involved in the development of a large batch
invoicing system. Subsequent to implementation, I was instrumental in
enhancing the system (adding in the bits which had had to be left out
of the original system development for reason of time) and supporting
the system in the live environment (bug fixes (not too many of those -
the major enhancement added only 8 post-implementation bugs) and minor
enhancements). The documentation for the system consisted of design
document (now woefully out of date and confusing), program specs, code
and JCL. No HOW-TO fixit notes....

I subsequently spent three months documenting the system in detail and
showing what ran when, what to do at any point where the system fell
over and also how to completely back out and rerun the system.
Unfortunately, the documentation quickly went out of date (as new
projects did not include elements to 'Update Documentation') and was
probably never used (the development project leader did not know the
file existed.

So.... what do you guys and gals consider to be the ideal
documentation for post-implementation support? For BATCH and ONLINE.

In my shop where the code base is probably a couple of thousand batch
and online programs containing 2 to 3 million LOC, the prime
contractor did a fair job keeping the documentation up to date for the
first few years of maintenance. Then they let go the two documentation
people as "overhead" :-) and the documentation was updated on an
occasional basis, but at least in "one place" which always had the
latest info (bug descriptions, design docs, new tables added, etc.
etc.) about the programs.

Then another contractor took over and for the last six years the
"primary key" on all documentation is the "trouble report ID number",
and the documentation about the fixes was never consolidated in the
"one place" per program/subsystem.  The result is that if you want a
wholistic picture of "what is the design and business logic for the
XYZ system" you have to go hunting all over the place on a shared
network drive, for the business specs, design docs, testing results,
production implementation, and the like.

*** EXCEPT FOR *** (and this is the ta-da! part) -

The developers were fairly disciplined about putting comments for
Revision History with trouble-report-ID and inline comments identified
by trouble-report-ID. This means that the comments in the program code
are the SINGLE MOST COMPREHENSIVE documentation on post-implementation
support for that program. At least they provide a framework by which
other, more extensive searches, of MS-Word .doc documentation, may be
conducted by looking for references to trouble-report-ID.

As an aside, my organization is currently conducting a "documentation
clean up" project which is, as you can imagine, a massive effort. My
fear is that they will do it badly, and delete a bunch of stuff that
is important. My point of view is that since we currently do not have
a credibly comprehensive and documented and complete inventory of (1)
programs, and (2) DB tables, that they start with that. But I don't
think that is what they are doing.

processed by networked systems very effectively. It is partly to do with the 
fact that the network provides multiple processors and parallel pathways.

batch processing (and in that environment it may well be an effective 
solution, although, with the changing role of mainframes in the current 
century, even this is becoming less true)). However, if the problem is 
approached from the point of view that "we have a few million transactions 
going on in a specified timeframe (where that timeframe could be very small)

process transactions, many systems easily process and differentiate between 
current, deferred, and pending transactions.

there are other much better reasons to do this. Neither would I accept this 
as a reason to dumb down code and limit the power of it.

4. I don't believe that any detailed documentation is of much use at all, 
and would prefer to see better (clearer, concise, and succinct) code being 
written.

5. I DO believe that graphic documentation is often better and more useful

Can't answer that directly, but can talk about Use Cases a bit. Though

guy himself (as opposed to "rational" :-) - of course, he contended
that batch processes COULD be represented in Use Cases, but when you
read this explanation it all a "sleight of hand" whereby the "actor"
was a "batch scheduler" and the "system" was the batch program, and
the "event trigger" was a time/date/clock event that initiated the

"dialogue" between "actor" and "system" had to be written as a

Gosh, sounds like traditional or modified Waterfall to me.

On the other hand, what I think are called "state charts" in UML could
be very, VERY useful for batch programs. Elsewhere, in differently
moniker'd methodologies, these might be called Entity Life Cycle
Diagrams (ELCD's), or State Transition Diagrams, a term which fell out
of favor because its acronym could easily be confused with non-
specific urinary tract infections. :-)

Anyway, what a state chart does is describe the events/circumstances
under which the very, very important STATUS variables of a record
change - and hence the record/entity/object attains a new status. Eg.,
an Order can obtain statuses of Entered, Fulfilled (picked), Scheduled-
to-be-shipped, shipped, back-ordered, etc. etc. It is important to
note that the state chart, though drawn like a flow chart, is NOT a
systems flow chart, or a data-flow-diagram (dfd). I tshows the entity
moving through its statuses over time, Sometimes this is called a
"behavioral" model.

Other examples of abstract documentation that provides a good overview
is the "data model" (for all DB's now almost universally represented
as Entity-Relationship-Diagrams (ERD's), and the "process model",
which in the Structured Analysis / Design days were either data-flow-
diagrams (dfd's) or structure charts (Hierarchy-Input-Process-Output,
or HIPO, in IBM-speak). For maintenance purposes, if the HIPO or
structure chart was not created as part of the original documentation,
it is usually not worthwhile to create one, unless the program is
very, very complicated and you just can't get a picture of what is
going on.

For dfd's, the story is different. In my shop, there are hundreds of
batch programs, and they are schedule to run at all different times -
some daily, some weekly (on different days of the week), some monthly
(on different days of the month - 1st Saturday, last Saturday, end-of-
month), and of course the annuals (1st Sat after end-of-year), etc.
etc. Sometimes when I had to track down a problem I had to draw a dfd
just to see how the input/output files were "chained" together,
because the CA7 scheduler Job File, for that particular day, did not

(channelling Dijkstra??)

For example: you encounter a program that you have never seen before and 
there are various written resources available:
- comments in the program
- a writeup about the program in the operations run guide
- a description and flowchart (perhaps) of the program in the department 
documentation pile
etc.
- a document prepared by an outside consultant describing (at great expense 
no doubt) how the 'system' works - including this program.

Is any of this documentation to be trusted? IF (and it may be a big 'if') 
any of it has a reliable timestamp, the recent documentation may be 
considered more reliable than older or un-dated material, but the real 
question is whether or not to trust any of it.

The point of course is that documentation is normally not vetted; there is 
no incentive to keep it correct or even debugged (as there is implied with 
code). Documentation or comments that may have been correct at one time are 
seldom edited to reflect changes or patches or emergency or permanent hacks.
