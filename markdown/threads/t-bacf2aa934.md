What I found interesting about all these responses was the complete 
bewilderment and lack of understanding, not of COBOL as a language, but of 
the environment it was developed for.

Whatever people may say about it, the main thing of interest to programmers 
is whether or not they can make a living at it. (Otherwise you are indulging 
in "hobby programming"... nothing wrong with that, but recognise it for what 
it is.)

In the last 20 years programmers have seen that possibility get eroded (as 
some of us predicted it would, long before it became obvious) to the point 
where today, a career as a COBOL programmer is no longer viable for a young 
person.

I absolutely loved this quote: "Cobol writers are quite old and are not part 
of our world."

That should be framed and on the wall... :-)

Load a 50 year old language environment onto a brand new Sun server and find 
that "delightfully perverse"?

I find it sad.

Pete.

Personally, I have been saddened by the demise of COBOL for some years now, 
but it had to happen. The world has moved on and the COBOL community simply 
didn't. I don't think anyone is gloating over it, Peter.

It is very hard to work daily with a language, achieve success and joy with 
it and NOT get emotionally attached to it.

But, all of it, even the languages-du-jour are simply computer software and 
getting emotionally attached to them is as sensible as falling in love with 
your toaster... :-)

I am finding the new challenges in web development, code generation, and 
legacy migration which I am currently undertaking, to be every bit as 
challenging and satisfying as COBOL application development always was for 
me. To a programmer, there is always intense satisfaction in making 
something work, and this seems to transcend any particular language. I am 
absolutely blown away by new packages like Iron Speed (simply amazing for 
web development), Stimulsoft (Crystal Reports on steroids and without the 
complexity), and even the latest incarnation of my own Toolset, which is 
being very well received and bringing COBOL legacy into today's world in a 
fraction of the time it would take to do so manually.
(OO COBOL is an essential part of this transition, and will be for some 
years yet...)

In the old days we wrote a solution for every problem; nowadays the 
solutions are there to a large extent and the challenge is how to employ 
them to best advantage. Class Libraries like .NET (currently 80,000 
"functions" available), and the latest Java Packages, mean that there is no 
longer a need to build everything from scratch and solutions can be provided 
in a fraction of the time that was once the case.

But it's still fun to sit back and say those three magic words.... "Start 
'er up!"

Pete.
