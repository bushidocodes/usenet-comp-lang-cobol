It was an interesting article.

I give less credence to sources like Micro Focus and Gartner talking
COBOL up because they have vested interests in it, but the article was
still good.

I was interested to see that 40% of major banks still use COBOL; that
means 60% do not... an unthinkable situation even 20 years ago.

It's also obvious to me that when they say people don't want their COBOL
translated into Java (for example), they don't understand WHY that is
true. The paradigm difference just hasn't filtered through to most
mainframe sites.

See: https://primacomputing.co.nz/PRIMAMetro/ObjectsAndLayers.aspx

It is very wrong to simply recompile mainframe COBOL and run it on a
network. (Even though you CAN do that.) It is just as wrong to convert
it to Java and do that... (The Java solution is probably not quite so
bad because there will be Java programmers involved and they DO
understand the need for objects and layers. Attempts to refactor code
during conversion will probably be made.)

The legacy code needs to be refactored into objects and layers for it to
run efficiently on the Network. This can be done manually or with tools
but unless it is done, the gains in moving to a network are largely negated.

There's nothing wrong with COBOL; it is still as good as it has always
been. BUT it needs to accommodate a different paradigm if it is to run
effectively on a network. You can use OO COBOL and it is very good, but
the languages DESIGNED for Object Orientation are "better".

I used OO COBOL for around 12 years then moved to C#. My productivity
increased by around 100% (largely because the tools are better but also
because the language is innately quicker to write)

There IS "life in the old dog yet" but it is sad to see a working
sheepdog moved to an inner city apartment and having to cope with that.

Pete.
I used to write COBOL; now I can do anything...

It is my experience that an organisation will hires someone for a job who
either knows the job or doesn't know it.

If they don't know the job they learn it... and then the company has an
asset who knows (x) and is liable to be poached by another firm.

If they know the job they stagnate... or they learn more, becoming an
asset who knows (x) and (y)... and liable for poaching.

The easiest way to deal with this kind of upward mobility is to get
together with your fellow HR leaders and promise not to hire each other's
employees. The small difficulty with this solution is that it violates
black-letter in the United States of America.
