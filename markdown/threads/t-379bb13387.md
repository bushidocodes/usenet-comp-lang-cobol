For those of you without E.S.P., "structured" programming is based on a
paper by Bohm and Jacopini - "Flow Diagrams, Turing Machines and
Languages with Only Two Formation Rules," written in Communications of
the ACM from May of 1966.  To summarize: a structured program consists
of only three types of logic structures - sequence, selection, and
iteration.  A structure chart goes hand-in-hand with structured
programming.  It is top-down left-right with single-entry, single-exit
point paragraphs.

Typically, you should gather your specifications, clarify your
specifications, design a structure chart, code, then test.  Of
pertinence to the original question is the structure chart.  The
structure chart consists of boxes, lines, and decisions.  Ultimately,
each box should relate to exactly 1 paragraph.  A paragraph can perform
either actual statements or other paragraphs - I don't think you're
supposed to mix the two.  There are alot of other rules of thumb to
follow regarding structured programming, they can be ascertained by
reading a book or through (E.S.P) - your choice.

Now comes the question of in-line performs...  It is ok to use an
in-line perform for something simple.  Any complex in-line perform
should usually be broken down into further paragraphs.  There is a
performance hit with calling more and more paragraphs, but if you've
ever worked in a maintenance shop you'd be of the opinion that
maintainability is of far more importance than a negligible performance
hit.  (The performance cost will be equivalent to performing 1-2 lines

Structure charts are associated with modular programming, not
structured programming. The content of the module could consist
of unstructured code.

Likewise, it is possible to create a program of more than 10 000
lines using structured programming. It is unlikely that such a program
would be modular.

Generally speaking, structured design consists of developing
programs from modules of structured code.

The concepts for modular programming and structured programming
were developed independently by the early 70's. By the mid-70's,
the concepts had been combined as the recommended method for
creating systems. Since then the distinction has been important only
for newsgoup discussions. ;-)

There is still a difference between SP and MP and I did not mean to
imply otherwise. What I meant was that after MP and SP were
combined as SD (or more properly SD/SP), the individual using SD
had no need to distinguish between SP and MP, except for newsgroup
discussions.

MP is decomposing the system into small, meaningful, pieces and
writing the code such that the pieces cooperate, but do not interfere
with each other. SP is writing the code to conform to certain flow
control constructs and is an acceptable means for writing code that
conforms to MP.

One other important difference is that MP provides guidelines (rules?)
for encapsulating data and defining interfaces. SP does not.

The definitional problem is that MP defines the structure of the system,
but not the (flow control) code. SP defines the structure of the (flow
control) code, but not the system. Hence, they both deal with structuring,
but do so at different levels.

Further complicating this is that "structured" won the buzzword war.
Hence, we see SA, SD, and SP rather than MA, MP + SP. (SP
being the method for writing the code for MP.)

>Anyhow, do you have any suggested reading for a fellow that is
>interested in the history and development of COBOL?  I have a few Grauer
>books but that's about it.

I imagine the definitive reference is the CODASYL COBOL Commitee,
Journal of Development. These publications might be available in the
libraries of some older universities or in the reference libraries at some
computer manufacturers, who produced COBOL compilers.

I have an article from Datapro Research, August 1980, which is titled
"Proposed CODASYL COBOL Language Standards -- 1978".
This article summarizes the history and approved proposals for changes
in the COBOL language from 1960 through 1978. There is, of course,
a more detailed description of the (not yet approved) proposed changes
for 1978.

Notably, the first item mentioned for the next discussion, in 1978, was
"A facility to define intrinsic and user-defined functions." Intrinsic
functions made it into the 1989 amendment to the 1985 standard and
user-defined functions are proposed for the next standard.

More relevent to modular and structured progamming was the approval
of nested programs, in 1978, and the addition of scope terminators,
CONTINUE, and in-line PERFORM, also in 1978. These were included
in the 1985 standard.
