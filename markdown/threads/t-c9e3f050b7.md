Early FORTRAN (pre version IV) did use all 9's in a record (a punched
card in everything I ever saw) to indicate. IIRC, version IV supported
the "END=" parameter on the READ command. NB, this is not necessarily in
a date field, plus the system had some pretty crummy date validation
routines if '999999' (or '99999999' to be Y2K compliant <g>) was a valid
date.

I've worked for one of the big banks since 1990 on a securities movement
& control system, and we used to use 990909 to indicate an open
settlement date. That has since been updated to use 770707 instead. Keep
in mind that our world of business dates is 5 years, current +/- 2
years.

I fully expect I'd have had a call or more by now if we were having
batch problems with 990909 - we'd be settling all those open trades and
everything would be out of whack.

Very interested in hearing of any other systems' problems/adventures
regarding 990909.

In some (poorly written) systems, 9/9/99 is the "date that will never
happen."  For example, a salesman is taking an order but doesn't have all
the necessary information or has something he needs to verify first.  He
gives a shipping date of 9/9/99 with the idea of changing it to something
more reasonable when everything is ready.

only wants everything after a certain date so 9/9/99 is entered (perhaps by
default in the program) as the upper limit for the date range.  Needless to
say, if anyone has something like that in their system now they will soon
have a problem if they don't have one now.
