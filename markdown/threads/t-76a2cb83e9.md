are truly hierarchical - that is, there is some row/record for which there
is never a good reason to access that record except when you also "from an
application perspective" will always be accessing the parent, then why not
have a DBMS which mirrors the application?

For example, I saw a nifty IMS database a couple of years ago in insurance.
The parent was the policy; under the policy were timestamped records
indicating what coverage was in effect in a given time period. This allowed
tracking of when the policyholder added an item of property to casualty
insurance, changed a deductible option, etc. Policyholder could never claim
"loss" for an item which was not added to the policy until after the date of
loss, nor claim a $200 deductible if he did not upgrade from $500 deductible
AFTER the policy period started but PRIOR to the loss.

Never any reason to go after the "coverage as of xxxx date" records without
also going after the parent.

That's all I meant. Today I'd probably write this application relational,
anyway. The Hierarchical skills seem to have left with.... Eisenhower.

No, Michael,

there are still a few of us around who understand Hierarchical databases
(and even the Network model [IDMS f'rinstance...).

I made a living for many years as an IMS DBA, wrote generalised software to
access IMS hierarchies and even found obscure codes to modify the DL/1 calls
into holding position at various levels of the hierarchy.

Your observations below regarding hierarchical structure are very valid
(although you neglected to mention the downside ...the need to traverse the
hierarchy to get to dependant data, but that's another story...).

When Relational DBs first appeared, many of us who had been using the
Hierarchic model successfully for a number of years were quite dismayed by
the apparent lack of support for hierarchic data...

We loathed Relational with a passion and resisted it fiercely. Fortunately,
the early Relational implementations were very inefficient and we could
benchmark our Hierarchies against them and win every time (of course the
data was always slanted to favour the Hierarchic Model...<G>).  However, as
implementations of the Relational Model became more efficient and Big Blue
dictated that it WOULD be used, we were forced to take it seriously and,
eventually, I for one, became persuaded of its applicability.

Today, the use of Foreign Keys provides for Hierarchic support within the
Relational model, and we have much more understanding, knowledge, and proven
Methodology with which to design  our DBs, so supporting Hierarchies within
RDB technology is no problem.

The fact is (as anyone who has ever read "Zen and the Art of Motorcycle
Maintenance" will attest) that hierarchic structures are a VERY good way to
organise information and much "natural" data follows this pattern. It is
natural and intuitive for Humans to absorb information in a "Top Down"
manner, gradually expanding on sound basic concepts or overviews. It is easy
to design systems on the same basis.

The success of Object Orientation is partly because it allows an incremental
inheritance Hierarchy.

There is NOTHING wrong with a Hierarchic approach in many cases.

The danger is in assuming that a Hierarchic Database will IMPLEMENT this
approach BETTER than a Relational Database. While, on the face of it, this
might appear to be a reasonable assumption, practice has shown that because
of technical limitations in the Hierarchic software, combined with huge
advances in the Relational software (especially in recent years) and better
design Methodology based on the Relational Model, today it is quite feasible
to implement a hierarchic structure with an RDB and get not only the
benefits of the hierarchy, but also the RELATIONAL benefits.

ADMISSION OF BIAS: I use ONLY the Relational model for new system
development (even where Hierarchic data is involved). I have never found it
to be inadequate when implemented properly.

Pete.
