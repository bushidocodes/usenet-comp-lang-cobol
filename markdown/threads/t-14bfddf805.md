You can't take a cuddle blanket into the Brave New World, Jimmy. There is a 
whole generation of PowerCOBOL applications that are incompatible too, it 
isn't just DIALOG. Oddly enough, a couple of years back I asked if anyone 
was interested in a tool to convert PowerCOBOL (and, later, DIALOG) 
applications to .NET and the result was a deafening silence. As it would 
have involved a considerable investment on my part I decided not to do it. I 
think that most people are either writing off their old COBOL development 
entirely, or, where they can't/won't do that they are refactoring what they 
can and moving to a different language.

It is a vindication of tiered development (Objects and Layers); systems that 
have a Presentation Layer separate from the Business Layer have little 
problem with how the GUI is implemented, or WHAT it is implemented in. The 
lessons learned in mainframe development 40 years ago are still as true as 
they ever were. CICS and IMS/DC applications can be moved to .NET 
comparatively easily because both systems use a data buffer between the 
screen and the application. You don't actually NEED a CICS emulator to 
migrate mainframe applications, but it does save a lot of work that would 
otherwise be required in manually defining existing screen layouts for Win 
Forms or WPF.

(I'm not advocating that mainframe COBOL applications SHOULD be migrated 
directly to .NET; just saying that it is easier to do than some PC legacy 
COBOL applications. Ideally, ANYTHING going to .NET should be "Objectified" 
first and separation layers for at least 3 tiers (Presentation, Business 
logic, and Database) should be implemented. The PRIMA Migration Toolset 
generates the separation layer between Business Logic and Database 
automatically, however, as noted above, I decided not to implement an 
automated separation for the Presentation Layer because no-one appeared to 
be interested, and it is a huge amount of work. It took me a couple of years 
to write the Database separation and generate Objects that can be used for 
data access, but for that I had clients who were very keen to move off ISAM 
to RDB and it was therefore worth the effort.)

manage its own presentation and data access. These are usually inextricably

components in that layer. A multi-tiered approach has HUGE benefits for 
maintaining systems. If you like, the "granularity" of the system is 
smaller, so you can swap components in and out without causing any knock on 
effects. Layers can be independently tuned and experimented with with no 
effect on the business logic, the business logic can be changed with no 
effect on other layers, and if the change DOES require something added into 
Presentation and Data Access Layers, the changes are in an encapsulated 
component that cannot effect other processing.  The concept of layering 
systems is  generally foreign to COBOL, but it is nonetheless, an extremely 
good way to build systems. Object Oriented systems lend themselves to it 
very well and that is one of the reasons why legacy code should be 
"objectified" before moving it to .Net.

If you consider a CICS or IMS/DC system on IBM mainframes, they lend 
themselves to this approach because the Business logic is already separated 
from the presentation by a screen buffer (Map, in CICS). This is the same 
principle as the interfaces I mentioned above. You can change a CICS BMS Map 
and it MAY have no effect on the Business Logic at all. The Business Logic 
populates a screen buffer (including attributes that affect the display) and 
that is all the Business Logic ever sees. It Neither knows nor cares about 
the sus=bsystem that will present the data to the User. Layered Object 
systems work in very much the same way.
