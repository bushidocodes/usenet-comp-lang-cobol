Thanks for the link Charlie.

I found it hard to take seriously because of the melodramatic presentation 
(and the fact that much of it was from people who have an agenda).

For example: "China stole large amounts of intellectual property and 
sensitive military information...etc."

That doesn't make the Chinese villains; rather, it makes the people 
responsible for said assets idiots.

It is obviously much cheaper to develop the cyber skills to steal someone 
else's idea than it is to have to do all your own research and development. 
I cannot honestly believe that the US military, even US corporate leaders, 
could not imagine that somebody might be after what they had. Why didn't 
they put the proper measures in place?

(Besides, the US did the same to Iran, and the Israelis are doing it to 
everybody even as you read this :-), so the morality is really moot; it is 
simply the practicality that matters.)

There is a certain attitude of disdain for geeks. Perhaps that should change 
and the real value of the "geek" in a connected cyber world should be 
realized and valued.

If you have something important wouldn't you make sure it was secure? If you 
trust to OTC software and decide that putting Norton on it makes it safe, 
then you deserve whatever you get.

For myself, yes, I run Norton, but I also have several layers of other 
measures in place (some of which I wrote myself) and despite several 
targeted attacks, so far my network has not been breached. (That isn't a 
challenge script kiddies... I'm not saying my network is impenetrable, just 
that the protection is adequate to my needs.)

Note in passing...Most of my (knowledgeable) friends are astonished because 
my WLAN is reported as "insecure".  (It's because I don't use WPA 
encryption;  if anyone wants to monitor the messages on my network with a 
scanner they are welcome... there is nothing critical or sensitive sent over 
my LAN; banking is done separately.) Not using encryption means my network 
runs faster, but every so often when someone mentions it, I invite them to 
try and connect. No-one has ever succeeded (even people who would know what 
to do...). And yet other friends come to my house, take out their notebooks, 
switch on and are immediately connected to my LAN and the internet. No 
login, no crazy long signon passwords, just instant connection.

Technology is not about "going with the flow"; it is about making it do what 
you want and having it work the way you want it to. That takes time and 
effort. You have to learn stuff. If you don't, deciding that it is all too 
hard (or too expensive, or the shrink-wrapped software will do the job), and 
suddenly the Chinese have a man in Space and they are building your most 
secret Doomsday weapon, whose fault was that?

It's NOT the fault of the Internet.

You can bet that if I was one of the US military in the video I'd  be 
allocating more time and resource to attacking my own system and I'd be 
getting people who know about networks and don't just see the Internet as a 
big creepy spiderweb that puts everyone in danger.

The video was a typical journalistic response from someone who knows 
nothing. (Probably the same people called the Higg's Boson a "God 
Particle"....)

Pete.
