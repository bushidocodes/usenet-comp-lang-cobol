I'm not quite sure, Mr Maclean.  As I understand it (from all the Latest 
Literature with which I am familiar) a company is, typically, confronted 
with two opposing business needs: the need to Just Do Stuff and the need 
to complete projects.  A project, by definition, is a temporary and 
limited series of activities which produce an unique product or service 
while Just Doing Stuff is how the company actually fulfills its raison 
d'etre, ie, Make Money.

Take as an example a delivery company.  The functional aspect of the 
organisation is fairly straightforward: stuff comes in the door, gets 
loaded onto trucks, goes out the door and delivered.  A vital function in 
delivery is routing, the determining of the most efficient/least expensive 
path to track for a particular load of goods.

Routing used to be done by folks with lists and maps and cups of coffee 
and endless streams of cigarettes.  Computers have replaced a lot of that.

So... when the project arises to switch over routing methodologies from 
the 'Louie with a cup of mud and a Lucky Strike' to 'Pat at a keyboard' 
two things need to be done simultaneously: the company has to keep moving 
goods (retaining its functionality) and the company has to allocate 
personnel and resources towards the completion of the project of 
computerisation.

Just about all companies have a balance between these two 
resource-competing needs, the functional and the project.  One extreme is 
so heavily weighted towards the functional so that the project rarely, if 
ever, exists... and these are called (surprise!) Functional Organisations.  
On the other extreme would be a consulting company that takes on tasks for 
companies that just want something done and don't have the in-house 
expertise to implement; the consulting company, doing only project, is 
called the Projectised Organisation.

The weighting of the balance between the two needs in a single 
organisation determines - in NewSpeak - the kind of matrix the 
organisation employs.  When it is weighted towards the functional the 
project manager's needs are subordinate to the functional manager's 
needs... 'We gotta keep the widgets moving' is heavier than 'We gotta 
implement the Widgetzip 9000 system' this is called the 'weak matrix'; 
project managers usually have less authority and are not so much managers 
as co-ordinators.

In a 'strong matrix' organisation the reverse is true; while the widgets 
still have to keep moving the project manager for Widgetzip 9000 has more 
authority over allocation of personnel and resources.

I can say that at my time at a rather large company changed for the
worse after becoming matrixed.  (Let's call the company Large Blue).

My experience was that roles become more strictly defined - no one
worked outside of their project or tried to expand their role.  The
idea of "team" went out of the window - you work for people close to
you but not those who live on the other side of the country/world.
You would either (a) Get a project of 50 hrs a week and be told to
claim less overtime, or (b) Get a project of less than 40 hrs a week
and be asked to find additional work (but no one had 5 hr a week
projects).  Ultimately, the matrix model hit the external customer
project and then the issue became "Find me a 40 hr a week resource to
work in Atlanta for a maximum of $23 hr" (this was a REAL posting
internally at the Large Blue company).

Eventually many people got burned out and left or got kicked out.
Many of the "clients" actually came looking for former Large Blue
company employees as it's cheaper to hire an independent consultant
than an independent consultant through Large Blue Projectized
environments.

Anyway.  I've mostly got out of coding now because it bored me (play
around with Drupal/PHP mostly).   Recently picked up a new career
where the strategic development is on a platform running COBOL on
Solaris. I thought COBOL was going away back in 2004 :)  I've been
thinking of starting a company to rewrite it in C# independently -
think it would take 4-5 programmers 2-3 years to get it right but
could probably sell a non-exclusive license for 2-3 million (incl.
support) and there would likely be about 4-5 interested companies.
Too much of a risk for me :)

Glad to see everyone is still out and about in COBOL land :)
