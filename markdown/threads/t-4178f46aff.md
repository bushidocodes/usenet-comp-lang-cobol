With Capability Maturity Model (CMM), the emphasis is on quality
through process improvement. Were you to artificially inflate the
LOC, more errors would occur, more lines would be changed
unnecessarily during maintenance, and overall quality would
decrease. Just the opposite of a goal of CMM. It is far more
likely that you would be given the opportunity to advance within
another organization. ;-)

From this point, I will ramble a bit, provide references, quotes
and observations.

The Personal Software Process (PSP), which is associated
with CMM, requires that the individual track their own productivity.
Under PSP, LOC per hour is a number for your use only! As an
individual, you use that number along with other numbers to
estimate the time you require to complete your tasks. The better
your estimates -- the greater your contribution to overall process
improvement.

The Software Engineering Institute at Carnegie-Mellon
University is the starting point for understanding CMM and PSP
and other programs. The home page is
< http://www.sei.cmu.edu/ >; but you might wish to start with
< http://www.sei.cmu.edu/managing/managing.html >.

There are also several books by Watts S. Humphrey which
explain these programs. In his book on PSP, "A Discipline
for Software Engineering," Addison-Wesley, 1995,
page 73-74, Watts S. Humphrey wrote:

"LOC can be defined in so many ways that it is easy to get
confused. It is such a conceptually simple measure that people
often treat the subject superficially. Because of the many
possible LOC definitions, the proper use of size measures
depends on the care with which you specify and apply the
measure. When usage specifications are not precise, different
LOC measures of identical products often very so widely that
the measures are useless. Flaherty, for example, found
productivity variations of 56 to one, largely due to differences
in LOC definitions."

On page 76, of the same book, is a template describing
statement types as: Executable, Declarations, Compiler
directives, Comments, etc. -- nearly the same words used
by Dwight Johnson. Apparently, Dwight Johnson is aware of
PSP and has chosen to define COBOL LOC as statements,
data description entries, and compiler directives while
excluding comments, blank lines, procedure names, headers,
etc. I don't agree with that definition because it leaves out
potentially significant entries in the Environment Division.

What we discover here is that the  notion of a metric is often relative
to what a specific observer wants to count, i.e., definitions vary.
So what you really want is a tool in which you can change such definitions
quickly.

The DMS Reengineering Toolkit is generalized compiler technology,
used to automate customized analyses and modifications of sources
for large scale software systems in many langauges, including COBOL.
See http://www.semdesigns.com/Products/DMS/DMSToolkit.html.

The DMS COBOL front end handles copybooks, including optional expansion.

DMS parses COBOL into compiler data structures that match the
structure of the COBOL language.   A DMS component,
called the "attribute evaluator", can be directed to visit these
data structures and carry out arbitrary computations.
So it is "easy" to write a metric over the langauge structures
that matches your definitions.  ("Easy" meaning you do
have to learn how to use the tool).

And if you change your mind about the definitions, you can
straightforwardly modify the computation to take that into account.

Last but not least... DMS can use such analyses to drive
automated modifications to the source code.
(The hard part is defining what to do under what circumstances).
