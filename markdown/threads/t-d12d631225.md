```
I have a situation in COBOL where I need to take a string and pull 
characters out of it.

The characters I need to pull depend on the length of the target field, 
which could be 6, 8, or 10 bytes.

The source string is 30 bytes.

An example may help...

SOURCE STRING:  "19/03/2007  17:21:05:83"

REQUIRED RESULTS:

6    byte =  190307
8    byte =  19032007
10  byte =  19/03/2007

Obviously, we can discount the 10 byte case as a simple move will do it.

There are several immediately obvious approaches, but I was hoping for 
something smarter.

1. The old REFMOD solution...

    move space to target
    evaluate function LENGTH  (target)
         when 6
                   move source (1:2) to target
                   move source (4:2) to target
                   move source (7:2) to target
         when  8
                   move source (1:2) to target
                   move source (4:2) to target
                   move source (7:4) to target
         when  other
                  move source to target
     end-evaluate

2. The old MOVE CORR solution...

01  source.
      12 dd    pic xx.
      12 filler  pic x.
      12 mm  pic xx.
      12 filler  pic x.
      12 cc     pic xx.
      12 yy     pic xx.
      12 filler  pic x(20).

01  target-6.
      12 dd    pic xx.
      12 mm  pic xx.
      12 yy     pic xx.

01  target-8.
      12 dd    pic xx.
      12 mm  pic xx.
      12 cc     pic xx.
      12 yy     pic xx.
...

    move space to target
    evaluate function LENGTH  (target)
         when 6
                   move CORRESPONDING source  to target-6
                   move target-6 to target
         when  8
                   move CORRESPONDING source  to target-8
                   move target-8 to target
         when  other
                  move source to target
     end-evaluate


While both of these will do the job, there's a fair bit of writing and they 
leave me feeling "uncomfortable"... It SHOULD be possible to simply strip 
out the slashes...

As far as I can ascertain the INSPECT ...TRANSFORMING feature which Robert 
recently reminded us about, will not transform something to nothing...

Both of the above will work correctly whether the system is using DD/MM/YYYY 
or MM/DD/YYYY except that the names for day and month will be swapped, 
another thing which, while not important in the context, leaves a bad 
taste...

Any thoughts?

Pete.
```

Pete (and Karl),
  It is my understanding that for any conforming compiler, "the results are 
unpredictable".  I tried to indicate this in my original note.  I believe that 
MANY (but not all) '85 conforming compilers will "work" if the editing symbol 
position has "bad" data in it (where "bad" is anything other than the correct 
editing symbol).  My *memory* is that on Windows MF and Fujitsu both work OK - 
and that RM has some "switch" for this.  However, I could be mistaken.

With the '02 Standard (as if there were any such compilers) this is explicitly 
defined as "incompatible data".  However, even then the results are 
"unpredictable" if no EC-DATA-INCOMPATIBLE exception is being checked for.

FYI,
 To accomplish what I think you want you could do the following (I think).

01  30-Byte.
  05  First-10.
       10   8-Digit   Pic 99,99,9999.
       10  6-Digit   redefines 8-digit Pic 99,99,,,99.
  05 Last-20  Pic X(20).
     ...
Evaluate  Function Length (Target)
   When 10
      Move First -10 to Target-alphanumeric
    When 8
       Move 8-Digit to Target-Numeric
    When 6
       Move 6-Digit to Target-Numeric

       01 target-length binary pic 9(4) value 0.
       01 target.
           03 pic x occurs 6 to 10 depending target-length.
       procedure division.
       begin.
           move function current-date to current-date-time
           inspect source-string converting
               date-time-pattern to current-date-time

               when 8

               end-evaluate
               inspect target

               display target
           end-perform

I have had a couple of occasions where it was convenient to use a 
table with a zero subscript.  Even though IBM mainframe COBOL 
compilers don't support it, it's relatively easy to do, as I am sure 
most readers here are aware.

First, you have to set the compiler option that turns off array bounds 
checking.  It is often turned off by default, simply for runtime 
performance reasons.

Second, you must define your data so that the zero subscript will 
reference a table entry immediately preceding the "first" table entry.

Here is a simplified example:

01  WS-TABLE.
     05  WS-TABLE-ENTRY-ZERO   PIC X(13)  VALUE SPACES.
     05  WS-TABLE-ENTRY        OCCURS 99 TIMES
                               PIC X(13)  VALUE SPACES.

In this example all subscript values of 00 through 99 are available to 
the programmer, because WS-TABLE-ENTRY (0) actually refers to 
WS-TABLE-ENTRY-ZERO.

This works with indexing or subscripting, but it will not work with 
SEARCH and SEARCH ALL, for obvious reasons.
