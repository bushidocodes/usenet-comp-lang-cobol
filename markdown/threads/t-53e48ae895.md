I can't tell you how many times I've heard or read about the death of COBOL.
Some people just can't seem to understand why after some new languages are
developed, we don't have everything converted over to them within a matter
of months.  They just don't appreciate the millions - if not billions - of
lines of code that are out there already (I'm starting to sound like Carl
Sagen).  Whole systems are coded in those older languages.  There's some
ignorance about what's involved here - especially among non-programmers.  I
mean most people like this reporter probably think converting a system from
COBOL to C++ is no different than running the Windows98 upgrade on a
Windows95 PC.

There are people out there who are trying to set people straight about this.
I've had some programming teachers who said that the death of COBOL is
highly exaggerated.  Though they had some of their own misconceptions about
other things that were dead, like Assembly language.  One teacher also
talked about key punch machines as dinosaurs that went out with the '70s.
This at a time when the office I worked in at a state agency had a keypunch
machine that was used everyday!  And this was only about 6 years ago (though
I think that keypunch machine has been retired since then).  When I got a
job as a programmer - at the same state agency - I found that most of the
existing code was still in BAL.  We're still converting over to COBOL!  Of
course we have to train most of our Assembler programmers in house, because
nobody teaches it anymore.

I've heard so much on this topic lately, this post really strikes a nerve.

I hate to rant, but the idea that COBOL is dead, and that COBOL jobs will
disappear soon is *totally ridiculous*.  I graduated school fairly recently,
and there are many people my age working in shops that use COBOL.  DePaul
University has a program (CCP) that allows people to transition from one
career to programming, and what do they teach? COBOL! On an MVS mainframe no
less!

These students are getting snapped up as fast as DePaul can crank them out.
Some would say it's because of Y2K, but that's just as absurd as the notion
that COBOL is dead.

The fact is, new COBOL applications are being written everywhere, and more
will be written.  Look at all of the mega-mergers happening in corporate
America lately.  Think the mainframe is dead?  Think again.  MVS sure is
optimized nicely for I-O, isn't it?

Another thing a lot of people just don't get:  Companies will almost *never*
get rid of stuff that *works*.  Think your boss is going to shell out the
bucks to replace all of those "old" COBOL applications that work great, just
so they can be migrated to a "newer" language like C++ or Java?

No chance.   Even if your boss would like to, someone up the ladder will
almost certainly shoot it down.  Need instantiates change, and if there is
no need, you don't go messing around with entire systems.

Fact is, COBOL, although cumbersome in some ways, is an ideal tool for a lot
of businesses.  This is an important point I think, languages are tools, a
means to accomplish a task.  Maybe COBOL isn't as "flexible" as C, (or so
the C purists would say) but it sure is handy for a lot of COmmon Business
Oriented stuff, isn't it?

I really wish these bozos would shut their pie holes forever.
