I thought about this. There are some very lively minds who frequent this 
forum and I reckon they are just dispirited and bored.

Fortunately, my News provider seems to be doing a better job of filtering, 
so I didn't see the original post in this thread.

But the responses are tired and lame.

The world has moved on and "computer programming" as most people here 
understand it, was a phenomenon that was useful in the latter half of the 
last century.

Nowadays it is about mashups, social media,  Web Services,  SaaS, the cloud, 
and gluing objects together. This is simply a mystery to people raised on 
procedural code.

So you have some really smart people who are at a loss as their power 
diminishes and their jobs disappear. Nobody seems to know where to go next.

Learn Java? Python? PHP? C#?  What's the point? Soon as you do they'll be 
replaced by something else...

I read a very interesting article the other day by someone who is saying 
that maybe the time has come to drop Java.  (I don't agree, but he made a 
very good case...) Ironically, this came at the same time as I was 
approached by the local Polytechnic who need a Java tutor for a few hours a 
week to help some struggling students. I declined, although I was tempted 
(and honoured to be asked). But it indicates that even Acadaemia is a bit 
bewildered by what is happening.

Kids are building apps in minutes, with no knowledge of "computer 
programming" and some of them are very good.

A whole generation is arriving who want cross-platform applications that 
will run on their phones and tablets and for whom the internet is a familiar 
neighbourhood. Business has to change to meet this way of doing things. It's 
no good saying: "Our central accounting system will process your application 
and we'll let you know."  or... "Sorry, we can't accept that form of payment 
as our systems can't deal with it..." or... "can you tell us your 12 digit 
account number so we can assist with your enquiry?".

Today's consumers just laugh and walk away. They take out their smart phone, 
log in to the Net and go straight to an online shop that can respond 
instantly, and take their money in any electronic form that suits them.

Today's small shopkeepers are becoming showrooms for the Net.

People come in, examine a product, ask some questions about it, then buy it 
from the Net at a cheaper price. (I've been talking to quite a few of these 
shopkeepers and they are being forced to close. The whole way in which 
people do business is changing and the new generation have no problem with 
it at all.)  It is only a matter of time before it "filters up" to Banks and 
Insurance companies and other pillars of the establishment.

Already, here in NZ we have a Bank (KiwiBank) which has almost no premises. 
It exists only online and does ALL of its business online. It posted record 
profits for the year and people are increasingly moving to it.  The account 
fees are minimal or non-existent, they don't treat you with suspicion and 
condescension, and the profits stay in the country and benefit the 
community. It is a pattern for things to come. (Don't think for one moment 
they process transactions in COBOL on a mainframe; they don't.)

I've been working on some SQL Server stuff recently and it involved almost 
no "computer programming" at all. It came down to researching and selecting 
libraries with the functions I wanted. Object libraries, of course, as 
everything is objects and layers. I glued it together in a couple of days 
(mostly spent on experimenting and testing) and was pleased to see 
everything work. In effect my solution was a single Gateway component that 
just served up the needed functions from prewritten libraries of objects.  I 
used C# as the glue, but I could have used any OO language or script. Here's 
the good bit... after doing some more research I realized I could improve 
it. I swapped a couple of .DLLs and it went from 16 MB to 3 MB and it ran 8 
times faster! No programming, just selecting different objects. (In fact, 
the upgrade was implemented by replacing .DLLs, not by changing any code.) 
It now supports SQL Server 2005 and 2008 (including R2), and runs on 32 or 
64 bit platforms. I haven't tried it with SQL Server 2010/2012 but I have no 
reason to believe it won't work, and, if it doesn't, there is no maintenance 
programming required, just a different set of libraries.

The bottom line is there is a diminishing requirement for "computer 
programming" skills, not just in COBOL but in anything. Learn OO concepts, 
learn scripting, and you're there. The rest is mashups, APIs, and Dynamic 
Link Libraries... (The current .Net framework supports over 150,000 
prewritten libraries of objects, that are tested, debugged, and good to 
go... That is a lot of wheels to re-invent...)

I plan to retire at some point  (ironically, I was thinking maybe 2015 :-)) 
and devote my time to writing. (I'll probably do some hobby programming 
because I enjoy it, but I won't delude myself into thinking it has any 
relevance in the real world...)

And I'll be spending less time here as it is becoming less fun...(some of 
you may have noticed I already spend less time here than was once the 
case...)

I might start my own blog. I haven't up til now because I haven't really had 
time to devote to it (a good blog requires a continuous effort) and this 
forum has allowed me to make observation and comment, without having to do 
it on a daily basis, but as interest here declines I feel increasingly I am 
just wasting my time posting here.)

Never mind... life goes on :-)

Right on!

The PC files are not really a "backup" they are the development site. (You 
can copy the whole directory, of course and save it somewhere else as a 
backup.)

Web servers (the device that runs your website publicly, and allows access 
to it from the Internet) always have a root directory which is usually the 
domain name. However, the physical directories on the server are redefined 
as "virtual directories" and it is the virtual directories that you can see. 
The virtual root directory is often defined as "wwwroot" and all the 
subdirectories from that will constitute that web site. (The server can have 
more than one root directory if it is serving several domains.)

So, in your example, the webserver will store your site at: 
C:\MyInternetDomain\wwwroot    (I removed the hyphens because they are best 
avoided and you should get used to using "CamelCase"). This is translated by 
the DNS server to be http://MyInternetDomain Don't worry too much about 
this, think of it conceptually as a "root address" for your web site.

When you have completed your web site and are ready to publish it, use FTP 
to connect to your web server. (I use SmartFTP which is a free tool and 
really excellent.) Of course, if you are using IIS as the web server, 
running it locally on your development machine, you can just copy your 
development root directory (C:\MyWebsite\*.*) to the IIS wwwroot directory. 
If you use Dreamweaver, it will allocate a server for you (the server 
facility is included in Dreamweaver) and you don't need IIS. You configure 
your Dreamweaver server through Dreamweaver and it will do the "FTP" when 
you tell it to. (It is really a local virtual redefinition of your 
development directories, or it can be a separate local copy of your 
development directories, depending on how you set it up, but it seems just 
like it is remote.It CAN also be remote; there is a fair bit in setting up a 
Dreamweaver develpment server and you need to experiment and read the Help 
extensively. ) The configuration process is where you tell it what virtual 
directories are to be used and they are set up on the new local server. (You 
can also use Dreamweaver to manage your actual remote server in this way, 
but I have never done that. I like to test locally before I ever make 
anything public. Generally, my web pages don't crash...)

Whether you use IIS or the Dreamweaver server (Visual Studio will also 
create a web server if you need to run ASP.NET pages) you test them in the 
local web server and then you FTP the whole virtual directory structure to 
your remote web server and try it from there.

Confusion can arise because FTP sees the real physical directories on the 
server, but TCP/IP (your http:/// access) sees the virtual directories.

>Are there "relative links" that are coded

Yes, you can code relative paths. In fact, coding absolute (physical) paths 
is highly discouraged. Dreamweaver has a tool that will scan all your links 
and change them if you need to.


>  Does Dreamweaver take care

Yes and no. You have to set it up and tell it to... See setting up a 
Dreamweaver server, above.


Some other points that might help...

1. If you use Windows explorer to double click on an HTML file in your 
development structure, it will open in your Browser just as you would 
expect. If you double click on an ASP or ASP.NET page in the same 
development folder, nothing happens. This is because the ASP pages MUST have 
a server to interpret them.

2. If you try to view the same pages in Dreamweaver they will both work 
because Dreamweaver provides the necessary web server.

3. If you try to view the same pages in Visual Studio they will both work 
(VS also provides an instant web server to run the ASP stuff), but they may 
not look how you expect. This is because VS sees them as isolated pages and 
is not aware of the "site" with it's ancillary CSS files, images, and so on.

4. I use Dreamweaver to create new (non-ASP) pages and Visual Studio to 
create new ASP.NET pages. I find the Dreamweaver servers a bit daunting to 
configure but it is really because I'm more familiar with IIS and it gives 
me (personally) a better level of control. YMMV. You have to try stuff and 
see what works for you.

5. If you ONLY use HTML you don't need a local web server (although it is 
really helpful to see that all the images, CSS, etc are working properly.) 
You can FTP direct from your development directory hierarchy to your remote 
web server. Some people debug on the remote server but there is always the 
risk that someone will try and access while you are working and it is not a 
good thing if they are frozen out  or get an under repair notice one minute 
and the next minute it all works again... It is also a hassle to have to 
keep posting and unposting "under repair" notices.

I realise all the above seems a bit intimidating but it really isn't so bad 
once you start doing it, and the results are well worth it.

those fancy .NET classes, useful as they are,  did'nt fell from the 
skies but had to be written and debugged.  Nonetheless the use of 
libraries and modules and framework will continue to rise (and is in 
deed a useful thing). Nothing bad about that, as long as there is

The increasing usage of frameworks is a convenient thing. But on the 
downturn it brings you a lot of complexity into your system. By writing 
stuff from the scratch, you have to fight your own bugs. By using a 
framework, you buy a promise that some (to you anonimous) programer has 
done the job well (or maybe even better than you could on your own). 
Most of the time, the promise is kept. But sometimes, by importing some 
fancy libraries,  you are importing just crappy implementations. And 
this is where serious trouble begins.

This is my point I'm never tired to stress on.

"Simplicity is prerequisite for reliability"  -- E. W. Dijkstra

In one of his transcriptions (EWD???) Dijkstra bemoans that there is 
still no scientifical method for reducing complexity in software. He 
died in 2000. I guess this problem is still unresolved.
