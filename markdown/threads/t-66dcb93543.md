Stricly speaking, BCD has nothing to do with COBOL. Although COBOL
is often the only language that implements BCD.

BCD is very much hardware dependent. On IBM mainframes, it's in the
machine. In other environments (say Borland PASCAL on PCs), its in
software.

› Anyway, I need to write a routine (in C) that

What you need is called a specification -- it describes EXACTLY
what format the data you will be processing is in. I suggest
you talk to whomever is sending you the data.

For small volume, low data rates, you can strip it down to
bytes or nibbles (depending on whether it's packed or not) and
just play ye olde power of 10 game.

There are two styles of packed numerics in COBOL: signed and unsigned.
Each digit occupies 4 bits. In a signed field, the last 4 bits hold
the sign whose value excapes me at the moment (it has been awhile since
I've read a dump). If I remember correctly xD is negative and xB or xC
are positive. There are no imbedded decimal points.

Unpacked fields hold a single digit in 8 bits. The value of these are based
on the codeset of your environment, i.e. ASCII or EBCDIC. There are two
types of signed fields, sign trailing, where a '+' or '-' is the last byte,
or sign zoned, where the sign is merged into the last digit. (sorry, I do not
have my translation table with me). Again, there are no imbedded decimal
points.

These are pretty much universal throughout COBOL. Differences primarily
have to do with the enforcement of boundaries.

Those data formats are dependent on the particular machine you are on, to
some extent. The packed BCD and zoned BCD terminology reflects the system
360 architecture, but some of the other COBOL implementations (in
particular Realia and Micro Focus) have carried it to the PC platform to
make life easier for mainframe experienced people (apparently). In the
packed version, two BCD digits are placed in each byte, beginning with the
first, in high order to low order sequence. The low order byte has only
one BCD digit in it (in the high order nibble), and has a sign indicator
in the low order nibble, x'C' for positive, x'D' for negative, or x'F' if
the the variable is unsigned. For example, +12345 would become X'12345C',
in a five digit, three byte, packed BCD. -12345 would be X'12345D'.
unused left digits (if present) are zero filled.

In the zoned decimal, or unpacked format, each byte contains one BCD digit
in the low order nibble, and a filler of x'F' in the high order nibble,
except in
the low order byte (confused yet?). The low order byte has a sign nibble
using the same values (x'C" for positive, x'D" for negative, x'F' for
unsigned) as in the packed format. The result is +12345
becomes x'f1f2f3f4c5', -12345 is x'f1f2f3f4d5', and an unsigned 12345 is
x'f1f2f3f4f5'.

Hope this helps.
