When I was 37, I went back to school because I was laid off from the factory and
went to school to get into the computer field.  I now work for a Community
College as a COBOL programmer.

Knowing COBOL and JAVA may be useful.  Some of the new COBOL compilers for the
PC use OOP.  I would guess a lot of jobs go to contractors and developers.  Look
for some of the local developers and see if they have temporarty contract jobs
available.  If you knew CICS and some Database system that would be a plus
anywhere.

Try banking, investment firms, the government, and almost every company in your
area.  Find a local job specialist who knows how to evaluate your resume.  There
are still COBOL jobs out there, but it could take a few months to find them.  If
you get rejected ask why, in a nice way, and then ask for leads on jobs for
other companies in your area.  Not all jobs are advertised.  Follow up on the
resume and ask for further leads.

If you cant find anything right away, taking some classes couldn't hurt.  Just
about all big corporations want people with experience, but they can't always
find someone with experience; never assume a very talented person with
experience is going to apply.  Many places have to accept people with less than
a Bachelor's Degree.  Every country is different, in the US sometimes we have
govt programs for layed off workers to get further training; if something like
that is available it might be worth it.  You could always go into networking.  A
few courses in Oracle might lead to a certificate program.

Maybe you are not just a COBOL programmer, but a systems analyst or a software
developer.  That is about the same thing, some people like fancier names.

"programmer/analyst" or "developer"  without much regard for consistency in
meaning, but I'd be real careful  about saying "[they are] about the same

I'll hazard a guess that most of the independent consultants here can tell
stories of clients who have staff  "programmers" who can program and program
well, but who are not able to to the design, development and system
integration. That's why consultants have any work at all: because there are
times when staff resources are inadequate to the task, and outside help is
needed.

Shakespeare had a point when he said, "That which we call a rose would by
any other name smell as sweet;" but so did my grandfather when he opined,

she'll still be a pig."
