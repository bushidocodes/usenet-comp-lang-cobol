Note I used the word 'merger' not 'take-over' - the latter is bad enough
with the blood-letting that goes on called 'rationalization' - but
unfortunately that's the name of the game.

One reason, cited in the newspaper article regarding mergers, was - so
often what is forgotten is the corporate philosophy of the respective
partners. Close to home it was talking about a merger between two
successful companies, TCPL (Trans Canada Pipelines) and Nova. To date
the shares have taken a nose-dive and they are about to lay-off in
excess of 1,000 staff.

Let's make it more personal; you and I each run successful software
companies, let's say 30 staff each. You are into an area I covet and
your approach/software appeals to me. I make a marketing ploy to
interest you in my offer. I'm not looking to benefit you, but me, even
though I make it sound that way to you. We eventually agree, although we
both have different objectives from this merger, and it takes place.

Silly 'staff' things like you give your folks free coffee and cookies -
me, I'm the tight-wad and demand they put a dime in the box for each
coffee. Stupid, but these small things can turn into grievous irritants.
(Pause : In reality I don't have this problem, 'cos I just go from my
bedroom/office and make a cup of coffee for moi <G>).

Ignoring the staff aspect - who's the boss ? One of us has to take
'junior' position and that also applies to our departmental heads -
here's where you hit the first level of dissatisfaction - right at the
top of the organization.

At the true business level, what about our respective standards
regarding system design and programming. You are not unhappy if your
people use GO TO in a restricted tight loop. Me - the phrase 'GO TO' is
absolutely verboten, and this goes on ad nauseum through are programing
approach. 

Move on to our customers, I am a little more lenient when the customer,
just as the project is being finished, asks for a couple of
enhancements, entailing say 3-4 weeks more work, (I've covered this by
'upping' the original price). You work to a tighter time scale, giving
the end user a 'fairer' price, but you are adamant, "A contract is a
contract" and if the customer wants that they are going to have to pay
more money.

Take all these staff or organizational differences and increase the
staff to 2,000 or 3,000 then you've got real problems. In short order
the good people from both of the partners will leave. Sadly, the only
way a merger really works is when one partner is the dominant, because
of cash resources, and 'merger' is used as a substitute word for
'take-over'.

My own examples to the problem - hope that helps explain it.

Jimmy, Calgary AB
