Although the real answer is much more complex than this, the short
answer is:

In the US, "W2" implies that the individual is an employee of the
company and a "1099" implies that the individual is an independent
contractor performing work for the company.

› I assume this has some kind of tax implication. Is the

› payment to a tax account?

...and yes, it does have certain tax implications. W2 statements list
employer witholdings such as social security tax (FICA), Federal
Income Tax, State Income Tax, Local Income Tax (if applicable),
Medicare Tax, employer sponsored pension payments, employee savings
plans, and many other various and sundry witholdings and taxes
depending upon the status of the employee.

There are a lot of specifics involved in non-U.S. citizens working in
the U.S. but generally here is what it is:

1) You need an H1-B visa. This visa allows you to work in the U.S.
for a set period of time, which may be extended. This is in lieu of
moving to the U.S., setting up a permanent residence and acquiring a
Green Card (which really isn't green!).

2) Most foreign workers in the U.S. that I know of are contract
programmers, but they don't have to be. The company that hires you
must give documentation to the INS (Immigration & Naturalization
Service) supporting your request for the H1-B visa by indicating they
are hiring you. You could be a W-2 or a 1099 employee. Either way,
you will have to pay U.S. taxes on any income you derive here in the
U.S.

3) You can do this on your own, but there are many consulting
companies both here and overseas that you can contract with that will
handle all of the details for you.

4) The only other thing you need to be aware of is the quotas set by
INS on H1-B visas. If I remember correctly, the quota is set on a
country by country basis. Many large corporations have requested that
the U.S. Congress increase the quotas because of the need for more
foreign (which sometimes equates to cheap) workers.

Hope that answers your questions.
