```
I recently retired after 12 years of contract programming in 
Cobol, C and SQL on Unix. Out of 16 contracts, 6 were bad and 
10 were good. In retrospect, I realize there is a 
criterion that would have rejected all 6 bad jobs while 
not rejecting (false positive) any of the 10 good ones. 

Here is a way to assess the employment tone of a city, using data from
a single source: 
http://www.bea.gov/newsreleases/regional/lapi/mpi_newsrelease.htm
Using C = Percent change 2004-2005 and I = Per capita personal income,
the formula is ROUND(((1+(C/100))^6) * (I/1000)), which approximates
income six years hence.
Passing score is 49 (,000)

Results:         
  Bad gigs
Greensboro NC     40 
Pittsburgh PA     46 
Bentonville AR    40 
St Louis MO       47 
Champaign IL      28 
Akron OH          42 
  Good gigs
Chicago IL        49   twice
Jacksonville FL   49 
Albany NY         43   (C was higher when I was there in 2000)
NYC-NJ            62   twice
Minneapolis MN    53 
Houston TX        62 
Orlando FL        53 

Highest scores go to Bridgeport CT, Washington DC, San Francisco CA, 
San Jose CA, Boulder CO, Trenton NJ, Boston MA, Houston TX and NYC.
Lowest scores (omitting Gulf Coast) go to Pine Bluff AR, Flint MI,
Yakima WA, Merced CA, Danville IL, Saginaw MI, Canton OH and Champaign
IL.
Large cities with failing scores are Milwaukee WI, Kansas City MO, 
Indianapolis IN, Providence RI, Cincinnati OH, Columbus OH, Seattle
WA, Detroit MI, Cleveland OH, Riverside CA, Buffalo NY and Rochester
NY.

The formula is measuring locals' belief other jobs are available.  
An employee puts up with a lot of abuse if (s)he believes, rightly
or wrongly, losing the job means selling the house and moving.
Companies in such places devolve to a management style based on fear
rather than accomplishment, a self-reinforcing dynamic that is a sure
path to eventual failure. 

Note the criterion is not specific to the IT industry.
```
