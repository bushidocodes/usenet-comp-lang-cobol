We use 2 terms - On-call - if they can get hold you, and you respond
it's 3 hours plus any actual work time, *but* you do NOT HAVE to be
available, eg if drunk or on family visit etc - in that case they go to
next on list
the other is standby, where you MUST be available - you get paid two
hours each night for that (three for each day at the weekend), and there
is sometimes a rota for that, mixing senior and junior folk (for
experience).
Personally, I am rarely on standby (only for weekends with major
implementations), and refused to go on the rota, as I have family and
other commitments - having said that, I've been on-call for nine years
continous, and because of being a 'family man' am usually 'in' when the
call comes ! Tough, eh, but comes with the job sometimes!!

We started up the production five nights a week after polling remote
terminals at customer sites. Reports came off in the early hours of
the morning.

needed.

I know that some practices are different in the UK but here's a list of
my experiences of the on-call thing.

Generally speaking, people get paid something extra for being on call,
because simply being prepared to be called out is socially disruptive.

Then, generally overtime is paid from the time of the call to the time
you get back home with a guaranteed minimum; sometimes two hours,
sometimes four hours.

Anything that implies that there is no recompense for being on call is
not acceptable. Also, there is some slack about when you turn up in the
morning - nobody expects you to be back in a nine if you only went home
at four that morning.

I remember an occasion back in the mid-70s. We had an arrangement where
there was one guy on call (stay at home) and another on stand-by (can go
out but leave contact numbers) in case the on-call guy couldn't be
contacted. One weekend when not on call, I was disturbed during a game
of hunt the sausage with a lady friend by an operator who actually came
round to my house (something to do with my phone being off the hook)
because he couldn't get hold of the on-call or the stand-by. Triply
livid, I asked him to go back and read me the bits from the dump over
the phone - another 0C7 solved by phone - and was able to continue with
my night's entertainment. There's nothing like being called out when
you're already on the job, so to speak.

One company that I am aware of is trying to handle this on an equal basis,
across all programming groups.

What they have found is that each group is different, and as a result has
allowed the "upper" management of each group to establish a fair plan for that
group.

The Operating Systems support group's have all adopted pretty much the same
plan.

Each group puts most of their staff on a rotating assignment for both primary
and backup support.

Some groups assign an individual to backup support for one week, followed by
primary support the next week.

Since there are 12 to twenty people in each of these groups, this means that
you have beeper support for two weeks,
and then nothing for a couple of months.

The Beeper schedule is established at the begining of the year, and it is your
responsibility to trade with someone else when conflicts in your personal
schedule arise.

Based upon historical research, you are payed an extra 21 hours of pay for the
week you have PRIMARY. (Secondary hardly ever gets called.) According to my
buddy, when they first started, the 21 hours didn't seem like enough - but
changes to support methods
reduced the calls, and now the guys-n-gals are actually slightly ahead of the
game.

Everybody caries a personal pager, and is responsible for his/her assigned
product set as the "expert". The primary on-call is the
first point of contact and will attempt to fix the problem by themselves. If
necessary they contact the product expert or other staff
members for assistance.

It seems to work pretty well, and since the product expert does not get payed
when called (unless they are the Primary), this adds a little more importance
to making sure that training and "how to" documentation is provided to the
rest of the staff.

/s/ Bill Turner, wb4alm
