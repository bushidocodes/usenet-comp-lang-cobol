I am fortunate enough to be a telecommuter, and for the most part a
COBOL programmer.

http://telecommuting.miningco.com/library/weekly/aa100797.htm

Eight years ago, I was a "Commuting" COBOL programmer, spending much
of my time on airplanes. But the internet has changed all that.

But not only the internet, mainframes have changed too. I connect to
three different mainframes via TCP/IP connections in three different
cities from my home office.

Now that we have the technology, the hardest part is re-training
middle management. With telecommuting, the counting of elbows and
a**holes won't work. The quality and timeliness of the work to be
performed has to be the main goal. Some managers will never figure
this out, and resist this change.

Still, telecommuting isn't want companies would prefer. The old
addage "What's In It For Me" applies. They would much prefer a work
force that is where they can see them, and/or cheap(er) labor. So, in
order to be a telecommuter:

You either have to:

1. Have a skill set or knowledge base that they are in desperate
need of.

2. More cost effective

3. You are an employee or contractor currently on contract for them
and they are endanger of loosing you, due to relocation, family
considerations or better opportunaties.

I've been telecommuting for about 2.5 years. I'm a 18yr veteran with
extensive Cobol skills. But it's not the 18yrs of experience or
Cobol, but more specifically my knowledge base of financial and
particularly mortgage processing AND my programming skills. And maybe
to a little luck. I didn't go looking for it, it found me.

For Cobol programmers, Y2K work might make employers more desperate.

Here in St. Louis, Edward Jones Company offers work to telecommuters
that are willing to work part time (no more than 32hrs/wk). I know of
4 former women programmers that "retired" to raise their family, now
telecommuting. Jones, signs them up as part time employees without
benefits. It's cost effective for Jones.

In my case, when my mother passed away and my father had his heart attack
(both happening eight hours apart), I offered to quit in order to be at home
with my Dad. Rather than risk losing me, I guess, management offered me the
option of working from home full time. Since then, I've decided that I like
this telecommuting thing, and now I only go into work on an as-needed basis.

And I don't even bother dialing in or anything! I simply make all my
appointments with all my end-users for a specific day, go in and get all my
spex, then come home for a week or two, write all the code (I use both COBOL
and Visual BASIC), then go back into work to do my final compiles and create
the finished product. Then it's simply a matter of installing, training the
users, and getting my next assignment.

On average, I go into work once a week, or once every two weeks. Works great
for me. No office politix, no having to stroke egos, no bothering with rush
hour commute, I have my own bathroom, I don't have to wear a stupid tie (or
stupid work cloths), I'm saving a bundle on gas, I get to set my own hours
(which can be as weird as I want them to be)... and the company saves on
office real estate (I don't need a cube any more), and they get the work
done on time (since I don't end up having to spend hours on end gossiping
with everyone about the latest events). And, since I have my own PC setup,
the company didn't need to supply me with any of the hardware or software.

For me, it's been a win-win situation all around.

I'm just sorry that it took a major family tragedy to make this all happen.

The answer might depend on the platform that you are developing for.
For my PC client, I use Tritus SPF/PC for an editor, and compile it on
my MBP package I have at home. Unfortunately, they run an older DOS
version, and remote access won't work because the application can't
find the C:drive. So, I drive to their shop with the source code and
recompile and test there.

For my mainframe customers, compiling at home isn't necessary, and
sometimes just not possible because of some the IBM extensions on
mainframe Cobol II. I have a local number dialup (and 1-800 too) that
connects to mainframes in 3 different states using SimPC and
connecting via TCP/IP directly into the mainframes (must have latest
versions of MVS to do this).

My work in this area for a service bureau. There are about 15-20 of
us spread out across the country. Most use SimPC (from Simware), but
some that are local to the service bureau, or live in rural areas (ie.
areas needing LD connection), dial into their client/server using
Shiva (prior to that, PCAnywhere), which in turn, connects to any of
the 3 mainframes. Since I lack access to the client/server, I lack
the documentation, calendar, and other miscellaneous facilities. But
that has been remedied by having an address on the client/servers
cc:Mail, having voice mail at the service bureau, and having all
documentation sent to me on a cd-rom using Acrobat Reader.

Technology today is absolutely wonderful.
