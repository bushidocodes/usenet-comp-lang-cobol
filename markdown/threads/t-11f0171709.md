No. Foxpro has its own programming language, which is not COBOL or even
"COBOL-like."

> He has copies of the Cobol progams in 'text' form (I'm not sure if that is

This is good, because this sounds like the 'source code.'  You definitely do
NOT want to lose this, as it more than likely contains details of the
business logic which are not documented anywhere else.

>   Should I be thinking along the lines of translating the lines from
Cobol's format to an ActiveX format

Cobol "format?" Active-X "format?"     Whatever you mean by "format",
"COBOL" is a programming Language, "Active-X" is an adjective describing a
certain type of executable file (if you really want to know, it's an
in-process OLE server).  You cannot translate/convert/port from a
programming language to a file specification... that's like asking if you
should convert your apples and oranges into dump trucks.

> Or is there some sort of utility that would make the job easier?  I'm
completely out of my realm here...

Yes, I'd have to agree.  You need to find someone a lot more knowledgeable
about "software development" in general and the business system in specific.
I'm not even sure there is a 'correct' newsgroup for this kind of thing.
But, since you are starting with a COBOL system which needs maintenance,
comp.lang.cobol is about a good a place to start as any.

No. Apples and monkeys-beating-drums.

He needs a COBOL compiler for Unix, not a different programming language.

He can:
1. Reprogram his existing program's concepts into a new language (Foxpro) -
if the new language can handle the concepts. This is very, very close to
starting over.
2. Get a new compiler to allow his existing source code to work with the new
operating system. This will entail some (usually small amount of ) work on
his part in adjusting his existing program.

Even if he opted for choice #1, it is problematic whether Foxpro is capable
of doing everything his COBOL programs accomplish. Foxpro is not a Turing
machine; COBOL is.

Converting batch command streams from one environment to another is not
necessarily easy.  IBM mainframe JCL of either the MVS (now z/OS) or VSE
variety is especially obscure and each contains functions not found in
other environments. 
The utilities such as sorts, file copy utilities or report writers may
not be present in the new environment or have different syntax (the
HP-UX version of SYNCSORT comes to mind where it has a modern and
readable syntax as opposed to the obscure position, length mess on z/OS
and presumably VSE).  Depending on how much JCL is used and the amount
of change being made to the application the exercise could be quite
interesting.  

My comments about mainframe JCL come from 10 years of DOS for S360
(releases 18 - 26) and 17 years experience from MVS to OS390 where I was
technical support either as an applications programmer or systems
programmer.
