Your sarcasm is appreciated, but I wouldn't put these languages
in this sequence. While this sequence may be historically right,
it certainly does not reflect the state of affairs.

Which is: Algol and Pascal are academic languages,
they have no commercial significance.

C is language of choice for almost anything.
It replaces both Assembler and Pascal/Fortran.
But ... it's too damn close to Assembler.

C++ has a good future, but most people don't understand it
or cannot program in it. As understanding grows,
it will be used in more sensible ways.
I saw a number of cases when C++ is used just as C
where declarations can appear in the middle of the program.

Java is a marketing trick, which, in my opinion, does not have
a big and bright future. Or maybe it does, but I don't feel it.


COBOL is predicatable, and (I don't believe, it can happen!)
it checks arithmetic overflows and does other things
useful for bean-counting. It is also well understood.

But ... the language is sick, I cannot write in it.
I've written COBOL lexer/parser and the joke of the day
was: "Can you write your parser in COBOL?".
It was supposed to be regarded as a hint about
working in a labor camp (gulag style).


As to job situation, COBOL is certainly on a cheap end.
I mean, there are many of them COBOL programmers out there,
so they are cheaper than, say, C++ programmers.

As I see a certain future for C++, I would recommend to
go and learn it.

The future for C++ is rather bright, becasue you can redefine
some aspects of language using classes. There are classes
(from Rogue Wave) that can do money calculations in decimal
digits with overflow checking. So in couple iterations C++
will be able to do COBOL thing, but not vice versa.

I understand, COBOL guys may have objections.


Vadim Maslov

Where I work, we have open positions for COBOL programmers with extensive
CICS experience that we cannot fill. Our management is talking about
special bonus pay to keep employees with COBOL, CICS, and mainframe
skills. I mostly do new development, rather than maintenance, so this is
not just keeping the ancient payroll system running.

Also, the contract programmer agencies that we work with (like AIC, for
example) do not have enough COBOL programmers to supply customer demand,
at least here in St. Louis.

Judging by the number of job ads in this NG, I would say there is plenty
of demand for experienced COBOL programmers.

It's anybody's guess how long COBOL will be around, but the "installed
user base" is big enough that corporate inertia will keep it around for
several more years, if not indefinitely.
