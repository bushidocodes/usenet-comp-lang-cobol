This is pretty much the same problem that DOS programs have in writing to 
USB printers.

First, why a virtual box? Most old DOS programs run well under XP's command 
prompt.

Second, an excellent fix for the issue is RPG (Report Program Generator). It 
intercepts writes to LPT1 and stashes them in a file. Meanwhile another part 
of RPG is polling the file's location looking for any activity. When a file 
is found, RPG sends the file to the printer of your choice.

Third, you can redirect printing to any printer via pooling a shared printer 
port. The technique is explained at

http://members.shaw.ca/bsanders/printfromdos.htm

Forth, its "queue" not "cue."

I don't think I have admitted that, actually; but it's moot, because

In US English, a distinction is typically made between "blue" (used
for the color, among other things) and "bleu" (used to describe a
variety of cheese). But "blue cheese" is also frequently used,
synonymously with "bleu cheese", and even in contexts where "bleu

doesn't appear to be meaningful.

For all these, and other homonymic substitutions, I prefer the
preferred or traditional form (what a prescriptivist would call the
"correct" form). This is a different case from variant spellings (such
as "meter" / "metre", "color" / "colour", etc); here two words with
different, well-established meanings and spellings (since English
spelling was regularized in the Early Modern era) are clearly being
confused.

Descriptivists and prescriptivists of various stripes can all agree on
what's preferred in cases like this. The difference is that the
prescriptivist describes it in terms of correctness - positing rules
for English usage - while the descriptivist does so in terms of
preference.

> and my personal bugaboo:
> 
> uninterested/disinterested

This is a third case - confusion of two similar words with
traditionally different meanings.

It's possible to have a descriptive view of English and take a
prescriptive stance: you can say that English is defined by, and
evolves through, usage, but go on to promulgate a set of rules for
using it. Those rules might appeal to etymology or regularity or
various other principles, or they might simply be stated as your
personal preferences. (The Augustans thought making English more like
Latin was a good principle, for example, which is where we got the
absurd dictum against the "split infinitive", still upheld by many as
holy writ.)

Those prescriptive rules might be taken up by others as
recommendations; in some cases, they can even be enforced to some
extent, for example by editors.

I personally recognize that the descriptive position on the nature of
English is correct, but that doesn't prevent me from trying to
maintain a fairly rigorous set of rules for my own spoken and written
usage. I don't regularly critique others' usage, because there's
little reason to do so; but when I taught college composition, for
example, I did my best to explain my preferences and the reasons
behind them, and to indicate when I thought it likely that other
readers would share them.

We used to live at the end of a dead-end road. There was a sign some 20 yards 
from the last intersection saying "Dead End." After we'd lived there about 
five years, the county highway department replaced the sign with one saying 
"No Outlet." 

Within a week or so, we noticed a dramatic increase in the number of drivers 
that would come all the way down to the end of the road, turn around in our 
driveway, and leave -- from maybe three or four a year, to two or three a 
*week*.

I finally called the highway department, explained the situation, and asked 
them to put the old "Dead End" sign back. Eventually, they did. And the 
traffic returned to its former levels.

The part that really baffles me is that:
a) the distance from the sign to the end of the road is only about 300 yards;
b) the road is straight, flat, and unobstructed for the entire distance;
c) athwart the road at the end is a guardrail, clearly visible from the sign;
d) behind the guardrail is a fence, likewise clearly visible;
e) behind the fence is a hedge, likewise clearly visible;
f) beyond the hedge is Interstate 69; the traffic is not only clearly visible 
from 300 yards, but *audible* as well.

How *anyone* could think, even *without* a sign, that that road went anywhere 
besides to my house, is completely beyond my comprehension.
