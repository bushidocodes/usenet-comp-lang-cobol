So, to be qualified to talk about object-oriented design, you need to
actually write code and do it, is that what you're saying? Fine. It's
been done in shops around the world. What's your problem?

› Object orientation is the latest fad but seems little more than the

The reason you are an old fart is because you don't know the difference
between modular programming, structured design, and object-oriented design.

You think because your crap works, it doesn't require improvement? I'm
sick of maintaining your so-called "simple" "non 0C7 abending" spaghetti
bullshit. Here's a clue: you don't have to perform through exit anymore.
Oh, by the way, you don't have to put periods after every statement either.
Here's another tip: try a fucking perform-until-end-perform instead of
your artificial "modular" method of pulling out loop code and making it into
a sub-paragraph.

And that's just structured design.

Object-oriented design allows a *small* group of people the ability to
make design changes that are automatically implemented at lower levels.
High code re-use and easier managability of large systems. Since my
system at work consists of hundreds of COBOL programs with about eighty
copybooks, I'd really like to see something implemented to manage that
design better than fourteen sets of increasingly-detailed structure charts.

Just because it works, don't make it the optimal solution.

-Remo
