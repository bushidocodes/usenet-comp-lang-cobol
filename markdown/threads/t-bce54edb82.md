```
So I've been doing some COBOL evangelism.

There's this posting on dev.to
<https://dev.to/bugmagnet/cobol-not-dead-not-dying-not-even-idle-1e3k>
which is generating the usual flurry of comments.

Then there's exercism.io which someone on dev.to pointed me at recently.
As you can see from my profile, I've been busy
<https://exercism.io/profiles/axtens>.

But it bugged me a bit: no COBOL. Perl's there. Tcl/Tk's in the wings,
but no COBOL. Not sexy enough? So I raised the issue
<https://github.com/exercism/exercism/issues/5020> and, yeah, got my
bluff called.

I'm not really in a position to start and maintain the language. Some of
you guys are. And some of you are in a position to mentor those who take
on the task of learning COBOL too. The infrastructure is there and the
path of least resistance is simply to convert the existing tasks into
COBOL rather than coming up with unique ones.

There are other language learning sites. What draws me to it and away
from CodeWars is that one downloads one's tasks, solves them on one's
own machine, and then uploads the solution.

It's a bit embarrassing really: I learned COBOL on a VAX 11/750 in 1983
and never once used it for work as I ended up in IT Support roles. The
last time I did anything in it was to write a couple of RosettaCode
solutions. Getting me to start and maintain exercism's COBOL stream is
like asking me, having had a couple of years of high school German, to
start and maintain a tertiary course on 1800s technical German.

There is work out there for COBOL programmers. It's just that the
current generation is dying out. Exercism provides a framework for
longevity of knowledge beyond the aging blood and bone of the current
experts.

-- Bruce
```

```
The problem is that it takes too long in COBOL.

The current generation are computer literate and will write a shell
script in minutes to solve things like file manipulation.

The age of EDP and the techniques we used is basically over. (EDP in the
sense of "Electronic Data Processing, or "IT" as we now like to call it,
is still a major part of computing in most corporations and is likely to
be for the foreseeable future. COBOL does it reliably and well, so you'd
think people would be using COBOL...)

Why aren't they?

It is because the whole world requires immediate gratification and no
one's going to wait for the overnight batch run... billions of people
are moving data every day and they are interacting with databases that
are themselves re-configured on demand; flexibility is everything,

Many "COBOL Programmers" are bewildered by things like FTP and editing
web templates in HTML...

( Those are just two of the things I have been asked to do recently...
provide components that can be plugged in to COBOL, so a "COBOL
Programmer" can make them happen. Much of my time now is spent "adding
value" to existing COBOL systems because no one wants to grasp the
nettle and rebuild. Eventually, they have to, and it is much more
painful than if they had migrated in a controlled way in the first place.)

And yet, the rising generation are learning HTML/CSS after school, and
they can use FTP from a command box...

I have nothing but respect for the incredible effort that has gone into
GNU COBOL and I would be using it if I could. Without OO and COM support
it simply is not a good fit for the environment I need to do business in.

As for tutoring people in COBOL, I have spent many years of my life
doing that and it was enjoyable and rewarding.

But I can't in all honesty do it now. People (non-hobbyists) spend time
learning something because they expect to get a reward from doing so;
How could I, in all conscience recommend someone to learn COBOL when
they could be learning XAML or HTML/CSS or Java or C# or Python?

Years ago ('60s into mid '70s) I jumped at the chance to help people
learn COBOL and even managed to get a couple of my friends to become
professional programmers... But the world has moved on.
```
