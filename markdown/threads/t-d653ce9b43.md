I was working in Information Systems at the shipyard which built the Hopper
(Bath Iron Works in Bath, Maine) during the time the ship was being built.
For the first time ever during the construction of a ship, a division (IS),
"adopted" a ship as it was being built.

We worked closely with the prospective officers (and later the crew as they
started to arrive) to provide them with information on the ship, BIW, Bath,
etc., as they readied to come to Maine and start their lives with the
Hopper. Members of IS participated in all of the major milestones of the
ships construction, including laying the keel, launching, etc.. I personally
participated in "driving the wedges" during launch (an activity whereby a
large number of people work together to drive wedges under the keel as the
ship sits in the ways, so that it rises to the point where it can be easily
launched with little pressure exerted), the initial "ignition" of on-board
computer systems and rode the ship during a transport from Portland back to
Bath.

Unfortunately, these activities struggled to an end after BIW was sold to
General Dynamics, as first a general layoff helped to decimate the ranks in
IS then a mass exodus from IS ensued (including me) when the IS division was
sold to the devil for pocket change (i.e., outsourced to Computer Sciences
Corporation).

Oh well, the Hopper remains a beautiful ship named for a remarkable woman.

Saturnalia
