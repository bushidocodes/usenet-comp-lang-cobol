As I understand the original debates, God said don't eat this bird,
that
bird, etc., but didn't say which could be eaten. So the rabbis tried
to
figure out the rationale God used (sorta like a programmer trying to
deduce (I almost said 'divine') the logic necessary to code a user's
request).

They finally gave up and instituted their own rule (again, similar
to business programming): those birds mentioned
in the Bible as being acceptable in the Temple were OK (pigeons,
quail, etc.) and by extension, domesticated fowl (chickens). All this
was
formulated and fixed into law by the third century. Then came
Columbus and turkeys in the new world.

"Can we eat turkeys?" said the colonists.
"What's a turkey?" said the sages of Europe.
"It looks like a big chicken (kosher), but it has this red thing
hanging down over its beak like a vulture (non-kosher). And
we don't know whether it's a scavenger (non-kosher)?"
continued the explorers.
"Mutter, mutter." said the rabbis, as they turned to the
original program's flowcharts.

Ultimately the rabbis decided a turkey was more like a chicken
than a vulture and said "Go ahead and eat the damn thing."

I don't know whether a specific request has been made in
the case of penguins, but I bet the official rabbinic response
would be "Get a life, fool."

P.S.,
I do know that you can't make a penguin kosher (or a chicken,
for that matter) simply by circumcising it.

Whether one "considers" penguins to be birds or not is irrelevant. They *are* 
birds. And chickens are not, in and of themselves, kosher. For a chicken (or 
any other animal) to be kosher it must, among other things, be slaughtered and 
butchered in a prescribed manner. I believe there are also religious laws 
governing who may perform the slaughtering and butchering, and establishing 
standards for cleanliness and for the handling of the meat, all of which must 
be followed strictly for the meat to be kosher. I'm quite sure that a chicken 
cannot be kosher, no matter how it's slaughtered, if it is butchered on a 
table that has also been used to butcher hogs.

unclean, and cannot be kosher under any circumstances. Cattle are clean, but 
beef is not necessarily kosher, depending on how the animal, and later, the 
meat, are handled. Processed and handled in accordance with kosher law, 
the milk and cheese of a clean animal (such as a cow) can be kosher, and as I 
said, so could beef -- but a cheeseburger can't.
