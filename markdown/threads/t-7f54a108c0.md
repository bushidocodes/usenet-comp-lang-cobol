A kind of news that never stop to amaze me how Cobol evolves.

Though I don't have an experience yet on the declining Cobol trend in
my area, it seems that Pete Dashwood comments makes me wanted to shift
if not associate my Cobol codes to other technologies. From day one
Pete suggestions for migration popup into mind, the main reason why
people wanted change is because of COSTING. It is not;
a.  Cobol programmers are declining (this is a myth because Cobol
language is easy to learn and new breed of Cobol programmers are even
growing, and most of them are coming from a desktop-based environment
who knows .NET)
b.  lack of functionality (this is even more a argument in disguise.
Cobol language is 'basically' created for business functionalities and
not API-based programming. Most developers tend to associate business
programming logic to systems programming and this is not supposed to
be. In fact, ALL that I need in business data computing is in Cobol
language already. Re-inventing the wheel is not an advice.)

If Cobol vendor thinks that a functionally matured language is the
advantage AND keep on raising their fees... then eventually they'll
killing the mere existence of it. And with this I will positively
agree with Pete.

I haven't look at PERCobol IDE (with JSPs) product yet but it's worth
a try. Maybe online Cobol product development cost will cut it down if
this product is reasonable.
