Open COBOL - my loosey-goosey definition :-

A COBOL compiler, written using object-orientation,  which is "Open",
that is, programmers and add-on tool developers,  have complete access
to the compiler's source to see, in COBOL syntax, how it handles COBOL
verbs/syntax, generates executable code and how it interacts with

<<<
Hmmm...who cares? Most of today's COBOL programmers don't even
understand the beginnings of what is generated. The days when we used to
patch object code are long gone. The skills of yesteryear (whether we
like it or not) are now irrelevant.

As for how it interacts with specific operating systems, again, who
cares? As long as it does. I have written COBOL on every major mainframe
(IBM and the BUNCH), at least 5 intermediate range machines, and PCs
using everything from DOS and OS/2 to NT. I have never worried about the
operating system. My code runs. I don't use vendor-specific constructs;
the same code runs across the range. Re-compile and away you go.  As far
as COBOL compilers go...I have worked on 2: 1 for Burroughs (remember
them? they were the half of Unisys that WASN'T Sperry...), the other for
a very obscure CDC machine. In the first case I was actually patching
machine code and disassembling object code to determine how the compiler
(which had been written for Burroughs on contract and for which they had
no source and no-one capable of maintaining it) worked. I then modified
it and improved it. Eventually, I was getting letters from Detroit
Michigan asking me (in Auckland NZ) what pass 7 of their compiler did,
and could I please fix it to take magnetic input, etc. All very
interesting and exciting at the time, but in the long run a total waste
of time. Today you will find the B500 in museums or, very occasionally,
as a comms server somewhere. I wrote stuff like syntax scanners which
parsed using transition diagramming and JIT keyword extraction, and all
sorts of interesting stuff...for what? It had a useful life of around 5
years and it took a year to do.

I believe the whole question is academic as "computer programming" as we
know it has a limited remaining life span. History will show that
"computer programming" was a phenomenon of the latter half of the 20th
Century. By 2020 there was no longer a requirement for it. (Just look at
all the rubbish surrounding standard laguages.  1st, 2nd, and 3rd
generation languages (4th generation is H.S., and 5th generation
exsts in the alcohol befuddled minds of some marketing men who never
programmed a computer in their lives and wouldn't know a compiler if it
bit them in the arse) like COBOL, C, BASIC, and Assembler, are already
in their twilight years. OO, CORBA, COM compliance (ActiveX, OCX) will
not push back the frontiers (although they are all steps in the right
direction and bring closer the scenario I shall describe shortly.))

It is too costly, labour intensive, and time-consuming.  OO is included
in this category. Once all the necessary objects and classes are
established the rest is just stamp collecting. The next step is to
develop intelligent classes which can spawn their own methods using
firmware building blocks.

No, it's NOT Science Fiction and I AM serious! The advent of artificial
intelligence, voice activated interfaces in standard English, and more
and more "user-friendliness" will enable non-technical people to
interact easily and effectively with a computer until it does exactly
what they want.

Imagine the following scenario:

Small businessman with no technical knowledge, goes to corner shop and
buys "small business" computer. Brings it home unpacks and connects it
using extensive colour handbook and video. Switches on and says: "Print
me an invoice."

computer: "What's an invoice"

user: "An invoice is a document which should be created whenever we SELL
PRODUCTS to a CUSTOMER. It shows what we sold and how many and how much
they cost. It also shows the taxes and other sundries which the CUSTOMER
has to PAY."

computer: "OK, I know what PRODUCTS and CUSTOMERS are and I recognise
the SALE and PAYMENT as standard business transactions, so I know when
to trigger this INVOICE. Shall I print a sample INVOICE using my sample
CUSTOMER and PRODUCT databases?"

user: "Yes, please"

The machine produces a very poor attempt at an invoice. The user
interacts with it and explains that the figures need to be in columns
and what should go on the document (like, description of the product,
quantity, unit cost, and the necessary calculations.) After about 20
minutes the machine is getting very close and within half an hour the
user has his invoice. 

There are 2 fundamental things which make the above scenario possible:
1. The CONCEPTS of CUSTOMER, PRODUCT, TRANSACTION etc. can be built into
firmware as standard intelligent agents. By today's standards this will
require some interesting programming using techniques derived from OO,
neural networking and heuristics, but there is no doubt that it is
achievable. (I used the INVOICE as an example. In fact, the "small
business" machine would almost certainly know how to produce an invoice.
It would start from a standard template and allow the user to modify it
by interaction.) Machines for all kinds of applications would be
produced simply by installing the appropriate firmware. LAN/WAN
connection and connectivity would be fundamental, built in, and almost
completly transparent to the user.

2. The use of ITERATION. Trial and error. The learning curve. Don't even
try and get it right first time (that is one of the major failures in
the way we currently develop systems...waste a ton of time getting it
"right" then find it all has to be changed anyway....(you may have
guessed I am a devotee of RAD for project management...<grin>)) Charles
Darwin showed that trial and error has been the way we have developed
over the last 3,000,000 years. It works. Compare what we are today with
what we were 3, 000,000 years ago and you start to see what I mean. Then
translate it into computer terms. 3,000,000 years against the 15 billion
year life of the Universe is the blink of an eye. For a computer, the
same blink of an eye can be 10 minutes. Hence I make no apology for
claiming the system can produce an invoice within half an hour. Not only
that, but the invoice will be EXACTLY what the user wants because he has
specified it interactively himself.

So, goodbye "computer programming" with its attendant mystique, jargon,
tedium,  cost, and intensive mental effort. I believe we will see
systems like the one above within the next 15 years. The technicians of
the future may study COBOL the same way we study Latin and Classical
Greek; purely as academic exercises which help to grasp the concepts of
what is going on. The man in the street will have no interest
in, or need to learn, computer programming as we know it.

Nowadays, I see COBOL as purely recreational to allow me to develop
useful and fun applications for today's primitive operating systems.
Why? I've done it all my working life (since 1965) and I enjoy it. It
also means I can generate a few quid on the side, but I am under no
illusions as to the future of it. My career (what's left of it) does not
lie in computer programming. It lies in managing the technology,
assisting users to come to grips with it, and showing Management how it
may be of benefit to the business. It lies in applying imagination to
technical projects and designing things carefully and iteratively, in
close co-operation with end-users. 

I therefore cannot share your enthusiasm for Open COBOL. It isn't worth
the effort and who would appreciate it anyway?

>>>

<<<
Yes, I agree. It's not just COBOL. The problem is endemic to all current

techies I have to manage can't B.S. me...) and was totally
underwhelmed. A breeding ground for hostile classes (viruses),
inefficient because of its virtual machine (no wonder MS are trying to
persuade everyone to their MS-specific version), and esoteric. 

>>>
Take my definition about Open COBOL and kick it around and modify it -
but I think you will see it represents at least a model of what we
should be doing. The way to achieve it - OO. Open source as with
MicroFocus for classes. As Adele Goldberg said of Smalltalk - in
hindsight they should have designed Smalltalk without being
platform-specific. Same applies to COBOL - write an indexed file on a
mainframe - why can't I read it on my PC etc......
<<<
I dunno...why can't you? I can. In fact I can transfer and manipulate
files on the mainframe or the PC running from either platform. You need
some layered software to do the ASCII to EBCDIC translation (if you are
transferring the files) but I have no trouble accessing data on a
mainframe indexed COBOL file from my PC and vice versa. I also don't
understand the fuss about transportability. COBOL was originally
designed to be, and always has been, transportable. I mentioned above
that I have always written transportable COBOL. (I've encouraged others
to do so too, with varying degrees of success...)

>>>
Let me know.
<<<
Well, I've certainly tried to do that...

I know you probably will disagree with what I've written but It is what
I honestly believe. I see little future for the existing methods of
interacting with computers, at least as far as current languages go. The
real answer has to be a direct interface with the end-user, in English
(or whatever....but not Technospeak). I believe it is not that far away.
There is no reason to believe that technology will stop its exponential
growth in the new Millennium. As hardware races ahead in leaps and
bounds, it would be tragic and pathetic if we continue to use the same
old dull, unimaginative, techniques for software development.

These days I no longer encourage young people to think about computer
programming as a career.

if we provide the right technical environment. Unfortunately, in some areas

I'm suggesting that computers in the next couple of decades will recognise

understand it will no logger be required. It's a bit like the collapse of
the cottage lace-making industries during the industrial revolution. Once
machines could do it faster (and everyone said it would be impossible to
build such a machine) the home industry was relegated to a hobby or pastime.

fun now...!)

1. We are able to build computers and operating systems capable of grasping
concepts.

2. The power of iteration. Trial-and-error. The user will interact with the

done so, it will then forever be able to reproduce that solution. As a
spin-off it will also have expanded its concepts, a kind of true learning.

networks. I see the concepts being embodied in firmware to provide cheap
computers tailored for a certain basic job. Add to this a smart operating

invoice-amount, account-name, and new-balance - and then invoke the correct
method/object.

When I presented this feature to one IBM mainframe group, they "shrank in
horror" at the maintenance ramifications of this "define-it-as-you-go"
