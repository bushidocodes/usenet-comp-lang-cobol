(Apologies to those who receive this note more than once - or for which this
is a duplicate of a previous request to "preview" this.)

If you do not like (or more accurately do not particularly want to
"tolerate") my usual BLUNT comments on COBOL and/or the COBOL Standard,
please feel free to delete this note NOW.

   ***

For the last year or so, I have been meaning to create some sort of
documentation of what I *personally* like and don't like in the (evolving)
draft COBOL Standard.  The opportunity has come up for me to share my
opinions with a private group in the near future, so I have created a
PowerPoint presentation on this topic.  I do not know what impact this might
have on others, but I did think that it would be worthwhile to share this
with others who might be interested.

Therefore, the slides of this presentation can now be viewed at:

 http://pw2.netcom.com/~wmklein/goodbad.pps

To view this, you do need to have a PowerPoint viewer.  If you use a current
Windows operating system, a free copy of the viewer can be downloaded (if you
don't have it already) from:


http://officeupdate.microsoft.com/downloadDetails/Ppview97.htm?s=/downloadCat
alog/dldPowerPoint.htm

(I do not know how/if those with OS/2 or UNIX systems can use PowerPoint.
You might want to check out one of the Microsoft WebPages for this.)

These slides are INTENDED to be accompanied by my personal "talk" - so some
of them may not be entirely self-sufficient, but I think that, in general,
they are understandable "as is."

  ***

Let me stress a couple of the bullet-items that do appear in the slides:

1) There is *NOTHING* "new" in this presentation.  Every "negative" comment
that I include has already been presented to and discussed (and rejected) by
J4 and WG4. (Some on multiple occasions and some for things that have changed
back-and-forth multiple times in the draft.)  This doesn't mean that they
won't change their mind <again>(with enough reasoning/input) in the future,
but does indicate that a "group - or two - of experts" does explicitly
disagree with me (either in principle or practice).

2) I do *NOT* claim to be representing anyone other than myself. I don't
claim to be representing the "average" user or implementor - much less any
group or national body. I think that even those who agree with many of my
general points, would disagree with some of my rankings or individual
evaluations.  Even I would probably change them from day-to-day depending on
what was frustrating me most at the time.

3) Although (as you will see in the conclusions) there are some serious
problems (IMHO) that would cause me to "vote against" making the draft
"official" if it were put to a vote today, there are lots and LOTS of really
good things in the draft (again in my opinion). I think that it is worth
"salvaging" and still hope that it can/will be.

4) I have marked this presentation as "copyrighted."  Therefore, please feel
free to pass on my URL (and/or download the presentation) - but please do NOT
copy it or modify it for your own use (or at least not without checking with
me first).

  ***

"Bureaucracy"

Earthlink, or whatever) has a monthly "bandwidth" restriction.  Therefore,

 - I will be out of town (and out of the country) from the afternoon of Oct
20th thru Nov 3. Therefore, if you want to give me feedback (PRO or CON;
flame or otherwise), you will need to do so before this Wednesday noon or in

find the slides interesting and thought-provoking - if nothing else.

folks who use it on a daily basis have no input to the direction it 
should take. Funny thing is that the market should be driven to supply

want to supply and the users simply have to accept it and make do with

  While it is unlikely that I would take the time to read the new

discussion as to the future of COBOL. It seems that the vendors are 
running the show and the last thing they want is any input from the 
users.. yes that's us. The folks who use COBOL every day and have to make 
it work and figure out what is going on. But, there job is not to make 
our lives easy.. it's to make money and the greater the differences 
between implementations the more we have to spend on various complies and 
tools to debug the code.. and the more money the vendor makes.
