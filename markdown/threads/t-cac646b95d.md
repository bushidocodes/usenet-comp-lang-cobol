Klint Kendrick wrote:

> Another thing that might bring my neophyte status into the light is the fact that
> I actually thought you were talking about headhunters in this thread rather than
> agencies.  Silly me.

My first encounter with employment agencies was back in 1976.  Back then
the word "headhunter" was quite pejorative.  Most would be offended if
called "headhunter", and less than 10% would use the term themselves.

By the mid-1980s the word "headhunter" was in common use.  Many agents
openly called themselves headhunters, and probably more than 90% would
(if asked) smile and say "Yes, I'm a headhunter".  

Now it's become the standard term.  There's not a shred of pejorative
left, and in order to be insulting we call you "pimp".  (If the client
screws me and doesn't pay me, will you go beat him up?  If not, then
you're not a pimp.)

If "headhunter" is now a legally defined term in the jurisdiction where
you operate, then use the word as required by your headhunting license,
but otherwise we mean anyone who makes their money matching people with
jobs, whether they make their money in a lump sum when I start a job (or
90 days later) or as an hourly percentage.  Note that the commission is
what makes the headhunter -- being paid by the head.  Someone who works
in the personnel office of a company is a headhunter if and only if they
get paid commissions.

Agents of headhunting companies are called headhunters even when they
work on salary.  Such people are actually apprentice headhunters trying
to work their way up to commissioned or partnership positions.
