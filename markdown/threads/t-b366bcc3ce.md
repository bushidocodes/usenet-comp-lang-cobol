COBOL is the most important programming language for business
applications. The sheer volume of COBOL code (including the volumes of
COBOL/CICS code) still runs the bulk of the most important aspects of
business. This situation alone dictates that COBOL will be around for a
long time. Many new lines of new lines of COBOL code are written every
day. COBOL has a long history of industry standards which has made it
more predictable and stable than most other languages. Business tends
to rely (or use to) on stability and predictability.

COBOL has had a history of being pronounced "dead on arrival" since its
inception. It has been pronounce dead so many times that the only
conclusion one might be able to come to now is that COBOL is immortal
(>g>). As for COBOL being an "older" language, virtually all of the
so-called popular languages in use today (BASIC, C, PASCAL) are old,
too. Most of these have their beginnings in the 1960's and 1970's. It's
fashionable to slap a GUI development environment around these
languages, created some new buzz words and call them new. Improved,
yes. New, no.

The COBOL's of today offer all of the GUI features as these other
languages. The big difference is that COBOL is designed for business
applications--the other are not.

Use of the term "older" nowadays seems to imply that the older item is
no longer fit for use. COBOL is a long-established programing language
that is periodically augmented with modern (sometimes trendy) features,
and which are internationally agreed under the ISO umbrella. It is the
only commonplace programming language that has this continual review
process.

A COBOL compiler _must_ conform to the international standard if it is
to be sold commercially. It will be very hard, if not impossible, to
find a C compiler that complies with all items laid down in the ANSI
standard. Thus, COBOL follows a widely understood and implemented
syntax.

The COBOL concept is that of a business-oriented language, providing
functions that are relevant to business applications and not some
pie-in-the-sky computer sciences idea of what a business application
is. Yes, COBOL is a real-world language and will continue to be so for
the foreseeable future, provided that business applications continue to
exist.

Generally the age of any product (including COBOL compilers) is a measure
of maturity. The longer a product has been around, the more opportunity the
vendor has had to incorporate small added-value functionality into it that
typically comes from end-user feedback and/or 'failed sale' analysis. While
COBOL compilers are standards-based (usually ANSI), all vendors that I know
of have implemented COBOL features that exceed or are not covered by
standards.

As an example of product maturity, although the Oracle database manager is
not exactly young, it still does not have basic database management
functions such as in-place reorganization and database rollback that have
been in Unisys DMSII since 197?.
