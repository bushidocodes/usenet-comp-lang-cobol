In December of 1988, shortly after the elder George Bush beat Michael
Dukakis, I took one of these tests.  Over the years I would say
that test was one "lucky break" for me, and I would also say the
test was not so fair.  It had nothing at all to do with COBOL, but
rather had a lot of strange questions, having to do with logic.
That is, if there where 5 balls of black and red color, with odd
and even numbers on them, and 2 balls of red color where selected
from the set, and they had even numbers on them... et cetera.

I remember halfway through the tests thinking the test was stupid,
and giving up, then five minutes later I decided to look at one
of the questions again, just for fun, because I had nothing else
to do... and I managed to figure that question out. Then I later
went on to answer another question.  Maybe even a third after
that.

In the end I'd imagine that I didn't do well on the test, but I
did better than some others, I am sure, because later they brought
about 7 of us into a small conference room and told us we were
the ones that passed the test.

It was the The Travelers Insurance Companies, in Hartford,
Connecticut.  Later, in January 1989, they put me through a
10 week training course... and that was the start of my COBOL
career.

12 months later I left the company, because I was tired of
Connecticut, and was offered a COBOL job in New York City, doing
securities processing on Wall Street. Actually, Broad Street,
a half block from Wall Street.

That's very similar to how I got into the business in '80.  A state
civil service comission (operating much like the federal civil service)
had a posting for a "Computer Programmer/Analyst Trainee" with
qualifications being any 4-year degree and a qualifying score on a
battery of aptitude tests.  All but one of the tests had nothing to do
with computers or programming.  They dealt with logic (like your
example), abstract thinking, pattern recognition, and reading
comprehension.  These are fundamental aptitudes that all programmers
must have and probably can't be effectively taught.  If a person has
these qualities, he can be taught the techniques and technology.  If he
doesn't have these qualities, he probably should seek a career in
another field.  I still think the use of these kinds of tests is valid.
