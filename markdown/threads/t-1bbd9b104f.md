```
I would like to start a thread which should be interesting to many of
us.  We've all heard enough Y2K opinions to make us sick, but that's
not what I'm asking for here.  In most of the Y2K posts I have seen in
the COBOL newsgroups, the professional programmers have expressed
opinions similar to my own: briefly, that Y2K may be a big problem,
but it won't kill us.  So let's get a little hard data, where our own
personal experience gives us direct knowledge.  I would like to hear
from as many of you as are able and free to answer "What shape is your
shop (or client's shops) for Y2K?"  In other words, where you have
direct knowledge, is your part of the world going to be ready?  If you
aren't a professional programmer, or you aren't involved in your shop's
Y2K work, then your knowledge is second hand; please refrain and let us
get some data from the actual troops doing the work here. :-)

I'll start out by saying that all my clients have either completed Y2K
remediation, or the work is underway or planned on a schedule which
should be finished well in time.  None of the schedules are overly
optimistic, and my conservative opinion is that they should all be
complete by the end of September, at the latest.  There is only one
area of concern, and that is one system that a client has contracted
with a company that I don't have confidence in.  It's an important
system, but I think my client has enough resources to get it done if
that contractor drops the ball.

In my little part of the world, I have full expectation that we will
be ready.  How about yours? :-)
```

```
Judson,

The Unisys side of our shop (where I work) is 99.5% good to go.  All I have
left to install is the Win95 Y2K patches on about 10 PCs.  Federal examiners
require us to do such things, even though I can't think of a soul who even
knows you can run "winfile.exe" under Win95, let alone actually uses it.
The IBM side of our shop should finish final testing in March, 1999.  Must
be all that JCL stuff :~).  We've already seen a couple of bounces in
January 1999, (i.e. tapes coming from the IBM side with 99000 expiration
dates), but we extinguished that flicker before it even became a flame.  We
expect some minor problems, but I'm sure we'll be able to react quickly with
as little disruption as possible.  We have our disaster recovery center
available, just in case there's no power for a period of time or we have
some weird system problem. Our local utility assures us that they are Y2K
ready at the present time.  Besides, if there's no power, people won't be
able to do any banking anyhow, since we won't be able to get into the branch
vaults <G>!  Fortunately, for us, the Unisys check side starts each day
brand new, so the files are cleared every morning.  January 3, 2000 tested
about the same as January 2, 1999 ran in production this year.  The float
calculations we use only allow a maximum of 3 days float on checks, so
testing was rather easy to verify.  We use many "dumb" terminals in our
department that are clueless as to dates, imbedded chips, etc.  We even
tested our software for the year 2015, even though the V-Series we're
running on will probably be replaced with an NX box sometime in the year
2000.

I might point out that 2 of the dates the examiners gave us to test we're
the famous all "9's" tests. I.E. 04/09/99 (the 99th day of 1999) and
9/09/99, which in their minds could be interpreted as all "9's".
Hmmm....I guess I need to learn more about dates.  In my mind, the zeros are
significant digits, but what do I know?  I'm just a programmer, not an
examiner.  So I guess if I use a 1 digit month and a 1 digit day and/or a 2
digit Julian day in my calculations, and check for all "9's" as an end of
file condition rather than the "old fashioned" COBOL "at end" condition, I
could be in trouble!

All in all, it's been a lot of work for us, but I feel comfortable with
where we're at.  It's kinda like riding a motorcycle, though.  You can feel
comfortable, but you should never feel TOO comfortable.

Denny
denden@prodigy.net
```

```
installing the next release before the Y2k test, but whatever release we are
running when we do our Y2k test, if the test succeeds, we plan on keeping that
release through to at least the opening days of the year 2000. One thing I have
```

```
Using (or expanding to) a CCYYMMDD format is the cleanest and safest
approach, and is often also the easiest approach.  However, there can
be complications, such as reformatting huge multi-terabyte databases.
In such cases, it may be more expedient to use some form of compressed
or coded date format which takes no more space than the old date.  For
example, a count of the number of days since some specific date, such
as the oldest date represented in the database.  It takes 8 digits to
store 20000101, but that date is only about 730000 days since the
beginning of the first millennium.  Using a six digit field to store
the day count, you could keep track of every date since the birth of
Christ until approximately 2737.  Anybody foresee a Y2.737K? :-)
```
