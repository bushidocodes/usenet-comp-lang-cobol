colleagues have gotten into a disagreement. my professor said that when
referencing a table you would use the name that is on the occurs
clause. let me create and example:

01 TABLE-DATA.
05 TABLE-INFO OCCURS 10 TIMES.
10 FIELD-1-TBL PIC X(10).
10 FIELD-2-TBL PIC X(10.

so, according to my professor, TABLE-INFO represents the table. i

TABLE-DATA reference the entire table while TABLE-INFO with a subscript
reference one element?

my definition would be that TABLE-DATA represents the entire table,
TABLE-INFO(SUB) represents one element of the table, and
FIELD-X-TBL(SUB) would represent a piece of the element or a field.

3. am i right, or am i wrong? and please explain.

thanks in advance.

Ricky, you aren't thinking it through far enough. In your specific
example you might be able to call TABLE-DATA 'the table', but you can't
make that general conclusion. Take a look at this:

01 TABLE-DATA.
05 TABLE-SUB PIC 9(02) COMP.
05 TABLE-MAX PIC 9(02) COMP.
05 TABLE-INFO OCCURS 10 TIMES.
10 FIELD-1-TBL PIC X(10).
10 FIELD-2-TBL PIC X(10).
05 TABLE-UNSUB.
10 FIELD-1-UNS PIC X(10).
10 FIELD-2-UNS PIC X(10).

Now, would you call TABLE-DATA 'the table'? I think not, because the
group item TABLE-DATA contains other items. You simply cannot make
the assumption that the group level above the item containing the
OCCURS is 'the table'. If sometimes it is not, then always it is not.

'The table' in COBOL is the collective of the item containing the
OCCURS clause; no more, no less. COBOL has no built-in way to refer
to the entire array collectively in a MOVE statement. The group level
TABLE-DATA in your example happened to encompass only those entries in
'the table', therefore it also encompasses exactly 'the table'. But
it IS NOT 'the table', any more than any other group item IS the
subordinate item.

Ricky,

IMO you are correct. At our shop we refer to a "table" as the entire
group item comprised of all elements (i.e., TABLE-DATA). The
occurrences within the table are referred to as elements or "buckets."
At least that's how we refer to them both in conversation, comment
statements, and internal documentation.

Of course, when referencing tables in a program you will almost always
be referring to a single occurrence (or the fields therein) via a
subscript or index. However, tables are often initialized by moving
SPACES, LOW-VALUES, etc. to the group item.

TABLE-DATA is a group element. when you move the group element to an
elementary element (of PIC (X), Cobol will move the entire
element contents and either pad or truncate the end of the sending item so
it fits into the receiving field. TABLE-DATA contains the table.

› my definition would be that TABLE-DATA represents the entire table,
› TABLE-INFO(SUB) represents one element of the table, and
› FIELD-X-TBL(SUB) would represent a piece of the element or a field.
›

TABLE-DATA does not represent the entire table. It contains the entire
table. There is nothing stopping you from inserting more elementary or group
items at the 05 level above TABLE-INFO and then TABLE-DATA will contain the
table and everything else at an 05 level you inserted.

TABLE-INFO (SUB) represents one occurrence of the table TABLE-INFO.
FIELD-X-TBL (SUB) represents one occurrence of an element of the table.

You are generally correct, and I know I am splitting hairs, however I think
the correct terminology is important because when things get complicated, it
is easy to trip up in mental knots. It's like using the right fingering
while playing the piano.

Ricky,

In theory, Toby Flugel and Judson McClendon gave you the correct answers and
definitions. In practice, what Johan Potgieter and David Furin told you is
also true.

Example:

01 theWhole.
03 elemBefore pic x.
03 tableRow occurs 10 pic /or subdivided 05 and deeper
03 elemAfter pic x.

would I in practice define as:

01 theWhole.
03 elemBefore pic x.
03 theTable.
05 tableRow occurs 10 pic /or subdivided 07 and deeper
03 elemAfter pic x.

so that I can MOVE theTable TO something, getting all the elements in one.
Of course, theoretically, again theTable is not *the* table, because now I
can add before and after again:

01 theWhole.
03 elemBefore pic x.
03 theTable.
05 subElemBefore pic x.
05 tableRow occurs 10 pic /or subdivided 07 and deeper
05 subElemAfter pic x.
03 elemAfter pic x.

but in practice I won't due to my standard to have a group item immediately
above an OCCURS which comprises all but onle the elements/rows of the table.

I think that under all these considerations (others an mine) you and your
prof will agree.
