Since "respect", in this context, is a function of tone, it has
everything to do with "'sound'ing like". Unless you believe there's an
essence of respect that exists outside human interaction (which, on
Usenet, is generally confined to language, and hence tone) - which
would be an interesting metaphysical stance, but I suspect hard to argue.

Further, and more relevantly to Bill's point, a title such as "Mr" -
like any other signifier - cannot by itself determine tone. "Mr" can
certainly be used in a disrespectful manner. Rhetoric is a function of
whole statements (enonces) in their contexts.

And I agree with Bill that the title is often employed in just such a
manner - for a false, mocking veneer of respect over an attack. I
don't believe the Good Doc employs it that way (at least I don't
recall him doing so here); and I don't think Bill was suggesting that
he does. Bill was merely noting that because it is often used that
way, as a reader he finds it jarring.

The author's intention does not determine the reader's interpretation.
(While there are still those, such as Walter Benn Michaels, who argue
that intention *should* determine interpretation, they're in the
minority, and it's a hard claim to support rigorously.)
