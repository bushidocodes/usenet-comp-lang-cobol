```
Here's a thought -

03  WS-SUB PIC S9(04) BINARY.
03  WS-HWORD PIC S9(04) BINARY.
03  WS-NUMERIC PIC  9(10).
03  WS-ALPHABETIC PIC  X(10).

MOVE +1 TO WS-SUB.
MOVE ZERO TO WS-HWORD.
MOVE 123456 TO WS-NUMERIC.
*
PERFORM UNTIL WS-SUB > LENGTH OF WS-NUMERIC
        IF  WS-NUMERIC (WS-SUB:1) > ZERO
                MOVE WS-SUB TO WS-HWORD
                MOVE LENGTH OF WS-NUMERIC TO WS-SUB
                END-IF
        ADD+1 TO WS-SUB
        END-PERFORM.
*
IF  WS-HWORD > ZERO
        MOVE WS-NUMERIC (WS-HWORD:) TO WS-ALPHABETIC
ELSE
        MOVE SPACES TO WS-ALPHABETIC
        END-IF.

At this point, WS-ALPHABETIC will contain C'123456bbbb' (the 'b' means a
Blank/Space).

HTH....
```

```
This is better; but the conditions for ending the 'perform'
need to be reversed to prevent the test 'A(11:1) not = zero',
when A is zero.

Also, prior to the move, a test is needed to prevent an
out of range condition, as in 'move A(11:0)' and to ensure
that spaces are moved to B if tally-count is greater than 10.

Also, 'length of A - tally-count + 1' is not necessary, the
compiler will take care of that.

Also, 'length of' is a common extension; but is non-standard.
Use 'function length (A)' for standard COBOL conformance.

With the noted changes:

compute length-of-A = function length (A).
perform varying tally-count from 1 by 1
  until tally-count > length-of-A
     or A (tally-count:1) not = zero
  continue
end-perform.
if tally-count > length-of-A
  move spaces to B
else
  move A (tally-count : ) to B.
end-if.

Efficient, bug free(?), and conforms to standard COBOL.

However, it may not be correct because it is not clear
whether 'initial zeroes' means all leading zeros or those
leading zeros which will result in B having at least one
digit, even it that digit is a zero.
```

```
Sometimes the compiler supplies default values as in   MOVE "A" TO X(3:)

Sometimes the compiler can trap for runaway indices as in   MOVE "A" TO

Sometimes the compiler can generate code to trap for things like divide by
zero.

In general, program failure caused by this sort of stuff or by an attempt to
reach outside the program is very rare in COBOL. COBOL does not often need
(C-type) pointers - I've actually never seen a program in the wild that used
them.

I think it's more of a programmer attitude. COBOL programmers tend to
manipulate the representations of physical things (widgets in the warehouse,
salary on a paycheck, etc.), and it's easy to imagine how these physical
things interact ("take one down, pass it around..."). C programmers tend to
manipulate more abstract, ephemeral, evanescent things. Sometimes these
gossamer constructs really ARE fictitious.
```

```
The ANSI standard specifies that if the POINTER is out of range at the 
beginning of the UNSTRING statement, then the OVERFLOW condition 
exists. If you haven't written an ON OVERFLOW clause in the UNSTRING 
statement, this results in execution passing to the next statement.

In other words, yes, you are correct: the implementation makes the 
checks, and is required to do so by the standard. (Depending on the 
system, this may be the run-time support or may be code generated by 
the compiler.)

However, note that your example shows a blank field for the value 0. 
This probably is not what you want. I don't remember if your example
```
