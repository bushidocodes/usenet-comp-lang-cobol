ML:

This is a complicated question, but I try to differentiate it as
follows:

Intellectual property can be more easily duplicated and sold as if it
were created by the person who merely duplicates it.  For example:

It is possible to duplicate:

films on video tape or DVD.
music on audio tape or CD
books and other printed material using a photocopier, etc.
software on magnetic or optical media

But it is extremely difficult to duplicate a car.  Can it be done?
Yes....but it involves a LOT of time, trouble and expense.  It is
likely not a profitable venture to duplicate a Mercedes, but
duplicating one of the above items can be done quite easily.

Therefore, General Motors or Daimler Chrysler doesn't need to concern
themselves with copyright law as much as they need to protect
themselves with patent law.

Software vendors, authors and musicians do indeed need to protect
themselves with copyright law, because those who choose to violate the
law reduce the financial incentive for writing software, authoring
novels and creating musical works.

This, of course, is my own humble opinion.

Since I started us down this road...

From a practical standpoint, there is no difference between intellectual
property and other property: what is different is how the the owners of
intellectual property choose to make the product available.  In general,
author/artist/owners of intellectual property do not sell - that is,
transfer the title to - intellectual property. (Although they certainly may
sell the title - it is the owner's property, after all). Intellectual
property is generally *licensed for use* rather than *sold.*

Let's say you own a building.  You may, of course, sell that building; or,
you may rent or lease the building to someone, in which case the renter does
not acquire any rights in the product other than those granted by the rental
agreement.  You may or may not grant the renter the right to sub-rent
portions of the building.

Your renter and you sign the rental agreement on the dotted lines.
Congratulations: you have just granted your tenant a  a "use license." The
tenant may use your property, but may not sell it, may not destroy it,
probably cannot modify it;   he cannot do anything with the property other
than whatever permissions you have granted him.

If you own (you created or otherwise acquired title to) intellectual
property, you may rent to whomever you want; and as the rightful owner, you
may specify how and where your property may be used.  Just as with a
building, you may or may not grant the renter the right the "sub-rent,"
solely at your option.

With intellectual property you have an added option: you may license on a
"non-exclusive" basis - that is, you may make copies and simultaneously rent
the intellectual property to as many people as desired. (This is difficult
to do with a building or other tangible property).

I have never seen - nor do I expect to see - in any wholesale or retail
setting "software" for sale; what one sees offered at Software Is Us is a
"license to use software."

On the "software as a service" thing, that is a not a new concept at all,
although the "Department of .NET Hype" at Microsoft Corporation would have
you believe that. Data Processing service bureaus have been around since the
1960's.
