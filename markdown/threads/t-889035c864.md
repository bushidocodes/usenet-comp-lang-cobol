COBOL rather pre-dates the BNF concept of langauge construction. I
suppose it would be possible to put together BNF for it, but it would
be an odd bird. (The fact that a period can terminate things without
waiting for an END -- stuff like that. The whole SENTENCE concept
kinda prevents BNF from being very useful)

The ANSI standard defines it in terms of formats, and any one verb can
have a number of formats. A format can have multipe paths paths that
fork and merge back to together. Actually, once you look at the format
diagram for a few minutes, it make sense.

Online? I don't know. I know it's available from ANSI in a book form.

We have a BNF-style Cobol grammar.
It is written in BTYACC -- YACC with backtracking.
In fact, it's not only grammar, it's a complete Cobol parser.

More info on it at http://www.siber.com/sct/

No standard BNF grammar exists.

The way grammar is defined in the standard assumes recursive descent
parsing with heavy dependences on semantics.
