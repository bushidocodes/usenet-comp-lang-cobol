This time is interesting for those of us who deal with highly technical
writing, as well as normal business communication.  I have made up
words, made nouns into verbs, and changed the traditional rules of
punctuation, in order to improve the clarity of my writing.  I will give
you two examples I would like to see gain acceptance.

ï¿½COBOLï¿½, as most of us know, is an acronym for Common Business Oriented
Language.  ï¿½Cobolï¿½, in my opinion, is a noun.  I, and others, have been
using the word ï¿½Cobolï¿½ as a noun in speech for several decades.  We
don't spell out the letters C-O-B-O-L, as we would U-C-L-A, we say
ï¿½Cobolï¿½.  I assert that the word ï¿½Cobolï¿½ is now an acceptable word in
the English language.  In my dictionary, I have defined the word
ï¿½Cobolï¿½, with a usage of noun, and meaning of  ï¿½The computer language
COBOLï¿½.  I use the word ï¿½Cobolï¿½ in my business writing regularly.

My second issue is punctuation.  You will notice in my previous writing
that all commas and periods following a quotation mark are outside the
quotation.  This is contrary to traditional English punctuation.  I have
changed the rules of punctuation to require commas and periods to be
placed outside the quote, unless they are part of the quote.  I assert
the traditional English rules regarding quotation marks are not precise
enough for this century.  I believe these rules were created to improve
the appearance of writing, not for clarity.  If I was to end a sentence
with a quote, the placement of the period changes the meaning of the
quotation.  For example:

  At the command prompt, key in ï¿½CD ..ï¿½.    (My rules of punctuation)
  At the command prompt, key in ï¿½CD ...ï¿½    (Traditional rules of
punctuation)

Which method is preferable?  Some might argue that quotations should
only be used to quote a persons speech, or a chapter or section name, as
is defined in traditional grammar.  I would assert that a computers
speech is in the same category as a persons speech, and needs to be more
precise than traditional punctuation allows.

I urge ï¿½Cobolï¿½ to be accepted as a proper spelling of the noun.  I also
urge English texts to change the traditional rules of commas and periods
following quotations.

```
I disagree with your spelling of the programming language as "Cobol" 110%
<G>.

The reason doesn't have to do with it being an acronym (which it is) because
that would mean it SHOULD be spelled "CoBOL." (notice punctuation <G>).

HOWEVER, the reason that I absolutely, positively REQUIRE that anything that
I deal with uses "COBOL" is the "Acknowledgement" that appears in the
current, past, and draft Standards (as well in most/all COBOL language
references).  This states in part,

"Any organization using a short passage from this document, such as in a book
review, is requested to mention "COBOL" in acknowledgment of the source."

Therefore, unless you suddenly want to try and take the language definition
out of the "public domain," you, I, and everyone else MUST keep spelling it
as originally dictated. (Alternatively, you may request copyright permission
from the "owners" of the language to refer to it as "Cobol."  However, as
most of the original owners have gone out of business or been acquired by
someone else, you will have some difficulty doing this.  If your "text"
references any extensions from current products, then you would need that
vendor's prior permission.  Obviously, this is a LONG and slippery slope.)

P.S.  The capitalization has nothing to do with it being a noun or not.
"NASA" for example, is always in capitals - regardless of how it is used, as
is IBM and several other nouns. Whether such acronyms are spelled out or
pronounced as a single word, also is irrelevant as shown by NASA vs IBM.

As far as punctuation marks before vs after quotation marks, I use both
variations (depending upon my mood).  The Standard has a "style guide" which
requires that it be OUTSIDE the quotation mark.  However, most "academic"
papers follow the Chicago Style Guide (in the US) and other similar style
guides that still require the "inside" rule.
```

Our standard does not allow "IS GREATER THAN", it requires ">".

This started as my preference to a symbol as I could then "see" the
statement and its meaning without having to read the words describing the
condition.  The important words on that line are the variables being
compared, and a textual relationship operator such as "IS GREATER THAN"

our standard.  One of the goals of our standards is to make all code look
the same, regardless of who wrote the code.  The style of logic constructs,
paragraph names, variable names, etc. are all addressed in our standard.  We
have succeeded in making our programmers a team, with everyone thinking and
writing the same way.  When looking at multiple programs or changes to one
program, we can no longer tell who wrote what sections.  All the code uses
the same style.

We take to heart the idea that effort to write a program is 20% of it's
life, with 80% being used for maintenance.  In our case, it's more like 5%
vs 95%.

Some people might say our standard is too restrictive.  We don't think so.
If a programmer comes up with a style he/she wants to use, it is discussed
with the top few programmers, and if the idea is a good one, it is
incorporated into our standard.
