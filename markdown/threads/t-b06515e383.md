Methinks you have a somewhat cursory knowledge of SAS....

In addition to a wealth of useful proprietary features, SQL (DML and DDL) is a
subset of SAS and can be used easily against all major DBs with very little
effort (e.g., no Compiles, DB2 DBRM's, BINDs, etc.) on different platforms,
even in the same program! After using these features, you'll wonder why anyone
pay big bucks to have DBA's and data modelers slow down their projects (OK, you
probably wonder about that already!).

Built-in reporting procedures (e.g., TABULATE, REPORT, PRINT, FREQ) can produce
complicated reports with no "programming" effort - COBOL programs of 2,000+ LOC
are often < 20 LOC with TABULATE! On the PC side, you can produce
Windows-quality output with the Enterprise Reporter add-on.

Multi-platform support is unsurpassed - within one program, one could easily
consolidate and report on data from mainframe (DB2, flat files, VSAM), Unix
(Oracle, Sybase), NT (SQL Server), and PC (Excel, text files, ACCESS) and write
data to ANY data source on ANY of the target platforms.

If you're using JCL, you'll appreciate how SAS' WORK file and multiple-program
capability dramatically reduces the number of job steps, programs, sort steps,
and files. In many cases, the resulting SAS code and JCL will have fewer LOC
than just the JCL for a COBOL/SORT based solution.

Like the Internet? Use the SAS Internet Server to easily deliver internet
access throughout the organization or the base features to read or write HTML
pages, FTP, etc.

In many systems, you'll see essentially the same report/sort/extract programs
over and over again (tens of thousands LOC). Use SAS' extensive macro
facilities to generalize these routines, and you might see 98% of your code
disappear!

Interactive full-screen data entry and browsing facilities are available with
ONE line of code!

TRY IT, YOU'LL LIKE IT!

First, there is no "SAS vs COBOL". Neither is a panacea and each has its
place. Second, SAS is _not_ a  "shorthand report language". It is a
powerful, full-fledged Turing complete 3GL with PL/I syntax augmented by
an incredible number of 4GL features. Before even comparing SAS
reporting capabilities to other packages, completely master PRINT, FREQ,
TABULATE, REPORT, MEANS, UNIVARIATE, SQL and _all_ DATA step automatic
features specifically designed for reporting. Then ask yourself if you
still believe that a typewriter (Easytrieve) is a more powerful
reporting tool than a PC, word processor, and a laser printer
(SAS).

> For large files, SAS ran very slow.

Because of the way it was written. Show me the program where you claim
SAS ran slow. In more than 2 decades of writing and tuning
high-performance SAS applications I have seen _no_ exceptions from this
rule. SAS is a package whose _basics_ are easy to start using without
understanding completely how the SAS program operates and what affects
its performance. If a programmer remains at this level s/he stops using
and starts abusing SAS. In 90% of all cases, the complaints of SAS being
slow occur when it is used for raw data reduction, i.e. as a sledge
hammer. In these cases, sane people use a several lines of a DATA step
or Macro Language to write a SYSIN for an appropriate OS utility and run
it straight from a SAS program just like a native SAS proc without even
thinking of leaving the current JCL step. The seamless combination of
SAS with OS utes makes it the _fastest_ software on the market today
capable of handling large data - one of the reasons SAS is #1 player in
the Data Warehousing today. I do not know what you mean by "large"
files. I use SAS _daily_ to process files of every conceivable
organization and DB2 tables anywhere from 70 to 500 million rows without
encountering any performance problems or any single DBA or MVS
performance analyst having complained that my SAS programs are "hungry
for resources". Just like in COBOL or anything else, it is the dancer,
not the floor. SAS gets a bad rap for performance primarily because
managers think it can be written by anybody, not necessarily a
professional SAS programmer, and wind up with a "program" representing
nothing else but a multi-level cannibalizing. Interestingly, those same
managers do not expect a COBOL program coded in this manner to perform
acceptably.

> For reports that require specialized logic that requires writing
> "code" going beyond the default available generator options, it's
> best to use COBOL. The report generators are best when you can
> easily use their automatic featurs.  If you have to write code,
> then you might has well do it in COBOL.

Of course, COBOL is a great language for writing "code", but what do you
think a SAS program consists of? Not code? Once again, SAS is _not_ a
"report generator". It is a general programming language that can be
used to write absolutely any program without ever touching its 4GL
features or "report generator" capabilities. Where did you derive the
conclusion that the SAS syntax is insufficient to implement "specialized
logic"? Would you also argue that PL/I syntax is insufficient for this
purpose and therefore it is "best to use COBOL"? Now let me tell you why
it is not best to use COBOL, or PL/I, or C, or what have you to write a
report or produce a list management file, and why it is best to use SAS.
It is simple: Give me the source(s) of data, criteria description, and
the way you want the output, and I will have a professionally looking
report with the _right_ figures on the boss' desk, or in a print file,
or in Excel, or in Access, or in RTF, or distributed over the Intranet
well before anyone coding in the above languages will manage to debug
the first compile. Such is the truth of the real world, and I and others
have a long track record to prove it and back it up. The nickname in my
e-mail address is part of that record. It was my fellow COBOL teammates
in GTEDS, not at all deprived of the sense of humor, who invented the
nickname, and I have worn it proudly ever since.

> SAS was originally developed as a statistics analysis system (thus
> its name). It has a powerful library of routines to do just that.

There is an almost 30 year gap between the system as it was developed
initially, and what it is now. Of course, specialized statistical procs
have remained an unalienable SAS's strength, and nothing in the world
has been able to approach their diversity, depth, reliability, and
precision. However, nowadays it has a "powerful library of routines" to
do not just that but about anything.

> Obviously it's good for that kind of work. Somehow years back it
> got borrowed into every day report generation.

It did not happen "somehow". It was the simplicity, conciseness, and
crisp, clear syntax of the language, and the incredible power and
flexibility of SAS's I/O capabilities that secured it its place in an IT
shop. System people had learned how easy it was to analyze SMF records
using SAS software, and eventually MXG - the Barry Merrill's collection
of SAS programs for performance evaluation and capacity planning -
became ubiquitous having virtually displaced anything else, even IBM's
own CP products. Such things do not occur "somehow", there is a cause
for them. With the course of time, SAS products have developed the
reputation for bulletproof reliability unparalleled in the industry. At
Ford, Quality may be Job 1 (and probably that is why it stands for "fix
or repair daily"); in SAS, it is the job.
