Unfortunately, the errors in the above (even after the marked corrections)
will cause
ANY compiler to produce error messages and they are often not helpful to the
newcomer.

Think about things ... counts, logic...

ask yourself questions like:
" How many friends will be on the input file?"
"How many am I reading?"
"Is there any point in setting an end of file flag if I never test it?"
"How many of my friends are actually customers?"
"Should I read the same file I open or can it be a different one?"

Some further major errors in the above are caused by misplaced and improper
full stops. That's why we got them almost removed from the Language and
replaced with "scope delimiters" (like "end-perform" "end-if" and so on...)
Review your use of full stops...
And have a look at level numbers. They are a most important part of COBOL.
A record must start with an "01" level...

encompasses 19 separate fields spread over 1,000 square miles, with
twice the number of wells and production drill sites as
originally anticipated, Pamela A. Miller, an environmental consultant,
told the committee of scientists appointed by the National
Research Council. 

In the years since drilling started on the North Slope, operators
there have caused an annual average of 409 spills of crude oil,
diesel fuel, and other substances, said Miller, who runs
Anchorage-based Arctic Connections.

"There's about a spill a day at Prudhoe Bay," she said.   Annual
emissions of nitrogen oxides are measure 56,427 tons, and annual
emissions of methane range from 24,000 metric tons to 114,000 metric
tons, she said. 

Those statistics, gathered from reports by state and federal agencies,
are among the environmental impacts that have been far
greater than what was predicted in the original environmental study
done before construction of the Trans Alaska Pipeline System,
she said. 

Despite the industry's spreading reach over the area, "No analysis of
cumulative impacts of North Slope oil development has ever
been done," said Peter Van Tuyn, an attorney with Trustees for Alaska,
an Anchorage-based environmental law firm. The U.S. Army
Corps of Engineers, for example, has not completed a single full
environmental impact statement on any onshore North Slope
development since 1972, he said.
