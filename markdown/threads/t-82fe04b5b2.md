Go to www.murach.com.  They've been selling mainframe texts for many
years - they are very reasonably priced and are designed to get
someone up to speed quickly.  Even experienced folks tend to keep
their stuff for reference, and buy new versions of texts whenever they
do appear.  They have branched out to newer topics lately, but as a
publisher they sell only those things they wrote themselves, and go
for quality rather than quantity of titles.  I have most of them..they
are letter-sized paperback texts, easy to use.  You can't lose.  Where
they have 2-book tutorial texts (yes - CICS and DB2) you will need to
get both.  Can start with their JCL book (their batch scripting
language) but may wish to follow that topic up with the industry's JCL
"bible", "System 390 JCL" by Gary DeWard Brown (do Amazon)
If you do get into an IBM shop and become an IBM "customer", IBM has
online fabulous libraries about EVERYTHING they make (COBOL, CICS,
DB2, etc, etc etc) that are >> FREE << as downloadable PDF books. (The
new stuff too - the UDB version of DB2, WebSphere manuals, MQSeries,
on and on.)  You can have the same libraries their specialists do  . .
on your PC.  Got over a gig on mine at work . . .more than you can
ever read but the trick is to get an idea of WHAT you need to know
(which CAN be scattered thruout that library)and read just that, and
study any topic you need to in whatever depth you need to.  Go to
www.ibm.com and explore a LOT and you may find a lot you can download
- they have improved their developer support to a remarkable level.
(And that's one reason you won't find very much at Borders etc. . . .
there's a virtual warehouse full available and it's all FREE!)
The hard part is learning WHAT you need to know - this was around
before integrated IDE's and each company or dept or more likely team
or developer has their own libraries of reusable code (unfortunately
most companies have never caught on to the reusabiliy thing..usually
developers have to take their own initiatives.)  You will need to know
a number of subjects - the editor, normally ISPF, and JCL, and their
native file structures in addition to DB2 (sequential files and PDS
files and VSAM files), source control programs (differing vendors),
and a number of utilities.  Hit the ones mentioned here to start,
they're the bulk of it.  A lot will be set up for you (like compile
routines)  . .great thing about a mainframe shop is that there
generally are specialists who take a great burden off the developer,
so can concentrate on developing . . sometimes seems a lot easier than
many other environments where one has to try to do it all, like being
an operating system expert, network expert, etc.
 
Do have some free stuff somewhere, and know of some downloadable, will
see if I can round some of it up, may be awhile though.

Hope this is good start - garyh
