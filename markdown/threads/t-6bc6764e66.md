```
Two things happened to me this week which made me stop and think.

1. Somebody I was helping solve a programming problem said to me: "Jeez, 
Pete, you have a lot more tenacity than me. I would've given up some time 
ago."  I assumed she was hinting we take a coffee break, but over coffee she 
expanded a bit. It turned out she felt she was just too stupid to solve some 
problems. I happen to know she is a very intelligent young woman and an 
excellent programmer (in COBOL and JavaScriot (if that counts... :-)) After 
coffee we went back to the problem and solved it. But I couldn't get over 
her self-doubt.

Then I remembered something from the past. I was a young programmer and had 
been tasked with the job of amending a COBOL compiler to do various things. 
(A major one was to patch it so it could read input from disk or tape, 
indstead of punched cards, so that will give you an idea of how long ago 
this was....) There was no documentation for it (it was written for 
Burroughs by a contractor who had since moved on and they weren't supporting 
it, but our business revolved around it.)

All I remember feeling at the time was a heap of doubt. I couldn't do this. 
WE didn't amend system software, people in Detroit, Michigan did that... It 
was totally unfair. I would get fired.  I remember feeling stupid and 
useless. (I am actually neither of those things, but this was a long time 
ago :-)) Fortunately, I had a Boss who was excellent and was also far from 
stupid. He motivated by example and wrote an entire Operating System for a 
machine he had never seen, in an Assembler language he didn't know, in the 
space of around three weeks, in his spare time. (And it ran in production 
for over three years without ever failing.)

I started brushing up my Assembler for the platform and disassembled the 
compiler. My Boss was supportive and encouraging and I really wanted to 
impress him. Two weeks later I had achieved all the things we were looking 
for and I had documented what each pass of the compiler did and things of 
special interest in it. I was a hero, completely unstoppable. :-)

Ever since then I have worked on the assumption that software written by 
humans can be fixed by humans, it just takes time and application. If 
somebody tells me: "That's not possible" I want an explanation as to WHY it 
isn't. Otherwise it falls in the category of : "YOU don't know how to do it, 
and you're saying it's impossible, just to cover your arse."  (An honest 
person would say:"I don't know how to do that, but I'll investigate the 
possibilities if you want me to." Such people have my respect and usually 
end up with my admiration as well.)

 You also have to believe that you are capable of learning anything. (You 
actually are, but life and circumstances, mixed with COIs and 
disappointments, persuade you you are only good at what you know. Not true. 
Given the right motivation you can learn anything.)

When  a certain COBOL vendor couldn't maintain their own product and left me 
hanging on a customer site, I took their product apart and have never needed 
their help since. (In fact nowadays I help others with it...)
It ISN'T stupid to not know something. It IS stupid to not WANT to know it, 
when it pertains to your livelihood.


2. I came across this article which raises a very pertinent point.  How many 
times in your IT career are you faced with the "unknown"? "Never been 
trained on that." "Don't know anything about this...".  Somebody comes to 
you and wants to port COBOL to a Bendix washing machine because their 
toaster just popped its clogs...  Nobody will touch it.

Be the one.

Apart from money and the satisfaction in helping people, there is the real 
joy of knowing you conquered something, that most people wouldn't even look 
at. I know from personal experience that delivering something which they 
said "Couldn't be done..." is one of the sweetest things in life.

In the Article, the author uses the example of a PhD thesis as an instance 
of delving into the unkown. But there are many times in IT when it happens 
too.

http://jcs.biologists.org/content/121/11/1771.full

Enjoy!

Pete.
```

```
Pete:

Excellent post! I think your point is more about the value of being
open to learning, and its intrinsic value, rather than the value of
stupidity.  If we know our limitations and have a desire to expand
them, there is little that we cannot accomplish!

In 1999 I went into the ER with serious breathing problems.  Long
story short, I had a large benign tumor growing in the right atrium of
my heart.  The heart surgery I had would have put a quintuple bypass
to shame, and I experienced some brain damage (a condition called
'pump head') from being on the bypass for 22 minutes.  For a long time
I had difficulty finding words, finishing thoughts, etc.

At that time I had been a COBOL programmer for 24 years, but had stuck
with the old character based types of programs.  What I decided to do
was learn GUI programming from the ground up.  Again long story short,
I learned all the new techniques and complete rewrote all my modules.
I benefited enormously from this exercise (as did all my users).  And
more importantly, because I exercised it, alot of the damage to my
brain seemed to be mitigated.

At whatever age, learning can be enormously helpful and productive.
Thanks Pete for yet another excellent post!

MattC
```
