```
Assuming your COBOL compiler will correctly return yymmdd after 1/1/2000,
you can get by for the next 100 years using this code:

           03  ccyymmdd      PIC  9(08).
           03  ccyymmdd-r    REDEFINES ccyymmdd.
               05  cc            PIC  9(02).
               05  yymmdd        PIC  9(06).
               05  yymmdd-r      REDEFINES yymmdd.
                   07  YY            PIC  9(02).
                   07  mm            PIC  9(02).
                   07  dd            PIC  9(02).

           ACCEPT yymmdd FROM DATE.
           IF yy < 99
               MOVE 20 TO cc
           ELSE
               MOVE 19 TO cc.

This will produce a correct ccyymmdd for all dates from now through
December 31, 2099.
```

```
You could go to a window scheme ... that way you
get the two digit date, then convert it in a way that
will work for the application.  For example, all years
before "50" are considered 2000-2049, and all dates
after 1950-1999. Windowing works fine when the range
of dates are somewhat controllable (like invoicing).  It
is no good for birthdates, etc.
```

```
Mrs Schwarz,
Your COBOL Compiler COBOL85 V2.3C31 already supports intrinsic functions
    DATE-TO-YYYMMDD, DAY-TO-YYYDDD and YEAR-TO-YYYY
that will be part of the next COBOL Standard. If you have a 2-digit
year, these functions provide convertions it to a 4-digit year based on
a sliding window (documentation can be found in the Freigabemitteilung
for V2.2C or in the language manual for version V2.3A).
```
