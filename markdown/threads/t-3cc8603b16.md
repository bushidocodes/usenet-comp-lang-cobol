```
I don't remember all the details, but I can think of at least one problem with 
using a period for qualification.

First, I want to preface this with the fact that allowing
  A.B
as equivalent to
   B of A

was first proposed BEFORE OO came along.

  * * * * *

At least one problem with "A.B" has to do with "text words" and COPY/REPLACING 
or REPLACE processing.  Currently, a period (full stop) is ONLY a separator when 
it is followed immediately by a space.  (There are implicit spaces at the end of 
lines of text and end of source code).

This means that
  99.99
in a picture string is a single text word.  If a period was allowed to indicate 
qualification, then EITHER
 A.B
would be treated as a single text word
   or
99.99
would be come two text words with a separator.

(Remember that during "text manipulation" the compiler doesn't know what 
division one is in or any other "structural" information)

  * * * *

There may well have been other reasons why this was rejected, but this one (by 
itself) seems pretty serious to me.
```

```
That isn't the point.  Various languages use different symbols for
qualification or concatenation or whatever.  Whenever the language is
defined the meaning of symbols from the character set is fixed.  I wouldn't
go so far as to say that ANY arbitrary meaning of a symbol could be
absolutely determined under all circumstances but most of the time it could.
But why bother?  A OF B is just as easy to use and understand as B.A - in
fact it is completely expicit so you wouldn't have to remember the correct
sequence of the variable names.  Remember that one of the original goals of
Cobol was to make the language easy to read (if not self-documenting):
therefore A OF B is preferable in this context.

Quite a few suggested changes to the Cobol language have come from people
used to other languages who prefer to retain their accustomed syntax.

But that is in fact an outlook that will cause a person trouble in using a
new language: if you can't revise your concept you will spin your wheels
until you switch.  E.g.: OO programming.

PL
```

```
Bill, your history in the front of the FAQ is very good, but I 
obviously feel it is not complete in some areas.
This is a hobby horse with me, as one of my favorite hobies is 
researching and documenting the history of
and evolution of computer languages.

There is an incredible amount of fascinating facts surrounding the 
early years of the development of
our industry, and especially around the COBOL, Fortran, Algol, and 
(much later!) C language trees.

I take this offline by saying that I do disagree with your history in 
some respects, but mostly my disagreement
revolves around the major impacts the basic developments, much earlier 
than the 1970's, had.
Starting at the idea of stored program code and progressing upward 
though the idea of using structure to control
the flow of program execution is a much more important chain of events 
than is usually credited.
We take all of that from granted today.

The impacts most of us remember, from events in the 1970's, all had 
their roots in the development of those
ideas by Aiken, Hopper, Mauchly, Eckert,  and more a in earlier 
decades. Especially the ideas that came out of
Burroughs. :)

It's really fascinating. :)
```

```
I have lost the ability to see who actually is saying what about those who 
"designed" COBOL.  If you want to read a (biased but MOST reliable) history of 
the early development of COBOL, just see the section "History of COBOL" in the 
'85 Standard.

There is no (I hope) doubt that the earliest COBOL "design" documents (and 
directions) were influenced by:

A) The desire to create an "English-like" programming language.  (In *theory*, 
one for which non-programming BUSINESS analysts could read the source code and 
"understand").

B) The original design was definitely done when both compile-time and run-time 
resources were "limited" ("memory" and "storage").  It was HEAVILY influenced by 
those doing "unit type" processing with paper tape, punched cards, and similar 
"input/output devices" (This was both for how source code was compiled and how 
object code was executed)

C) Run-time "performance" was (slightly) less of a concern than run-time 
"memory" requirements.  This lead (in part) to some actual "anti-structural" 
COBOL design issues (Sections with priority numbers, ALTER statement, etc).

D) When the "general" information processing (and to a lesser extent computer 
scientist) theoreticians gained "steam" on the topic of "structured" 
programming, CODASYL (who were then doing the "design" changes for COBOL) 
relatively quickly begin looking at and adding "structured" syntactic features. 
These included (but were not limited to:
 - inline performs
 - scope delimiters (End-xxxx)
 - NOT conditional phrases
  - CONTINUE statement
 - (eventually) nested programs

The cut-off for new FEATURES in what eventually became the '85 Standard was late 
in the '70s or very early in the '80s. At that time, "ANSI" (X3J4 COBOL 
committee) did *NO* new development work.  They only took features that had 
already been designed *and* added to / approved in  the JOD (CODASYL Journal of 
Development).  Then X3J4 had to "select" features to include in the next 
revision and then adapt them for inclusion in the ANSI Standard.

Bottom-Line:
 It is true that the very first COBOL document ('60 or '61) did NOT include 
"structured" design as a goal.
 It is also true that CCC started *relatively* early in determining what 
features needed to be added to the COBOL design to support (but NOT mandate) 
structured programming.
 It is also true that "verbosity" (at least with OPTIONAL words) has always been 
seen as a "goal" not a "problem" with COBOL.  (In recent decades, there has been 
movement to make the more "verbose" English-like features optional rather than 
required.)

If there was "contention" within CCC (or X3J4) any other "early) designers of 
COBOL about ALLOWING for structured programming, I certainly have not heard of 
such, nor do I see it in the actually history of the development and design of 
COBOL.  I do, however, see that it was not a "design" goal of the first 
specification and was not a PRIORITY until (at least) the publication of the '74 
Standard (and development of what eventually became the '85 Standard).
```

```
Paul,
  At the time that COPY/REPLACING and REPLACE are processed, the compiler does 
NOT know any syntax (except for those statements).  That is why, for example, in
   Pic 9(10)9.99

the TEXT words are:
  9
  10
  9.99
with the separators ( and ).

". " (period followed by a space) is a separator while "." (period without a 
following space) is NOT a separator.

As others have pointed out, different languages have different rules and 
different things are significant in different ways.

What, in COBOL is called the "text manipulation phase", where COPY, 
COPY/REPLACING, and REPLACE are processed is quite different than other phases 
of COBOL compilation.  Making a period with no following space a separator (for 
qualification) would have DRAMATIC impact on other (existing) code.

FYI,
  When the '85 Standard added the colon as a new separator, this had surprising 
impact on some existing code and compilers.  Depending on which vendor you are 
(were) using, you might want to look at its migration information from the '75 
to '85 Standard for some implications of this.
```
