I don't know the history of unionism, but it would appear from your description that
the guilds were doing 2 of the functions that some unions still do - ensure excellence
in the work and keep the salaries high.  If not every Tom, Dick, and Harry is able to
set up shop as a silversmith, the ones that have proved they know what they're doing
are more likely to provide a superior product.  The public learns that the wares
produced by a real silversmith are of high quality, and so they can command a higher
price.

It seems to be a lot like some of the unions, such as the IBEW, which fosters
education amongst its members and, I believe at least among the electricians and some
other trades, still has an apprenticeship program.  These union members are known for
their higher knowledge of the job.

I think all this is as it should be.
