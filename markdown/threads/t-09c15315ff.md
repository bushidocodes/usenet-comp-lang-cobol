As a result of reading the topics Soundex and Metaphone, I checked up on 
the topic both historically and in terms of programming. Starts with US 
Census and then I see that some 6 states in the U.S. have applications 
for using names associated with vehicle licensing.

At this point I'm not clear as to its usefulness in the general 
environment We can look at Michael Turk's Soundex program for the basic 
algorithm. Then we have Jerry fine-tuning Soundex to make it more 
effective on different sound groups. As the web literature indicates 
there are also problems with German names, Jewish names originating 
either from German or the Slavic languages, plus problems with Hebrew 
names, and it goes on an on. There's the problem with "British" names :-

Pronounced		Spelling
----------		-------
Affleck			Auchinleck		
Chumlee			Cholmondeley
Fanshaw			Featherstonhaugh	
Taves			Towes

OK, Ben Affleck's family used the pronounced version. Imagine the 
surprised look on the face of the Univac sales manager for Calgary, (in 
'75), when I told him the generally accepted pronunciation of his 
surname in the UK was 'Fanshaw'. And the above  are just the tip of the 
iceberg. (see 
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_names_in_English_with_counterintuitive_pronunciations#C_3).

Those US states using it for vehicles - I wouldn't mind betting there 
are six customized versions, built up over years of experience.

I'm aware that both Oracle and Microsoft have Soundex syntax as part of 
their SQLs - but are their end results any more precise ?

PECD : "I've implemented it (Soundex) with database searches and found 
it extremely useful. Especially for people who don't have English as a 
first language but must do searches on things like English names".

I guess you just got lucky in view of the following comments.

Soundex Accuracy :-

---------------------------------------------------------------------
http://www.immagic.com/eLibrary/ARCHIVES/GENERAL/LAS_US/L030206B.pdf

"Two recent studies looked at the performance of the basic Soundex 
algorithm, using statistical measures to gauge accuracy.

Alan Stanier (September 1990, Computers in Genealogy,
Vol. 3, No. 7) extracted all 411,716 surnames from the 1851 U.S. Census 
sample and linked related name forms based on information provided in a 
dictionary of surnames. Results :-

a) 33% of the matches that would be returned by Soundex would be correct.
b) 25% of correct matches would fail to be discovered by Soundex.

A second study by A. J. Lait and B. Randell (1996) compared the 
performance of several name-matching algorithms, including the basic 
Soundex method.  Searches were conducted on a data set of 5600 unique 
surnames, chosen to represent names beginning with each letter of the 
alphabet at a frequency of occurrence reflecting actual alphabetic
distributions of names, and including as well names of varying lengths.
The study found that Soundex (judged to be the best of the four 
algorithms compared) returned - Results :-

b) 36.37% of the actual correct matches
c) 60% of names that were correct matches for query names were not 
returned...."

----------------------------------------------------------------------

So could both Jerry and Pete illustrate examples of how they are using 
Soundex ? Does somebody say "The guy's name sounds like 'Mack-mood 
Arm-dine-jad', I think". Replies the operator at the keyboard, "Macmood 
WHO ?".

Frankly the Soundex approach looks cute but fiddly and it would appear, 
based on the title type, (surname, product description etc.), that you 
are searching on, it isn't very precise. I'd be foolish to make a 
suggestion at this stage without knowing *exactly* how they are applying 
the Soundex search results. There just may be a simpler alternative to 
searching on title types. Let's see what they have to say first. My idea 
is not restricted to COBOL but in theory at least, should be available 
in any OO language.

Jimmy, Calgary AB

As I read it, Mr Maclean, 'Auchinleck' contains an initial diphthong.  
Diphtong pronunciation can vary within the same language as well as across 
languages.  'Au' might be rendered as 'oh' as well as 'ow'.

>
>And:
…[4 more quoted lines elided]…
>Also, does Goldstein get pronounced as Goldstine or Goldsteen?

I've heard both.  Notice that the ambiguity rests in the 'ei' diphthong; 
the long 'i' pronunciation (indicated by your Anglicised '-ine' suffix, 
where the common rule is that a final, unpronounced 'e' gives the 
immediately-preceding vowel a long pronounciation (compare 'ban' with 
'bane', then compare 'bane' with 'bain' (Fr.)) is the in accord with the 
German pronounciation of 'ei' whereas the long e (indicated by the doubled 
vowel) is usually the... Anglicised pronounciation.

I seem to recall George Gershwin writing a ditty about such difficulties.

Without getting off in the weeds over what names sound like what, here's our 
application: A customer approaches the desk and requests that the purchase 
be charged. "And what is your name?" asks the clerk.

"Citizen Kane" says the customer. The clerk begins typing "C - A ..." and 
pauses for two seconds. The screen then fills with (from an actual database 
on my computer):

Michael a Cabrera
John Cadamuro
Ward C. Caddington
Lillian S. Cahill
Timothy Cahill
Dough Cain
Brett Caine
Katheryn Caine
(and about another 200 names followed by)
--- Sounds like ---
Citizen Kane
Richard Kay
Edward Kay
Jeff Kaye
Martin J. Kehoe
Matthew Kehoe
Dr Michael Key
Boo Soon Khoo

If the clerk is overwhelmed by the first 200 names, she can add another 
letter "C-A-I" and the screen responds with mere six names (on my 17,000 
name database) plus the "K" guys as above.

In our rendition, Soundex doesn't attempt to uniquely identify the request, 
it only assists in cutting down the collection to a manageable list.
