-----Mike Yawn's message follows.

Walter,

I'm not an expert on how the Java Community Process (JCP) has evolved.
You can see how it's currently structured at http://java.sun.com/jcp.
Some of the criticisms about 'letting everyone have a vote' may have
been true in previous iterations; I'm just not sure.

Currently, for each specification that is launched, there is an
expert group formed.   I know HP has participated in some expert
groups, and I also know of other vendor employees who participate;
my impression is that if you care about something, it isn't that
difficult to become a member of the expert group.  The expert group
has the real power; there is a community review process, and the
opportunity for anyone to provide feedback, but decision-making on
what is or isn't part of the specification rests solely with the
expert group, as I understand it.  This probably keeps the timeline
manageable, but if you don't trust the expert group to fairly
consider all community feedback, then I'm not sure there's any
way this process would be considered satisfactory.

One thing that keeps the Java process manageable -- the specifications
are very narrow.  They don't just throw open "Java" to the world at
large to debate on new features.  Instead, someone proposes something
very specific -- such as Generics, an Assertion Facility, a Logging
Facility, an API for XML parsing (all of these are recent
specifications).
An expert group is then formed to put together the specification for
just that piece of functionality.  Sun is a member of every expert
group,
and I think their role (well, at least their publicly admitted role)
is
to make sure there is some coherence between groups, so that each
group
is aware of what others are doing to the extent they may overlap,
conflict,
or complement one another.

Mike

The principle referred to above is only true in Waterfall environments.

I am proposing a RAD type evironment  with an OPEN Forum contributed to by
an EMPOWERED User Base. The process will proceed by iterative interaction
from the User Base within a series of TIMEBOXes.

Unlike the Waterfall (where phases have to be extended if they are not
completed on schedule, and consequently, the whole process "slips" as soon
as the slack runs out...)TIMEBOXes are RIGID and CANNOT be extended. (That
is why the assignment of priorities is crucial. You work through the
priorities within the time allocated to the Timebox. It doesn't matter
whether there are 10 or 1000 people involved, when the time is up, that's
it. You go with what you have, until the next iteration of it is budgeted,
resourced, and started (could be a year, could be 6 weeks, depends on the
priority and how critical an issue it is)).

This means the TOP priority items are "trickled out" as the process
proceeds.

I realise this concept is foreign to most of the people in J4/Alphabet Soup
who have mainframe or large Corporate backgrounds (and so understand the
Waterfall), and may not necessarily have had extensive experience in Project
Management. (Many people on the Committee are Technical and have programming
backgrounds; nothing wrong with this... it is very desirable, but the
Management of their effort has failed dismally, and I believe it stems from

Standard...>)

completed on schedule, and consequently, the whole process "slips" as soon
as the slack runs out...)TIMEBOXes are RIGID and CANNOT be extended. (That
is why the assignment of priorities is crucial. You work through the
priorities within the time allocated to the Timebox. It doesn't matter
whether there are 10 or 1000 people involved, when the time is up, that's
it. You go with what you have, until the next iteration of it is budgeted,
resourced, and started (could be a year, could be 6 weeks, depends on the
priority and how critical an issue it is)).

This means the TOP priority items are "trickled out" as the process
proceeds.

I realise this concept is foreign to most of the people in J4/Alphabet Soup
who have mainframe or large Corporate backgrounds (and so understand the
Waterfall), and may not necessarily have had extensive experience in Project
Management. (Many people on the Committee are Technical and have programming
backgrounds; nothing wrong with this... it is very desirable, but the
Management of their effort has failed dismally, and I believe it stems from
failure to "manage" the process of Standard production properly. <it's an
OPINION, I could be wrong <G>, but the fact is we are still waiting for a
Standard...>)
