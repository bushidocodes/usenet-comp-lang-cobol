*New post to avoid the same trap Pete Dashwood ran into.

Holly wrote:

<<Didn't they just find the book of Judas was valid  ...>>

I've done a fair amount of study on old and new testament apocrypha and 
noncanonical writings.

Valid, as being genuinely from a very early period?  Yes, although it's a 
translation into Coptic (Egyptian using basically the Greek alphabet) from 
the original (most probably) Greek.   The copy, poorly preserved, has been 
radiocarbon-dated to 220-340AD.

Genuine, as in consistent with what we know about the contents of the 
previously-lost Gospel of Judas?  Well, hard to say.  What's preserved 
doesn't seem to contain the information Irenaeus, Bishop of Lyons, 
complained about in around 180AD, though it does include information 
Epiphanus of Salamis, Bishop of Cyprus, cited some two centuries later.  .

<<and should have been included so everyone's idea of Judas might have been 
misinterpreted for
centuries?>>

Aye, there's the rub.  I believe during the first few centuries of the 
Christian era the New Testament was evolving from various collections of 
writings into a single approved list.  I don't think it started out as an 
approved list.  The Gospel of Judas didn't make it.   It's not a "given" 
that it should have been.

There are a number others that were also excluded, and I think we could all 
be enlightened by some of them (in particular, for me, the epistle of 
Barnabas and the epistle of Clement of Rome to the Corinthians, which I 
believe are acknowledged by most scholars as genuine in the form we have 
them).  I'm not convinced about the Gospel of Judas.

    -Chuck Stevens

The Gospel of  Aloysius the Cobbler  was translated from the original JOVIAL 
(Jules own version of the international algorithmic language) , through 
Pascal, into COBOL.  Unfortunately, scholars disagree on whether three of 
the full stops in the original translation were later changed to scope 
delimiters by COBOL coders who wanted to put their own stamp on it during 
the mid to latter 20th Century.

The preponderance of the evidence indicates that the purity of Aloysius' 
Algorithm (which was believed to involve merging any number of 80 column 
card decks onto magnetic tape) may have been compromised by the 
adulteration.

Apocryphal copies of the original have been offered on e-bay for 6 figure 
sums, but the figures were poorly formed and written in crayon, so no sales 
are known to have been transacted.

The details of the life of Aloysius the Cobbler are obscure (and subject to 
much conjecture in Assembler and PL/1 by various whisky Priests of 
Procedure) but he is believed to have decided that there was no money in 
making shoes by hand, as everyone was making sandals out of old car tyres, 
and far too stoned to be doing any serious walking anyway.

He therefore picked up an Assembler manual and decided there was more money 
in making computers do stuff. The only problem was that this Assembler 
programming was completely unintelligible to anyone with a  mere human 
brain, and that was what he was equipped with.

After a torrid love affair with a punch girl named Julie, he smoked a few 
joints, dropped some acid, and received the divine revelation that 
programming should be removed from the machine. JOVIAL was invented on the 
spot.  The subsequent refinements led to COBOL which was named after 
Aloysius' original calling  and the rest is history.

Any more questions?
