I have a PC on my desk, and I'm using it now.  But for mainframe work, my
little lovely 3278 IBM Infowindow terminal sits, and I'll use it over the PC
any day.  24 PF keys mean no shifting to get my customized keys (and I've
customized them all).  I can use it's layout for what I need faster. Plus 24
play-keys (I know the emulator can do hot-keys, but I couldn't remember more
than 24, anyway).  I always use 80col by 43 row screen, and my PC emulator
display's this format with the characters oddly stretched out.  I have yet
to figure out how to make the crosshairs appear on my PC, but it's only one
button away on my dumb (read: smart) terminal.

When the PC server goes down, I can still work on the mainframe.  When the
gateways are jammed or down, I can still work on the mainframe.  The
reliability of that simple and archaic SNA connection is astonishing.  Our
PC server has gone down mayber ten times in eight months, and the gateways,
two or three.  Several times, that was the whole day blown for people, but
not me.  How many times has my SNA connection gone down during that period?
Not once.

My dumb terminal is configured for two logical sessions at the controller,
and since I'm the one of the few smart terminal holdouts, I'm sure I could
convince network to configure it for more if I really wanted them.  We
already have TPX, so in one session, I can have TSO, CA7, and two or three
CICS regions, and whatever else I want (and that on both logical sessions,
because I have two accounts).  I always use my ISPF split screens--I can't
tell you how often I see people stumble through them or not use them at
all--and use TSO HOLD frequently.

My PC emulator has but one main and one secondary advantage for me.  Easy
file transfer, and easy screen prints.  Oh, easy screen copy and paste are
good, too.

Note, though, that out of about 70 programmers here, I'm about the only one
to regularly use a smart terminal.  Am I an old maniac, holding out for
yesteryear?  Nope.  I first did computers on an Apple IIe, and then moved to
the Mac, which blew my Apple IIe away, so I'm an old GUI interface advocate.
I did not come to mainframes until much later.

I just think existing emulators . . .

Carlos,

You need the following characteristics for the following different areas an
Operator can work in.

1-Print Room: Lift up to 100 pounds.  Learn the use of, commands for,
cleaning off, and jam clearing methods of all the printers you will be
required to operate.  Learn to rapidly break down print and distribute it
with 100% accuracy to any one of possibly thousands of destinations.
Whatever you do, don't let your work pile up, dump work onto the next shift
by putting it off a while, or blow off what you're doing as unimportant.
If you are called to personally deliver reports (I'm thinking on a regular
basis, here), then do your best to convince some of these recipients that
you actually care about what you're doing, and what they're doing too.

2-Computer Room (Tape): Start studying the relatively incomprehensible IBM
manuals.  The JES2 and JCL manuals, and the MVS console commands manual.
Learn and memorize as much of the MVS & JES2 Display commands as you can;
you won't be called on to execute very many of the others (a few, like Swap
and Mount), and the Display command is mostly harmless and knowing it makes
you look good (whatever you do, don't use JES2's Display command to show all
elements on the spool taking up 0% or more of the spool; if your spool has
even a few thousand elements, this will take a long time to scroll past the
system console).
If you are responsible for doing backups/DRA, double and triple check the
volser of every tape you send out; sending out the wrong tape is
embarrassing.
Don't put tapes away in the wrong slot in the racks.  Nothing will drive the
next person who needs to go after that tape more mad that having to search
through the entire library for it (and some libraries are huge).


3-Computer Room (Console): Know those JES2 and MVS Display commands by
heart.  Know how to use the majority of the others (some are useful only to
system programmers) with a cheat sheet.  Know JCL backwards and forwards,
including all the technical points of restarts, you will undoubtedly know
JCL better than the programmers you deal with (spotting an obscure JCL error
the programmer cannot will get you noticed (one way, or another)).  Know
your job scheduling package, and if you have one, your job restart package.
Get a good grasp of every support application in your shop, or at the least
know what they do.

If you're in a UNIX environment, then you need to know the appropriate UNIX
system commands, and how to read and write shell scripts.

Showing some basic network proficiency in all this can't hurt.

It may be obvious to some, but I'll say it anyway: be professional.

If you can do all this, and at the same time, show you can write programs of
business value in the language of your shop, you'll have an excellent
chance.
