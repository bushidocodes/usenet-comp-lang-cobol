Judson, that's not the way evolution works. Mother Nature is not
teleological. Half a wing is no good, a tiny hole that serves no purpose
until millions of years later, are no good and are NOT how it works.
Evolution works with what is already there and what already serves
a purpose and what already is an advantage for the organism.
Then slowly, the precursor may assume a new function while
all the way still performing some of its old function albeit less and
less.
A classic case is that of the eye. Even Darwin himself said:
"To suppose that the eye, with all its inimitable contrivances
for adjusting the focus to different distances, for admitting different
amounts if light, and for the correction of spherical and chromatic
aberration, could have been formed by natural selection, seems,
I freely confess, absurd in the highest possible degree."

When we speak of 'the' eye we are not doing justice to the
problem. It has been estimated that eyes have evolved no
fewer than 40 times, and probably more that 60 times,
independently in various parts of the animal kingdom. In
many cases these eyes use radically different principles. Nine
distinct principles have been recognized among the about 50
independently evolved eyes.
These eyes, BTW, are all eyes of modern animals. No animal
ever made a living by being an intermediate stage on some
evolutionary pathway. The simplest eyes are so simple that
they scarcely deserve to be called eyes at all. It is better to
say that some area of the body is slightly sensitive to light.
This is true of some single-celled organisms, jellyfish, leeches
and the like. All they can sense (dimly) is the presence of
light. This light-sensitivity might mean the difference between
life and death, when a predator is looming over you and
blocking some of the light. From such simple beginnings
the rest follows, not in anticipation of the complicated eye
that will be there eventually, but because each step on the
way is useful to the animal at the time. There are no half-
eyes or half-legs needing another million years of change
to become useful.
Do yourself (and the rest of us) the favor of purchasing,
borrowing, stealing, whatever, a book by Richard Dawkins
"Climbing Mount Improbable". Dawkins explains how
evolution really works. It might convince you, but maybe not
as the difference between a scientist and a religious man
is that the scientist knows that he may be wrong, but the
believer knows that he can't be wrong. In order for something
to be scientific it must have the property of being potentially
falsifiable, while religion is concerned about absolute truth
and therefore does not admit falsifiability.
