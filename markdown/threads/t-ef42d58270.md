COBOL does not have a built in date format.  It is stored however the
programmer decided to do it.  Useful forms are text yyyymmdd,
yyyy/mm/dd, mm/dd/yy, yyyyddd, and of course the more dangerous versions
of these with two digit years.  I rarely see it stored as a packed or
binary number - but that was used to save space sometimes, or when there
were going to be lots of date compares.

Big-endian, little-endian refers to how the machine stores it's 16 & 32 bit
integers.  1.......32,  or 32........1.  When the least significant byte is
stored first, it's a little endian machine.  When the most significant byte is
stored first, it's a big endian machine.  Intel & DEC are little endian
machines.  Most servers, RS6000, SUN, IBM mainframes are big endian machines.
Motorola chips are also big endian.

To convert 16 bits, just swap the two bytes.  When converting 32 bits, convert
the two 16 bit qtys, then swap the 16 bit values.

I think the first reverse byte format  (1....32) was made by DEC.  The circuitry
was cheaper.  Other mini computer makers also used that format.

Cheers,
Ralph Jones.

I've seen a stranger one: 1999-DEC-31 stored as 0xC76D.
It makes marginally more sense in octal: %143555
The first seven bits (%143 = decimal 99) are the year, offset from 1900.
The last nine bits (%555 = decimal 365) are the day of the year.

And it's Y2K-compliant, too! (but it has a Y2.028K problem: when the first 
seven bits roll to all zeros, 2028 will look like 1900)
