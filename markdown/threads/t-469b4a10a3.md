```
I Received call from a man with foreign accent who
said he was from ATT.

He told me that ATT had determined that there was
a problem with my server (I use Web.com and Time-Warner, not ATT)
which was flooding their servers with 'error messages'.

I asked him where he was from and he sent me to
the ATT website as though to demonstrate that he had
some legality, perhaps? He said he was in Washington DC.

This is actually the second call I have had from this
ATT outfit so I was prepared to see how much I could
get from the guy who called. I assumed the role of a
concerned, inept and non-technical user.

He then had me run the DOS command 'assoc' which
displays at the bottom the CLSID.

.ZFSendToTarget=CLSID\{888DCA60-FC0A-11CF-8F0F-00C04FD7D062}

The guy then told me that he would read the CLSID
to me, and he did. After reading it, he asked if I
'trusted' him!

This creates the impression that they have access to
your PC since he could read your CLSID.

My three PCs all have the same CLSID, and I assume
that the CLSID is the same for all and never unique
to a single PC.

Next, he had me run the Event Viewer (eventvwr).
This, as you can imagine, shows what seems to be
thousands of 'errors' in the System Folder (2,860
actually) the majority of which are 'information'
with a fair number of 'errors' sprinkled throughout
which, I would expect, would be expected.

The existence of the 'errors' is proof that there
is something bad with my PC.

Next, he wanted me to go to www.eventviewer.com
so that he could log on to my PC and delete all
those bad files and generally make everything OK
again.

The alternative to that would be to have my Internet
connection PERMANENTLY disabled.

By now, I had consumed about 20 minutes of his time
and informed him that I knew all about his scam
and hung up.


The next day, I received another call but this time
it was from 'Verizon'.

They told me that I had two options:
a. to allow them to access my PC to fix the problems.
b. to be disconnected from the Internet for two months.

I chose to be disconnected from the Internet and said
goodbye.

Coincidentally, the next morning, when I turned on
my PC, I could not connect to the Internet, but it
was just a temporary outage and I was shortly
back online.

There must be people who fall for this scam and
let them access their PC. Of course, this lets
the scammer really screw up your PC and you then
have to pay them to remove whatever they installed.

In both cases, the scammer had what most would perceive to
be an Indian accent. There was a lot of background
noise as though there were lots of people making
calls in the same room - if they weren't making
money they wouldn't be doing it.

The ones I spoke to also seem to get quite exasperated
when you don't do exactly as they tell you.

Back in 2012, I had had a horrible virus encounter
which is documented at:

http://www.foodman123.com/virus.htm


Tony Dilworth
```
