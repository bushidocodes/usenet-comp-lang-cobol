Hee hee hee... this is why I learned to concatenate ISPF line-commands; I
was taught 'Your terminal is in Omaha and the datacenter's in
Altoona; every time you hit 'Enter' you want to send as *much* as possible
to keep your own lag-time to a minimum.'  The typical example (used from
the long command-line of the ISPF Main Menu) is:

=2;;S CMP;C PROGNAM1 PROGNAM2 ALL;SUB;CAN;SPLIT;SDSF H;PRE MYUID*

... which is, of course, Very Obvious to some and Utterly Incomprehensible
to others.

(explanation: it invokes the ISPF editor panel, where your last library's
name can be left over by default, enters the selection screen for members
of that library, selects the member 'CMP' (for CoMPile, of course),
changes all occurrences of PROGNAM1 to PROGNAM2, submits the job to the
internal reader, cancels out of the changes (so you don't have to remember
PROGNAM2), splits the screen (similar to invoking a second session, sorta
kinda almost maybe), invokes SDSF (the System Display and Search Facility
one uses to monitor batch jobs which have been submitted or have already
run, among other things) to Display Active jobs and sets the
viewing-prefix to MYUID* so I can watch my compile run)

Well, actually, the water in a water-cooled mainframe never gets
anywhere near the chips.  Typically the chips are either in a helium
atmosphere (helium's a really good heat conductor) or there are metal
"thumbs" to conduct the heat away, then there's a heat exchanger with
water pipes to cool that first-level cooling system.  It's plain old
tap water in the pipes.

There's some really spiffy technology in modern mainframes -- a
complex of 12 processors, 10 of which are active, the other two are
hot spares which are swapped in automatically by hardware if any of
the 10 fails, redundant fiber optic I/O buses, etc.  But as others
