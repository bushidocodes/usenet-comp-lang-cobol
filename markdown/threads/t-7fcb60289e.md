Ugly, ugly, ugly... *my* sense of aesthetics (which, as is well-known, are
flawless) would key it in as:

   PERFORM VARYING WS-TITLE-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
    UNTIL WS-TITLE-SUB > 10
       IF SAL-TITLE-CODE=TITLE-CODE(WS-TITLE-SUB)
           MOVE EXP-TITLE(WS-TITLE-SUB) TO REPORT-TITLE
       END-IF
   END-PERFORM.

Now... leaving aside what did or did not work previously (which is a fancy
way of saying 'I haven't been following this closely') the easiest way to
break out of a loop is to trigger the condition.  It looks as though you
have a stack of ten items... so how about *this*:

   PERFORM VARYING WS-TITLE-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
    UNTIL WS-TITLE-SUB > 10
       IF SAL-TITLE-CODE=TITLE-CODE(WS-TITLE-SUB)
           MOVE EXP-TITLE(WS-TITLE-SUB) TO REPORT-TITLE
           MOVE 20 TO WS-TITLE-SUB
       END-IF
   END-PERFORM.

   IF WS-TITLE-SUB = 11
       MOVE 'DAMDFIKNOW' TO REPORT-TITLE
   END-IF.

(of course the numerics should not be hard-coded... but this *is*
Studentenarbeit)

Does this 'trick' make sense?

           OR SAL-LOCATION-CODE EQUAL LOCATION-CODE (WS-LOCATION-SUB)
                      ADD +1                  TO WS-LOCATION-SUB
    END-PERFORM.

    IF WS-LOCATION-SUB > 5
        MOVE 'NOT FOUND'           TO   REPORT-LOCATION
    ELSE
     	  MOVE EXP-LOCATION (WS-LOCATION-SUB) 
    	                             TO   REPORT-LOCATION
    END-IF.

this takes a little more effort to code, but you have more control...   

this gives you the ability to vary more than one variable / index at the 
same time and guarentee that they are in sync...  it also avoids testing 
an index against a numeric field...  hint you can set the index to +1 and 
also move +1 to a counter....   then increment them both at the bottom of 
the perform loop. then only check the counter in the UNTIL portion of the 
PERFORM verb...

for small tables ....   no appreciable difference....

for 2 dimensional tables with 100,000+ entries which you are searching 
millions of times....   the difference is significant...


If you are interested in performance tuning  consider a DIRECT INDEX 
access table...  I'll leave that for another discussion...
