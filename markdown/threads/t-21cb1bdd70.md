No, he didn't.

If you are going to use quotation marks, the least you could do is put
the poor guy's own words between them.

Samuel Johnson wrote: "Sir, a woman's preaching is like a dog's walking
on his hinder legs.  It's not done well, but you are surprised to find it
done at all."

Make a note of it this time.

Even if you hadn't already been told the actual saying, you should be
able to recognize that the words you are using are not from Johnson's
era.  The reference to a mammal in the neuter gender is a dead giveaway! 
Note particularly the difference between the eighteenth-century viewpoint
"you are surprised" and the twentieth-century viewpoint "it would want
to".

Your sentence itself is a grammatical jumble that simply could not have
reached print in the eighteenth century.  Consider "a dog raised up on
its hinder legs".  Aside from the gender gaffe, this is in the passive
voice.  An unidentified person is raising up the dog.  Then comes the
awkward "it does it poorly".  What is the antecedent of the first "it"? 
Certainly not the dog, which is not doing anything, much less doing it
poorly.  What is the antecedent of the second "it"?  The raising up of
the dog?  What is the antecedent of the "it" in "it would want to"?  The
one who is raising up the dog?  This sentence could not have reached
print even in the nineteenth century; it could come only from one who was
suckled on the glass teat.
