Report on February 23-28, 1997 ANSI NCITS J4 COBOL Committee meeting,
Chelmsford, Massachusetts, USA and the February 17-21, 1996
Object-Oriented COBOL ad hoc group in the same location. When a vote
is shown, the numbers are for, against, and abstain (f-a-a).

Membership stands at 11 (we lost one member, the COBOL Research Group,
and gained one, Wizard Systems). The primary topic at this meeting
was more processing on the comments on the Committee Draft.

It now appears that because so many changes are being made to the
current draft, mainly in the object-oriented area, that a second
committee draft (CD) review will be needed. This pushes the schedule
out to the end of 1999 or perhaps into 2000. We are going to attempt
to reduce this if it is at all possible.

The name of the Committee is no longer X3J4. The X3 organization
changed it name to NCITS (I have no idea what this stands for, but
they changed it because X3 seems to have some connotation of feelthy
pictures or some such). So, we are known as NCITS technical committee
J4, or J4 for short.

The major topics/proposals we discussed were:

1. The object-oriented COBOL ad hoc committee met for five days and
made many significant changes. One big one was to delete the concept
of a create class and instead make a CREATE statement. This should
make it easier to write a class library in COBOL.

2. The public review comments were discussed again (the entire
meeting) and votes of direction were taken again. Essentially this
was a rehash of the December meeting but with more detail.

The next NCITS J4 meeting will be in, Durham, North Carolina, USA, on
21-24 April, 1997. The main topic will be addressing the actions
taken at the WG4 meeting in March (see below). There will also be an
object-oriented ad hoc meeting in Durham on 18-20 April, 1997.

There will be an ISO/IEC JTC1/SC22/WG4 COBOL meeting in Monterey,
California, USA, on 17-21 March 1997. This meeting will also be
devoted to comments on the CD review. There will probably be several
features removed from the standard at this meeting.

COBOL Development, Tandem Computers, Inc.

In reviewing the Year 2000 effort one of the sets of routines that seems to
be used in a large number of programs is julian to julian conversion and
vice-versa. I have coded (or rewritten for efficiency) a number of them in
COBOL, assembler and DYL280. Many installations have multiple subroutines to
do this. Rather than rewrite them to upgrade to use of 4 digit years it
would be better to replace them with COBOL functions. I propose FUNCTION
DAY-OF-DATE (argument-1) to be the equivalent of FUNCTION DAY-OF-INTEGER
(FUNCTION INTEGER-OF DATE (argument-1)) and FUNCTION DATE-OF-DAY (argument-1)
to be the equivalent of FUNCTION DATE-OF-INTEGER (FUNCTION INTEGER-OF-DAY
(argument-1). This would provide a function that I somehow thought was
already available.

The need for these functions is immediate. Something must be done about the
large number of subroutines and inline code that will have to be upgraded to
handle the 4 digit years. In order for the functions to be of major use the
must be available by the end of 1997 at the latest. By they most
organizations will have begun the coding for year 2000 upgrade.

The code itself should be fairly straight-forward since the INTERGER-OF-DATE
routine probably contains an implied gregorian to julian conversion within it
and the DATE-OF-INTEGER probably contains an implied julian to gregorian
conversion within it.

I am well aware of the length of time the standards process takes. However
there needs to be a process that will accomodate a time constrained
requirement. If the readers of the newsgroups that I am posting this to
agree that adding of these functions would be of aid in the year 2000 effort,
I urge that they have their organizations contact their COBOL vendor and
their national standards body.

THIS IS THE LAST STRAW. (Yes, I am yelling)

I have need for true binary, usage bit, COPY REPLACING LEADING and several of
the other constructs in programs that I am writing today for my client. In
fact I needed them in programs I was writing 20-30 years ago and had to use
work arounds.

The screen section and the various record locking mechanisms would be a
tremendous help for many of the online environments.

I strongly recommend that ANSI and ISO release all uncontroversial parts of
the new standard that have been through at least one review and all items
that have had comments dealt with as a 1997 standard. Then schedule new
function for release and approval on a semi-annual or annual basis. If a
committee can't handle this have ANSI form a permanent company to do the
actual work of co-ordinating and validating. IBM is now releasing upgrades
to their major mainframe operating system on an approximately semi-annual
basis. Windows seems to be on a 2-3 year cycle for major upgrades with some
new function being released by fixpack upgrade. Certainly we should be able
to get new function into the COBOL language on a timely basis.

The delays in the standard are costing our companies money in many ways.
They may be the thing that leads to the downfall of COBOL. If I had the
money I would attend the annual meeting of every company that produces a
COBOL compiler and ask them why they tolerate a process that is execessively
delay ridden even by the standards of our field.

I have to agree with Clark on this. While the standards committees are
important, pushing anything from the '97 standard to 1999 or 2000 is
simply too far. There are simply too many important features that are
needed now. And, while I agree that the standards process is and has
been important for Cobol, I think that the delays, such as the one
proposed here, is particularly damaging to Cobol. This process must be
speed up. The rest of the computer industry is running a warp speed.
The Cobol standards groups should run at least a 100 mph by now.

All of the so-called "noncontroversial" components should be released
while the others are haggled over. I sincerely hope that the Cobol
vendors don't wait for the entire standard to be finalized. While I
appreciate the importance of the standards, I think that it is equally
important to remember that there are hundreds of thousands of people
waiting for these enhancements.
