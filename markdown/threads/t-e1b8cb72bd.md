I've written up some thoughts about the application of Object Oriented 
techniques -- when they're appropriate and when they're not.  It's a 
bit long to post in a news group, so I posted it on my website at:

	http://home.swbell.net/mck9/cobol/ooc/whenoo.html

I'm not sure how much I agree with my own conclusions.  I hope to spark 
some debate among those who are better qualified than myself (or at 
least more opinionated).

Summary:

I. OO is good for some things and not so good for others.  It's
   useful to know which is which.

II. Factors which favor the use of OO:

   A. Requirements are stable and well understood.  E.g.:

      1. Mathematical objects

      2. Principles of accounting

      3. Physical objects

   B. Requirements are largely under the control of the developer,
      typically because the classes represent entities which are 
      internal to the program.  E.g.:

      1. Graphical User Interfaces

      2. Data structures, i.e. container classes

      3. Games

   C. Classes can map recognizable entities in a natural and 
      intuitive way

   D. There is a natural and satisfying use for polymorphism

III. Factors which render OO less likely to succeed:

   A. Requirements are complex, capricious, and unstable, typically 
      due to:

      1. Laws, regulations, or business rules

      2. Poor or muddled understanding of problem domain

   B. Difficulty in coming up with a satisfying class design

   C. The use of classes as coding gimmicks rather than as
      representations of recognizable entities

   D. The need to store persistent objects

   E. The need, or temptation, to use multiple inheritance

Finally: happy Thanksgiving to all.  (For those who are not in the 
U.S.: Thanksgiving is a holiday whereon we give thanks to God for 
letting other people starve instead of us.)
