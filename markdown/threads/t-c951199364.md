Although sometimes in CoBOL discussions we are a bit redundant to emphasize this
meaning.   A full stop period terminates all clauses and the sentence,
regardless of whether there are commands that have been explicitly terminated.

This feature can be useful if you have some code inserted by the compiler, or by
a pre-compiler and don't know if everything is terminated the way you expect.  
And it can make reading the program a bit easier - knowing the exact start of a
sentence.   On the other hand, periods can be hard to see, and if you cut and
paste logic from sentences to clauses, you have to remove them.

Of course, if you admit to using more than the minimum amount of periods
required by the syntax, you will find yourself abused by someone even though
that someone has expressed that he likes the way CoBOL is like English.

LX-i wrote:

> I'm new to the newsgroup, so pardon the seemingly dumb question...
> 
> I've been a COBOL developer for several years, and I'm not familiar with
> the term "full stop".  Is that a fancy term for a period?

This is an international group and not all spelling or terms will be 
American, some may be English.

The character '.' is a full stop. There is a gradient of pauses in speech 
indicated by ',', ';', ':', and '.', the last being called a full stop.

'Period' refers to a time span between a start point and an end point, for 
example an 'historical period' is from its first date to the last; a 
musical period is the time from its first bar to its last.

In English grammar the 'period of a sentence' should be from the start of 
its first sylable to the pause at the end. In fact dictionaries may give 
one definition as "complete sentence, esp if complex".

Use of the term 'period' to mean the full stop is recorded in England in 
the 17thC but America seem to have adopted its misuse entirely.
