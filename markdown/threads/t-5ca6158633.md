Willem,

There is validity to Robaire's comment, "not exactly cutting edge", and also
accepting that you were around in the seventies, bright eyed and bushy tailed <G>.

In my own case there are two things which have stood the test of time - Decision
Tables, which I think Richard said were around in the fifties. My use of these was
initially in a systems analysis capacity. Their usefulness still holds true.

It wasn't called a technique per se,  but Dan McCracken's "Structured Programming"
set me on the right course. The essential thing here is that the book gave coded
examples, whereas much of the material on design approaches, waffles on in
generalities. A case in point - I have two books on OO COBOL - based obviously on
very early renditions of the proposed J4 Standard. They aren't coupled to coded
examples and where they do show code are so generalized, it requires a stretch of
the imagination to discern where their examples could be used with a 'live'
compiler.

At the current time we have "Teach Yourself COBOL in 24 Hours" and  "Teach ......
etc.....in 21 Days". The first being the better of the two, in my view. Possibly
Thane's book will see a 10 year run before it is considered 'old hat' and replaced
by something more up to date - a new offering would delve more closely into the
Internet.

Here's an interesting example, Pete unashamedly promotes RAD - absolutely nothing
wrong with that. Two points :-

- He takes a swipe at the 'waterfall' approach. About two weeks ago, with a couple
of hours to kill waiting for my wife shopping, pulled off the bookstore shelf
'RAD" by Steve McConnelll - one of the acknowledged gurus on RAD. Paraphrased
quote, "Under certain circumstances, for some applications, the waterfall approach
might be the best to follow" - I offer no comment one way or the other - but
that's from the guru.

- System design has evolved over the four decades that COBOL has existed. Pete
sensibly makes the point that an application has to have a design team, and as an
absolute MUST  include end users as active participants - agree 100%. BUT when I
started  back in '63 young 'Dashwoods' were learning how to program the damn
things and we 'elders', mainly ex bean counters, were learning to design. Then we
didn't ask the users because we were the former 'users'.

EDP has become a specialty, so it behooves us to involve users in teams. I meet a
Scottish friend when walking the dogs, invalided from Petro Canada. During his
time, roughly some five years ago - as an accountant he was an ACTIVE member of a
design team - and this sensible inclusion of end user personnel wasn't given some
fancy technical name.

I'm not knocking the various design tools that come up - but look at what each is
offering under a microscope, pick out the good stuff, possibly 10% common sense
ideas, and reject the rest as waffle.

Anybody here who is into OO who is extensively using UML or CRC cards ?

Jimmy, Calgary AB
