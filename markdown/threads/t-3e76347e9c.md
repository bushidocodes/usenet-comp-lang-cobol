It's easy to get discouraged by quizzes, I think that's why instructors
like them so much:-)

Hopefully though your instructor is explaining the answers and _how it
is possible to get tricked into picking the wrong answer_

What you have to remember is that programming is not the same as
answering a short quiz. If you can write good code and can maintain and
enhance existing programs nobody is going to care what grade you got (as
long as you still manage to pass). After all, writing a 10,000 line
COBOL program is an open book problem where you are allowed to
collaborate with your colleagues, and you can keep on working until it
passes all of the acceptance tests.

To answer the other question, I found programming is hard until my mind
decided it could do it. Every time I learn a new programming language
(about 1 per year for the last 17 years) I go through a phase of utter
confusion where nothing seems to work right, then just before I decide
to give up everything clicks and the new language becomes really easy to
learn. I've now decided that this is the way my mind learns, it dives
deep into confusion until it manages to make the appropriate connections
between all of the new stuff, then it's easy

One slight modification that I would add/make to what Steve says is that
MUCH of programming has to "getting it right in the end".  This means that
it is NOT critical that your first attempt be perfect, but that you need to
be able to "debug" your code when it isn't doing what it should.  (Similar
to using proofreading tools when sending out notes on usenet).

I know that you (Derrick) have asked several questions about code that you
have created and were having problems with.  My question to you is "Are you
getting a 'feeling' on how to find the solutions yourself?"

I have never "judged" a programmer on their ability to create 100% perfect
code on their first attempt - but I do look at how they try and solve the
problems that come up with their first (and subsequent) attempts.  To me,
this is the MOST important thing that you can be learning from your
programming classes.  I do NOT want you to think that you shouldn't keep
asking us for help - most of your questions have been reasonable and you
have given us all the information that we needed to help you.  HOWEVER, I
would ask you to judge yourself on whether you are understanding your
problems better - and figuring out "techniques" for fixing your problems
yourself.  If you miss questions on a quiz/test - I don't consider that
crucial.  On the other hand, if you are getting frustrated with not being
able to "solve" your own programming problems time after time, that may
indicate more of a problem.  On the third <G> hand, I am certain that EVERY
programmer in this NG can tell you bugs that they have spent "weeks" on that
(once they found them) seemed "obvious."  So that is part of the profession
as well.

language is not the same as programming, but is related.

What the original poster is doing today could be related to
chess.  They are learning how each piece moves, but, they have
not yet learned strategy.  Or, it could be related to flying. 
They are learning to land the plane, but, their ultimate goal is
to fly in the clouds by instruments -- meanwhile, the ground

well in the first semester of a two semester course on S/360
Assembly language.  However, I persisted and did quite well the
second semester.  And with more persistence, I became a systems
programmer, and then went on to become a software developer.

thinking literally.

#2 drove those non-programmers, that I was around, nuts
(including my wife).  I forced myself to stop accepting idioms
and look at phrases for what the actually meant.  Example:  Hit
the lights.  The intent is, if they are off, turn them on, and if
they are on, turn them off.  By doing this, I learned more and
more about logic.  After a while, this came naturally and then I
went back and learned to speak to normal people (which pleased my
wife no end).

Programming is related to inventing:  1% inspiration and 99%
perspiration.
