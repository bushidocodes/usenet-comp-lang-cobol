```
In many ways, the year 2000 is already here, and its cost increases as
we speak. I work for an insurance company, for instance, and clients
often want to know the cash-in value for insurance well beyond 2000 --
for the simple reason that they may want to finance college for
someone at that point. Previously, we could go to our system and
enter the Two Digits necessary to figure this. Not so past the year
1999. We have to send for manual calculations, and pay for the
man-hours required. So far it is still cheaper to do this than to
convert a company wide system -- but I imagine as 2000 approaches, the
man-hours will increase.

It would be interesting to hear if anyone else out there is Already
experiencing similar year-2000 costs, and when they think the
breakeven will occur (i.e., when the cost of manual adjustment exceeds
the cost of system-conversion).
```

```
I'm currently contracting for a mortgage service bureau. Since most
of the loans are 30yr loans, most year 2000 problems have been
addressed.

What they did years ago, has made things a lot easier now. The shop
standard for ALL DATES is 9(7) Comp-3 with a format of CYYMMDD.

Originally, these fields were 9(6) Comp-3. (BTW, this is a mainframe
EBCIDC system). Two conversions were done. One, convert all dates to
YYMMDD. Then second, change all references of 9(6) Comp-3 to 9(7)
Comp-3, and where needed in the data files, change the leading 0 to a
1 to designate 21st Century.

This conversion method proved easeist. Physical field expansion was
not needed because in an EBCIDC packed 9(6) field, the leading packed
digit is not used. We logically expanded the field by changing the
fields to 9(7). The only potential problem is if you have to
calculate dates greater than 100 years.

As a programmer, the best part of this conversion (I've worked on and
off w/ this client for the past 14 years), was standardizing all date
fields. When you see a date field, you know the format w/o having to
guess.
```
