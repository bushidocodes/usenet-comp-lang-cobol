The original question was what the interviewer should expect to be asked. 
"You" is the person being asked, not the person doing the asking.

Smart questions, stupid questions, whatever questions - he wants to know
what to expect:

e.g.

What are your long term career goals?
Why did you pick CoBOL?
What do you know about our business?
Do you have CoBOL II experience? ( I had this asked once when I had the same
type of programming experience on a VAX - but they didn't call it CoBOL II.)
What is a VSAM file? (Mention KSDS in your answer)
Have you worked with accounting/sales/whatever?  (be ready to say what you
have done)
What is structured programming?
How do you write out data so that a user can read it in his spreadsheet?

Any question that interfaces CoBOL with the company's database.

What do you look for in a walk-thru?
How do you write a test plan?

I think you have to worry more about the stupid questions like: 

"If you have an employee that you can not get along with, how do you handle it?"

"What is the one thing you have a problem with on the job?"

"On this job you may have to work some overtime, is that OK with you?"

"Tell us about the worst problem you ever had in the workplace..."

To the division question, I was taught to say "In Every Damn Program".  (The
first letters stand for the divisions.)  They might get a laugh out of that!

Actually it only takes one division to compile a program.

A good one would be to show someone a picture clause for a COMP-3 field and ask
them how much space it will take up, And then ask them how to take a ssn from an
alpha-numeric field and make it a 5 digit packed field.

Tables are always good question- 

"If you had a table with 5 totals for each of the 12 months out of the year, how
would you code the 2 level table for it?

Or better yet  "I just copy a similar program and change it a little bit, why
reinvent the wheel!"

If they really want to ask you this many questions they should just have a
formal test or something.

Questions are not worth much, it would be better to hand someone a program with
10 errors and be asked how to fix the problem.  

I think it is perfectly acceptable to consult a Language reference or
programming guide from time to time.
