```
As regards the recent back-and-forth about the new Microsft interface and 
the related loss of ease/productivity incurred during a period of 
familiarisation:

Back in the Oldene Dayse, before attention-enslaving telephones or 
personal computers or mainframe computers or moving-picture shows... a 
fair amount of heavy industry in the United States of America relied on 
machinery powered by water.

Essentially the 'eternal' motion of a river would used to rotate a large, 
heavy shaft; the rest of the machinery would use this shaft's energy by 
the means of belts and eccentric cams and the like.  Researching the 
intricacies of these methods might prove engaging.

In the late 1880s Thomas Edison, George Westinghouse and a few others 
began to make large-scale production of electricity available.  This 
allowed factories to free themselves from the dictates of a central 
shaft's motion powering individual machines.

It took a few decades for this freedom to be disseminated into factory 
design.  One reason might be that everyone who would trust or be trusted 
with the task of designing a factory had been trained on water-powered 
systems.

DD
```

```
"Central shaft" factory power systems were also run via steam power
and (centralized) electric motors.  As electric motors became cheaper,
and preexisting (externally driven) machinery needed to be replaced,
that became more decentralized (so you went through a phase were
having several machines run off a single electric motor was common),
until we got to the electric motor per-machine stage.

Water power was attractive for its low cost, assuming you had the
proper site for your factory.  Water power was also subject to a
number of external factors limiting its reliability, although, of
course, early steam power was not particularly reliable either.
```
