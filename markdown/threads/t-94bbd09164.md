```
I try very hard not to be an Old Fogey and I'm generally comfortable with
the technological changes which have occurred in my lifetime.

I think the Internet is amazing and I really don't mind the way it is woven
into so many aspects of our lives.

But this gave me pause...

http://arstechnica.com/security/2013/07/disabling-a-cars-brakes-and-speed-by-hacking-its-computers-a-new-how-to/

The article itself is interesting, but the comments are every bit as
interesting.

I used to hand my father tools when we worked on the family car, an old
1936 Morris 8 (4 cylinders, 8 horsepower, top speed around 45 MPH (going
down hill)). It didn't even have indicators; instead it had "trafficators"
which were orange arms with a tiny bulb inside that popped out on the side
of the car you were turning to, if you activated them. (And if the arm
wasn't stuck or clogged up with dirt...) My father showed me the ingenious
solenoid that dragged an iron core down inside a magnet (thereby raising the
arm) and I remember gazing in wonder as he made it raise the arm by toching
a wire on a battery terminal.

It seems to me that when cars are becoming fly-by-wire and we entrust our
lives (not to mention other people's lives as well) to non-mechanically
controlled brakes and steering, it might be a step too far. Sure, mechanical
systems can fail, but they can't be deliberately hacked (without access to
the vehicle with a hacksaw...)

Computerized engine management, making sure fuel and spark arrive at exactly
the right millisecond in exactly the required quantities, is one thing;
extending it to brakes and steering is quite another.

This is the first time I have ever read an article on the technological
state of things that are available to everybody, and felt unease.

Am I the only one?

Hopefully my current vehicle which I bought brand new 23 years ago (and it
looks like it just came out of the showroom) will continue to meet my needs
and I won't have to replace it with any of this new-fangled stuff...

Meanwhile, I'll be very carefully watching the new generation of vehicles
when I encounter them on the roads...

Pete.
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."
```
