I've got to disagree with what some of the others are saying how the equates
would be converted over to COBOL.  I work with both Assembler and COBOL and
I see equates like this coded in a LOT of the Assembler programs I maintain.
One person here said that equates are done by assembler programs so they can
call the registers by something other than just a number.  That's correct
(in fact where I work we use a canned initialization macro that among other
things, equates all the registers to R0, R1, R2, . . . R15), but that's not
all equates are used for.

It's also used to set up constants.  For instance we have a lot of one-byte
binary fields in our files, and if you wanted to check one for a particular
numeric value - say 37 - you could do this a few ways:
1)         CLI   INBYTE,X'25'

2)         CLC   INBYTE,COMPBYTE
        . . . . .
        . . . . .
    COMPBYTE DC    X'25'

3)         CLI   INBYTE,COMPBYTE
        . . . . .
        . . . . .
    COMPBYTE EQU   37

Using the equate you can still use the CLI instead of the CLC and you don't
have to figure out what the hex equivalent of 37 is (I know some folks have
been doing this so long they can do the conversions in their heads, but for
newbies it doesn't come naturally).

It can also be used to load a specific value into a register.  For instance
in your example you could load the value of 4 into register 15 (say for
setting a return code) by saying:
          LA    R15,BACK

If your assembler program is doing things like this, then the previous
responses were wrong.  MAIN, SCND and BACK are not level88 elements (which
would mean that they describe states that COMPUSE could be in), but rather
they are constants by themselves.  So the COBOL equivalent would be:

       01  ERIC.
              03  COMPID01            PIC X(8).
              03  COMPUSE            PIC X(1).
              03  MAIN                       PIC X(1) VALUE X'01'.
              03  SCND                     PIC X(1) VALUE X'02'.
              03  BACK                     PIC X(1) VALUE X'04'.

Not meant as a flame, but this is a crude use of the EQU. If you want a
symbol to represent the length of a field, than use the length
attribute, e.g.:

fielda_length  EQU  L'fielda

Let the Assembler calculate these things and you can change the data
structure without having to change your EQU's. If you code:

SOMETHING  EQU  37  or

SOMETHING  EQU  X'25'

these are had coded values which won't change if the field whose length
they represent is changed. In cse anyone's wondering, the length
attribute (L') may be used in open code. It's very powerful and I was
very happy to see COBOL II offer the LENGTH OF special register.
