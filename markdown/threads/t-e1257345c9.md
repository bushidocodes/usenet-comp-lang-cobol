Jimmy,

If you find a summary of Grace's accomplishments, I believe you will find
that she was the creator of the first "algebraic" compiler known as A-1.
From there other things she did included B-1, and B-2 (became known as
Flowmatic).

I have no doubt, however, that someone somewhere can point to some other
development that fits into the chain and muddels the picture.

For others following this subject, Flowmatic was introduced to commercial
and military customers prior to COBOL.  Work at the AFLC in Ohio extended
B-2 by creating a cross  compiler on a Univac I to generate programs for
other makes of computers in the Univac barn at the time.

Grace's office (including her of course) did a PR job on potential customers
for Flowmatic.  Some of them joined in her search for the next business
compiler, and a number of large companies in various lines of business
(users) created CODASYL, and set up three committees.  Short range, Mediam
and Long Range Committees to start the ball rolling.  Among those who were
on the Executive Committee was Charles Phillips of the DOD.  The short range
committee was given three months to use FLOWMATIC, AMICO (
AFLC's version of FLOWMATIC, AND two works in progress, one by Honeywell,
the other by IBM.  It took the short range group a year to reach agreement
on the first spec. That group was disolved, and the COBOL Committee set up.
At first it operated like congress (a vendors group, and a users group).
This became unworkable (you see it in Washington all of the time), so those
two groups were merged, and rules adopted as to membership between groups,
and other elements.  Bob Beamer, Grace Hopper, and someone from Honeywell
were named to the long range group, and the game plan to obtain vendor
compliance began.  I was one of the people who introduced Flowmatic into our
company, worked with AFLC to create a larger language to reflect the IBM705
line, became a member of the Users Group of the COBOL committee, and was
otherwise involved over a period of 20 years.  Summaries of these events
reflect the view of others who where involved, and these views may not
coinside. While other details might clarify the case of who invented COBOL,
I believe that we should say Grace did, and lots of folks agreed with her.
As far as FORTRAN is concerned, I believe it was an IBM development to
support their scientific systems.  In any case, to the end, I believe Grace
claimed the first algebraic compiler, A-1.

Warren Simmons
warren.simmons@worldnet.att.net

Here is an example of the difference of view surrounding COBOL. While
Richard's view is correct in the minds of many, 'IBM Commercial Translator'
was the name of the contribution of the IBM at that time, Grace, and Bob
Beamer worked closely to take care of each other's needs. The Honeywell
language was under development by Computer Science, and specs for it were
not available to us.

DOD's membership of the CODASYL group was in a position to require the
yearly declaration of each vendor doing business with them to sign and
return a contract each year. Charlie arranged three things for COBOL.

1. He arranged with the DOJ that users and vendors could in these
circomstances meet together legally.

2. In that first year following the publication of COBOL, vendors selling
computers to the Government's Military branches at least had to answer one
question before the renewal would occur.  That was something to the effect
of :
Will you or will you not supply a COBOL Complier for your hardware?
Those who failed to answer had to wait for their funds until they did.

3. Made it easy for various branches of the service to become members of the
Users Group of the COBOL committee.
