Perhaps you could call a complete porting of all COBOL code from a
mainframe to a wintel environment with client PC's and one/more servers a
migration to C/S. In that case I have experience: am just doing that (not
alone happily). We try to convert as much 1-to-1 resulting in thick
client in de C/S paradigma. (Remember the famous Gardner-scheme for
2-tier C/S:1 all on client / very very thick client
2 db on server, rest client / thick client
3 db&business logic on server, rest client / normal
4 server now has also validation, client is just interface (net-PC, html)
/ thin client
5 all on server, dumb terminal on client / traditional mf
)
Now most problems come from intertwining business logic, validation,
screenhandling all in one cobol program cluster. Code separation
(interface // validation // bus.logic // i/o-handling) was never thought
about in relation to concepts as "client" and "server" those days when
the app. was originally built. So porting to a C/S like platform is quit
a job ...

The Frog

Usually the problem lies not in generic mainframe vs c/s
antipathy, and not in perceived bad performance of c/s
(c/s is now on a par with mainframe) but in concrete 
subsystems that tend to be entirely different:

1. User Interface. CICS or IMS DC on mainframe.
Screen Section, or httpd on c/s. 

2. Database. IDMS, IMS, Datacom, or DB/2 on mainframe.
Oracle, Sybase, DB/2, SQL Server on c/s.

Just one of those can make migration very difficult.
Two of them make it even more difficult, and what ytou are
fighting in this case is not the changing syntax of calls
but moving to a different paradigm: from network model DB
to relational DB, from CICS to HTTP daemon, and so on.

We are now studying a conversion, where in addition to the
two standard UI/DB migartions there is Y2K remed.

Still conversions like this need to happen and 
we at Siber Systems believe that they are even automatable.

The usual problems that I have witnessed have much to do with
environment and less to do with c/s itself.

1.  The mainframe programmers take one look at the c/s environment.
1a. They wonder if the bosses are serious.
1b. They realise they always knew their bosses are serious.
1c. They wonder if their bosses are idiots.
1d. They realise that they know their bosses are idiots.
1e. The rearely get asked about the system being replaced.

2.  The UNIX guys walk into the building.
2a. They know the application on their first day, it's easy.
2b. They discuss strategy, methodology, design features, etc.
2c. The discuss the editor to use and what hilight colours to use.
2d. They then all disappear onto Oracle/Sybase/you-name-it courses.
2e. They discuss strategy, methodology in light of the courses in 2d.

3.  The UNIX guys have coded their project and it works.
3a. The c/s team get some live data from the mainframe guys.
3b. They don't understand the data.
3c. They ask about delimiters, mainframe guy shrugs.
3d. They ask about data structures, mainframe guy shrugs.
3e. They ask about VSAM believing it to be ISAM.

Struth, I wish I had the energy for this but pretty soon I'm going to be
watching live ML baseball so I'll stop here.  The main problem is lack
of communication, a lack of respect for the old farts on the mainframe,
the evangelical belief that any UNIX system can outperform any S/390
box, etc.

I have not yet witnessed a successful c/s conversion though I have seen
a few fail and a few get cancelled before failure becomes evident.  This
may all be a bit unfair to the c/s community but IMHO a conversion of a
working mainframe application to c/s is a waste of time and money.

The point is that in the exercise that I started to outline, the UNIX
guys arrive with a lot of preconceptions about how a system works and
what suers do.  Often these preconceptions fail muster.  Often they
don't understand data that has no delimiters, they have trouble with
simple stuff like packed decimal, etc., etc.

In short they decry the old system without either understanding that it
might actually work, and they feel that they have to not only reinvent
the wheel but ignore that a wheel aready exists that couuld be used for
reference.  The users detect this, a lot of users are not stupid.

Fact.  At one site, I was expressly forbidden to talk to users without
project management in attendance.  I should point out that this was a
c/s implementation using a well-known financials package and the users
ahd been told from Day 1 that it was the best thing since rat's fur. 
One lunchtime, I was backed into a corner (literally) by some users and
asked one simple question "We realise that this new system is going to
happen; but they can't honestly be serious that we are going to get a
better system from this, can they?"  My answer was that I had that day
depreciated a thousand assets on the production machine which was
otherwise empty at the time, and it had taken forty-five minutes.  The
users knew that they had a hundred & twenty thousand assets that were
depreciated every month.  We parted on the basis that I had told them
nothing and they went off to ponder this problem.  I understand that the
project was cancelled about six month later but I had moved on by that
time.

It's not really that strange.  However, first a note to point out that
the compile job does not go into a compile queue.  It goes into a queue
for an initiator which runs the compile or whatever other sort of job
that is being run.  A compile may join a queue that is dealt with by one
or several initiators, depending on the system usage at the time, and
the compile may happen instantaneously or not.

The reason that things are done this way is for efficient use of
available system resources, so that a compile, for instance, does not
chew up resources that are needed by the live online systems that are
also probably running in the mainframe virtual environment.  This way, a
quart is squeezed into a pint pot.  Compared to what typically goes on
in a mainframe environment, a UNIX system does remarkably few things.
