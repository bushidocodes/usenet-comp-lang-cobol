On Wed, 26 Aug 1998 14:09:04, GeorgeValentine@usa.net wrote:
 
> I have some, possibly stupid suggestions, that you may not have though of
> or may have considered too much trouble. (If you are considering having
> one built, that probably puts these back in contention.)

These are not stupid suggestions... well, most aren't, one is.
 
> First, IBM used to make systems in a truck for the Army, and it may be that
> one or more of them still exists somewhere.  If you have good contacts in
> the military you may be able to locate one.  I would assume that DOD
> has an inventory list somewhere. (Similarly, GAO or somebody probably
> has an inventory for civilian agancies, one of which may have one.)

Great tip, we were looking for a contact at the DLA, the Defense 
Logistic Agency but the GAO might have records on other systems.
 
> Second, if the trickle-down theory works, there must be some S370s in
> remote countries that you might be able to get to.  Along those lines, I
> would try Russia first.  I would expect that you would have to do some
> off-net scrounging, maybe with the help of the State Department.

I don't have any contacts at State but this is a good suggestion.
 
> Third, you might call the "maintenance" vendors, particularly those that
> are global, and see if they know of a site, in-country or out.

We've checked with exporters and domestic hardware maintenance
companies... no luck.  This is early 1970's technology and vanished in 
the late 1980's.
 
> Fourth, if you are into _very tedious_ you might have the pack disassembled,
> have the surfaces developed, and have the bits read microscopically. (I
> am assuming that the surface on the 3330 is similar to the surface on a
> tape so that it can, in fact, be developed.)

This is the one stupid suggestion... and I'm not pinging on you. We 
wasted several hours debating magnaflux development.  It got heated for 
a while. So we're the dummies, not you. 

Here's the problem, the device contains 200+ megabytes.  The magnaflux
will reveal the individual magnetic flux changes including IBGs,
ECCs/Parity, start-stop framing (if 3330's have such a thing).

To read the data, you have to read the ones and zeros, keeping track of 
positions. I can imagine someone staring into a stereo microscope and 
calling out "One, zero, one, one, one, zero".  At a ten cents a byte,
a penny a bit, this is a twenty million dollar labor charge.

It would be cheaper to have an engineering house mount a modern head on 
a stick and servo.  Even if they charge fifty grand to build the rig,
it's much cheaper and more reliable.
 
> Fifth, since money seems to be a small matter, why not get your client to
> offer a $10 or $20,000 finders fee for someone locating a usable 3330, and
> publicize it on the web? Enough people will probably make enough calls
> to find one if it exists.

Well, cost is always a consideration.  I've put hundreds of hours into
this, pro bono...  when services are free, the client doesn't mind 
asking you to check on just one more issue.
 
I don't blame them for that; it's human nature not to appreciate that 
which is free.
