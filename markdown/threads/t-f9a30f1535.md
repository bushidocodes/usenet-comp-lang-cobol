Well, I have to disagree. I find them conceptually different. The only
three things being common to both:1: They support multi-user concurrent
access to data while ensuring the ACID properties of transactions, logging
and recovery.
2: Both have a DML defining access to data (CODASYL DML defining how access
should be done, SQL defining what access should be done) and a DDL defining
the structure of the database.
3: Both DMLs can be accessed using a general purpose programming language

The abstraction is ofcourse not of the RDBMS, rather on the structure of
the data in the database, and yes it does not imply "hide entirely". If it
was totally hidden, how could it then be accessed? The only kind of
"database system" being able to hide such info are persistent programming
languages supporting orthogonal persistence.

Agreed, RDBMS do not offer this kind of optimization, and the optimization
would ofcourse only work if the record-size of all referenced records fit
within one page. Most OODBMS on the other hand offers this kind of
clustering. Some RDBMSs supports dynamic or static arrays (which may be
included in SQL3). The arrays are defined as relations themself, but
usually contained within the same page(s) as the row they belong to (if it
fits within a page). This would probably be a part of SQL3 since SQL3
should be able handle objects.

Well, I can't answer for everyone, only for what is lectured at University
of Oslo, but I suspect that UiO is no different than any other
university. The lectures/courses do not consider network DBMS to be a minor
improvement of hierarchical ones. They are considered as two separate DBMS
concepts with different properties, as is RDBMS, ORDBMS, OODBMS, and
persistent languages as well. No lecturer in his/her right mind would say
that one of them are better than the other because it would all depend on
the problem domain and the universe of discourse, and in the end it all
comes down to application and database design.I think that the RDBMS is by
many considered to be the "best" because they are more common (even though
network and hierarchical may be the most frequently used and definitly the
major DBMSs found in legacy systems, or the biggest DB in the world is
based on an OODBMS) than other DBMS, and the RDBMS vendors are very good at
marketing and showing off their view of the world.
