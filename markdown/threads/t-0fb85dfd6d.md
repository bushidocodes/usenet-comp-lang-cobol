The following will be published in "Year 2000 Journal" in an upcoming
issue:

COBOL, Coca Cola and Year 2000 Things built on core principles endure.
Coke is the real thing; COBOL is clarity in computer systems. How
wonderful to see COBOL programmers in such demand these days. COBOL has
remained a technically relevant programming language for its entire 38
year history and will remain so well after January 1, 2000. The
American and International COBOL Committees continue to assure this.
COBOL has been fighting a public image problem for nearly all of its
existence. COBOL programmers have been at the receiving end of abuse for
too long. Academia, more interested in computer science than in
commercial computing, turned its back on the COBOL language from the
very beginning. It was left primarily to private industry to train
employees in business computing subjects including COBOL programming.
Many people have pointed to COBOL as one of the reasons for the Year-2000
debacle we are approaching, citing the large number of COBOL programs
that must now be "fixed." The millennium problem facing the computer
industry today is not a COBOL problem as many have ascribed. If
anything, the COBOL language will prove in the end to have helped in the
solution of this problem. The millennium problem, is caused by the
shortsightedness of the computer application designers who chose to take
shortcuts - using whatever languages were available - to represent dates
with two digit years rather than 4 digit years (i.e. "97" rather than
"1997"). The computer industry - indeed the entire international
business community - is rediscovering the lessons learned 2 and 3
decades ago: for computer applications, clearness is a virtue, not
cleverness. COBOL shines above all other programming languages in its
clarity. The perception that the 38 year old COBOL languages is obsolete
and irrelevant for today魹ｽs computer systems, while being an accurate
assessment of current opinion is nonetheless an incorrect conclusion.
The notion that COBOL programmers will become "dinosaurs after 2000 as
the industry moves beyond hot languages such as C++ and Java" is
mistakenly presumed by the subsequent generation of computer programmers
who grew up with the first home PC魹ｽs and who lack perspective of the
computer industry魹ｽs history. COBOL is not irrelevant now nor will it be
after 2000. It is true that the new generation of programmers (and
managers) prefers C, C++, Java, etc. This is because these languages
arose from the operating environments of the personal computers (DOS,
Unix, Internet, etc.) with which they were most familiar. But this new
generation of programmers (and managers) is ignoring the reasons that
COBOL became so popular 30 years ago for programming business computers.
COBOL is simply the clearest language around to develop complex computer
applications. We need only look at the huge inventory of COBOL programs
still running on computers today to observe that these applications, some
of which were developed 10, 20 and 30 years ago, are so durable that
rather than needing to be discarded and replaced over the years, they
needed simply to be modified and updated. There are few things in the
world today, so solid and durable, that can boast of such a legacy.
Unfortunately the enormous popularity of more technical (lower-lever)
languages such as C, C++, Java, etc. will result in an enormous computer
application maintenance problem in years to come. The computer industry
is already seeing this emerging problem. In many ways it is a repeat of
the problems that the computer industry faced 30 years ago as it
struggled to replace its "assembler" (low level) applications with more
maintainable systems developed in languages such as COBOL. The move away
from COBOL in the last decade has been felt very subtly by the entire
commercial world. Many have observed that the world is becoming a
little less "humanized" and more impersonal in its inter-relationships.
Computers are often cited as the cause. The real problem is not the
excessive use of computers. It is more often caused by the rigid design
and clever development of modern computer applications. This is one
consequence of replacing clear COBOL instructions with terse and clever
C, C++, etc., instructions in the computers that run the commercial
world. So here魹ｽs to COBOL and Coca Cola. I suspect they will both be
around well after January 1, 2000. Jerome Garfunkel The author is a
member of the American COBOL Committee (ANSI X3J4), the International
COBOL Committee (ISO WG4); as well as other computer industry standards
committees.

After I finished reading the post below, I had to stop and reread it again.
The view of reality it projects differs enough from my perspective that I felt
I have to respond. To preface this, let me say that I have been a diehard
COBOL "code cranker" for over 23 years (25 if you count the time I spent as an
unpaid "assistant" in college). Oh, I've programmed in other languages -
FORTRAN IV, BAL, DBASE III, CLIPPER, C, NEAT/3, IBM Series I assembler, even
Lotus 1-2-3 macros - but COBOL has been always been my main language.

That said, the picture painted is way too rosy, particularly since I read
recently that the new standards for COBOL are going to be delayed for some
time. Mr. Garfunkel seems to assume that 1) C, C++, and JAVA are the
primary threat to COBOL programming, 2) the "dehumanizing" aspects of "modern
computer applications" (read that as PC apps?) is a result of the language
those applications are written in, and 3) COBOL will conquer all.

C and C++ are not major threats to COBOL and are just as endangered as COBOL.
Let me tell you a story to illustrate "the danger":

Dave is a friend of my boss. Dave retired last year after being a Dean at a
major university for many years; he came by our office to chat shortly after
that. Well, my boss was working with Visual Dbase and showed Dave how it
worked. Dave got excited, got a copy for himself, and within TWO DAYS had
produced a working application. A simple one, but still it was a correctly
working application.

Now, before you jump to conclusions - I don't believe VdB is going to replace
COBOL; the jury's still out on whether Borland survives. The above story is
not typical. But thirty years ago, the above story wouldn't and couldn't have
happened; today, a significant percentage of small businesses can buy
commercial software packages that they can customize to their needs WITHOUT
NEEDING A PROGRAMMER. Although RAD development tools - and other "terse"
languages - have not done as well as hyped by their vendors, this does not
imply that the concepts are bogus. With the cost of hardware in a constant
downward spiral, the cost of software development increases in importance.
COBOL must address this or die a slow death - relegated to the huge legacy
system(s) that are too "expensive" to rewrite.

As for the "dehumanizing" PC, don't blame the language; blame the designers.
A badly designed app is a badly designed app and I've seen several in COBOL.

If COBOL is to remain "king of the hill", it is going to have to change and
become an even "higher" level language. I see encouraging developments; all
is not lost. But I'm advising everyone I know to stay current with all of the
latest developments.

P.S. I'm also a die-in-the-wool, sugar-free, caffeine-free, Pepsi drinker :]

William:

I took Mr. Garfunkel's comment, "the world is becoming a little less
"humanized" and more impersonal" became that way not so much because of
PC's, but because he felt that lower-level languages such as C, C++,
Java, etc. traps the programmer into a great deal more technical
problems than Cobol. I felt he may have been trying to state that this
may have a tendency to take the programmer away from his customers too
much because of this. He kind of seems to imply design choices as being
part of the problem as you point out.

As you pointed out with your example of the individual creating a simple
application using Visual dBase in a couple of days, I think this was
also part of Mr. Garfunkel's line of reasoning. While these products
allow development of applications, they have been mistakingly been hyped
to be able to accomplish the same results as industrial-strength Cobol
and mainframes, for example. This has caused important, critical
systems to be trivialized and has lead to the on-going "the mainframe is
dead" march, for example. His comments seemed to debunk the
"silver-bullet", "pull a rabbit out of the hat" and market hype as a
significant part of problems we have today.

I agree with you completely when you said Cobol has to evolve. And the
delays to the '97 Cobol standard (if this happens) is a mistake. One of
the reasons for Cobol's great staying-power has been its standards. But
I also believe the times between standards and the dealys is also one of
Cobol's biggest enemies. This process must be stream-lined and a lot of
the politics eliminated. Cobol must remain competitive.

As you said, it is important to stay current with the latest
developments. I agree. But I also think you have to stay current (and
educated) so you can bypass the junk that's out there, too.

It may not be just an issue of evolving, but also that of improving upon
what is already in the language. It can be difficult to determine that
language "A" has a feature which is more evolved than language "B". It
might just be that language "A's" implementation of a feature or
function is easier.

While it is true that OS/VS Cobol is capable of supporting business
applications as well as VS Cobol/II, I have found that a few of the
Ansi-85 features contained in later releases of these compilers make it
much easier to do certain things. For me, the most important
improvements are the End-If scope delimiter, in-line performs and
reference modification. I have found these items extremely useful and
have contributed to making coding tasks easier. I view these things as
improvements more than envolving. There are new features in the '97
Cobol standard that I see as being extremely important in reducing
coding time and perhaps making the program a little less complex by
using these new features.

Probably the one feature you might argue to be an evolutionary process
in Cobol is the object-oriented methods. While I think the jury is
still out on this approach, I think it is important to provide it to
give the industry the opportunity to evaluate its merit. And you're
right, I don't want anything that will require 3 manuals to understand
it. If it requires that, then the process is more complex than it is
worth. For the most part, Cobol has done a good job in providing easy
access to functions without the need for complex coding. STRING,
UNSTRING and the Intrinsic Functions provide a wealth of useability
without a lot of fuss.

While Cobol is primarily a business language, its availability on other
platforms other than the mainframe allows an even greater departure from
Cobol being just a business language. I've written applications that
are more utilitarian the business-oriented. I like the ability to do
this. I don't believe in a strict relgation of Cobol to business
applications only. Perhaps this can be viewed as an evolutionary step
for Cobol.

I think Cobol must be competitive and responsive to the needs of its
users, while preserving its main objectives that has given its
tremendous staying-power all these years.

Tom:

I agree with you (and the many others, including myself) who have stated
that the date problem originating three decaced ago was based on
extremely limited and expensive computer resources. Looking back today,
it's easy for one to claim short-sightedness. I have to admit, though,
that I was surprised to hear Jerome Garfunkel make such an explicit
statement. Jerome has been a primo Cobol supporter for years. And in
all fairness, the comments in this particular article were very pro
Cobol and Cobol programmer. That's why I'm surprised he made that
comment, knowing how most programmers who know of the past era of
computing, would jump down a person's throat when they make that kind of
comment. I believe Jerome's own experience covers a very long period of
time.
