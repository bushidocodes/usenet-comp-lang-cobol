```
Came across the following whilst doing a bit of Y2K remediation:

IF TABLE-MO (JURIS-SUB, RATE-SUB, TYP-SUB, MONTH-SUB) =
                           (4 OR 6 OR 9 OR 11)
    MOVE 30 TO WRAR091-TO-DAY
ELSE
    MOVE 31 TO WRAR091-TO-DAY.
IF TABLE-MO (JURIS-SUB, RATE-SUB, TYP-SUB, MONTH-SUB) = 2
    MOVE 28 TO WRAR091-TO-DAY.

What is it trying to do?  It is trying to force the end-of-month date to a
field based on something buried in a four-dimensional array.

What does it succeed in doing?  It succeeds in *never* giving February 29
days.

When was it written?  DATE-WRITTEN is 12 Nov 1984... so, about 14 years
ago.

When was it last modified?  Last mods appear to have been done 16 Jan
1997 (found in MODCODE, or 'comments' area); the Prod load module was
created then, as well.

What does a Good Y2K Remediator do about it?  Notify the Project Lead and
suggest that the Appropriate User generate a ChangeReq... if *that* much.

DD
```

```
The correct answer was given a while ago - but just to clarify it (with the
cross-postings),

The ANS'74 COBOL Standard required that a compiler must support *AT LEAST* 3
levels (or dimensions).  There was no requirement that it support exactly 3
levels - nor that supporting more than 3 levels be treated as an extension
(via flagging).

Some '74 compilers did support only 3 levels, others supported 16 or 25
levels - or the reported 48 levels.  During the development of the '85
Standard, there was a proposal that a  compiler be required to support 48
levels until it was pointed out that the fastest then available computer
would take several days (or some other large time amount) simply to
initialize such a table - if it only had 2 bytes at the "bottom" of the
levels (i.e. 2 ** 48).

I never verified this, but it ended up with the current Standard requiring
AT LEAST 7 levels - with (again) no upper limit.
```
