There is a (relatively) common misconception that a "group item" (non-National
group in the 2002 Standard) is treated  EXACTLY the same as an alphanumeric
elementary item.  In truth and fact, there is a relatively obscure "difference"
which you have discovered.  If you have a copy of the '85 Standard, you will
need to look at the MOVE general rules (3 & 4 specifically) on page VI-104.

The bottom-line is that EDITING in a move to an edited field *only* applies to
MOVEs where *both* the sending and receiving items are elementary.  In the case
of a group sending item, it is "as if" the receiving item where either redefined
as not edited or as if it where reference modified (1:).

So yes, both of your compilers are working AS REQUIRED by the Standard (Old,
very old, and NEW).

Please do NOT ask me WHY this was ever defined this way, but it certainly would
"break" some programs if the rules were changed now.

De-editing MOVE is a requirement of ANSI X3.23-1985; some earlier
COBOL implementations may also include it as an extension.

In our case (Unisys MCP/AS COBOL85) "de-editing" is quite a bit more
costly than the corresponding MOVE in the other direction; that's
because there's direct hardware support for the composition of the
edited field, but not for its decomposition.

In terms of reliability, however, I'd stack its behavior up against
anyone's, and would suggest that anyone who has a problem with it in
which de-editing fails to work as required file a high-priority
trouble report, as the failure is directly indicative of user data
corruption.  I just went through our records, and our customers have
reported exactly two problems with de-editing MOVE (both were with
floating insertion editing), one in 1989 and one last year.  Software
containing the correction was promptly made available for distribution
to (or retrieval by) all users.

What I can't find in the '85 standard is an explicit statement that
the contents of the source field in a de-editing MOVE have to be a
possible result of an edited MOVE to the item in the first place.  If
indeed that's an omission, it's been rectified in the '02 standard
(page 398, paragraph 14.5.12.2 (2), Incompatible data).

I suspect some user problems with de-editing MOVEs result from
attempts to use them with data that wasn't produced (or isn't
producible) by the corresponding edited MOVE in the first place, and
my response to anyone complaining about that is "well, duh ...".

    -Chuck Stevens

> Chuck,
>    Although it is not ALWAYS possible where "MOVE de-editing" is possible, do
> you have any opinions (for your compiler or in general) on when to use MOVE
> de-editing vs NumVal / NumVal-C - where the compiler supports intrinsic

Point 1:  NUMVAL-C, and particularly NUMVAL, are more restrictive in
content than the de-editing MOVE.  Anything that's allowed in the
PICTURE is allowed in the source argument of the de-editing MOVE;
that's not true of the functions (NUMVAL-C doesn't cover asterisk
suppression or inserted zeroes, as I see it).

Point 2:  The functions return a *numeric* value.  In our case, for
example for a dollars-and-cents value, they will return a
*floating-point* representation of the value instead of the
integer-pennies internal value that the de-editing MOVE would produce.
 This means that the answer for these functions may be *less exact*
than that produced by the de-editing MOVE.

Point 3:  I'd expect NUMVAL-C to perform about the same as, or a
little worse than, the de-editing MOVE in similar contexts.  I have
not tested this expectation.

Thus:  When the programmer has a reasonable expectation that the
information in the source field does indeed match the PICTURE
character-string, I would strongly recommend the use of de-editing
MOVE, even to the point of trying de-editing MOVEs with a few
different PICTUREs.  Avoiding a floating-point internal representation
of a fixed-point noninteger value is a huge plus in terms of accuracy.
 I'd personally reserve NUMVAL-C (and NUMVAL) for the cases in which
the programmer hasn't a clue what the data might look like *and* an
approximation of the numeric value is OK.

> Note:
>   The 2002 Standard would even allow you to "detect" an invalid argument
> (telling you exactly where the "bad" data was) in an argument to NumVal /
> NumVal-C *or* to detect incompatible data in the MOVE Statement.

Yes, but the '89 amendment doesn't have this.  In our implementation,
if you do a de-editing MOVE with data that doesn't match the PICTURE
you get whatever it is that you get; the logic doesn't account for the
possibility that it might not match.  In contrast, data that violates
the rules for NUMVAL and NUMVAL-C arguments cause a run-time error.

    -Chuck Stevens

Unisys MCP/AS COBOL dialects have had this extension for some time.
However, the way it was originally implemented (back in the early days of
COBOL(68) on the Burroughs Large System) it wasn't just the hyphen -- it was
any character not in the strict syntax for ANSI compliance that was treated
as a "simple insertion character".

The consequence of that particular convention was essentially that it became
impossible for the compiler to distinguish a syntactically-correct PICTURE
character-string from one that was not, unless the PICTURE exceeded some
sort of limits (overall length of the PICTURE string and length of the
described item, in particular).   I speak from lengthy and bitter
experience:  travel any distance at all down that path, and yonder be
dragons.

Unisys now actively discourages the use of this convention.

The Burroughs Medium System (V Series) had a different convention:  any
character except the blank could be used as a simple insertion character by
the simple method of preceding it with "I".  Recently this latter convention
was introduced to MCP/AS COBOL74 and COBOL85, and has become the default in
current software.  These compilers are now capable of recognizing that , for
example, "PICTURE X(7." is more likely to be a typographical error and thus
a syntax error than it is to declare a three-character edited-alphanumeric
data item with a single variable characters trailed by the insertion
characters "(" and "7".    If you really wanted PICTURE X(7 then the
unambiguous way to code that is PICTURE XI(I7 according to this newer
convention, and by default PICTURE X(7 will now produce a syntax error.

Over the decades, there have been similar proposals to INCITS/J4 to allow
arbitrary insertion characters in PICTURE character strings; as I recall the
most recent proposal is to enclose the character in quotes.  But at least in
part  because of the fragility of the PICTURE rules as demonstrated over the
history of COBOL, andin part because of some conflicts between the proposal
and currency-sign conventions, the standards committee has been reluctant to
incorporate this capability.  I believe it remains on the "candidates" list,
but not for the upcoming standard.

Had the Burroughs Large System COBOL(68) compiler limited the rule to
hyphens (and perhaps colons!), the approach might *perhaps* have been
viable, but alas, that wasn't the case.

> there would be no ambiguity with its use as a sign, which would always be
to
> the left or right of any data characters.

The current preferred approach in the MCP/AS COBOLs would result in PICTURE
999I-99I-9999; the most recent standards proposal, as I recall it, PICTURE
999"-"99"-"9999.  I have to admit, having implemented (and re-implemented!)
PICTURE parsers, preferring some sort of EXplicit indication of such
insertion characters (and allowing a whole range of such characters) to the
suggestion relating to hyphens alone, or its extension to anything that's
out-of-context according to the rules.  .

    -Chuck Stevens
