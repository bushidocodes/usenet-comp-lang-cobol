```
A Computerworld article headlined with subject, subhead "Experienced workers
nearing retirement age", dated March 2002, reads:

--begin quotation from 
//www.computerworld.com/hardwaretopics/hardware/story/0,10801,68764,00.html

IT managers looking to cut labor costs during the ongoing recession
might want to think twice before laying off any of their workers who
have mainframe or data center skills.

With mainframe shipments on the rise and legions of traditional
mainframe IT workers nearing retirement age, technology managers last
week said it's becoming increasingly difficult and costly to replace
employees who have deep experience in Cobol, CICS and other mainframe
technologies.

For example, the Workplace Safety and Insurance Board of Ontario is
shelling out 10% to 15% signing bonuses to new workers with mainframe
skills. The Toronto-based board, which oversees workplace safety and
workers' compensation issues for the province of Ontario, also pays
"hot skills" premiums to mainframe experts. That can boost their
annual salaries by as much as 10%, said David Londry, director of the
board's corporate information center.

--end of quotation

Sounds good, so I went to //www.wsib.on.ca/wsib/wsibsite.nsf/public/home_e to
claim a signing bonus and high-paying job. Bzzzt. There are no job openings.

The article describes similar desperation at Auto Club of Southern California.
//www.aaa-calif.com/ shows no openings either.

Reality check -- job boards such as dice and monster show buyer's market prices
as low as $20/hr, average around 30 for a six month contract with no benefits or
expenses. After factoring in six months idle time between gigs and moving
expenses, the "hot skilled" programmer ends up with less than $30K. By
comparison, unemployment pays almost $20K.

It could be worse. Freelance writers of stories like this make $300-500 per
article. If they work at it all year, they make about $10K. Most are women who
write to supplement hubby's real job, or work Temp jobs during the day and write
at night. My source: personal friendships with half a dozen of them.
```

solely to be sacrificial goats for the annual 10% purge. While I was there, the
company announced first quarter 2000 had the highest quarterly net profit in the
company's history -- $2B. The company usually makes that much per year. A month
later top management announced a 10% layoff. By no coincidence, every department
had 10-12% highly paid contractors doing non-essential work.

When a Roman Army unit screwed up, the dicipline was lining the soldiers up and
killing every tenth man. This is the origin of the word "decimated". 

In the early days of Microsoft, employees filled out annual 'report cards' on
co-workers they knew. The employee list was sorted by average grade and the
lowest 10% were fired. That's not a skills assessment; that's a popularity
contest. It gives new meaning to the phrase 'Works well with others.'
