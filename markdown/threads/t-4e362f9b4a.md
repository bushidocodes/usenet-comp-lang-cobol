I have seen this behaviour before, but it is quite often dependant on
the particular COBOL compiler. You did not mention who wrote your
compiler.

COBOL compilers treat the strings inside DISPLAY statements differently.
Most of them put the strings into the literal pool, which is in the same
location as the data. Changing the DISPLAY statements would make the
data area align differently when compiled, and therein lies the secret.
You see, quite often the DISPLAY statement is not at fault. If your
program is behaving like this, it is probably due to a bad address
reference. Adding or deleting DISPLAY statements just changes the pointers
to data, and the problem may be masked. By that, I mean that the program
may be referencing a bad pointer, one which points outside its memory
boundary, or by moving everything around, the pointer is suddenly "good".
However, you may be suffering other, more subtle problems.

The most common reason for this failure is in the linkage section. For
example, if you call a subprogram and pass parameters, and the subprogram
then declares the parameters, BUT IN THE WRONG ORDER, you will have
bad address pointers. Another problem might be if your main program has
a variable that is a different size than the corresponding variable in
the sub program. For example, you might have a PIC X(500) in the main
program, but the sub has PIC X(250). The fields might even be declared
differently.
