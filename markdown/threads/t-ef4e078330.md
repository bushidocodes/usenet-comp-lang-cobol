```
Well, been a while since I posted anything technical... so here goes:

As some of you might have suspected I'm a COBOL jockey, working on Y2K
fixes for a utility... ugly, smell, dull, dull, DULL work, sifting
through code old enough to vote and applying unimaginative fixes to it. 
Pays the bills, though.

Anyhow... the working routine is such that when a programmer starts
remediating a chunk o' code the first thing that's done is to rund said
code through a scanning utility, looking for certain combinations of
characters... DATE, YEAR, MONTH, YY and the like.  If hits show up (yes,
this does *not* work for 'cutesie-poo programmer names' like LUIGI,
ELIZABETH or EVE-OF-DESTRUCTION) the code is examined to see what *kind*
of manipulations these fields are subject to; if an unequal comparison
is made (greater or less than) then Y2K mods must be made but equality
is ignored... 00 = 00, usually.

Well, a bunch o' months back I checked through a program (let's call it
PATTY) and the only hit I got was on a field called WS-YRMO being
checked for equality... IF WS-YRMO = quack quack.  I ignored it, checked
the rest of the programs in the jobstream, made my mods, tested the
jobstream against Production data and outputs and all was good.

SIDE NOTE:  Testing consisted of remediating all programs in a
jobstream, running the job against Prod data (as mentioned above) and
then running file-compares of the output of the remediated jobstream
against the present Prod output, 'ageing' the files with a utility I had
written which adds a few years to the dates in the files, re-running the
remediated code agains the aged data and comparing the output with the
dates masked out... in other words, if I know a record of 80 characters
contains a six-digit date in positions 10 - 15 I use parameter input to
the file-compare utility (SUPERCE) to check positions 1 - 9, skip 10 -
15 and check 16 - 80.  The reasons for this should be obvious.

This was months and months ago... last week another programmer doing
testing came to me and said 'You know program PATTY you modified a while
back?  It isn't working, fix it.'  I checked the output she was getting
and sure enough, PATTY was throwing bad data... how was this happening,
I hadn't modified the code?  I checked more deeply and found...

... that in the intervening months *another* programmer, a rather junior
lad (but *very* enthusiastic and earnest!), had been given a different
jobstream to remediate, one of the steps of which invoked PATTY.  He did
a source-scan on PATTY and latched on to the statement I had skipped. 
(Now things get technical)  He found that WS-YRMO was a four-digit field
defined as a numeric:

05    WS-YRMO  PIC 9(4).

... and that the comparison being done, while being an equivalency test,
was... inaccurate.  The code was looking to force the date to month-end;
with that in mind the programmer had written, during the last mods done
in 1988:

IF WS-YRMO = 4 OR 6 OR 9 OR 11
    MOVE 30 TO WS-DD
ELSE
IF WS-YRMO = 2
    MOVE 28 TO WS-DD
ELSE
    MOVE 31 TO WS-DD.

Now, ignoring the utter lack of leap-year checking for the value of 2...
WS-YRMO is a *four*-digit field.  The first comparison would be true
only for the months of April, June, September and November in the year
2000 (0004, 0006, etc.), the second one only for February, 2000... so
the program had been defaulting to the last value...

... and ever since 1988... in other words, for nigh a decade... this
program had been saying that *every* month has 31 days.  For nigh a
decade it had been spewing out Bad Data.

This young programmer, full of urine and acetic acid, then coded a
change... it has to be *right*, don't you know!  He made use of a
feature of COBOL II called 'reference modification'; since he knew that
the month began in position 3 of WS-YRMO and had a length of 2
characters he changed the code to read:

IF WS-YRMO(3:2) = 4 OR 6 OR 9 OR 11
    MOVE 30 TO WS-DD
ELSE
IF WS-YRMO(3:2) = 2
    MOVE 28 TO WS-DD
ELSE
    MOVE 31 TO WS-DD.

There, isn't that nice?

NO!  Reference modification treats fields as *alphabetic*, not
numeric... so that on one side of the equation you have an alpha of two
characters (WS-YRMO(3:2)) being compared for equality (=) to, for the
most part, *single-digit* numerics (4, 6, etc.)... and the months were
*still* being forced to 31...

... *EXCEPT* for the month of November, because the 11 in the comparison
*also* has two digits... and *that* was where the tester found the
errors... because when she tested the output and found discrepancies she
saw that *all* months had 31 days *except* for November, which had 30.

After finding out this lovely state of affairs the tester and I went to
our Group Leader and explained the matters... and he said 'Oh yeah, I
remember a couple'a months back ol' Junior came to me and said that he
had found a mistake which had slipped past you (pointing to *me*)... but
that he'd corrected it and I didn't think much of it after that.'

DD
```
