COBOL programmers tend to wear suits and work in a professional
business environment (banking, insurance, manufacturing,
transportation, financial markets, etc.). C++ (and Java, etc.), tend
to work in web-based endeavors (dot-coms) and wear the same clothes
for a year.

COBOL projects are pretty pedestrian: payroll, accounting, inventory,
and the completed program runs in production for many decades. C++
jobs are exciting, high-stress, and whose lifetimes (like some games)
are measured in months.

The 'Right Direction,' then, depends on what you want to do with your
life.

If you think C++ is not overly complicated, thrn just what is a "protected
abstract virtual base pure virtual private destructor: and when was the
last time you needed one?" -Tom Cargill, C++ Journal, Fall 1990 as quoted
in Expert C Programming, Deep C Secrets, by Peter van der Linden, page 293.

Personally I prefer not to have to deal with this kind of complexity but
should decide for yourself.

Also a very practical consideration is what kind of jobs are available in
you geographic area.  Unless you can demonstrate programming experience it
may be difficult to get a programming job at a salary comparable to that of
a production manager.  There is much more to life than money but your
personal circumstances and values will determine what is possible.  Some
people can take a short term salary decrease when  it looks like long term
prospects will result in a more satisfying job which in time may provide a
higher salary.  For some just having a more satisfying job is enough
regardless of the money.  Family circumstances are obviously important.

In my experience, the hardest part of making this kind of switch, once you
have decided that is what you want, is perserverence in the face of seeming
rejection.  In fact I would suggest that you not think of it as rejection
but use each experience as a learning for making adjustments in presenting
and selling yourself.  Keep you eye on your goal.

Cobol is probably THE business language. There are millions and millions of
lines of code out there running all sorts of business functions and who knows
what else.  While other languages are also used most large companies have their
systems in Cobol. 

As said in other replies - which language you learn depends on what you want to
do. If you're happy doing things for financial applications then Cobol is
probably it, manufacturing applications - well I guess that depends on which
ones but where I work they are mostly in Cobol (we have a few non-cobol systems
floating about).

You mentioned CICS and DB2.  This implies mainframe.  While not the exclusive
balliwick of Big Iron that's where you'll find most applications using these
resources. 

On that note you'll be looking for a job at a large company, not your ma and
pop type company (although there are those out there that use small mainframes
they are hard to find).  So the next question you have to ask yourself is do
you want to work for Large Corporation.  

Maybe you're considering consulting, either on your own or through a consulting
firm. Well - it's a buyer's market out there (at least in my neck of the woods)
and beginners aren't welcome (which takes me back to Large Corporation - which
also doesn't look too benignly at beginners).

Not trying to discourage you (although it may seem like it).  More Cobol
programmers are needed, beginners (everyone of us was a beginner at one time in
history) or not, it's just the big muckity mucks on high just don't see it. 
They are anamoured with the system dejour and fail to realize that what they
have is still quite alive and kicking.

As you go through the course we're here to assist you with any questions you
may have with the language.

Eileen
