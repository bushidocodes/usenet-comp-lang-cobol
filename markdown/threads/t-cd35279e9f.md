Reminds me of a stories I heard in math grad school about how the grad
students of the legendary John Vom Neumann would tease him.

One day a grad student described a "story problem" of two trains on
the same track, d distance apart, one traveling at speed s1, and one
at s2 speed, toward each other, and a bee buzzing back and forth
between the two at s3 speed (with zero reversal turnaround time). How
far will the bee have traveled by the time the trains meet, or uh,
crash.

Whatever the answer was, Von Neumann answered instantly.

"Oh, I see you figured out the trick of calculating how long it takes
for the trains to meet and then using that elapsed time with speed
s3."

"Oh, no!" Von Neumann exclaimed, "I solved it with infinite series!"

The other story is how his students would torment him: They would work
in groups all night long on a particularly difficult problem. Then at
the next coffee hour, one would say offhandedly, "Dr. Von Neumann, I
saw an interesting problem the other day, blah, blah, blah, how would
you find the answer?"

Von Neumann would think for less than a minute, and just as he opened
his mouth to tell the answer, an accomplice would blurt it out just
before him. It drove him apoplectic. Poor guy, he suffered so much for
being so smart. :-)

It's hard to say if this is a poorly thought out question, or one with
a double trick.

But yes, the trains will crash.

No trains traveling 100mph can stop in 250 yards*, even with full
application of brakes.

Trains have fail-safe air brakes, and the pressure in the brake line
keeps them *released.*  If there's a failure, the springs in the
brakes causes them to clamp, so a general brake system failure will
lock all the brakes at the "maximum friction" setting (so the answer
that might have been intended is "the trains will stop because the
brakes fail safe").  But that's not going to stop a 100mph train in
250 yards.


*The stopping distance for a typical 55mph freight train is over a
mile.  Heavy rail passenger trains (which are very light compared to
loaded freight trains) will do better, but not enough to matter at
100mph.  Even light rail trains are going to blow the 500 yard
distance at 100mph by a considerable margin (you'd expect light rail
from 55mph to manage about 200-300 yards).  Obviously all performance
figures vary considerably based on local conditions.

Sorry, that is incorrect. There are infinitely many circles close to the 
pole whose circumference is an integer fraction of a mile: 1 mile, 1/2 
mile, 1/3 mile, 1/4 mile, etc. Start one mile north of any of those 
circles and walk due south to the circle, then walk around the circle as 
many times as is necessary to make one mile. Walk one mile due north, 
and you'll be right back where you started.

There are infinitely many possible starting points, all [1 + 1/(2k*pi)] 
miles from the south pole, where k is a positive integer.

The simplest case is at k = 1, where the distance north of the pole is 
[1 + 1/(2pi)] = approx. 1.159 miles. Walking south one mile puts you 
0.159 miles away from the pole, where the circumference of the line of 
latitude is one mile -- and thus walking east (or west) one mile from 
that point takes you in a complete circle around the pole, bringing you 
back to the same place, and walking one mile north from there obviously 
returns you to the starting point.

At k = 2, the distance is [1 + 1/(4pi)] = 1.0796 miles. One mile south 
puts you at 0.0796 miles from the pole, where the circumference of the 
line of latitude is 1/2 mile, and walking east one mile makes *two* 
complete circles around the pole, returning you to the same point.

Reminds me of a stories I heard in math grad school about how the grad
students of the legendary John Vom Neumann would tease him.

One day a grad student described a "story problem" of two trains on
the same track, d distance apart, one traveling at speed s1, and one
at s2 speed, toward each other, and a bee buzzing back and forth
between the two at s3 speed (with zero reversal turnaround time). How
far will the bee have traveled by the time the trains meet, or uh,
crash.

Whatever the answer was, Von Neumann answered instantly.

"Oh, I see you figured out the trick of calculating how long it takes
for the trains to meet and then using that elapsed time with speed
s3."

"Oh, no!" Von Neumann exclaimed, "I solved it with infinite series!"

The other story is how his students would torment him: They would work
in groups all night long on a particularly difficult problem. Then at
the next coffee hour, one would say offhandedly, "Dr. Von Neumann, I
saw an interesting problem the other day, blah, blah, blah, how would
you find the answer?"

Von Neumann would think for less than a minute, and just as he opened
his mouth to tell the answer, an accomplice would blurt it out just
before him. It drove him apoplectic. Poor guy, he suffered so much for
being so smart. :-)

The way I heard the train /infinite series story was that it was at a 
cocktail party ond the wife of one of Von Neumans colleages was talking with 
him.

Another story is that a group of scientists was seeking funding to build a 
special purpose computer to solve some complicated problem.  They gave a 
presentation to Von Neuman describing their problem.  he left the meeting 
and returned an hour later with a solution to their problem!
