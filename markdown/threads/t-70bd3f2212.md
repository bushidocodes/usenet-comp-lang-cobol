of digits in the target, left-most zeros included. So if you have 9

There was an interesting article in an English publication, "The
Computer Bulletin", December 1968, p. 309: "The Theory of Modulus N
check digit systems", by W.G. Wild.

Basically, he showed that Mod 11 type 1 systems (the kind you were
discussing) have a 91% chance of catching random errors. (It caught
100% of other types and slightly less than 100 of others).

Similar performance was acheived by a Mod 11 type 2 system.

A Mod 97 type 2 system showed random error catching of 99% and 100 of
most of the others.

And the kicker? Type 2 is simple division. Divide the code by 97 and
the remainder is a 2-digit check number.

Are you stuck on how to do it or how to use it?

If it's on how to do it, my way is as follows:

DATA DIVISION.
WORKING-STORAGE SECTION.
01 A-INPUT.
05 A-NUMBER OCCURS 9 TIMES
PIC 9(01).
01 CHECK-FACTOR.
05 FILLER PIC X(009008007006005004003002).
01 FILLER REDEFINES CHECK-FACTOR.
05 CHECK-FACTOR-N OCCURS 9 TIMES
PIC 9(03).
01 WS-SUB PIC S9(02) COMP.
01 CROSSFOOT-SUM PIC 9(03) VALUE ZEROS.
01 WS-MOD-11 PIC 9(02) VALUE 11.
01 WS-QUOTENT PIC 9(03) VALUE ZEROS.
01 WS-REMAINDER PIC 9(03) VALUE ZEROS.
01 CHECK-DIGIT PIC 9(02) VALUE ZEROS.
PROCEDURE DIVISION.
000-START-OF-PROGRAM.
PERFORM VARYING WS-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
UNTIL WS-SUB > 9
COMPUTE CROSSFOOT-SUM = CROSSFOOT-SUM
+ ( A-NUMBER (WS-SUB) * CHECK-FACTOR-N (WS-SUB) )
END-PERFORM.

Accuracy and final division and subtraction routines are left as
excerises (I did this from my head while referencing the following
pseudocode).

Thanks to Tyler Welburn's book "Advanced Structured COBOL."

I made the CHECK-FACTOR-N entry three bytes long is that I remember
the implicit intermediate field would about the same size as the last
field within the parenthesis. If I'm wrong, then either reverse the
multplication or break up the COMPUTE into seperate MULTIPLY and ADD
statements, make a table with the same number of entries as the
A-NUMBER fields but with two digits in each entry, store the
intermediate products in there, then sum into CROSSFOOT-SUM.

Hope this helps,
Boyce Williams
