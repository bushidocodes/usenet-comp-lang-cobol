```
Thought I'd add my two penneth worth to the debate on the Year 2000
problem.

The issue is not whether or not there are enough programmers out there to fix
the problem, its more, who the bloody hell wants to!

Imagine the scene, the boss calls you over and says
'We are starting a high profile Year 2000 project affecting all the
company's systems and we want you to be a key member of the team -
we've just been given our budget clearence for it so expect a nice
bonus at your next pay review"

I'd tell him to stuff it where the sun don't shine because personally
I'd interpret what he said as
'We've been forced into starting a Year 2000 project so I want you to put
your career on hold for three years while you trawl through millions of
lines of prehistoric legacy code looking for date fields and adding a
century indicator. We'll get all the spotty University graduates doing
the leading edge stuff while you become an expert in systems that
won't last much after the millenium and we'll give you a few more quid
because come the 2nd of January 2000 you'll be so far behind everyone
else that we'll probably get rid of you'

I can't believe that any programmer worth his/her salt would want to
search through hundreds of programs looking for dates to change and
test for the forseeable future - I don't care if they doubled my salary
for the reason's Ive indicated above - my head would be totally done in
after about a week!

whew...Im glad I got that off my chest...

=========================================================================
Michael McSkeane
m.··.@sch··o.uk
```

```
Of course, if all your co-workers take the same attitude then it is more
than likely (hell, I'd bet good money on it!) that your employer will be
getting rid of ALL of you in the spring of 2000.... 'cause your employer is
going to be one of those 15-20% of all businesses that go under because they
were unable to process orders/issue paycheques/collect receivables/pay
bills/balance their books/etc. and will therefore be unable to compete with
their competitors whose systems were made compliant. The Y2K programming
staff of those surviving companies will be handsomely rewarded ... paid for
by the flush of additional revenues coming over from their failed
competition. And not just rewarded monetarily, but with new and exciting
challenges as their employer can now afford to finance all those wonderful
"gee, I wish we had the money to ..." projects.

Good luck, Michael.
```
