Here's another question for those who speak IMS with COBOL, a bit trickier
than the last.

Suppose you had an IMS database defined to hold Bills Of Material, a common
manufacturing task. Structurally the database is made up of two segments:
  A001   root segment, made up of one field KEY-A001   pic x(12)
                 and
  A002   child segment, made up of one field KEY-A002  pic x(12)

Occurances in the database can represent one of two types: assemblies
or components. An assembly is defined as a combination of components and
subassemblies. A component cannot be made up of anything. An assembly may
itself be a subassembly of another assembly.

Thus, all A001 occurances are assemblies. A002 occurances beneath a given
A001 occurance may be subassemblies or components. 

Question: how would you go about generating an exploded Bills Of Material
report, listing each component and recursively each subassembly, given a
"top assembly" ?  

Positioning on the top assembly and GNPing would easily get all components
and subassemblies, but how would you recursively list out the subassemblies?
Keep in mind that subassemblies can be nested to an unknown, variable depth.

I can see how as you GNP you through the A002s you could test each value to
see if it existed as an A001 thus determing whether it was indeed a subassembly -
but once you do that you lose your positioning. You could stash your positioning
keys before making the test but that'd work only for one level of nexting - if
the test was positive but it too contained subassemblies you'd lose your path
back to where you started.

This is a classical problem but I'm stumped. Any clues/hints/ideas would be
greatly appreciated.

reversing "tom" in the following address:
antip23spam610@email.tom.com


Thanks,

Motorola SSTG

The situation you have described is non-trivial, to say the least. There are
many demons lurking in this supposed simple recurse of yours.

First off, let me mention that the well intentioned, and also well phrased,
recommendations that you get a PSB with a second PCB with access to the same
data base will not solve your problem.  If your first PCB provides you access
to level-1 and level-2 of a bill of material structure, then the second PCB is
only going to give you level-2 and level-3. ((Generalized, n PCBs provide n+1
levels of the structure)).

As mentioned, you can use working storage to save off some intermediate levels,
and perhaps bounce down to level-n and level-m.  But you then need a kind of
stack push and stack pop algorthm, 

Thus with any simple program you will need limits to how deep a structure can
go (to match depth of your stack-like data structure in WS).  For class work,
that actually is achievable. You can just reject structures that are too deep. 
In the real world that will not work too well in many manufacturing
applications (or design applications).

Also, you need nice tools to attach to this innocent algorithm to detect
circles
   PART-1 ->> PART-2
   PART-2 ->> PART-3
   PART-3 ->> PART-4
   PART-4 ->> PART-2
   peg that CPU needle.

In the real world it might be better to conceive of this application as a multi
pass algorithm, spining recurse lists off to external storage rather than main
memory.

Either way you need logic that scans the list more than a pair of levels at a
time to detect problems.

It is not unusual for such part structures to be logically stratified, so that
initial scans can have some sense of how much deeper they have to go by the
strata (or depth) they have currently reached.  For example, a wing is part of
an airplane, just as a tail is: either one may have many other parts.  But a
fin is not so high-level, and a servo-motor is getting real low.

Another way to look at this is to ask what kind of B-O-Ms are really practical.
 We could list out a whole airplane, but who would read that?!

Structure scans have other issues, too - such as depth first or breadth first
explosion, left-to-right or vice versa.

Some times your key sequencing, as in the ordinal values of your A001 and A002
segments will determine this (or limit your options).

Anyway, you may need depth limit, and breadth limit checks to prevent runaway
sequences. Even small databases can fill memory fast, and if you are writing
the push and pop logic in COBOL, it could take a lot of time to execute.

Try to cut it down to size, with perhaps a two pass concept: pass one could
recurse to perhaps a depth of five, marking nodes that go any deeper. Pass two
could be similar.

Try to conceive of your presentation logic in practical terms, what kind of
views of the part explosion are comprehendible.  If you have linear pages that
are indenting 35 or 40 levels deep to show structure, and the parent and
grand-parent are 17 pages back, what does that page really mean? ((That is
especially true online)).  Try to define your output in practical terms, that
will communicate useful limits back to your explosion logic.

Pass two, and presentation logic should have loop detection (that is recursive
in itself, even across a fixed number of records).  If your data had logical
stratification, you could detect faults in the second pass and / or
presentation logic: a tail should not be part of a wing (I am being simplistic
here, no doubt some one knows of a contradiction to that, this is the internet
after all). This kind of fault would be encoded in a type field or modulated
into the key field structure.

Anyway, two PCBs is not likely to be enough, although it will be a lot better
than one. A DBA would not like it, but if your pass one is designed to handle
five levels, then four PCBs would be better.

Also note that you can use logical relationships (in full function databases)
for this stuff as well. That logical relation ship can point back to roots,
such that you would have a 
structure of 

  A001
     :
  A002 / A001 concatenated segment

that will require even more support from DBA. When pros implement B-O-Ms with
logical relations, they try real hard to prevent loops or structure faults from
ever getting recorded, which is supposed to relieve scanners (parts exploders)
from having to check for that.  Some times applications have loop/fault seekers
(that do clean up logic).

Yet it is true that your scanner can still encounter them, especially if trying
an all out explosion in a single phase scan.  Early implementations, and
student implementations, would do best if they have artificially forced limits
on depth, breadth, and total parts per single parent.

Before an application gets to an intermediate stage of development it has some
kind of type code on segments to prevent invalid structures. The idea is that
perhaps any kind of an A002 'could' get inserted under an A001, but that does
not mean that you will chase it.
 
Once you get accustomed to that idea, you realize easily that the notion that
we should only present to a certain level (or in a certain manner) for a
presentation to certain viewers, is just a special case of not chasing
inappropriate siblings.  So you can avoid a chase if it is not appropriate for
the current presentation view, or not appropriate at all.
