(Posted to comp.lang.cobol and distributed to J4 and WG4)

I thought the following note that I sent to another COBOL "list" might be of
interest in this NG.

    **

Replying to several recent posts in this group on a topic that I did *not*
bring up, I thought I would make some comments on this area. Many (if not
most) of this group know my views of the current standardization process,
but I think that there are "fair" and "unfair" (or accurate and less than
accurate) things one can say about it. I hope that others may correct any
"factual" errors in the following.

1) Both the selection of and the prioritization of "new" features and
enhancements in this (2002) revision were done at the WG4 (international)
not J4 (US) level. It has always been MY impression that WG4 has a FAR
greater representation by "real end users" than J4 does. In fact, looking at
some of the countries that have been involved (not all still involved)

    Netherlands - no vendors
    Austria - no vendors
    France - no vendors
    Canada - no vendors
    New Zealand - no vendors
    England - only vendor also in J4, others not vendors
    Germany - 1 or 2 vendors, but usually MORE non-vendors
    US - varies - but traditionally about "half and half" (at WG4 meetings)
    Japan - all (???) vendors
    X/Open (liaison) - All vendors

Now as the 90's progressed and travel budgets were cut and various "war"
issues made travel difficult, there have been SOME WG4 meetings with heavier
vendor participation, but I don't think it has ever been as much as half.

2) In J4 (where the actual "drafting" of Standard's text occurred), I think
there have always been at least 1/3 non-vendors on the voting lists. I also
think that the majority (but definitely NOT ALL) of the vendor
representatives have "regular" (at least once a week) direct contact with
end users. I do think that it is COMMON (but again not universal) that most
of the vendor representatives have NOT written a "production" program *in*
COBOL within the last decade, but most of them DO see "real world" COBOL
code as part of their jobs.

3) If you were to chart the actual papers that "added text" to the draft
Standard, I think you would find that at least 75% (possibly more) of the
text was added by papers written by vendor representatives (and if you took
out Report-Writer/VALIDATE and Standard-Arithmetic "stuff" - both primarily
written by non-J4 members, it would be even HIGHER). I think that this is
due to
 - expertise
 - exactness in style
 - paid part of their jobs
 - knowledge of the "historical" document they were adding to
 - personalities involved

4) I think that the "best" year/date for discussing the last major
"addition" to COBOL was 1989 (at least in the US). The Intrinsic Function
Amendment REALLY did add "functionality" - while the "Correction Amendment"
(almost by definition) did not. If I were to GUESS, I think that both
many/most in J4 and WG4 *today* would say that doing the "Correction
Amendment" was a mistake. (I have mixed feelings about it). But it is
interesting to think about what MIGHT have been done as an amendment
instead:
 - FIMS (Forms Interface Management System)
which is "basically" a dead-end, unless its inclusion in COBOL might have
resulted in a GUI as well as character based definition)
 - VALIDATE (very popular in Europe, but still NOT implemented by any
compiler vendor)
 - MOCS (possibly the most important, but the one that has actually been the
MOST volatile/subject to change during the current revision, so would an
amendment have gotten it right?)

5) When first "seriously" working on the current revision, two possible
"time-tables" were looked at (for different "revision scopes"). Plan A would
have been a "larger" revision with the HORRIBLE ending date of 2002 <G>.
Plan B (which was "limited" - and was selected) was intended to result in an
"official full revision" in 1996 or 1997. I think (but could be mistaken)
that both J4 and WG4 view that their "biggest" failure in this revision was
in NOT "strictly" following the initial Plan B approach and getting an
official revision DONE by 1997 (or 2000 - at least). I have my personal
opinions on how/why this slipped, but I would think that this group (in
particular) would be "amused" that if there had been an official '96
Standard it MIGHT not have included ANY OO (as this was categorized as
"important" not "critical" - with the statement that it should be moved to
"critical" IF it was ready in time.)

6) I personally think that AT LEAST three years of delay in this revision
was due to a problem that many/most outside the committee don't know about.
That was getting a "valid" electronic base document. At least 3 major
"efforts" were used in doing this:
 - converting from NROFF/TROFF to WordPerfect
 - Demodularizing the old text
 - Converting from WordPerfect to FrameMaker

Much of the "early" years of the revision work was almost "wasted" in trying
to write papers against a moving (and often just "virtual") document.
Planning and debate was possible, but true "revision work" was limited.

***

I hope that this helps answer some of the "issues" raised in this group (and
elsewhere). I do NOT want to imply that I am happy with either how things
have worked out or the structure in place for the future, but I did think
that some "historical" perspective was needed.
