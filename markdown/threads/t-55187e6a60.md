```
The city of Christchurch (NZ) was hit by an earthquake which measured 7.5 on 
the Richter scale, at 4:35 am this morning (Saturday 4th, local time)

I had some private mail from people who post to CLC, asking if I was OK. 
Most of the country (apart from Christchurch) is fine and I understand 
things are getting back to normal in Christchurch with a massive clear up 
being undertaken.

Christchurch is a major South Island city and the South Island of NZ is 
generally sparsely populated, apart from Christchurch and Dunedin, the two 
major cities, and a sprinkling of larger towns. Most of the time earthquakes 
occur in unoccupied areas, but occasionally we get a town being hit. It is 
very unusual for a major city to be hit by such a devastating shock and if I 
had to guess which city would be, I would've guessed Wellington as more 
likely because there is a fault line that runs right through it.

Both major islands are subject to earthquakes (there are around 15,000 
recorded every year) of which around 100 can be felt. However, the North 
Island is much more densely populated, so earthquakes there are far more 
likely to hit population centres.When I was growing up in Wellington they 
were a fairly usual occurrence and I remember one that knocked chess pieces 
off the board as we were playng at school. Mostly, there is just a tremor, 
like a heavy truck going past and no-one really pays much attention to them. 
It has been a couple of years since I felt anything here in Tauranga (North 
Island, Bay of Plenty), and that was a tremor which vibrated the house for 
about 20 seconds. My neighbour phoned to see if I had felt it and we agreed 
it wasn't a passing truck :-). The last serious one I expereinced in 
Tauranga was around 30 years ago when I was home for a visit. That one 
cracked my concrete driveway but did no damage to the house itself or the 
contents.It was centred 20 KMs off shore out in the Bay and did cause a mild 
tsunami.

The point is that earthquakes generally are not problematic for us due to 
sparse population density, being taught defence drills from the time we are 
little, and all modern buildings being earthquake resistant. I understand 
there have been no reports of death or serious injury despite the extensive 
property damage in Christchurch. All high rise buildings in cities like 
Auckland and the major centres have been subject to an earthquake code for 
over 75 years now (there was a serious review of the codes in 1931 after the 
devastating Napier earthquake which killed and injured hundreds of people), 
and NZ is one of the leading researchers into the effect of earthquakes on 
buildings.Although it is probably impossible to build an earthquake proof 
building, it is certainly possible to build structures that can withstand 
high degrees of shock.

Nevertheless, it is a timely reminder that we can't get away with it 
indefinitely and sooner or later there will be hits in major cities.

This site answers most questions about the nature of earthquakes: 
http://www.gns.cri.nz/what/earthact/earthquakes/earthquakefaq.html#1

My thanks to all who expressed their concern

Pete.
```

```
There was another earthquake yesterday on the East Coast of the North Island 
about 150 miles from where I live.  (Christchurch is around  450 miles). 
Fortunately this one was in a sparsely populated area and resulted in very 
little damage and no injury or loss of life.

Sadly, there were some serious aftershocks again today in Christchurch, 
which have really hampered restoration. These "aftershocks" are over 5 on 
the Richter scale and would be considered "earthquakes" in most places.The 
Government has come out and made a statement that aftershocks will be 
considered "earthquakes" for the purposes of insurance and compensation. 
This is a relief to many families who thought they may get no compensation 
on the technicality that their house was damaged by an "aftershock" and not 
an "earthquake".

Whatever your politics, you have to admire the Prime Minister, who cancelled 
a trip to U.K. and has been in the quake zone assessing damage and talking 
to people. He has made himself available on TV and in person to answer 
questions  (very directly and honestly) and provide help and support to 
anxious people.  I didn't vote for him or his party, but I've been very 
impressed by his leadership and concern through this. Latest assessments 
place the damage repair at between 2 and 4 billion dollars. (Remember, we 
have a population of 4 million...)

As a nation we are reeling from the ongoing effects of this (it has been 5 
days now of continual aftershocks that make it almost impossible to continue 
cleanup; it is all we can do to ensure housing, water, sewage and 
electricity for the homeless).

It is absolutely heartbreaking to see the carnage here and the toll in human 
terms. Although no-one has died (and we are all thankful for that) lives 
have been destroyed by this.

All of us are doing whatever we can to help Christchurch, but it is very 
difficult when the situation is so unstable. Today's shocks destroyed some 
of the work already done. People on the spot are facing nights of 
uncertainty and anxiety. It is particularly hard for the kids. Schools are 
closed in the area and some that would have re-opened tomorrow, now won't 
because of the aftershocks.

Given the other earthquake yesterday in the North Island, it looks as if the 
whole plate is in a state of motion and we really have no idea what will 
happen next. The best we can hope for is stability, and a chance to clean up 
the mess.

Hopefully, once things settle down, it should be quite some time before we 
experience more of this.

On a more positive note, a huge amount of invaluable data is being gained 
and experts from all over the world are flying in to view and study the 
cause and effects. I was amused to see a couple of American Geologists 
almost ecstatic at how "perfect" this quake was and really getting excited 
about the knowledge it would provide and the light it would shed for 
"earthquake science" in the future.

Aerial photographs reveal a 32 KM rift along an ancient fault that no one 
even knew existed. Best guess as to the last time this fault ruptured are 
between 6000 and 8000 years ago. All visible surface signs of it have been 
obliterated so farms and suburbs were built on it, in blissful ignorance 
that it might tear again. Modern science is going to provide us ways to 
detect these ancient faults, and this earthquake is providing some of the 
data which will go towards doing that.

It is indeed an ill wind which blows nobody any good...

Pete.
```
