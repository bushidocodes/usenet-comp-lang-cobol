Being designed for backpacking, Svea is lighter (550g) and lower
output (5K BTU) than the others. It too will burn anything but the
problem to be avoided with all is solids that clog the nozzle -- wax
(paraffin) and tar, hydrocarbons above C20.  They also clog
electronic fuel injectors, which is why they're filtered out of
vehicle fuels.

White gasoline is the same as regular gasoline, the only difference
being that it's natural. If you heat crude oil in a distillation
column, about 10% will form a layer of 'first-run gasoline'. Before
the 1920s, all gasoline was produced that way. It's 80% octane (C8)
and 20% heptane (C7), same as synthetic gasoline. Naphtha (Coleman
fuel, cigarette lighter fluid) is found just above gasoline in the
distillation column; it's C6 and C7. Kerosene is just below gasoline;
it's mostly C12. Below that is heating oil/diesel, which is C15.

Hydrocarbons in petroleum are in a straight line (aliphatic). Those
produced from coal, wood and grain are ring-shaped (cyclical). Shape
doesn't matter; molecules with the same number of carbons have the
same properties.

Ok, if you want to get TECHNICAL ..  enthalpies of individual bonds in
a cyclical vary with their angle and the strain they are under. In
other words, two cyclohexane molecules with different stereochemical
configuration will have different bonding energy. Cyclohexane has
about six stable configurations. The boiling point above is just an
average. 

Acyclicals don't have as much variation. Typically, the OH can move to
the middle, and that's about it. So when bonds do change, the isomer
is given another name. For example, hexane and isohexane. If there's
more than one isomer, they prefix it with a number.
