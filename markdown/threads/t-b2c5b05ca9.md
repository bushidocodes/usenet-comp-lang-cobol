Anybody (contractor or permanent employee) who allows a "standard contact" to remove his/her intellectual property rights is an idiot.

By signing this agreement you are allowing someone else to prevent you from implementing whatever innovative solution you came up with, anywhere else, but even worse, you are assigning all your rights so they can make money from your idea. I cross this clause out of any contracts I am presented with that contain it, and when they protest "But it's a standard contract, everybody signs it..." I say, "This is my standard response to people who try and curtail my rights..." So far, I have never had an Agency withdraw the job offer. (If they did, I'd go down the road and get a job somewhere else...you should too.)

So much for "intellectual property"...My intellect remains my property. I don't mind sharing it with you, but there's no way I'll assign it to you <G>.

"To secure a position with better educational advancement"  is simply bullshit, and, as such is best avoided.

As Michael already pointed out, what happened 13 years ago, especially if it was beyond your control, is really irrelevant. If you had a track record which showed you have been fired from EVERY job you ever did, there might be cause for concern (personally, I'd still employ you if you passed my very stringent interview <G>). The fact is that how you come across at interview is 90% of whether you get the job or not.

Things to remember when going for an interview:

1. Don't chew gum while being interviewed.
2. Wear shoes.
3. Wash your hair. (especially if it is long...)
4. Make sure you know how to spell COBOL, even if you can't write it.
5. Look vacant...this makes people think you are intelligent. Running through multiplication tables while the Interviewer outlines the job requirements is quite effective (much the same way as some women do while having sex...ah, you've met my girlfriend...)
6. Fake enthusiasm. Pretend you are really interested in the fact that their overnight Batch run takes 7 hours and they think there is a Sort that needs attention... (Faking enthusiasm is a lot like faking an orgasm...see "When Harry Met Sally").
7. Wear pants. (Even, or especially, if you are female and wear a skirt...)
8. When they ask you about your hobbies don't tell them about the mud wrestling and cross dressing...say you like bungee jumping and hang gliding (it takes guts to write JCL...a job for REAL men...)
9. Never appear desperate.The image to promote is nonchalance and the fact that if they are so stupid they can't see what an asset you could be, there are a dozen firms out there who can...
10. Try and fit the following words into the interview somewhere...it really doesn't matter whether they make sense or not; (probably better if they don't)..: Enterprise, Network, Web Enablement, integrated, compiler entropy, cross-politicization, functional, asynchronization, distribution, mainframe deprecation. (Any word with a "z" is very useful, even "zoo", as in "programmer zoo".)

Good Luck!

No Howard.

That was the whole point of my post. Agencies intimidate contractors into
BELIEVING that unless they toe the line (like accepting outrageous
curtailment of their rights), they won't get a job. Part of this is to
exercise control, part of it is to gain competitive advantage over the
contractor.

What I am saying is that a contractual arrangement can be entered into
FAIRLY, and still be of benefit to all parties concerned. (The Client, the
Agency, and the Contractor.)

The fact that you have hungry children (if you do) is even higher motivation
for getting a better deal.

The FACT is that once you have passed the interview (and we don't get around
to signing contracts until this has happened) the Agency are extremely
unlikely to go back to the Client and say "Well, we couldn't get that guy
who had all the skills you required, and who looked like he'd fit right into
your Organization, because he wouldn't accept a restrictive standard clause
we put in all our contracts...". If , as an Employer, an Agent came to me
and said that, I'd be reviewing my list of "Approved Suppliers".... Besides,
by this point, they are already counting the money they are going to make by
placing you and they are not about to incur more expense by repeating the
exercise.

Whether you accept these arguments or not, I can assure you that in 25 years
as a contractor I have never accepted a contract I felt to be unfair or
restrictive, and I have NEVER had an Agency say "OK, no signature, no job".
If they did, I would happily walk to an Agency that was more reasonable.

There are a lot of new, young contractors, who are so thrilled to get a job
that they will accept anything. Some Agencies prey on these guys and bind
them into restrictive contracts that are really extremely unfair.

Anyway, this was a small part of my original post and I notice you have no
comment on my suggested interview rules...<G>
