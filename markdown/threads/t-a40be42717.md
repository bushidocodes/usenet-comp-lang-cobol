I posted a question to the group, as to how would one get a hold of
the n'th ascii char in COBOL. I got some amazing and beautiful
replies, and I would like to thank all of those who posted.

I would like to address here only the solution that uses REDEFINES.
Those who suggested that I use REDEFINES, required me to waste one
byte with a filler:

One solution:

01 WS-UGLY-CONSTANTS.
05 WS-ASCII-LINE-FEED-COMP PIC S9(04) VALUE 10.
05 WS-ASCII-LINE-FEED-REDEF REDEFINES WS-ASCII-LINE-FEED-COMP.
10 FILLER PIC X.
10 WS-ASCII-LINE-FEED PIC X.

Another solution:

01 NUM-VAL PIC S9(5) COMP-1.
01 NV REDEFINES NUM-VAL.
02 FILLER PIC X.
02 NV-CHAR PIC X.

Now I'm really curious: I'm using an Alpha OSF1 running DEC COBOL. I
typed in the following:

01 a-mess.
05 ten pic 99 comp value is 10.
05 ascii-ten redefines ten.
10 filler pic x.
10 lfd pic x.

But the file kept getting these annoying ascii 0's rather than the
newline. I finally agreed, that not knowing the internal
representation of the pic 99 comp, I didn't understand what the filler
pic x was for. So I took it out, giving:

01 a-mess.
05 ten pic 99 comp value is 10.
05 ascii-ten redefines ten.
10 lfd pic x.

and everything worked. I now had a usable newline character. So the
original problem is solved.

However, since several of you have mentioned the filler, I'm curious:
What representation of the redefined field did you have in mind? What
representation would require skipping a character in the redefining
field? Perhaps this would be a good place for me to learn just how
comp integers are represented ...
