Some government agencies are projecting over 10 years to complete Y2k
remediation. Many development projects are being put on the back burner
to complete Y2k. Y2k will be hot through at least the early part of
2000, possibly much longer. I do not foresee any drop in Y2k demand
until late 2000, possibly 2001. There has been a significant move away
from C/S back to mainframe based applications since the early 90's.
COBOL use is actually on the rise, and even for C/S development, 1996
surveys showed COBOL a close second only to Visual Basic as preferred
language. COBOL is not going away anytime soon. It is simply cheaper
and easier to develop and maintain core business applications in COBOL
than any other language. Business has been relearning that lesson in
spite of all the hype, and business will not ignore the bottom line.

Many people do not understand COBOL's advantages. In my opinion, one of
the strongest points of COBOL is its simplicity. Simpler means easier to
learn, easier to use, easier to maintain and more reliable. For example,
subscripts are easier to use correctly than pointers, and their misuse is
easier to detect and correct. And pointers are really not needed for the
kinds of applications that COBOL is designed for. I have programmed in a
large number of languages, and use several on a daily basis. I would not
want to write a text editor in COBOL, but I would not want to write a
payroll or general ledger program in anything else. One should use the
best available tool for the job, and COBOL is the best tool for core
business programming. And why would it not be, considering it was created,
and has been improved over 40 years, specifically for that purpose?
