From a mainframe perspective, I agree with Volker's answers.  But you
also need to look at your non-computer courses.  You also need to
decide (if you can) what kind of programming you'd like to do and take
elective courses that will give you some background.  For example, if
you decide you want to do business programming (banks, insurance
companies, brokerages etc.), you should take at least two or three
accounting courses to get a solid background in finances.  Programming
for a bank and understanding debits/credits as they relate to deposit
and/or loan systems makes the programming a little easier to figure
out.  If you're more interested in systems programming, then you'll
want to take more then one semester of Assembler and you should take
whatever courses the college offers in this area.  You didn't mention
what university you are attending, but from my experience, it is only
engineering colleges that have the systems type courses.  There is
also scientific programming you might be interested in.  For this you
should probably take some higher math courses and chemistry.  Remember
that the electives you want to take should enhance you knowledge in
the area you plan on working in.

HTH and Good Luck.

I think Java should be a top choice - it will teach you the
object-oriented paradigm, and a language that is poised to be dominant
relatively soon. (C and C++ will force you to do too much of the job
that rightfully belongs to compiler writers, and get in the way of your
conceptual learning.)

Cobol, of course, is a bias of everyone in this group, and is a very
good, disciplined, business language, although Computerworld says that
demand for cobol-ers will decline after Y2K. Of course, supply is
retiring and dying too, so unemployment is unlikely :).

As with the other correspondents, I suggest ensuring that your studies
are well-rounded. The coming net world might suggest learning about

and code, and 50% people-skills.
