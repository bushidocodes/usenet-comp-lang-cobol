Dress code seems to have caused the usual range of views. My own
opinion (which is, of course, the only correct one) on this is as
follows.

In any work environment where there is no direct face-to-face contact
with people from outside the organisation, there is absolutely no
justification behind the demand for the wearing of suits.

When you have contact with people from outside, then accord them the
respect of wearing a suit - this is basic good manners - because it is
likely that they will be wearing suits. In this respect, the wearing of
a suit is akin to establishing neutral territory.

I think that a company that insists on a particularly formal or
old-fashioned dress code for all workers should buy the prescribed
clothes, pay for their laundering and replace them when they become
ragged. The military does; so also do countless service organisations
that have dealings with the public.

As for the dress-down Friday, I can't stand the concept. You will wear
a suit Monday to Thursday, you will wear casual clothes on Friday.
Next, they will be dictating what we wear on weekends.

My own preferred dress code is wear whatever you're comfortable in, but
please wear clothes. Have the good manners to dress appropriately for
outside contacts. That has been the case for me at my three previous
contracts but, sadly not for this present (soon to finish) one.


Charles F Hankel, Wirral, UK
Freelance Systems Professional

At work we pretty much dress in a range from suit to jeans and a tee shirt. But
most of us usually have no contact with "the public".

I still remember back at high school seeing a film that recommended good
manners and their recommendation was that at the interview one should dress a
little bit better than one would for actually working in that position. When I
last went interviewing for a job as a programmer, I wore a suit. When I was
interviewed at the community college where I presently work, I skipped the tie
and they were the first ones to offer me a position, and since then I have been
wearing nice jeans, rugby shirts or dress shirts, and, until a few years ago,
polished leather boots, but now Nike light hiking boots. Even then, I am better
dressed than about a quarter of our instructors. 8)

Some of the staff will wear shorts in the summer, but I think for me it is
better to have my legs covered, though I'll pull off the golf shirt after our
lunchtime walks, but my tee shirts are opaque and I don't go to meetings that
way.

At the place I first worked as a programmer the owner said nothing about dress,
and I was in jeans, rugby shirts, and polished harness boots. After a couple
years the owner had codified a dress code and presented it to me, and I
surprised him when I found where it said "no denim jeans" and pointed out to
him that technically I was in violation of his dress code--apparently because I
always wore jeans in good condition (no holes, minimal fading), he never
thought my clothes were inappropriate. I'll confess to a radical period where I
had horseshoe taps on the harness boots, but the owner never said a word about
that.
