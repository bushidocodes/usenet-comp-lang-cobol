This is the way subtle propaganda develops; by people omitting unpleasant 
facts, suppressio veri rather than suggestio falsi. I am sure this poster was 
himself unaware of them, himself deceived. While this is all true as far as 
it goes, it omits serious and highly relevant information. It's like saying 
that the USA annexed Hawaii with the full consent of its government and 
omitting the fact that that government was itself the stalking horse used to 
destabilise independent Hawaii - anyone reading that without knowing the 
whole story would come away thinking there was no aggression, that the USA 
and Denmark had simply got together peacefully and made a deal.

The omission is the US seizure and the run up to that (i.e., the seriousness 
of the threat of German infiltration). The purchase was just the settlement 
wrapping that up. The purchase was made under even more duress than the 
Gadsden Purchase. As far as violating neutrality went, the actuality of US 
actions exceeded the hypothetical contingency of German action. It was a 
"cannot refuse" kind of offer, at bayonet point.

By the way, "granting citizenship" is itself a weasel worded way of 
describing deprivation of rights - people get citizenship thrust upon them 
regardless, with no effective means of exercising it as against their 
conquerors or of maintaining their former ways (we saw the Argentinians try 
that one as a pretended way of looking after the Falkland Islanders). The 
correct way of handling these things, the one that respects rights and was 
fully understood at the time, is the "optant" method. I regret that Australia 
did not offer it to the Aborigines.

What remains outstanding in this matter, as before, is the full detail; I had 
thought that the seizure was in 1915 and was closely followed by the 
annexation. I now find that the latter took place in 1917; so I still have to 
check out whether my first recollection was correct, or if the seizure took 
place in 1917 too - which would have been less reprehensible than doing it in 
1915 or thereabouts, though still unjust.

In the circumstances, I will have to check some primary (and non-US) sources. 
I'll try and let you know how it goes. Oh, and I do not wish to suggest that 
the USA is the only perpetrator of this sort of thing - just the one that 
matters directly in this day and age. PML.

Ah. He made a speech right after the end of hostilities, in response to one 
of Churchill's in which, as well as celebrating a job seen through to the 
end, Churchill made a back handed swipe at Ireland for taking a free ride (he 
distinguished that from the behaviour of various individual Irishmen).

how meaningless sovereignty was if it could be overridden at pleasure or need 
of others, and how valuable it was for the whole world that greater powers 
should in fact respect the sovereignty and even neutrality of smaller ones.

way that, despite great temptation, he had not after all violated Irish 
neutrality.

I believe the text is available on the internet, though no doubt edited and
