```
At least as far back as the early 1990s I have had 
the opinion that, as far as intensive I/O processing 
is concerned, there isn't any Intel type PC that can
compete with mainframe systems.  Yes, for calculations
PCs are ahead of mainframes, but as far as running 
simultaneously 10 programs, each of which is reading
in 2 or 3 100 meg files, and writing out 2 or 3 
100 meg files, mainframe machines are still the king
of the hill. 

But you know, lately I have been looking at the new 
leading edge Intel PCs, and it seems to me that Intel 
is finally starting to fill in the missing parts.
It's no longer just a question of a 2 giga-hertz CPU,
but also these new 1066mhz memory chips, and fast 
buses as well.  And about a year ago also I read that IBM
has made some breakthrough in hard disk platters, and
2 or 3 years from now desktop PCs will have 400 gig
hard disks.  Put 3 or 4 of these new 400 gig drives
into a RAID device, and it will be able to (maybe, I 
wonder) feed a lot of data to these 4 gigs of 1066mhz
memory chips. 

For a total cost much less than $20,000, I'd guess.   

Somewhere along the way, large corporations decided 
they liked their mainframes, because that way they 
could centralize all their data in one system that 
was backed up at least once a day.  When I was working
in New York 10 years ago, everybody knew that a copy
of all the data was shipped out of the city to "Iron
Mountain", or whatever.  The CEO of the company knew
that all the company's most important data would 
always be somewhere.  All of it, backed-up every night.

Only mainframes could do all the processing, and do it
in one room, that was in a secure building.  And then
even have all that data back-up and shipped out. 

I am no expert, but I am looking at these new PCs,
and wondering about them.  Maybe even in terms of 
heavy I/O processing they are starting to match 
mainframes ?  Maybe in a few years we will have Intel
PCs that can do everything a mainframe can do ?

Of course, there would still be the enormous cost of
porting stuff off of MVS, but then corporations 
would finally be able to forever break free of these
expensive leasing contracts with IBM.

I realize this has been discussed before, and even my
non-mainframe friends tell me, a mainframe programmer,
"you have nothing to worry about for the next 20 years",
but I am starting to wonder if maybe one should revise
this to "nothing to worry about for the next 10 years".
With a lot of "porting work" between now and then. 

These new Intel "chipsets" are going to make for killer
PCs I think, even in terms of heavy I/O processing.  I
wonder if us mainframe folks are going to have to revise
our opinions of PCs during the next few years. 
 
Sincerely, I am not looking for trouble with this subject,
but merely wondering if I am wrong, and/or what others
think.
```

```
Earlier today I found out something interesting. IBM has,
for years, been working with densely packed decimals (dpd).
This technology allows more efficient decimal floating-point.
The technology is already implemented in Java and other
languages; but IBM is moving it into hardware.

Hmmm! What might that mean?

dpd makes its way into the PowerPC for wide distribution.
The demand for accurate decimal calculations means that
all business applications will be executed using dpd as
the underlying technology. The technology runs faster where
there is hardware support. The hardware support is
provided only with IBM and other PowerPC platforms.
Everyone writing business applications migrates to these
systems to obtain the highest possible speed. Microsoft
loses a substantial part of their installed base to Apple.
Linux loses out to Linux PPC. Unix(tm) vendors move from
Intel to the PowerPC. Intel, losing all their hardware
platforms, files for bankruptcy!

Will 'killer decimal' decimate Intel? <G>

dpd has some bearing on ARITHMETIC IS STANDARD
in the draft COBOL standard. A 16-byte decimal double
supports decimal floating-point for 33 digits with an
exponent of three digits. See
< http://www.alphaworks.ibm.com/tech/decNumber >
for implementation in C. See
< http://www2.hursley.ibm.com/decimal/decifaq.html >
for the Decimal Arithmetic FAQ. See
< http://www2.hursley.ibm.com/decimal/DPDecimal.html >
for information on dpd.
```

```
In number of customers, mainframes are probably dying.  But the mainframe
customers who remain are very large customers indeed.  
Very large users worry a lot about reliability.  When their systems fail, they
have to deal with very large lawsuits.  Mainframe systems tend to be
more reliable than C/S systems.  That is probably because of the lessons
learned during 30 years of disasters.  The C/S systems seem to me to
have learned nothing at all from the mainframe heritage of mistakes.  
Mainframes have good security.  Client Servers do not have good security. 
If a user wants to have both client-server / internet and good security, he
must be prepared to spend money well in excess of the cost of a high-end
mainframe.  

In terms of appearance, the mainframe is not dying--it is dead.  The rows
of tape drives featured in the movies have disappeared from most sites, and 
a modern mainframe is about the size of a deluxe microwave oven.  

In my opinion, there is not as much demand for Powerbuilder as I thought
there would be.  I think both Powerbuilder and C++ are dying, at about
the same glacial pace that the mainframe is dying.  I would not recommend
either one to a beginner.  I would recommend C or Java or COBOL.
```
