Though I agree with your reasoning, unfortunately, that *is not* the
reasoning at my two largest clients, both local government.  At one
of my clients, top management met some yahoo 'consultant' (the kind
that talk, but don't actually know how to DO things) at a cocktail
party.  This yahoo said "Client Server GUI are the *only* way to go,
and mainframes are archaic."  Now, that organization is spending many
millions of dollars building a Client Server architecture and hiring
Oracle programmers.  The old MIS people had been living on a shoe
string because management refused to fund them properly, but the MIS
clients were pretty happy.  Now, they are blowing money like it's
going out of style, have spent millions already, increased the staff
in the budget by *24* programmers (when the mainframe MIS people had
to struggle to get one extra slot!).  Now, they're hiring Oracle
programmers, and there is a moratorium on hiring *any* more COBOL
programmers.  Of course, Oracle isn't even installed yet (though it's
paid for), and the Oracle programmers are sitting around reading the
newspaper, for naturally, they know zip about COBOL.  It will take *at
least* three to five years to rewrite all their application software,
and that is a very optimistic estimate.  The MIS staff is fairly old,
and people are retiring.  And sadly, one of their staff, a friend of
mine, recently died from long term cancer with many complications (he
was a heavy smoker from his youth).  The good news is that I am sending
four copies of a signed contract down there this morning to put three
COBOL people in their shop for a year, with expectation that the
contract will be signed by them (it ain't real until it's signed!),
and be renewed for one or two additional years.  I may wind up making
more money from this client, at least in the near future, then I would
have had management had better sense, but I hate stupidity and waste.

At the other client I mentioned, top management has gotten ticked at
the MIS department.  All I will say is, I know all the top management
and the MIS people; some are very good friends of mine.  This is an
unfortunate personality issue that should have been resolved.  But
management is Yeah Verily Ticked, so they have 'replaced' (if that
is the correct word) their core business systems with purchased C/S
software.  Essentially, the vendor obtained five separate C/S systems,
and spend many months trying to make them work together.  Knowing it
would be ugly, I have intentionally avoided actually looking at the
abomination that resulted, but I understand from my friends in MIS
and top management that it is flaky and much slower than the old
systems.  They seem to shudder when it is mentioned. :-)  The word
'inefficient hardly covers it, since every one of the originally
disparate systems keeps demographic and other data separately.  But
the thing 'will work', for management is not about to admit they
did something really stupid.  I didn't tell them it was stupid, but
I did tell them it was a serious mistake and they would regret it.
I have a contract with this client too, and have a good rep with both
MIS and top management (though for what seem to me to be emotional
reasons, they didn't listen to me on this), so I hope to keep getting
at least some work from them.

Note that in both cases, cost/benefit never entered the picture.  In
both cases, people were simply convinced, for their own reasons, that
C/S and GUI were 'the way' to go.  I fear that this, as stupid as it
sounds, is the real danger to mainframe COBOL systems.  As we have
hashed to death already, I believe the PC COBOL vendors themselves
are doing things which will kill, or at least severely damage, the PC
COBOL market.  You know, last figures I saw were that over 45% of all
new software development efforts failed from cost and time overruns
and inability to meet design goals.  I can tell you that if I had ever
had even remotaly that kind of 'success' rate, I would have been out
of business a long time ago!  Why would any normal business application
have less than a 100% success rate?  Why can't people see that all this
'gee whiz' stuff is expensive in both time and money, can be horribly
inefficient, is fragile, and in many cases, offers no advantages over
previous technology?  This "C/S and GUI are the answer to everything"
mindset is truly mind-boggling, at least to me.  Not that I dislike
either C/S or GUI, as long as they are used where appropriate.

For over 3 decades I have been very optimistic about the future of
COBOL.  But within the last two years I have seen changes that disturb
me greatly.  I am no longer optimistic, but pessimistic, about COBOL's
future.  I'm not happy about it, either.
