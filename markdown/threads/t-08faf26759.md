As many of you know, with version 6 of the Fujitsu COBOL product, we
introduced a software product license protection mechanism. This new
mechanism was introduced to ensure that Fujitsu Software can continue to
offer customers a competitively priced product and ensure that we can make a
profitable business. As with all such mechanisms, it does introduce some
regrettable inconveniences to our customers. We hope that this note will
help to explain some of the technical as well as business issues surrounding
our current product licensing strategy.

From a technical point of view, several people have expressed concern that
the licensing mechanism is not compatible with certain common operations to
their computers, such as defragmenting hard disks or upgrading hardware. As
recently posted to comp.lang.cobol, most defragmenting programs do not
introduce a problem. The only exception we are aware of is Speed Disk that
is shipped with Norton Utilities. Settings exist in Speed Disk that allow
defragmentation to be performed without affecting the Fujitsu COBOL license.
Upgrading any hardware other than the hard disk on a machine also does not
affect the license.

The registration software for Fujitsu COBOL also has a mechanism for
transferring a license from one machine to another without any interaction
with Fujitsu Software. In some cases, it is necessary to contact Fujitsu
Software to resolve product license issues. We are happy to work with
individuals to quickly resolve any such problems. We believe that you will
find our customer support staff to be very responsive.

As a business model, we have always sold the Fujitsu COBOL product on a
per-installation basis and not on a per-user basis. This did not change in
the latest version of our product. The only thing that changed is that we
now have a mechanism that provides us with some assurances that the product
is being used as licensed. Per-installation licensing is common practice in
the software industry and we believe it meets the needs of most of our
customers. These are choices, however, that we must constantly reevaluate.

We are aware, as someone recently pointed out, that we, as a COBOL vendor,
not only compete against other COBOL vendors, but also against other
languages. As such, we are forced to strike a balance - we believe that we
have the most competitively priced COBOL compiler suite on the market. At
$900 for our base product offering, our product is certainly comparable to
other software products on the market and we do not charge per copy runtime
distribution fees as many of our competitors do.

The market for COBOL is sufficiently different from that of other
programming languages that it is not possible for us to compete with many
other programming languages on price alone. Building COBOL compilers is a
costly investment and we must be able to show profit on our investment. We
do believe that we can continue to make a good business selling our COBOL
development products, which is why we are committed to building products,
such as a compiler for Microsoft's .NET, that will ensure future success for
our customers.

Basim Kadhim
Fujitsu Software
cobol@adtools.com

Hi, Jimmy,

We use a simple copyright-violation detection (not a copy-protection)
mechanism in GUI ScreenIO that causes it to temporarily revert to
"evaluation" mode when we detect you've got the same serial number
installed on more than one computer.  Plus, we pop up a messagebox
notifying you a possible copyright violation has been detected.

It resets to registered mode the next time you run it, so it's just a
reminder - until you move it to yet ANOTHER machine, when you'll be
reminded again.

Typical phone call:

User:  "How come my GUI ScreenIO says it's an evaluation copy?  I
registered it 6 months ago."

Us:  "It's because you're using the same serial number on more than
one machine on your network."

...long pause...

User:  "Um, I'd like to purchase another license."

It happens with about five (5) percent of our installations, maybe a
shade more.  

And, it's not only small shops.  We've encountered this with
governments and large companies, too.  I think users do it for the
sake of expediency most of the time, but, hey, if they need more than
one copy, they really ought to license additional copies.  

No one has commented adversely on how we encourage compliance.  Our
approach is to encourage folks stay honest (most of 'em will, given
the chance) without being punitive about it.

Nothing.  None of our production software has licensing routines in
them.  That includes sp2, FormPrint, Thin Client and Web Client.  We
won't put any licensing software in our tools for a couple reasons....

1.  We don't want anything to interfere with the operation of our
editors or our dynamic link libraries.  Although licensing schemes can
be written to minimize these types of problems, we would rather not
have to worry about them interfering and would rather not have the
customer worry about them interfering with the operation of their
software.

2.  We know that we have customers who don't follow our licensing
policies, but we believe that the vast majority of our customers are
honest and are willing to pay us for the software they use.  If only a
very small percentage of the customer base is cheating or attempting
to cheat, then why worry about it anyway?

I'm sure that many out there would consider us naive, but we've been
selling software like this since 1986 and it hasn't hurt us.

We do have a 60 day timeout in our evaluation version, but there is
nothing in any of the production software.

When we have discovered customers who have taken advantage, it is
usually when the customer decides that they don't want to pay AND they
want technical support anyway.
