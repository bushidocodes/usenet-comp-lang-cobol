Eric,

If all it took to fix software problems was a simple recompile (as the book
you consulted appears to say) then a lot of programmers and support staff
would be unemployed; we simply wouldn't need them ! Of course things aren't
so rosy in the real world.

The 'illegal operation' message, a truly wonderful feature of the Windows
family of operating systems, is usually caused by:

* File corruption. For example, a corrupt '.EXE', which is why the book
  you consulted suggested a recompile.

  Of course, the 'corruption' may also be present in one of the run-time
  modules, for example, a '.DLL'  or '.OCX' file. This is why a
  complete reinstall of all relevant application files is sometimes
  recommended (in this case, the RM compiler and run-time).

* Corrupted Hardware Drivers. Where any drivers (printer, etc) recently
   added / removed / updated ? Same deal as above, just re-install.

* Hardware problems, particularly memory. Was your machine recently
  altered / upgraded ?

The above are the 'classic' sources of the 'illegal operation' message.
However, before taking any drastic steps, perhaps you should consider any
recent changes you made to your program. Consider:

* Do other compiled executables run-ok when recompiled ?

* Does it run ok without calling the sub-program ?

* Has the sub-program been executed stand-alone, (within a
  test-harness) and does it work correctly in this way ? The fact that
  a 'window' is being opened when you execute your program
  suggests the use of a 'system' call. If so:

  - Does the specified executable actually exist ?
  - Does it operate correctly stand-alone ?
  - Is the correct 'path' used to access it ?

* Have you carefully checked your 'Linkage Section' and other
  relevant parameter passing issues ?

The above are just a few ideas to help pinpoint the problem source. It is
difficult to provide more specific advice in the absence of a specific error
code / error message, and without knowing what your program is actually
doing.

I hope this helps.
