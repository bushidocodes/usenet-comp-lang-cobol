An interesting article but the authoor is firmly stuck in "here-and-now". 
Yes, if you repetitively have to move your arm to touch a screen, you may 
get "gorilla arm", but that is not the goal of the "Touch screen" 
technology.

The "touch screen" won't be a screen; it will be ANY surface. Your hand, an 
exercise book, the wall, your car windscreen... any surface close to you.

Touch screens are just the beginning.

Microsoft are betting on us becoming completely interactive with devices, to 
the point where, after a while, we won't even see the device; just the 
interaction. And that bet is not just a few million spare dollars hanging 
around, it is BILLIONS...

It will be a while yet and some of us here probably won't get to see it 
become as everyday as mobile phones and the Internet, but my personal 
opinion is that it will.

The mindset regarding computers and the use of them that most people here 
hold, is NOT the mindset of the rising generation. These people never saw a 
mainframe, they never learned to program in COBOL, they are only interested 
in functionality. If they can do it on a mobile phone, then how could they 
NOT do it on a "computer"?

Here's a different viewpoint from the one expressed in the article:

http://www.pcworld.com/article/2020268/meet-microsoft-the-worlds-best-kept-randd-secret.html

The OmniTouch rig is a prototype. When it is produced it is likely to be a 
tiny device that can be pinned to your shirt like a button or brooch. If you 
think about Web Cams when they were first developed they were fairly large 
devices that clipped to your notebook screen. Nowadays they are buried as a 
single spot in the frame of most notebook screens. It is a similar 
evolution.

Take the trouble to view some of the videos referenced in the article and 
you will see what I am talking about.

Pete.

As it is you, Charlie, I'll respond to your last question anyway.

I am using Windows 8 and find it to be the best OS that Microsoft has ever 
produced.

The "touch screen interface" is actually implemented as a "touch or click 
interface" and is no problem at all.

There has been some fierce debate (and I have been involved in some of it) 
in other forums where people have largely bleated about the loss of the 
START button, without realizing that the whole START screen replaces this 
button and you can configure it to suit yourself very easily, on the fly.

The Windows desktop has not gone away, it is just not the first thing you 
see. (You COULD configure things so it IS the first thing you see but that 
would be a stupid thing to do; it would be like buying a Ferrari then 
chopping the roof off because you are pining for your VW convertible...) The 
desktop is a press of the Windows button away. (Or you can use the "touch 
interface" to get to it.)

If you don't like tiles and apps (and they are a taste which I am slowly 
acquiring) then don't use them. You could stick with only using the Desktop 
and STILL enjoy a quantum leap in performance and reliability. (Cold boot in 
under twenty seconds, resume from Hibernation in under 5 seconds... plus 
much more seamless updates and resource management). I'm gradually coming 
round to having the news and weather and things like Kindle and an "All 
Blacks" tile at my fingertips, but I still spend most of my time in the 
Desktop.

I have no interest whatsoever in Facebook or social media so that part of it 
doesn't affect me. I DID go and retrieve the Windows Chess game which is not 
provided with Win 8 but that's about it as far as games go and I had a look 
through the games store but things like Angry Birds just don't ring my bell. 
(Millions of people enjoy it and, as I have never played it, I am not 
qualified to judge.)

Most of the criticism is from people who fiercely resist change. I find it 
staggering that people who you would reasonably expect to be innovators and 
early adopters are, in fact, the exact opposite.

There is nothing "hard" about Windows 8. Newbies to computing take to it 
like ducks to water; kids who have mobile phones just use it intuitively. I 
know a 7 year old who is showing her parents how to get around from Metro to 
Desktop and uses a wireless mouse as if it were a touch screen. (In fact, I 
learned an app shortcut from her the other day which I didn't know, (Alt/F4 
to close an app) and she had learned it from an older kid at school.

If you really can't cope with change to the Windows environment then your 
alternatives are Mac OS or Linux.

Start over with a clean slate.

Good luck!

Pete.

be able to press the "windows" key to toggle between the tile interface 
and a desktop that looks almost exactly like Win7 (but without a start 
button).  Depending on where you start from, you might need to press the 
windows key and then the "D" letter key (Win+D) to navigate from the 
tile interface to the desktop.

Classic Shell can be configured to look like either a WinXP or a Win7

http://retroui.com/     ($4.99 for personal use)

are some of the most useful Win8 keyboard shortcuts:

Windows-C Open the Charms bar (sort of a start menu replacement)
Windows-D Show the Desktop
Windows-E Open File Explorer (formerly Windows Explorer)
Windows-F Go to Files in the Search charm
Windows-H Go to the Share charm
Windows-I Go to the Settings charm
Windows-K Go to the Devices charm
Windows-L Lock the device
Windows-M Minimize all windows (only on the Desktop)
Windows-Q Go to Apps in the Search charm
Windows-R Launch the Run box
Windows-W Go to Settings in the Search charm
Windows-X Open a menu of tools for power users
Windows-Z Launch the App bar (or make it disappear if it's already showing)
Windows-Home Minimize non-active Desktop apps
Windows-up arrow Maximize a Desktop app
Windows-down arrow Restore/minimize a Desktop app
Windows-F1 Run Windows Help and Support

A few points on that topic:

- Certainly there are tremendous differences in the end-user computing 
experience today, versus that of, say, twenty or thirty years ago. 
"User-visible" computing devices (as opposed to embedded ones that 
users often aren't even aware of) are ubiquitous among the middle 
class in the developed world. For many users, portable devices from 
PDAs and laptops through smartphones and tablets mean even 
general-purpose computers are almost always available.

- However, many of the most psychologically significant aspects of the 
end user experience haven't changed discernibly over that time. OSes 
and applications are still plagued by bugs and vulnerabilities. The 
user experience is full of random reinforcement - similar behaviors 
get different results. All of the problems Platt describes in _Why 
Software Sucks_ (a book that should be mandatory reading for anyone 
who develops user-facing software) still exist: programmers write 
software for themselves rather than for users, etc. Even firms that do 
usability research often make it secondary or do it piecemeal, 
focusing on a handful of techniques.

- The so-called "digital natives" - people who grew up with ubiquitous 
computing - are often assumed by pundits to be more skilled and 
knowledgeable users, but methodologically-sound studies have refuted 
those claims in a number of areas. That shouldn't be surprising. After 
a century of widespread automobile use, many car owners and drivers 
can't change a tire or check their oil, and in places that don't have 
self-service gas stations, often don't even know how to fuel their 
vehicles.

- It's a truism of the UI/UX field that "intuitive" means "what the 
user is already used to". So in that sense, each "generation" 
(obviously a term of convenience, since there's really no such thing) 
of users will have different expectations of computers, since they'll 
have had different experiences of them. But the assumptions made by 
the likes of _Wired_ writers about those users and their expectations 
do not hold up well under scrutiny.

Things - users, expectations, interfaces - change, yes. But we should 
be leery of trying to predict those changes.

if you have to repeat an action for any length of time, and is an RSI 
disaster if you have to use it continuously for the entire work day.

Despite my disdain for the concept in general, I actually own a Texas

The eZ430 has accelerometers, and people have used it for gestural

> Flutter, a start-up in Palo Alto, California, is allowing anyone with

Yes. It's basically a simplified Kinect. The Kinect benefits from

Then there's the *original* Microsoft Surface (now "PixelSense") - not 
the tablet, but the coffee-table-computer-thing they came out with 
back in 2007. (They actually sold a few of those, mostly to hotel

"touchscreen coffee table", but it actually used cameras inside the 
table to recognize objects placed on and moved across its surface.

> And MIT in 2010 developed something akin to what was seen in Minority
> Report.  See
> http://web.mit.edu/newsoffice/2010/gesture-computing-0520.html

Yes. It's still a lousy idea for any sort of long-term or repetitive work.

There are also 2D mouse-based gestural interfaces. The Opera browser 
was one of the first pieces of commercial software to promote them - 
it introduced mouse gestures for common operations like "back" and 
"forward". Other browsers picked the idea up, but it never seemed to 
become very popular.

For a decades, people have been experimenting with eye-tracking as an 
input channel. (It's long been used in UI research to study user 
behavior, so trying to do more with it is an obvious direction.) These 
days you can find free software that tracks your glance using a webcam 
and tries to do something useful with it, like changing keyboard 
focus. (That would be disastrous for me, since like many touch-typists 
I often don't look at the window I'm typing in.)

Gestural interfaces work well for some applications - conducting an 
orchestra, directing taxiing airplanes, semaphore and sign language - 
but they have severe limitations that restrict their general 
applicability. They tend to have poor ergonomics, as I've already 
noted. They have a poor signal/noise ratio. Recognition systems need 
to distinguish between meaningful and accidental movements, which 
either means a lot of context processing by the system (and even then 
it's hard to get decent failure rates) or an excessive burden on 
users, who have to restrict and/or exaggerate their natural movements.

It's like anything else. A research team or a company comes out with 
something. They're all excited about it because they've been working 
on it and they're invested. Often there's a flurry of interest in the 
press, or among the pundits, or even in the mass market (as with the 
Wii). Then the thrill wears off and folks realize that no one mode of 
interaction is best suited for every use.

There is a *huge* amount of research being done in the various natural 
language processing (NLP) fields, including speech recognition (SR) - 
primary and applied, academic and commercial. There are dozens of 
major conferences and journals, and it's one of the larger and growing 
areas not just for CS students and new grads but for many people 
working in cognate fields; there are a lot of people in the humanities 
doing NLP research, thanks to interest in (and funding for) "big data" 
projects.

People have been trying to "perfect" SR for decades, and they've 
basically gotten to the point where it's usable for some purposes, by 
some people, some of the time. It's not at all clear that pouring more 
resources into it would make it get better any faster.

And SR is a terrible input mechanism for many problems. Try reciting 
source code or a spreadsheet, for example. There's a reason we use 
symbolic notation and high-bandwidth, high-signal input devices 
(keyboards, since no one's come up with anything else even close) for 
a lot of applications.

That said, it is an interesting field, and it's very easy to 
participate if you're so inclined. You can pick up a second-hand copy 
of Jurafsky & Martin's /Speech and Language Processing/ relatively 
cheaply, you almost certainly have adequate hardware for many 
projects, and you can use free software infrastructure. (I use a 
combination of plain ol' Java and IBM/Apache UIMA - both free - for my 
NLP research. For SR, you could add the free CMU Sphinx software.)
