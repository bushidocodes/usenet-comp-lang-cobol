Ummmm... Either add 0.005 and *truncate*, or round without adding. But don't 
both add *and* round, e.g. suppose 16.087 instead of 16.082:

The exact value of 1896.59 - 16.087 is 1880.503
1896.59 - 16.087 + 0.005, truncated to two decimals, is 1880.50 (correct)
1896.59 - 16.087 + 0.005, rounded to two decimals, is 1880.51 (incorrect)
1896.59 - 16.087, rounded to two decimals, is 1880.50 (correct)

But why make it more complicated than it needs to be? Simplest of all is

COMPUTE ADJ-MEMO-CREDITS ROUNDED =              
        ADJ-MEMO-CREDITS - RSRV-ACC-INT

Using your example data above, 1896.59 - 16.082 = 1880.508, which will be 
rounded to 1880.51 just as you want.

Or, 1896.59 - 16.085 = 1880.505, rounded to 1880.51
1896.59 - 16.087 = 1880.503, rounded to 1880.50
and so on.

I'm going to start over a bit and describe the issue in more detail.  I will
include the reason why I am doing what appears to be 'pre-mature' rounding. 
You can decide whether or not this is justified...

Here are the relevant fields:

reserve limit (RSRV-LIMIT) PIC S9(7) COMP-3 (whole dollars only)
reserve balance owing (RSRV-BALANCE) S9(7)V99 COMP-3
reserve interest accrued (RSRV-ACC-INT) S9(6)999 COMP-3 (note three decimal
positions)

There is also reserve available, but it is a calculated field.  In general,
here is how it is calculated:

compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance

So if you have a reserve limit of $4500 and you've used $1000 you have $3500
available to use.

This is pretty obvious.  Here's when it gets a bit weird...
Let's say that you own $1000 (principal) plus 5.097 accrued interest.  If
you wanted to pay down the entire amount, including the interest, your
payment would be 1005.10, because we rounded the interest up.  (Don't worry,
if the interest was 5.094 you'd pay only the 5.09 in interest!  <g>)

Anyway, lets say you call in and say you want to pay $500 of the reserve
from your savings account.  Once you did this we'd immediately bump up your
available reserve by that $500.  So now our calculating looks like this:

compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance + pending-payments

Thus: 4500 - 1000 + 500 = 4000 available

Again, all very simple and makes sense.
But what if, you want to may off the entire balance plus the interest.  So,
in addition to the $500 you did a minute ago you do another payment of
$505.10).  So now there's a total payment of $1005.10.  We may 1000 to the
reserve and 5.10 to the accrued interest.

One thing to explain.  All of these are simply 'memo posts', as it were.  We
don't post real time, so technically the balance is still $1000 and the
accrued interest is still $5.097.  But we do adjust your available to use
based on the payments that are pending to post.

Anyway, if we were to use the above calculation we'd end up having
rsrv-avail as $4505.10.  But this is not right, because the extra $5.10 is
being paid to the bank and should not affect your available to use amount. 
So here's part of how the calculation works now...

if pending-payments > rsrv-balance
    move rsrv-balance to pending-payments
end-if
compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance + pending-payments

All we're doing here is say that if your payments exceed the principal owed,
when making the calculation we'll only count the payments that don't exceed
the principal owed.

Slightly confusing, but still fairly straight forward.  Let's give some
examples:

You owe $1000 + $5.10

If you pay $1000, then we add the $1000 back when calculating your
available, giving you the entire $4500.
If you pay $1005.10 then we add back only the $1000 from the principal
(rsrv-balance), again giving you $4500 available.
If you pay $1003.00 then we again add back only the $1000 from the
principal, again giving you $4500.
But what if, for whatever weird reason, you make a payment of $1010?

First I'll describe what would happen during posting, forgetting for the
moment 'available reserve'.

- We would credit $1000 to the balance owing, leaving you with a total
balance owing of $0.
- We would credit $5.10 toward the accrued interest, leaving no interest
owing
- We would credit the remainder ($4.90) back to your checking account,
increasing it's balance by this amount

In the 'real time' world what we end up doing (or trying to do!) is giving
you an available reserve of $4504.90, which is your credit limit plus the
amount over-paid.

Thus, we end up with the probably not quite right code:


if pending-payments > rsrv-balance
    if pending-payments > rsrv-balance + rsrv-acc-int
        compute pending-payments rounded =
                pending-payments - (rsrv-acc-int +.005)
    else
        move rsrv-balance to pending-payments
    end-if
end-if
compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance + pending-payments

Our goal here is to have 'pending payments' in this case be 1004.90, so that
rsrv-avail ends up being 4504.90.
Specifically, $4500 - $1000 + $1004.90.  
(I guess we should call it 'pending-principal-payments-and-overpayments',
but gosh is that verbose!).

Anyway, let's see if the code actually calculates this correctly:

pending-payments starts out as $1010.
$1010 is greater than rsrv-balance of $1000
$1010 is greater than rsrv-balance + rsrv-acc-int ($1005.097)
So we use the 'compute pending-payments' statement above...
1010.00 - (5.097 + 0.005)
1010.00 - (5.102)
1004.898
rounded to 1004.90

And now we use this in our final compute
4500.00 - 1000.00 + 1004.90 = 4504.90
This is the result I wanted.

But!  Let's say that the accrued interest is due to be rounded down; for
example 5.094.  Now our first compute looks like this:
1010.00 - (5.094 + 0.005)
1010.00 - (5.099)
1004.901
rounded to 1004.90

This is not the desired result, however.  The desired result is,
essentially
pending-payments - accrued-interest-to-pay-if-all-paid-today

accrued-interest-to-pay-if-all-paid-today is simple actual accrued interest
rounded from three decimal positions to two decimal positions.
Thus, in this case 5.09, and not 5.10.

All this being said, this is why I chose to round first, before subtracting,
eg:

if pending-payments > rsrv-balance
    if pending-payments > rsrv-balance + rsrv-acc-int
        compute rsrv-acc-int-rounded rounded = rsrv-acc-int
        compute pending-payments =
                pending-payments - rsrv-acc-int-rounded
    else
        move rsrv-balance to pending-payments
    end-if
end-if
compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance + pending-payments

Thus, we have:
1010 - 5.09
1004.91
Which, gives us a rsrv-avail of
4500.00 - 1000 + 1004.91
= 4504.91

In other words, the over-payment now is a penny more than before because
with the original accrued interest (5.097) we 'paid' 5.10, whereas with the
new accrued interest (5.094) we 'paid' only 5.09.

All this being said, I also did the following and got the same results as
with the above:

if pending-payments > rsrv-balance
    if pending-payments > rsrv-balance + rsrv-acc-int
        subtract rsrv-acc-int from pending-payments rounded
    else
        move rsrv-balance to pending-payments
    end-if
end-if
compute rsrv-avail = rsrv-limit - rsrv-balance + pending-payments

Let's see how this works...
1010.00 - 5.097
1004.903
round to 1004.90

1010.00 - 5.094
1004.906
round to 1004.91

These are both also the expected results.
But it still makes me nervous because I can't *see* it in my mind always
working.  Nor can I think of an example to disprove it.
I'm thinking that it *should* be right, and it eliminates what appears to be
the 'pre-mature rounding'.  I guess I just need some way of generating
different inputs and seeing if I always get the same results with both
methods.  But certainly I've already proved that the original method is
incorrect.

Thoughts?

Frank

I'm not sure if you are agreeing or disagreeing with me, but you have
accurately stated my belief.  In this instance, as you say, accrued interest
is not an intermediate result.  If a payment is made today that pays the
current *rounded* accrued interest then the accrued interest is set to zero
and we start over.  This is done as follows...  (Warning, it's kind of weird
at first glance, but it works.)

The total amount of the payment is subtracted from the reserve balance
owing.  This can, temporarily (a few milliseconds!) make the reserve balance
owing a negative amount.

Reserve Balance Owing = 62.86
Reserve Interest Accrued = 0.153

Payment made for 63.01
Reserve balance owing = -0.15
Reserve Interest Accrued = 0.153

"Loan" credit generated back to account for $0.15
Reserve balance owing = 0.00
Reserve Interest Accrued = 0.153

Finance charge debit to account generated for $0.15

Here's where it gets a bit weird:

    IF D-RSRV-ACC-INT                               
                   GREATER THAN D-SPLT-TRAN-AMOUNT  
        MOVE D-SPLT-TRAN-AMOUNT TO WORKFLD          
        PERFORM 2550-CREATE55-CONTINUE THRU         
                2550-CREATE55-EXIT                  
    ELSE                                            
        COMPUTE WORKFLD ROUNDED EQUAL D-RSRV-ACC-INT
        PERFORM 2550-CREATE55-CONTINUE THRU         
                2550-CREATE55-EXIT.                 

2550-CREATE55-CONTINUE.
    SUBTRACT WORKFLD FROM D-RSRV-ACC-INT.    
    IF D-RSRV-ACC-INT LESS THAN .005         
        MOVE ZEROES TO D-RSRV-ACC-INT.

In this case the accrued interest is more than the transaction amount.  So
we subtract the transaction amount (.15) from the accrued interest (.153)
and get a result of .003.  Since this result is less than .005 we simply
zero out the accrued interest.

[On the other hand, lets say the accrued interest was 1.896.  The payment
for the interest would me 1.90.  This is more than the accrued interest, so
we simply "bump up" the accrued interest to this amount.  We then subtract
this amount (1.90) from what is now the accrued interest (1.90), and now the
accrued interest is zero.  (I'm guessing there might be a more sensible way
to do this and get the same result, but it's been that way since 1989 or so,
so...)]

Now I can see a case could be made where the accrued interest at this point
should be 0.003, rather than zero.  But since there is no way for the
customer to actually pay that 3/10's of a cent, we simply write it off.  Of
course if the accrued interest were an amount requiring rounding up then we
also 'write it off' rather than crediting the customer a partial cent.  If
we were to do this, rather than zeroing accrued interest out when it is less
than .005 and more than -.005, then I believe that the rounding that Doug
and others have been pushing would probably be correct.  But that is simply
not how our business is functioning.

Now there is certainly an option for us to accrue only to two decimal
positions rather than three.  I'm sure there was some business decision made
many years ago to make it the three positions, and round to two positions
only when a full pay-off is made.  Right or wrong?  I have no idea.

For what it's worth, here is the formula of how interest is accrued:

     05  WS-ACCRUAL-DAYS     PIC S9(3)     COMP-3.
     05  ANNUAL-INT-RATE     PIC V9(5)     COMP-3.
     05  RES-INT-FACTOR      PIC V9(7)     COMP-3.


     COMPUTE RES-INT-FACTOR ROUNDED =                        
                            ANNUAL-INT-RATE / WS-ACCRUAL-DAYS
     COMPUTE RES-TODAY-ACC ROUNDED =                         
                            D-RSRV-CURR-BAL * RES-INT-FACTOR 
     ADD RES-TODAY-ACC TO D-RSRV-ACC-INT                     

Essentially, we accrue interest (to three decimal positions) daily on the
balance owing, and we then add today's accrued to the accrued interest up
until today.  I guess one thing that I can't say for sure is "right or
wrong" is should that final ADD have a ROUNDED in it?  Without it we are
simply truncating, which can only be to the customer's benefit, so that
seems OK.

every bank does it this way).
