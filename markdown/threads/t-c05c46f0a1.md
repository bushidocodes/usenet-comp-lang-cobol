Yes.

Fujitsu Corporation has more than 40 times the capitalization of MicroFocus. 
They also own ICL, a major UK computer provider, and Siemens, a major German 
computer provider..

Fujitsu Corporation is SO diverse and they report over a number of 
Geographical areas, but in IT alone (leaving aside devices like heat pumps 
etc. ) estimates place their capitalization in 2007 at around $US 45 
billion.

MicroFocus: 538 million UK pounds = (apprx) $US 1.028 billion.

>
> I am only talking about 'nix and Windows systems.

Neither company is forthcoming about their customer base in these areas.  As 
far as COBOL goes, judging by searches on the web and general discussions in 
forums, it looks like MF have the larger share of this market.

Fujitsu made a concerted effort to penetrate the workstation marketplace 
some years back (late 1990s), riding on the back of MicroFocus' failed VISOC 
product. (Fujitsu offered version 3 of their NetCOBOL and PowerCOBOL 
products free, along with tools to help migrate from MF). Although this was 
an agressive move by Fujitsu, in many ways they failed to press it home. 
They cut back on their support and laid off some very capable people, and 
the whole business of getting support (particularly outside the USA) became 
impossible.  As people dropped maintenance (they were paying for something 
that wasn't forthcoming), they lost the right to upgrade at an advantageous 
price, and so this gave them further incentive to move off Fujitsu. Fujitsu 
licensing required remote server registration, and transferring a licence 
from one machine to another was via a very unwieldy process and led to 
people simply giving up on it. Today, it is possible to use a virtual 
machine to circumvent it, which makes me wonder what all the fuss was about 
at the time. It isn't about trying to defraud Fujitsu; it is about having 
COBOL available 24/7. Apart from all this, COBOL is declining anyway, and it 
is simply more economic to define a migration strategy and get off COBOL for 
good. (For mainframe shops this tends to mean Java, SOA, and Web based 
applications. For others it means .NET, C#, SOA, component based development 
and Web based applications...)

MicroFocus has also had it's share of problems, but they have weathered them 
better than Fujitsu have.

Both Companies have excellent products (compilers and utilities... stock 
IDEs are still primitive compared to VS 2008 or the latest versions of 
Eclipse), but MF have targeted committed COBOL shops (mainly mainframe) 
where they can offer added value for COBOL development.

(Fujitsu have similar "mainframe compatibility" and services, but without 
maintenance and support it is much harder to get onto such sites. I know 
from my own experience there were a couple of occasions when I would have 
loved to recommend Fujitsu COBOL to a mainframe site and even set up and run 
pilot projects with it, but the risk was simply too high because I had 
already been burned with abysmal support, while doing a project on a 
non-mainframe site. I could not, in all conscience, recommend it.)

Fujitsu are a giant who could drop COBOL and never miss it; MicroFocus have 
their future much more closely tied to it. (Given the new wave at MF, and 
the astuteness of the new helmsman, I suspect this problem is already being 
addressed... :-))

Which horse should you back?

In my opinion, neither. Both good horses, but do you really want to gamble 
your future on COBOL, when there's no need to?

Pete.
