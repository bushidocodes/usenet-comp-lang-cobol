What I am seeing is that there are many jobs where experience on both the
mainframe and in a client-server environment makes for a very valuable resume.
Many companies want to do conversions of mainframe systems to client-server but
I believe you need to know both to manage such a project well.

There are tons of people with C and Unix experience who have no MVS or 3390
experience. Having a foot in both worlds is where I think the money will be.

I am a Micro Focus programmer who uses Cobol in both mainframe and
client-server development, so being a Cobol developer doesn't prevent you from
doing non-mainframe development, if you are working for an employer who is
friendly to the client-server cobol concept. Otherwise, you can learn and program
in both C *and* Cobol. Why not? I program in Rexx, Cobol, Visual Basic, and several
proprietary script languages. It's not hard to be a multiple language developer.
