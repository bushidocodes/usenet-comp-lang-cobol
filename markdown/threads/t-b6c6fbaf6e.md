```
When you add instant coffee to water boiled in a microwave, it foams.
When you do the same to water heated on top of the stove, there is no foam.
Why?

1.  In order for oxygen in water to change from liquid to gaseous state it must
contact a crystal. Pots are made of metal or ceramic (glass, porcelain), which
are both crystalline. Oxygen bubbles are formed only where water touches the
surface. Water heated by convection is circulating because the bottom is hotter
than the top. In short course, nearly all oxygen molecules will touch the
surface and some will become gaseous. Water heated in a microwave is not
circulating because all parts of the water are heated uniformly. Oxygen in the
center never touches the cup. When you add crystals such as instant coffee, the
oxygen touches a crystal for the first time and some of it becomes gaseous. 

2.  Heat merely indicates molecular movement. Various kinds of movement can be
imparted to water molecules. Convection heat makes he lattice vibrate, like
plucking the springs of an old-fashioned bed frame. Microwaves make the water
molecules rotate like tops at the frequency of microwaves -- 4 million rpm. When
you add coffee crystals to vibrating water, they simply dissolve. The air
trapped in them also dissolves in the water, which is no longer boiling at this
point and has a shortage of air.  When you add coffee crystals to water
molecules spinning like little power tools, the structure of the crystals is
ripped apart in less than a second. Gaseous air trapped in the coffee crystals
is released and floats to the surface causing foam.


3.  Coffee is acidic. There are many acids in coffee, of which the highest
volume is chlorogenic acid (3-caffeylquinic acid) with formula C16H18O9.
http://www.coffeeresearch.org/science/sourmain.htm
As we know, acids are electron recptors. It is not so common knowledge that the
water in a cup is a single molecule. The bond in water, called hydrogen bond, is
unique. It is looser than covalent and ionic bonds, where particles hold hands
tightly. It is more like a soup of oxygen atoms floating in a broth of hydrogen.
Bonds between the two are weak and constantly changing. Microwaves bombard the
water with electrons. Most pass thru but some are stopped by collision with
oxygen or the cup and stay behind. The water now has a surplus of electrons,
which temporarily reside in the outer (only) shell of hydrogen atoms.  When we
add chlorogenic acid, each molecule bonds to two of these unstable hydrogens,
thereby releaving them of the extra electron and making them normal. The
resulting C16H20O9 is unstable. It quickly releases one CO2, thereby converting
chlorogenic acid into carboxylic acid C15H20O7, a harmless substance found in
all foods containing DNA. Carboxylic acid tastes less acidic than chlorogenic
acid because it forms fewer acetate anions in solution. This explains why
microwave coffee tastes flatter. 
Foam is caused by the carbon dioxide released. 

4.  Boyle's Law says that when a gas is heated it becomes bigger. Water has air
bubbles in it. As we heat the water those bubbles become bigger. Some become big
enough to overcome the water's viscosity and float to the surface. Convection
heats both air and water, which explains why you see so many bubbles when water
is boiling. Microwaves do not heat air, only water. Thus the air bubbles are
cooler than the water, at least for awhile until they have a chance to absorb
heat from the surrounding water. Coffee crystals have sites that transfer heat
from the water to the air bubbles in less than a second. The rush of
suddenly-big-enough air bubbles causes the foam.

------------------
The above is original; I didn't copy any of it.

It is a good test because the seemingly obvious answer is incorrect. Looking up
an explanation will increase your chance of choosing the wrong answer. When it's
over I'll give a single very simple experiment that corroborates the wrongness
of three answers and the correctness of one.
```

```
Water heated for coffee is not superheated; it is at or just below boiling, so
the comment about superheating is irrelevant. Dissolved air will be gasified
("driven out") if it contacts steam bubbles or a rough surface. Lack of
circulation ("convection") leaves some air and water which had not contacted
either. You are correct: they cause the foam.

Two conditions are needed for phase transition from liquid to gas, technically
called nucleation: heat and a 'rough' surface with  bumps or pits at least a few
microns deep (technically, the criterion is shape rather than depth). Once the
first bubble is formed, rising bubbles are themselves nucleating sites which
enable the formation of more bubbles. 

When you heat water in an old metal pan with scratches, have you noticed bubbles
forming first along the scratches? That's because scratches are rougher than the
rest of the surface. Roughness causes irregular heat distribution.

>When the coffee (or sugar or salt or inactive sand or just about
>anything) is added the particles provide points where steam bubbles
>form and this also drives out the dissolved gasses to create the foam.

The addition of anything _rough_. A smooth glass rod will not do it. A polished
metal spoon does it poorly. I said "crystal" to make it sound New Age. I wasn't
'wrong' because coffee, sugar and salt crystals are rough, as are the inside of
all coffee cups and most glasses. (FWIW, drinking hot coffee from a glass is
called 'Russian style'). 

You can stop coffee and cocoa from foaming by first stirring the water with a
wooden spoon, a pencil, anything with a rough surface. 

>This has caused some severe burns when the water has 'exploded' -
>never heat pure water in a microwave.

The first statement is true. Putting anything into superheated water starts a
nucleation chain reaction. The first bubble becomes a nucleating site for more
bubbles. The whole cup of water will vaporize in less than a second. 

The second statement is overly cautious. In order for this to happen, one
condition is necessary and the other is a practical necessity. The interior of
the glass must be free of rough spots.  If a single bubble forms, it will
trigger others. The practical necessity is lack of dissolved air and other
gasses such as chlorine. If any gas bubbles form, they will trigger steam
bubbles. I said practical necessity because gasses such as air nucleate more
easily than water, thus a surface feature not rough enough gasify water could
gasify air.

To create superheated water, put distilled water in a high quality crystal
glass, boil it once to expel dissolved gasses, stir gently with a wooden spoon
to insure they have gasified, put a very thin coat of oil on top to prevent
evaporation, then set the microwave to 1.5x normal boiling time. If you don't
see bubbles by the end of that time, you have a glass of superheated water. When
you set it off by adding a grain of anything, superheated steam will hit the
ceiling. Needless to say, you don't want your hand or face in the path.

Incidents of accidental explosions are reported a few dozen times per year in
the US. It's rare because nearly all containers have _some_ rough spots. All
inexpensive drinking glasses that I've tried did. You need laboratory-grade
polishing, which _might_ be found in a crystal glass. As a practical matter, you
also need to remove dissolved gasses by boiling the water twice. It's difficult
to create superheated water when you're trying; the chance of it happening by
accident is very low. If you're still jumpy, look for bubbles. Bubbles indicate
it's not superheated. 

The incorrect answers played on common misconceptions. There are no air bubbles
in water; if there were, they'd float to the surface. Microwaves don't make
water molecules rotate like tops. They start to rotate at 2.45G rps, then
quickly bang  into each other. There are no 'extra electrons' left in food. If
foaming were caused by coffee acid or air in coffee crystals, salt and sugar
would not foam.
```
