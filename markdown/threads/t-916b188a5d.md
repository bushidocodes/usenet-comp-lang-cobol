Saw an interesting recruitment ad in this morning's paper. Safeco, a major
Seattle insurance company, is looking for (their words) "all kinds of"
college graduates for a company-paid 4- or 5-month COBOL training program,
culminating (presumably) in a career with their company. I'm a
foreign-language major who has been working on the periphery of IS for
several years, and the idea of a major career change -- with PAID TRAINING!
-- is very appealing. (The idea of working for an insurance company
isn't... but then again, if I could retire by 40, I guess I could put up
with it for a few years!)

But I do have a few concerns. I've read a few posts on this NG to see if
COBOL is in danger of extinction, but haven't been able to form a real
hypothesis. If it is, would a grounding in COBOL be useful in migrating to
other languages? Has anyone else on this NG been through a similar
training program?

Any thoughts, comments, experiences would be welcome.

-Peter Hartikka ... Bainbridge Island, WA

I think that COBOL will be around for a long time. In fact
I think it is going to undergo a major revival. Cobol always
has been the major language for business. The problem
for the last few years has been that it has not been the
major language for all the "flash" software that gains the
public eye. That does not mean that it has not been used.

Over the last five years, well over 60% of all programming
jobs required a knowledge of Cobol.
I have all the work I can handle, and have had for years. I am 51,
Comp Sc. Grad (72), Psychology grad (69). I've programmed in
about 70 languages over the last thirty years (that is including dialects),
including Cobol, Pascal, 10-15 assemblers, Basic, PL1, Lisp,
Fortran, Modula-2, Apl, and a whole bunch of silly one-timers.
Cobol is the only language that I have used regularly for my
entire career. I expect that it will be the only one that I use
until the end of my career.

There are lots of things that Cobol is entirely unsuited for, but
dozens of little languages (like Delphi) exist to do that stuff. You can
learn one of those in a day, since they are based on mainstream
standard languages.
