Rune,
In my opinion, you should be careful to differentiate between a true COPY member
(processed by the COBOL compiler), and 'includes' (processed by a library
manager tool). They offer different capabilities; I prefer the COBOL COPY,
since I can then use REPLACING clauses - this could offer you the solution to
your problem, if there are 88's already present and you just need to have more
values tested.

Otherwise:
Why not redefine the "01 W6-FORS-REG." item? The major drawback is that you will
have to define enough FILLER to get to the record positions you want to test,
and if the definition changes, you'll be forced to change your redef. I marked
up your "want to" example to show what I mean; I hope it is clear.
Colin Campbell

I think you will be making a mistake to code your program working storage layout to be dependent upon the
copybook layout. As Colin points out, if the copybook definition changes, so must your working storage
definition. If your working storage definition is not updated in sync with a copybook change, how much
damage, if any, could be done to the data in your system?

If I am changing a copybook layout, the last thing I want to find is some program(s) working storage layout
that is dependent upon the copybook layout that I must change as well. And if you do this in one program, I
suspect you will make this change, or a change like it, in another program.

I am curious as to why you are not allowed to change the copybook? At any rate, I think you are much better
off implementing your change the first way. While you may not think it's elegant, from a maintenance point
of view, you will be better off in the future.

Redefining copybooks is a common, but poor practice. All
redefinitions should be within the copybook.

The purpose of the copybook is to provide a standard definition of a
structure. Redefinition external to the copybook is only slightly
more desirable than creating a separate working-storage or linkage
structure. Everything works until the structure changes.

When the structure changes, the changes are not completely covered by
merely recompiling the programs referencing the copy member.
Additional maintenance is required to proliferate the required
changes, thereby defeating the purpose for having the copybook.

There are a number of reasons that RENAMES is seldom used. Like many
things in COBOL, it works very well, but belongs in the same list with
the ALTER verb...The one that begins with "Thou shalt not....

In a less than perfect world, use your DMAP to help you get your
displacements correct, and redefine the beginning group level of the
copy member. The first element in this redefined group should be a
filler for determined by the relative displacement derived by the
locations in the DMAP. It is not a bad idea to use a trailing filler
for the length of the remainder of the copybook. If you are lucky,
you MAY, at least, get a warning diagnostic if the copybook length
changes in the future.
