People have been predicting the demise of COBOL since at least 1970.
Back then the theory was that COBOL would be replaced by PL/1.
It hasn't happened yet and the current state of PL/1 may be
instructive.

A computer language is a tool. Any tool is built for a particular
job, and it survives as long as:
(a) it continues to do that job better than any other tool, and
(b) that job still needs to be done.
The finest screwdriver in the world will never replace a hammer.

COBOL is built primarily for batch-oriented data processing, and it
does that very well. In that area it can meet or beat other tools
and that area is not going to disappear as far as I can see. Therefore
I conclude that COBOL will survive.

Yes, I know that COBOL can be used for other kinds of applications.
I once did a heavily mathematical network simulation with COBOL.
But in these other areas, I think the best we can claim for our
favorite language is that it's a viable alternative, not that it's
the clearly superior tool.

You can abandon COBOL when you have no more batch processing and no
more large data files. You tell me when that's going to happen.

-- David Martin
