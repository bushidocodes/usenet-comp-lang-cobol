It seems to me that there is an "interesting" question that has been asked
about "who cares" about portability (of COBOL source code) in another
thread.  I have some opinions on this - but others may certainly disagree.

1) For operating environments where *multiple* COBOL compilers are
available, it is OFTEN considered desirable (by programmers) and UNdesirable
(by *some* vendors) to provide portability among COBOL compilers.  This is
true in the Workstation (PC, Unix, Linux) environment where I think it is
"medium-common" to want to port source code from one compiler to another -
and from environment to environment with the same compiler. However, this
represents a RELATIVELY small amount of the existing COBOL source code.  It
may well, however, represent a growing amount IF AND ONLY IF the
"perception" of COBOL changes in these environments.

2) For "proprietary" operating systems where the OS vendor is ALSO the COBOL
vendor (one and only), then portability of source code is usually (not
always) a NON-issue.  IBM certainly discovered this when they TRIED to
introduce "SAA COBOL" for portability between MVS, VM, OS/400, and OS/2.  I
believe that the percentage of COBOL source code that is EVER ported from
one proprietary environment to another is MINISCULE (e.g. Unisys to Tandem,
or IBM to HP, etc).  This *does* happen occasionally, but it is such an
unusual event that it should NOT be viewed as a "major" motivating factor
for the future of COBOL.

3) "Right-sizing" of COBOL source programs (from a proprietary COBOL
mainframe environment to a client/server or workstation environment) has
been tried a LOT.  All evidence that I have heard seems to indicate that the
only way to do this successfully is by approaching it as a "major
development" project and NOT as a simple "source code migration".  This is
due BOTH to the change in application design *and* differences in COBOL
implementations (even when both are "ANSI conforming").  It is CERTAINLY
true that the "closer" the original code is to being limited to "ANSI
conforming" and the more that the original design separated user-interfaces
from business logic from external data access, then the EASIER the migration
was.  However, again, I view this as a "relatively" unusual situation with
existing COBOL applications - but a "desirable" way to design future ones.
This, however, raises the separate question of how much NEW COBOL
application development is being done (rather than enhancements and
maintenance of existing COBOL systems).

4) I agree with those who think (in large enterprises especially) the *TERM*
"portability" is viewed as not only a "good think" but also as having
business advantages. However, I don't think that this is any better
justified than many other "middle management" desires to look for "silver
bullets" to fix application development backlogs.

Bill,

I haven't read the prior postings on these threads, but thought I would
throw in my bit anyway.  The telephone billing company I work for runs
the COBOL code in a service bureau environment on an IBM mainframe.  The 
code is also ported to the AS/400, RS/6000, and PC environments.  Before 
all else, the code must be portable.  However, we do use a preprocessor
to isolate code, as in FILE-CONTROL statements, that cannot be ported.

I can't think that this scenario is all that unusual, but I've only
worked in two COBOL shops.  The experience of others may indicate that
this is a rare development environment.

That's exactly what we do.  We develop in MicroFocus COBOL-85 under
Windows.  Then we port to MVS, VSE, AS/400, DEC VAX, DEC Alpha, Unix
(many flavors), and Unisys A.

We port from 50 to 80 million lines of COBOL-85 code per year to these
platforms.  The resulting executables are packaged and shipped to our
several thousand customers.  Almost all of our customers are on a
maintenance agreement that requires us to ship them updated programs 4
times a year.  We have been doing this since 1990.

Bill, the only reason you don't hear people like us complain is
because we work around the portability problems with a preprocessor. 
And there are way too many portability problems.

One of our highest priorities is portability of COBOL.  The fact that
COBOL is still the most portable lanaguage was the deciding factor to
go COBOL in 1990.  If some other language was more portable, we
probably would have gone to that.

Very true.  Rehosting COBOL applications (if they are very big) is usually a

optimism than actual ease of movement.  In the case of the most important
migration (again, by value), IBM 3X0 -> something else, the problems are

amalgamation of COBOL code, CICS artifacts, DB2/IMS/Adabase etc., and 360
environmental oddities.

I have noticed a curious view of the COBOL "standard" in the IBM developer
community.  As often as not, when they refer to the "standard," they are
actually referring to the particular IBM COBOL that they use.  This has led
to many misunderstandings over the years (in our business).  They seem
largely unaware of the content of the ANSI/ISO/FIPS standard.

I believe the only "new" COBOL (hyperbole here) is being done by COBOL
programmers who find their way into a new development environment or
company, and have the freedom to choose the language for their project.

organizations.

Yep.  The managers (decision makers) seem to place great "value" on souce
portability.  The actual programmers, however, will use non-standard
features without hesitation in almost every instance.  I am not sure ANY of
our customers have significant applications that are written entirely in
either 1974 or 1985 standard COBOL.  This is not to say they don't "believe"
they have standard COBOL.  There are, however, some add-on development tool
suppliers that have products written mostly, or entirely, in standard COBOL.
They can do this because they are usually replacing or augmenting a standard
COBOL feature, and they have little interaction with the host application
flow or data, other than through their defined interfaces.

By the way, and slightly off topic, I continue to be amazed at the lack of
use of 1985 standard enhancements in actual applications.  I would guess
that some of the most difficult (i.e., expensive) features for us to
implement, such as nested programs, have never been used by a single one of
our customers in the 15 years the've been available.  Many (perhaps 50+%) of
our customers' applications have used few or or no '85 standard  extensions.
Conversely, virtually all of our customers use our (extra-standard)
extensions to the language.
