```
I have recently been required to provide some demo versions of various 
tools.

It is always problematic from a development point of view because there are 
risks attached. (Theft, failure to install, learning curves, etc.)

At the same time, I have been looking to review and enhance existing web 
sites and one of the best ways to do that is to use video.

However, adding video to web sites is a bit of a minefield at the moment as 
there are many formats and different Browsers support things differently. An 
attempt to overcome this is the HTML5 implementation which seems to have the 
best chance of anything I've seen so far. Unfortunately, not all Browsers 
support this (emerging) standard. I spent a number of hours looking at the 
problem and gathering information.

The best solution I can see is an HTML5 implementation with "fall back" in 
cases where the Browser doesn't understand it.

(People who are interested or are currently building web sites should look 
at: http://videojs.com/  ...apart from anything else, the demo video on 
their site is spectacular...)

I got to thinking...

Maybe, a short video showing software in action, could replace a demo 
version.

The idea is that people could watch the video and, if they were interested, 
they could then request a sample copy. The difference here is that I know 
who has the sample copies and can fingerprint them so they will only run on 
that site, rather than having a demo disk that can run anywhere and be 
perpetuated on a VM.... Furthermore, watching the software being used as 
intended could avoid some of the frustration people experience when coming 
to grips with a new package.

Within the next few weeks/months both the PRIMA web site 
(http://primacomputing.co.nz ) and the COBOL 21 site 
(http://primacomputing.co.nz/cobol21 ) will be reviewed and updated. The 
free software will remain and may be added to, and I am seeking to provide 
some basic OO tutorials for COBOL people in the form of videos, although 
that will depend on time available.

In the meantime, I'd be interested to hear people's opinions on whether 
they'd prefer to see a video or download a trial, when assessing suitability 
of new sofware.

Pete.
```

```
TechSmith's Camtasia (www.techsmith.com) is very popular for this sort
of thing (screen recordings, software demo videos, screencasts, etc).
They also sell a fancy screen-snapshot product, Snagit.

I know some people at TechSmith - they're a local firm - and have a
good opinion of the firm. They do robust user research, for example.

They also have a free screen-recording / screencasting program called
Jing, which stores recordings online; you install Jing and set up a
free account, then you make a recording and send the URL to your
audience. It's a "freemium" product - if you like it, you can buy the
upgrade to the "professional" version with more features. Jing's an
easy way to try out the demo-video approach.

I've also used some other screencasting software. For Windows, there's
a nice free program named Wink that creates screencasts in Shockwave
format, which is widely supported. It's more complicated than Jing,
less feature-rich than Camtasia Studio; but may be worth experimenting
with.
```
