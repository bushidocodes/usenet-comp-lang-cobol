As you will learn with experience, the above underlined phrase is an
unwarranted assumption.  If you become a good programmer it will be in
part due to learning one particular thing: There is a great difference
between human perception and reality.  Just because you do not perceive
a difference (or a problem or an error) does *not* mean that there isn't
one there.  Working with computers will teach you a great deal about
human fallibility.  Once you fully appreciate how fallible you are, and
how unreliable your perception really is, you can begin to develop habits
which protect you from error, in a way analogous to how we build error
correction into data transmission protocols.  All the best programmers
do this, though they may not consciously think of it in this way. :-)
