I first started learning OO design in 1990 or so with (Borland) C++.  I even
started rewriting in C++ a text editor that I had been writing in (Turbo) C.
 Then I hit a stumbling block (I can't remember exactly what) and lost
computer access for a few years.  Once I got a computer and came back to
programming I started learning Cobol because the bank I worked at had a
Cobol programming opening.  Only after using Cobol for a few years did I get
back in to OO with Java.  But at that point I was a full time Cobol
programmer, of course, and only 'played' with Java.

Other than the failed C++ text editor effort I can't really say I've
developed anything close to a "real life program" using any OO language. 
I've been investigating many languages lately.  Some are OO (Java, C#, Ruby,
Python, Objective-C) and some are "functional" (Scheme, Haskell, a bit of
OCaml).  I love many things about those other languages; sometimes to the
point of hating Cobol for not having those features!  But Cobol is great at
making simple things very simple.  Other languages say they do that, and to
some degree they do.  But other languages tend to make fairly easy some
things that Cobol makes difficult.

Anyway, I am neither for nor against OO.  I don't think that I fully 'grok'
OO, and until I actually sit down and try to write an at least somewhat
large program in an OO language I don't think I'll ever be absolutely
comfortable with it.  But I do see many of it's attributes.  And some of its
downfalls.

Just my thoughts.  I don't know that I'll ever get around to seriously
developing anything outside of the Cobol I do at work.  I enjoy
investigating the languages, but I don't want to spend all of my free time
programming!  :-)  Now if I transfer from the mainframe team to the web team
here at our shop I'd certainly get a good dose of Java.  Or C# on the
Windows team.  But I don't know that that is what I want.  At this point
we're finally starting to get moving on using DB2 from the mainframe, so
I'll be too busy with that for the next few years at least to consider any
sort of move.

Anyway, in some ways I do think that Cobol can 'cripple' one's thinking when
it comes to learning other languages.  In some fairly drastic ways it is
quite different than any other language.  Memory management, for one.  Cobol
just doesn't deal with it!  The use of subroutines for another.  No other
language that I know of has anything like paragraphs and sections.  Of
course Cobol does have callable subroutines, both external and nested.  But
I don't find their use as flexible as in many other languages.

Those are my thoughts.

Frank
