(Thought I would start a new thread on this)

Just FYI,
   I have used Norton products and more recently CCLeaner.

I do (fairly often) "registry cleaning" (although I am aware that the benefits 
under Windows XP are minimal - if any).

I do this because it is part of their "full cleanup" options.

I do a registry backup every few months or so (whenever I think about it)

  * * * *

Using these two products, I have NEVER had a problem with my registry (either 
for booting up or running specify products).

I don't know if this is because i have VERY few "non-commercial" applications 
(i.e. free downloads or self-developed) applications on my machine - or if it is 
simply that I am lucky (or REALLY LUCKY).

For both register cleaners, I did do some research first on user reports and 
didn't see any "messed up my register" user reports.  I am certain that such 
must exist somewhere, but I didn't see them before I started using the products.

 * * * * * *

Obviously, I am not disputing the reports that others have made in this 
newsgroup on their experiences or those that see reported by others.  I am 
simply trying to state that I (along with - I think - many others) do use 
"reliable" software to run register cleanup regularly and have had NO problems 
with doing so.

Similarly, I can well imagine that this is one of those things that , once it 
has happened to you, you REALLY never want to happen again.

I've never had a problem either, Bill.

Nevertheless, the risks are real. HeyBub's point was that there is little 
real benefit in doing it. I think he's right.

Unfortunately, there is an industry in selling people software they don't 
really need. (And some of this, especially registry cleaners, is marketed 
agressively and in a misleading way to frighten people into buying it.)

Messing with Registry hives is always a risk, even when you are doing it as 
part of an application and only using keys which are pertinent to your 
specific software. The Registry is so fundamental to correct Windows 
operation that changes to it always carry a very small risk. Many of us will 
have had the experience of software not working correctly, spending hours 
looking into it, then finding that changing a single Registry entry resolves 
the problem.

Even though the Registry software itself is very robust and Windows 
maintains a previous copy just in case, to manipulate the Registry because 
of a perceived need to "clean it up" is really a bit like playing with fire, 
especially if you don't really understand how the Registry works and simply 
put your faith in a piece of third party software which you were really 
frightened into buying :-).

In the light of these discussions, I'm re-evaluating my current Registry 
clean-up run and may stop doing it altogether, even though it has never done 
any harm (that I know of...). I think a defrag once a year may be a better, 
lower risk, option.

I've already commented on registry cleaners. I will offer this additionally:

The most insidious of registry cleaners is one that actually works and does 
no harm. Why? Because the fact that it exists implies that OTHER registry 
cleaners may be equally benign. By you reporting you've never had a problem 
with Brand X registry cleaner you have given false hope to (admittedly 
foolish) people contemplating Brand Y.

Moving on...

Norton is the barnacle of the software world. After you run their uninstall 
program to rid yourself of this mess, you must download and execute 
Symantec's sooper-sekret removal tool. THEN you have to manually scour the 
registry of any resistant entries bearing the name of "Symantec," or 
"Norton." Next you must explore your system for residual folders and files.

After all this effort, you will have removed most (but probably not all) of 
Norton's anti-virus offering.

Why go to all this trouble? Two principle reasons:

1. Because NAV (Norton Anti-Virus) can suck up to seven percent of system 
resources.

2. NAV, moreover, interferes mightily with the installation of other 
products without warning (that's why many installation programs demand you 
disable any virus protection before proceeding).

In their defense, reports regarding the most recent release of NAV indicate 
that Symantec has taken criticism to heart and improved the product's 
efficiency significantly.

Meanwhile, there are several alternatives to NAV - some of the best are even 
free.

Pete Dashwood wrote:
> Paul Richards wrote:
>>
…[5 more quoted lines elided]…
> substitute words and have an alias. I had a 1200 baud dial up also.

Actually, you probably didn't. I don't think there were any standard
async 1200 baud modem protocols, and there weren't any 2400 baud ones
until V.32 in 1989 - and that's almost certainly not what you mean.

What you had were, respectively, 2400 bps (bits per second) and 1200
bps modems. Both of those were 600 baud protocols.

The baud unit is symbols per second, not bits per second. This was a
common error in hobbyist publications of the 1970s and 1980s, and it
stuck around until most people had migrated away from async modems
over conventional telephone lines.

Standard 1200 bps async modems used the Bell 212A, V.22, or V.23
protocols, which were 600 baud with 2 bits/symbol, using PSK
(phase-shift keying, for 212A and V.22) or FSK (frequency-shift
keying, for V.23). With FSK, you send one of four tones (frequencies),
600 times a second: the four tones give you the four possible
combinations of two bits. PSK is similar except you shift the phase of
the carrier rather than the frequency.

Standard 2400 bps async modems used 600 baud with the V.22bis
protocol, which used QAM (quadrature amplitude modulation). QAM uses
two carriers, which are 90 degrees out of phase, and modulates the
amplitude of each; that lets you carry four bits per symbol.

The first standard 2400 baud async modem protocol was V.32, which used
2400 baud and allowed up to 9600 bits/second.

I did some hobby stuff with a 300-baud, 300-bps Bell 103 modem, but
