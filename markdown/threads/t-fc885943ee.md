```
Please forgive; I don't see a FAQ, and I haven't posted to the usenet in
11 years (roughly.) I may not be working the new-fangled mobile usenet
reader correctly, (is this thing on?) so if I'm violating any FAQ rules,
please have mercy. I'm delighted to see that it's popularity has waned
enough that most of the spam has at last gone away:)

I'm also completely new to COBOL. I spend my work day dealing mostly
with Ruby, Perl, Java, SQL, Javascript, and the markup crap (though I've
managed to steer my way away from most of the god forsaken web front end
stuff at last.) 

Check it out: COBOL just got a new user. Why? Because I've never had a
chance to learn it, it had meteoric popularity once, and there must have
been a reason for that. Also: I'm intrigued by the faux-English syntax.
In any event, in the network age, programmers from all corners are
discovering the legendary "lost languages" with unbridled enthusiasm.

After pulling down the list of messages, I couldn't help noticing the
funeral thread. 

Are we completely sure it's dead? Maybe all COBOL really needs is one
really radical, out of box idea. Something genuinely new that moves the
needle for the language. A better story on slashdot than "lots of money
maintaining COBOL code as there's almost no one left who knows it" or
"startup specializes in automatic translation of legacy COBOL systems to
Java."

Why not burn the disk packs and start over? 

Lisp got continuation based web frameworks, which make modeling control
flows through web applications much less painful. People are starting to
write web apps in Lisp: but only because Seaside touched an untapped
nerve, having copied the technique. Seaside was written in Squeak, but
might not have been, were it not for the fact that Squeak was written in
itself, which made it ultra-malleable, and attractive to people who like
to hack on languages. Forth is all the rage again! The hipsters are all
raving about Factor, what with it's portable Smalltalk style
GUI/development environment, but the reason it all really works is that
it lifted the best ideas from CLOS: it's object-forth, and object in the
radical sense of the word, not the Java sense of the word; meanwhile, ML
is reborn with new syntax and ideas from various other languages as
Haskell. 

I would bet:

COBOL is too compatible. Being used primarily in business, breaking
compat with older standards has mired potential new variants in
unnecessary complexity. Why not do something radical and invent a
completely new Cobol? Something that retains the essence of what keeps
everyone here reading and posting to this list, without the accumulated
cruft which presents unneeded cognitive overhead for new people who want
to learn the language?

COBOL is too big. Too many keywords. This makes it hard to learn. For
decades, companies have saved money by hiring kids straight out of
school, and the simplest languages have begun to win in industry. This
is why I'm paid to look at Ruby. Why not find the smallest usable subset
of COBOL? Or better yet, why not throw out the idea of keywords all
together, and make everything user defined, and written in COBOL, a la
Forth or Smalltalk?

Here's a whopper: what might COBOL be like without static typing? What
might COBOL look like with duck typing? These days, web companies want
to be able to make small, iterative product cycles go out the door
quickly, and the mission criticality is generally lower, as hot fixing
has become a way of life. These requirements regularly eclipse the
confidence that a business can derive by eliminating the category of
bugs that go away when one checks types at compile time. The performance
advantages of inlining functions make far less of a difference when the
hardware is so much cheaper than the time of the development team, and
when the dynamic optimization techniques developed for Self have in most
cases mitigated the worst overhead of dynamic systems. Besides, Ruby is
about as slow as a tortoise (though it doesn't need to be) and it's been
all the rage lately.

One last completely heretical idea: run it in the browser. V8 takes
advantage of the best compiler technologies of the last 20 years; if
your compiler is any good, the benches should blow away what you had at
the end of the eighties, which was good enough then. Implement COBOL in
Javascript. If you implement the language in Javascript, your programs
will live forever (well, until we're all dead anyway.)

If you made it all the way to the end, I hope you enjoyed my crazytalk.
Thanks for listening:)
```

I doubt that COBOL will ever get rid of static typing, BUT it also has 
"dynamic" typing.  In the 2002 ANSI/ISO COBOL Standard, TYPEDEF and TYPE 
phrases were added.  There are also CALL Prototypes for "CALL conformance 
checking" at compile-time. These, along with Object Orientation and user 
defined functions add some of the "newer but not so new" programming 
language features that were missing in the 1985 COBOL Standard.

Several (not all) existing vendors and providers have some or all of these 
features.

I'm not a regular COBOL programmer, but I *am* happy that I taught
myself parts of the language. I have a better understanding of:

- representations of data and data conversion issues
- databases
- business data processing

Let me cite two short examples.

1. An old Fortran program used keyed access on VMS to access a
database. Emulating some of this functionality was the only option.
(Standard Fortran has no such thing.) I tried to explain ISAM to the
other programmer, but he did not have the background. Understanding
COBOL helped me write the Fortran program I needed. [Knowing some
dBase3+ did not hurt, either.]

2. A customer gave me an Excel spreadsheet with a key field in col 1
and data in col 2. The assignment was to group data in col 2 for
identical key values in col 1. The data was pre-grouped by key. So I
was able to write fairly simple control-break logic in VBA thanks to
having seen it in COBOL.

IMO there is still a reason to TEACH COBOL.
