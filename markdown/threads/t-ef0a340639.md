Both PeopleSoft and Oracle are companies that sell software products. So
why is there a demand for skills in those venues? The demand is for
road-capable consultants to install their packages, not so much for
coders back at Oracle headquarters.

One oft-spoken theory of avoiding the 'problems' of continual
'maintenance' of systems is to buy canned packages, such as PeopleSoft
HR (Human Resources) and Oracle Financials. Then the people who have the
title similar to 'programmer' (at the client company) are tasked with
installing the packages.
Instead of writing code, they are determining and setting up
'parameters' for the canned software to use. 1000's of parameters, from
which the canned code derives its execution paths. The choice of
parameters allows the canned package to be twisted to fit the client
company's business practices. :) :) :) :)

Consultants who are experts (i.e., it's not their first installation
project) accompany the installation process, generally depicting the
proper selection/use of each parameter. This expertise may be more in
the business aspects than programming aspects, although the best combo
is some former programmer who is familiar with writing a system such as
HR in 'native' code. They are very costly.

After the installation, there is an outside demand for the in-house
installers. Because they then understand a lot about the choices for
parameters, somewhat in the programming sense, but more-to-the-point, in
the business sense. For example, what's the result downstream and on
the companies policies if the 'programmers' choose certain parameters
that cause LIFO instead of FIFO inventory practices.

So Oracle or PeopleSoft (or consulting firms that specialize in
installing their packages) are grabbing these people from the
last-place-installed for the next-project-somewhere-else. And the money
is BIG compared to working at Mundane Industries (even with the usurial
appropriation to the consulting firm's yacht-owners).

But really, the packages are rarely installed 'vanilla'. So coding is
also performed. In Oracle, for example, one uses their Developer 2000
package to create 'custom' screens and programs. And 'SQL Plus' for
EXITS that are SQL routines. So: yes there is some SQL.
And the 'installers' also get some hands-on programming under their
belt.
Which makes them even more valuable to be hired as a consultant at an
installation somewhere else.

To summarize the above rambling...
Jobs doing PeopleSoft, Oracle, etc. are not so much for programmers as
they are for installers. However, programming background is handy,
especially if the programmer has written systems of the same nature in
past lives. And generally, the jobs are those of roaming consultant,
going from installation to installation about every two years. And big
bucks compared to writing code for a company.

All the above are my opinions/thoughts and not those of any company that
would hire the likes of me.

John
