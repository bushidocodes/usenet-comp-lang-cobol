Wang Labs went out of business around 1990. It's doubtful you'll find anyone
with _current_ Wang COBOL experience. The good new is you don't need to. 
Wang COBOL conformed to the '85 standard, same as Micro Focus and every other
COBOL compiler. Wang COBOL is not a different language nor dialect; it's
standard COBOL. Its only non-standard extension is in writing data on text mode
screens using DISPLAY and reading it therefrom using ACCEPT. Many COBOL
compilers have similer extensions. 

It sounds like the client is converting from Wang to Micro Focus. In any
conversion project, skills with the target environment are far more important
than detailed knowledge of the old one. The converter needs only to read and
understand the old code long enough to get it converted; (s)he's not developing
new Wang code. Since Wang is so similar to Micro Focus, any COBOL programmer
could understand it. I recommend you advertise for Micro Focus, and just mention
that it's a Wang conversion. 

Robert

No. That is incorrect.  It is not 'the same' between, say, MF and
Fujitsu.  Fujitsu has no COMMIT (except for SQL) and cannot gain a
record lock on a REWRITE as MF can.  MF has READ WITH [NO / KEPT/
IGNORE] LOCK, Fujitsu doesn't have 2 of those options. MF also has
WITH WAIT to wait for the lock which Fujitsu does not do, and RM
always does (AFAIK).

It may be that for some compilers there is a simplistic subset of each
which more-or-less works the same.

That is: Hypothetically it could be the same, in practice it isn't.

> One compiler, either IBM or MF, became the de-facto standard
> copied by others and now ANSI.

As IBM does not have LOCKing - except for the rebranded MF systems,
then it wasn't that company that led the way.

RM had LOCKING from the late 70s at least. I used an MF CIS-Cobol
compiler with automatic locking (but no syntax) in 1981.  Locking
syntax was in the 'X-Open' standard, but has moved in different ways
since then.

MS also had different syntax in its own version 2 compiler (not MF).

To use the REGARDLESS or ALLOWING options, the program must include

ALLOWING option of the OPEN statement
--------- """

Wang Cobol:
"""  ------------------------
Figure 3: Typical COBOL open statement

------------------------- """

The '02 standard is different again, but a subset may be similar

Actually it _is_ a consideration, but it may be that you have just not
considered it.

There is a SELECT .. FOR UPDATE in some systems which should produce a
row lock(s) (or in some systems a page or even a table lock).

My understanding of the normal behaviour of SQL systems is that rows
that are UPDATEd are locked until the COMMIT or ROLLBACK and that
SELECTs may read those locked rows.

This makes the system work for most purposes.  But it is not suitable
for some applications - such as Order entry for distribution, or not
for how my customers want to do it interactively.

If their customer rings and asks availability of a product then the
stock level should be locked until the customer confirms the quantity
required. If this is not done with a WITH LOCK or FOR UPDATE then the
stock may 'disappear'.

It is also useful to report back when records are locked, rather than
just waiting until they come free.  In the situation above where the
customer is deciding how many he wants (or whether to take something
else instead), the other users will be told that stock item is locked
(while displaying the details) rather than 'hanging'.

Of course we also want to avoid the problem where two users find that
there are 10 items available and both take 1 and issue UPDATE ... SET
stock = 9 ..
SQL will carefully wait for one to complete then apply the other -
oops.

One can avoid this by adding WHERE ... AND stock = 10 .. This will
fail for one of them and have to go back for reentry.

One could also arrange for the UPDATE to do the subtraction to the
existing figure whatever it is, but then the user may not find out
that the stock is now overallocated (gone negative) and that will
cause an exception at despatch - which order gets the stock is now
dependent on how fast each warehouseman shuffles around (technically a
'race condition', but I've seen them work) rather than on the sales
order taker.

Different solutions are required for different situations.  If there
are lots of products, most in stock, and few concurrent orders then it
doesn't matter how it is done.  Where stock levels often run down
resulting in back ordering as a normal process then locking on
read/select may be best.

At the other end there are order systems with few products and lots of
interactive orders where locking of the stock rows for anything would
cause a disaster.  In that case the initial read must not lock in any
way and the update must be done based on the current stock level by
reREAD WITH LOCK or UPDATE doing a subtraction.

CI/CA splits. After building an (alternate) index around the ESDS file (using

>that are UPDATEd are locked until the COMMIT or ROLLBACK and that
>SELECTs may read those locked rows.

Yes. The return code warns you the data may be stale. UPDATEs are held in memory
until COMMIT.

>If their customer rings and asks availability of a product then the
>stock level should be locked until the customer confirms the quantity
>required. If this is not done with a WITH LOCK or FOR UPDATE then the
>stock may 'disappear'.

What if a clerk goes to lunch and leaves it locked for an hour? Just because the
customer inquired doesn't give him an 'option' on the merchandise. 

I never lock data on first read. When it is time for update, I reread it to make
sure it hasn't changed, then rewrite it and release the lock. The lock persists
for a millisecond. 

On databases I avoid the overhead of rereading and locking by putting a WHERE
clause on the UPDATE. With that technique, you don't need locks at all. The
technique will work on any file system, including lock-less ones like the old
Realia.

>One can avoid this by adding WHERE ... AND stock = 10 .. This will
>fail for one of them and have to go back for reentry.

Not reentry, recalculation of the quantity available. If the quantity has
changed, I don't make the clerk reenter the order. If there is still sufficient
quantity to fill the order, I silently update it. The order taker is notified
only if the quantity has become insufficient, and sometimes not even then (see
below). 

>One could also arrange for the UPDATE to do the subtraction to the
>existing figure whatever it is, but then the user may not find out
>that the stock is now overallocated (gone negative)

That would be done in a trigger -- a stored procedure which CAN abort the update
and return an error. 

>At the other end there are order systems with few products and lots of
>interactive orders where locking of the stock rows for anything would
>cause a disaster.  In that case the initial read must not lock in any
>way and the update must be done based on the current stock level by
>reREAD WITH LOCK or UPDATE doing a subtraction.

It can also happen when the number of products is large. There may be a
chi-squared distribution such that the top ten products appear in 80% of the
orders. Imagine a supermarket warehouse with 25,000 items on hand. Nearly every
order will include empty sacks, bags of sugar and flour, etc. There is no reason
to make the customer and order taker wait when quantity on hand is 100 times a
normal order.

------------------------------------------
A related issue is rationing. Suppose you don't have enough quantity to satisfy
the week's demand. Should the early birds get all they want and the late-comers
get none? To make it worse, customers will often be aware there is a shortage
and go into hoarding mode, ordering more than they need to avoid being out next
week.

It's bad business to have some stores out of a staple such as onions or lettuce
while other stores have a surplus. The order entry program should record the
quantity requested. A batch program can later ration the quantity available so
that every store gets some, even though it's less than they asked for. Stores
might catch on and ask for twice the quantity they really want. To avoid that,
rationing might be based on order history rather than quantity requested. 

Rationing is common practice with mass merchandisers and supermarkets,
especially on seasonal and promotional items.
