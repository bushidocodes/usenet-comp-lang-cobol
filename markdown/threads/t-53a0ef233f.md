```
I've never heard the term 'accounting date', but in most business programs
you have a one, possibly two, dates that you may want to track.  For certain
you want the 'transaction date', which is the date the transaction actually
occurred.  This date will be input by the operator, because it may be earlier
than the date the transaction was entered into the computer.  Then you may
want to keep the 'entry date', which is the date the transaction was entered
into the computer, which can be automatically recorded by the program as the
transaction is entered, of course.  Hope this helps. :-)
```

```
We use the term "accounting month ending date" and "accounting quarter
ending date" all the time. Our accountants pick a Friday to be the last day
of the month, quarter or year, since our people get paid on Friday and it
makes things neater.

You may get the "accounting date" from a file, from operator input, or by
calculation. (If it was ALWAYS the last Friday in the month you could
calculate it.)

Anyway, your first step is to find out where they keep the accounting date.
```

```
We often used the term monthly closing date. Reporting requirements
often demand that more needs to be done than could be done without
running the month on a logical basis vs. physical basis. A virtual
month. It is possible in some organizations to have many of these
because of the problems of consolidation, logistics...  A notice would
be published giving the various dates for those who needed to know.
```
