I issued a challenge to the opponents of GOTO's a while ago in this
group, offering a simple example of live code which was to be rewritten,
with the idea being that the result should demonstrate why GOTO-less code
is better. It is only fair to report on the results, which are - not
much.

- 12 people asked for the example
- one person - Andy Styles - sent me an
e-mail of the reworked code, but for some reason it didn't get to me. He
thereupon published it in the newsgroup.
- Colin Campbell corresponded with me by e-mail on the subject, and went
and looked up some of the very early articles that were published on the
subject (thereby getting a far better understanding of the history of this
argument than nearly anyone else). Djikstra was not the originator of
this idea; it goes back to Boehm and Jacopini's article in Communications
of the ACM, May 1966, which Djikstra says "seems to prove the (logical)
superfluousness of the goto statement" (quoting Colin's quote here).
(Dkikstra's article is in Comm. ACM, March 1968, which is a lot earlier
than I remember it coming into common consciousness). I urged Colin to
publish his findings in this newsgroup.
- no-one else has got back to me.
- if there has been discussion in this group, I have likely missed it, as
I only check it out once or twice a week.

As to Andy's rework, the only one I've seen, it doesn't prove the point.
(I must assume it got posted accurately). It is the same length - 45
statements - as mine; introduces an extra switch; and has a
double-barrelled termination condition at the beginning of the important
PERFORM UNTIL. (This was the one I had trouble coding, and it is a neat
solution). It appears that Andy's code would work. He remarks on the
"Convoluted GOTO version"; this is an odd statement, since both versions
operate in exactly the same way, the difference being that his
code uses IMPLICIT GOTO's, whereas mine uses EXPLICIT ones. Obviously
one's body of experience colours one's appreciation of things. Anyway,
there is in the rework nothing that made me leap from my bath crying
"eureka", indeed nothing that I could point to as an improvement.
Remember that that was the challenge: to make it obviously better. And,
with respect, it hasn't.

Of course, this won't convince anyone. I am disappointed, though, that
there was so little response; and I'm also disappointed that the ones I
got implied that the problem I have with GOTO-less coding isn't an
objective one. I am a skeptic, not
someone whose mind is immovably made up; I welcome better ideas. I have
never denied for a second that programs CAN be written without GOTO's;
I've never seen anything convincing that turns CAN into MUST.

Regards to all ...

›› the difference being that his code uses IMPLICIT GOTO's, whereas
›› mine uses EXPLICIT ones.

Implicit GOs exist in many places. A NEXT SENTENCE is a GO TO
[next .]. It is even possible that a PERFORM could be an implicit
ALTER [implicit paragraph at end] TO PROCEED TO [implict label]
and an implicit GO TO label.

In fact ICL's XE13 did implement PERFORMs like that - actually
modifying the code at the end of the performed paragraph. As a
consequence of this overlapping performs did not work.

In fact the issue of 'GO TO being bad', in my view, disguises where
the problem really lies. It is *not* the GO TO that is the problem,
it is the label that is the target of the GO TO where the problem
arises.

When you see a GO TO in a program, it is obvious what it does, it is
obvious where the logic flows to, there are no hidden complications.
(Unless, of course, it is subject to an ALTER, but we will ignore
that possibility as all such programs should be destroyed
immediately, as reading them will affect your mental health).

The problem only arises at the label: the paragraph or section-name.
If you look at a small part of a large program that does a specific
job and it is required to modify this, then it is *not* possible to
determine from a local inspection all the ways the logic may flow
around this label.

A label may be subject to a PERFORM, PERFORM THRU (or as part of a
PERFORM section), a GO TO, or a drop-through. In order to determine
which actually apply it is necessary, in the general case, to
inspect the whole program.

Many programming standards are aimed at reducing the number of
different ways that a particular label may be arrived at, or to
reduce the scope which needs to be searched for ways of arriving at
that particular label.

For example if the standard is that only sections may be performed,
and this can be relied on, then it is only necessary to search the
current section for GO TO and drop-through in relation to a specific
paragraph within a section.

If the standard also says that only forward GO TOs are allowed then
this reduces further the scope to be searched.

With fewer reliable standards the amount of searching and the
complexity of the program may increase. If both PERFORM paragraph
and PERFORM paragraph THRU paragraph (or section) are allowed then
very complex situations can arise. I use a measure of complexity
for these situations where labels are counted for each different
type of access: perform, perform thru, goto, drop-trough (see
sidebar in previous message).

Note that implicit gotos, such as next sentence in ansi-74, are not
an issue because there is no additional label associated with
these. I would consider next sentence to be an error when used with
end-if as the full-stop (implicit label) could be moved, or removed,
with unfortunate and unwanted results.

The [informal] 'complexity index' indicates how much effort is
required to ensure that the program will work correctly after
changes have been added.

In a very unstructured program with no particular standards, or
where they haven't been followed, the complexity index may be very
high, and the effort required large, with a confidence of working
correctly low.

If the program has a very low complexity index then the amount of
effort to ensure that it works correctly should be low.

I am a very lazy programmer. I want to make changes in the shortest
possible time with the least amount of effort, and the smallest
necessary amount of testing.

By reducing the complexity index to the smallest possible number
means that I can expend the least amount of effort in making the
program work correctly.

To reduce this index, and the underlying complexity that I assert
that it is measuring, the following steps can be taken:

1) Reduce the number of labels - paragraphs and sections
2) Reduce the numbers of ways each of these can be reached

Other languages (such as Pascal and C) achieve item 2) by providing
different types of labels that are specific to the way they are
referenced. Labels may only be subject to goto (or implicit goto in
the switch statement), functions may only be called, and not dropped
into or gotoed.

I tend to take an extreme view of this (because I am basically lazy)
and only allow one way in which any label may be used, this
drastically reduces the complexity index, to the point where I
always have a very high confidence in the logic path of the program.
This does not mean that the program is always correct, I am not
perfect, but it will not fail in mysterious ways.

If all ways were equally suitable then the choice of one would not
be an issue. However, most ways are not uniformly acceptable or
possible. Only using drop-through would be pointless. Perform thru
must involve drop-through or goto. Perform section also must
involve drop-through or goto unless it is being used exclusively on
single paragraph sections.

Goto, by itself, could be used as the sole means of control, but it
would be extremely difficult to imagine a useful program so done.

The only candidate for the single control mechanism within a program
is perform paragraph, with its options of varying and until.

If the 'complexity index' has any validity, and if producing
programs with a lower index makes them more maintainable and easier
to understand (and my 25 years of development indicate these to be
true) then the way forward is reducing the types of control.

This strategy was formed as the result of studying and using other
languages and identifying where the strengths of these languages
came from. Applying this experience back into my Cobol programs
gave me enormous benefits as I came to change those that were
'de-complexified'.

In fact I would be very happy if the compiler could enforce clues as
to these issues. If we could distinguish between controls at the
paragraph level by adding keywords:

paragraph-name LOCAL. >* scope only within the section
paragraph-name PERFORM. >* may only be performed
paragraph-name FROM. >* may only be subject to go to

Adding these, and having the compiler complain on a non-local
reference, or a drop-through or go to into a PERFORM paragraph,
would increase the confidence enormously and would result in more
reliable programs with less effort (Don are you still there?).

While any set of standards is usually a step in the right direction,
the rules tend to be pragmatic without any underlying measure of the
consequences or results of imposing them. They were known to make
the programs better, but not why.

While I may be viewed as being 'anti-goto' here, I am in fact not as
adamant about goto as I am about drop-through. When working on
existing code where there are go tos as a consequence of a perform
section, then, if I can't easily remove a label, I will add an
explicit goto to replace a drop-through. This, at least, indicates
the path and is findable with a text search.

I will also always allow an ultimate escape from the bowls of an
uncorrectable error, or an interrupt from the user, with a
GO TO Abandon-Program.

Complexity is at the root of all maintenance problems. Luckily,
modern COBOL constructs can be retro-fitted to old programs, eg:
* END-IF does away with the need for most paragraph names;

No. It wouldn't. You had written the original, presumably to the
best of your current standards, any change would make the program
subjectively worse to you, and subjectively better to the rewriter
(this is an observation, not a criticism).

This is one reason why I didn't take up the challenge. With you as
the judge and jury you would naturally see any differences as being
less than compelling. Short examples are also trivially uncomplex
and present no challenge in that they can be completely understood
whether they are entirely done in goto, or in nested ifs.

And in any case you already *fully* understood your version, yet had
to decipher any variant of it. What is required is an example
written in two styles that you haven't seen before, you then have to
determine which is 'better'. As it is trivially easy to
deliberately bias examples to favour one style over another, this is
unlikely to convince you either.

The driving idea behind structed, or modular, or OO, programming is
that the program should be divided up into relatively isolated parts
where the amoumt that needs to be understood at one time is
manageable. As this is inevitably greater than a short example
there is little difference between styles at this level.

What I have tried to present is an objective measure: the complexity
index, which can be applied to a program and to its revisions. My
contention is (do please try to show that I am wrong) that the lower
the index the more isolated the code is, and the less consequential
damage occurs from changes in the code.

So it is not only that the example must be recoded, but the
structure of the original and the revision must be *tested* by
applying a variety of changes in the specification and determining
how much change and rewriting must occur in each version.
