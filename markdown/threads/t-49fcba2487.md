```
Here's how Fujitsu's install works:
1. You install the product and enter the serial number of the CD.
2. The installation process generates half of the required unlock key
and the installation process e-mails this half-key to Fujitsu's
license-registry computer. The half-key presumably contains a hash of
the installation computer's physical characteristics.
3. Their computer updates its license file (subtracting 1 from the
number of licenses you've bought) and emails the second half of the
unlock key.
4. You enter the second half of the key and everything is golden.

Except for the following:

1. In the best of all possible worlds, you CAN move the license from
machine "A" to machine "B."  IF you've done something to return the
license from "A" back to FJ and fresh installed on "B," you can then
move the license from "B" and "A" via a floppy (and back again). Their
license server does not have to get involved (unless something goes
wrong, of course, in which case, you're in trouble).
2. If the machine on which the license is installed has to have a new
hard drive, you're in trouble.
3. If you have to install/reinstall the OS, you're in trouble.
4. You can't take your laptop from home directly to a customer's site;
you must first go to the office and perform the swap in #1 (and hope
it works). You're going to a lot of trouble.
5. If you - for whatever reason - remove (as in "Add/Remove") the
software, the software cannot be re-installed without a lot of begging
and whining via a voice call. You're in trouble.
6. If you have a dual-boot machine (say Win95/W2K) and want the
compiler in both partitions for compatibility testing, you're in
trouble.


Perhaps it got lost in the translation:
'Site' to Fujitsu means 'seat' - to the rest of the world it means
'location'
'Enterprise Edition' to Fujitsu means a set of features, to most of
the world it means 'unlimited license.'

So a Fujitsu Site License for their Enterprise Edition means a lot of
features are available on one computer.

All in all, a most unsatisfactory situation.

P.S.,
I may have a few of the details slightly wrong, but it's tough to get
through their thirty-seven page installation instructions (replete
with phrases like: "Uninstalling a registered product may result in
losing your license to the product" and "Failure to transfer or kill
your license prior to uninstalling will require you to buy a new
license to the product if you want to reinstall it" ), without
throwing a hissy fit and losing one's sanity.
```

```
Jerry P wrote:
<snip>
> ## Yeah, but, see, some of our users are not as diligent about
> immediately upgrading operating systems as we so we have to make sure
> our product works on their dinosaur operating systems (Win98, WinNT,
> etc.).

This brings up an interesting idea.  Why, as a user, do I have to
change to the latest and greatest operating system?  

Suppose that I'm using an accounting system written in COBOL
(since this is a COBOL n/g), and nothing about my business has
changed, except, I've gotten more business.  Do I need to go from
W/3.0 to W/3.1 to W/95 to W/98 to W/me just because M$ put them
out there?

If I am running a printer that works just fine, and I have forms
that meet all my requirements, why do I need to upgrade computers
and/or O/Ses?  So, why should I have to upgrade my accounting
system? [I have a good one for this later on.]

We all know that payroll changes.  It is the nature of the beast
because the US Congress and the several states can't leave things
alone (they have to do some thing to earn their perks and get
contributions to their various political parties).  However, most
of those changes are in the form of tables that must be
upgraded.  So, why would I have to get the latest and greatest
payroll system once I've gotten one that does what I need?

This particular line of thinking is one that IBM has taken --
compatibility.  When they put out a new release of the O/S, the
old code doesn't break just because!  From what I could see, IBM
used this same methodology with OS/2.

So, how did IBM make money on these things?  By something called
license fees.  You paid them monthly (well, actually, quarterly
or better), for the use of their code.  This then covered all the
maintenance costs of your software (hey, we found a new way to
break something so, here is a patch so that you won't stumble
into this).  All the ISVs do the same thing.  

The problem is, with the PC world, this was not taken into
account when software was developed, and so, M$ in order to
generate profits, has to make sure that one release of Office is
not compatible, in some way, with a prior release.  This forces
businesses to upgrade because their customers may get the new
Office, and won't be able to exchange documents as a result.

If you will notice, M$ software does not get better, it gets more
functions/features, which in turn require more Service Packs (or
is that service releases), and for some products, this just means
that M$ puts out a new release period, which you have to buy, to
get the fixes for the bugs in your current release.

I much prefer to pay a license fee on an annual basis for my
software.  I have one product that we are currently not using. 
So we did not renew the license, and on a certain day, it stopped
working (well, I think it has stopped)!  Well, since we aren't
using it, not a problem.  But, if you had to get an annual
license to keep functioning with that software, you'd pay the
fee.

Now, competition comes in.  The competitor to product xxx does
not charge a fee once you have purchased the product.  So, what
will you do?  Most people buy the product with the pay once fee
structure.  Now, they get locked into having to buy the latest
copy every 2-3 years.  

Looking at Windoze, what was the cost for the W/95 Upgrade?  What
was the W/98 Upgrade cost?  And the W/ME Upgrade cost?  Now look
at Office and see what it costs to buy it new, and what it costs
to get the upgrades.

What would it have cost had you been paying an annual
license/maintenance fee?

Now let us go back to the IBM mainframe world.  In 1991 I knew of
a company that was running VM/SP w/ OS/VS1 on a 4381 and had
gotten the 4381 used.  Why?  Because COBOL still worked, JCL was
still functional, they could still get support for their
printers, CMS handled the online users just fine.  All of this
for the cost of electricity and A/C -- since their software was
not supported by IBM, they weren't paying any fees!  That changed
when they had to upgrade for Y2K -- I hear they went to NT
systems with COBOL because it was cheaper than the upgrade to
OS/390!

In the business world, particularly manufacturing, you don't
replace a machine just because it is old.  You replace it because
it is too expensive to keep it running (e.g., maintenance), or,
because you need some new function/feature that can't be put on
the machine in the field and the two machines can't co-exist
(e.g., not enough room for both).

So, since we all know that software wears out ;-) , what do we do
to make/earn money on what is essentially a turn key situation?

Now, add to this the problem with piracy (or a perceived piracy
problem), and what decisions do the immature IT people bring to
the table (I say immature because most of the PC only people do
not have the experience with multiple platforms that we got in
the '60s-'80s with DEC, Honeywell, Univac, etc.)?  Ones that only
make use of a billing idea that they know and understand.

Hopefully I've given some food for thought here that can be taken
to Fujitsu (and others) to stop this nonsense.  And, something
that will get us all to think about the software that we develop
and why people would want it (and its upgrades).
```
