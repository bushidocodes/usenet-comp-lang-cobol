Please understand that while CLC is a fun and interesting place to be (for
the most part), it is a "backwater" in terms of what is happening in the
mainstream computing World. The majority of people here are more interested
in "fortress COBOL" than in emerging technology and, as a result, are often
unaware of trends in the industry which are tunnelling under the fortress...

Yes, if you use components, then the total space occupied by them will be
equal to the space occupied by each one, multiplied by the number of
components you have....(Duuuuh!) So what? The same formula applies to any
code you have.

>
> Nobody is controlling this -

On the contrary, it is being extremely well controlled in organisations
which are utilizing this technology. There are superb software packages
available for exactly that purpose and they give complete statistics on
where any given component is used throughout the Enterprise, as well as a
complete "component inventory". Your statement indicates that you haven't
done your homework on this Jimmy.

If you mean there is no equivalent to the ANSI Alphabet soup that has done
so much to constipate COBOL, you are wrong on that too.

The only part of component technology which NEEDS standardizing is the
interface. ActiveX is MS proprietary (an extension to their COM) but is OPEN
in the sense that it can be downloaded, and this is the standard. ANYONE can
implement this interface WITHOUT requiring permission from MS or paying a
licence fee.

The Component Object Group provides the same facility for CORBA.

So the "control" you refer to comes down to the individual organization
implementing the technology.

Many Universities are now teaching Component Based Design.

Please visit http://www.flashline.com

Spend some time and browse the site. You may be surprised, not only at what
is available (in terms of component inventory control software) but at the
reactions and comments from Organizations picking up this technology.

COBOL will be dragged kicking and screaming into this scenario  only after
it embraces OO.

(As discussed elsewhere here, COBOL is being dragged kicking and screaming
into OO and there is still fierce resistance to this methodology from
"fortress COBOL"... so what hope for COBOL based components?

I am the ONLY person I know of who is writing them and using them with other
components. When I talk to other component authors they are amazed that I am
using COBOL. Yet it is ideal for some types of component. (BTW, if anyone
reading this is also using COBOL to produce ActiveX or COM components,
PLEASE drop me a line...it's lonely out here on the frontier...<G>))

 I believe it will probably be too late by then for COBOL to make any
significant contribution even though I still believe COBOL is the best tool
for many kinds of job.

The resistance to change and the attitude of COBOL programmers, coupled with
the kind of mentality that believes it is OK to take 17 years to produce a
standard, is sounding the death knell for COBOL as anything other than a
Batch processing language, or a mainframe CICS support language. It seems it
will never make the leap into Client/Server (the COBOL User base for
Client/Server is less than 7% of the total COBOL User base).

This is a tragedy as far as I'm concerned. I feel like someone who is
clinging to his Betamax while the World goes VHS... <G>

The Vendors have provided excellent implementations of COBOL for
Client/Server but even enterprises using COBOL as their mainframe language
have refused to pick them up. And when you look at the posts here, it isn't
hard to see why...

> when somebody latches on that there should be a
> committee (Uggh !) - it might be just too late to put things
> right ?????
First off, there won't be a committee, and there shouldn't be, other than
the existing ones which are serving the Component User community in a very
satisfactory and timely way.

Secondly, there is nothing to "put right". All the component developers I am
in touch with are perfectly happy with the support being provided in their
language of choice (Usually VB or Delphi, sometimes C++) and both Borland
and Microsoft seem quite happy with the way the Interface standards are
being administered.

As far as Fujitsu PowerCOBOL  (V5) goes, support for component development
is outstanding and I use it all the time.

None of us have any problems getting our components to play nicely together.
(I had a few problems with this in the early days but none currently).

> I'm
> guessing at this - as an example the various vendors produced DB tools;
after
> the fact they probably came together and that's when we got the ANSI/ISO
SQL 92.
> While partially 'tools' these components are 'additional software' units.
>
Bad analogy. SQL was released in 1983. The need for a "Standard" was
recognised and the standard was attained in 1992 as you note. There was no
need for a standard until the late eighties because there was resistance to
RDB technology (particularly from an entrenched IMS base on the mainframes)
and the only credible implementation of it was DB2. By the late eighties
this changed and other "Relational Database Systems" became available on
other platforms. This has led to the proliferation we see today (and it is
still expanding))

SQL NEEDED a standard for it to work across platforms.

Components only need a STANDARD interface. They have one, and new releases
of it appear in response to user feedback and vendor enhancement driven by
market forces. It works the way COBOL SHOULD work if it wasn't bogged down
in it's own bureaucracy and resistance to change.

I realise that what I've posted above is not what most people here would
want to hear, but you asked for observations and these are mine. From the
heart.

Pete.
