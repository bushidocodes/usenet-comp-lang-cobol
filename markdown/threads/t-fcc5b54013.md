I haven't seen the aritcle you refered to, but it sounds as though it
is talking about the new object-oriented features that came with the
COBOL/370 (or COBOL for MVS and VM, depending on IBM's word flavor of
the month) compiler. By reviewing the postings in this newsgroup, the
new features hasn't quite reached the COBOL communitiy. Heck, we're
still arguing over the value of GO TO's and SECTIONS vs. PERFORMS.

Don't get me wrong. I would love to learn the new features. The IBM
manual falls short in teaching the subject, there is no after-market
books to teach oneself, and thrid-party courses that's now comming on
line cost thousands of dollars making tight-waded bosses balk. I
suspect it would be a decade before the new features become the norm
when new graduating students enter the workforce and chafe against the
existing methods of development (look how long it took to have
structured programming widely accepted, almost a generation). By
which time, most potential employers would be buying third-party
packages and deciding there is no need for in-house programmers other
then those needed for maintaining the package for lot less money.

Just my two cents worth, adjusted for inflation

Many of the "information hiding" type claims require the use of
"contained" or "nested" program structures. With this structure,
subroutines are embedded in a mainline (good performance), but you can
hide data from the subs, and limit what they access.


Unfortunately...

1. many 3rd party COBOL add-ons did not support these structures when
intruduced in 1986 (and some still do not).

2. Average COBOL Programmer does not understand the constructs, so they
tend not to be used.

Perhaps 50% or more of IBM mainframe COBOL is still on OS/VS COBOL (a 68 /
74 hybrid compiler), so none of these constructs are present... (makes
lots of business for me teaching how to get to the new technology).

Hope this helps.
