Sure.

Amazon recently revised its privacy policy, announcing that they would
obtain information about their customers from other sources, that they
would share that information with their business partners, and that
the data collected is viewed as a business asset and would be treated
as such (e.g., sold) under certain circumstances. Further, there does
not seem to be any way to get your private data purged from the Amazon
system.

Here, for example, is an e-mail from a large California bookstore:

---- Copy follows

Dear Patrons,

Some Amazon.com customers got a jolt this last week when the company
revised its privacy policy.  Along with the expected phone numbers,
credit card numbers, email addresses, and delivery addresses, Amazon
now
states that it also collects other information including social
security
numbers and driver's license numbers, that it compiles permanent
records of every search each customers does on its website, and that
it
sometimes buys even more information on its customers from third party
marketing research firms.  In addition, Amazon is now sending out
marketing email (known as spam to the rest of us) on behalf of other
companies with which it has a business relationship.

In response to Amazon.com's statement and to a number of customer
inquiries, we thought it time for us to put in writing the policy to
which Just Books has always adhered:

We believe that your private information is private.  We use the
information you provide us (addresses, phone numbers, credit cards)
only
to fill your orders.  We have not and will not share that information
with any third party.

We do not spam.  We do not add anyone to our mailing lists without
permission, and we do not send mailings on behalf of third parties.

We would never stoop so low as to BUY information about our customers.
As to what Amazon thinks it needs with social security numbers and
income profiles, we respectfully decline to comment but assure you,
Just
Books will never ask you for such information or acquire it in any
other
way.

If you'd like to read the privacy statement we've just added to our
website, follow this link:
http://www.justbooks.org/page3.htm or
http://www.justbook.com/page3.htm

--- End copy

Further, an article in Publisher's Weekly (e-mail edition of 9/6/00)
explained further:

--- Begin copy

Oh Big Brother!: Alarmists Slam Amazon Spam Plan

Amid growing public concern over the security of information collected
by
Internet retailers, Amazon.com has encountered particular scrutiny.
Thus,
late last week, when Amazon.com released a revised privacy policy, the
press and others feared that Amazon's customer information would be
shared
freely with third parties. After a closer look, it appears that the
new
privacy policy does not abrogate customer's rights so severely--but
opens
the door for some massive transfers of information.

The new policy explains that Amazon specifically tracks "the Internet
protocol (IP) address used to connect your computer to the Internet;
login;
e-mail address; password; computer and connection information such as
browser type and version, operating system, and platform; purchase
history;
the full Uniform Resource Locators (URL) clickstream to, through, and
from
our Web site, including date and time; cookie number; products you
viewed
or searched for; zShops you visited; your Auction history, and phone
number
used to call our 800 number."

Certainly, this array of information is astounding, but what some
papers
have found most worrisome is the addition of a clause that explains
"in the
unlikely event that Amazon.com, Inc., or substantially all of its
assets
are acquired, customer information will of course be one of the
transferred
assets."

More amazing is how Amazon defines "transferable asset." This includes
"your name, address, and phone number; credit card information; people
to
whom purchases have been shipped, including address and phone number;
people (with addresses and phone numbers) listed in Gift-Click or
1-Click
settings; e-mail addresses of Trusted Friends and other people;
content of
reviews and e-mails to us; personal description and photograph in
About
You; and financial information, including Social Security and driver's
license numbers." But not your blood type.

Some critics have claimed that there is no longer an ability to
"opt-out"
of this sharing of information. But unless Amazon itself is sold
(which
appears unlikely, at least until the German juggernaut Bertelsmann
gets
bored backing B&N.com), it is not entirely true.

Users cannot opt out of sharing their information with companies in
partnership with Amazon, such as www.drugstore.com, or those that
assist
Amazon in running their day-to-day business, including companies that
work
"fulfilling orders, delivering packages, sending postal mail and
e-mail,
removing repetitive information from customer lists, analyzing data,
providing marketing assistance, processing credit card payments, and
providing customer service." (Excuse us while we get ready to answer
the
computer-driven sales calls�)

Amazon.com clearly states that those companies have access to personal
information needed to perform their functions but may not use it for
other
purposes, such as cloning. Yet all customers do have the option to
change
their Customer Communication Preferences to opt-out. Otherwise, the
site
says that customers will be informed whenever information will be
shared
with third parties and will again be given the opportunity to opt-out.

The most likely result of this adjustment to Amazon's privacy policy
may
not be a loss of customer loyalty but increased traffic as customers
return
to the site to re-set their preferences in order to prevent unwanted
Spam.
Who knows, the extra traffic could lead to spontaneous sales, perhaps
of
some of Ralph Nader's consumer rights titles.


--- End copy
