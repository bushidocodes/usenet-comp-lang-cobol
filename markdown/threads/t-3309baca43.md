This confirms what I was coming to suspect... "religious" , at least in this

1. The city was Varanasi in India. In English it is called Benares and is 
quite a remarkable place. The Buddha  (Gautama Siddhartha) is alleged to 
have sat there for forty days and nights (where have we heard that before?) 
vowing not to move until he either attained enlightenment or died. He used a 
tree for shade and the descendant of that tree is there to this day. (It 
gets very well looked after :-))

I have visited the city and it is very interesting and colourful. It is a 
Holy City to a number of different faiths, not just Buddhists.

The Holy river Ganges runs through the town and it is believed by Hindus to 
have restorative powers. Anyone who dies of leprosy or any other wasting 
disease is put into the river, and it is quite an experience to take a boat 
ride in the early dawn...The fish are very well fed and you can buy large 
specimens in the local marketplace. (You never know, you could be dining in 
the evening on a fish that dined on your Granny in the morning...It is food 
for thought.)

 People who don't need the restorative powers of the Ganges are simply 
cremated on pyres in the open and the smell of burning human flesh is one of 
the ineradicable memories I have from that visit... (I should stress that my 
visit there was 33 years ago and it may have changed much since...)

Many pilgrims take to the river to wash and bathe in its Holy waters. The 
town water supply for the city is taken from the same source.

In the 19th Century, the British decided to clean it up a bit and were 
amazed to find that the water a few hundred yards downstream was perfectly 
clean and potable. The locals had known that the river was restorative for 
thousands of years (that's one reason why it is considered Holy) but the 
British were baffled. It was many years (and a number of detailed surveys) 
before the mystery was solved: The river bed has a large number of fresh 
springs in it which are powerful enough to remove the effluent and replace 
the polluted water.

The usual filth of Asia is present in large doses and I saw rats as big as 
cats (no, I'm NOT exaggerating, I mean it literally). I can understand how 
the Buddha was too exhausted to continue his journey and needed to sit a 
while...

2. Arjuna was the charioteer of Krishna. The discourses between him and the 
incarnation of the Godhead are detailed in the Mahabharata and the subset of 
it, Bhagavad Gita. It is an interesting allegory with the chariot 
representing our senses and emotions and the charioteer needing a firm hand. 
The backdrop of mighty battles with good ultimately triumphing over evil, is 
a fitting setting for the discourses between man and God. Arjuna has all the 
doubts and fears that humans encounter, and his questions to Krishna are the 
kind of questions most of us would like to get answers to, if we had a 
chance to chat with God.
