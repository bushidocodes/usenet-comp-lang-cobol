I have an incoming field, up to eight numeric characters, delimited by
anything non-numeric. I tried figuring how to use INSPECT. I was
expecting something like

INSPECT INPUT-DATE TALLYING K FOR INITIAL NUMERIC
or
INSPECT INPUT-DATE TALLYING K FOR INITIAL "0" OR "1" OR "2" OR "3"
OR "4" OR "5" OR "6" OR "7" OR "8" OR "9"

but there doesn't seem to be any such feature. I tried

PERFORM VARYING K FROM 1 BY 1
UNTIL K > 8 OR INPUT-DATE(K:1) NOT NUMERIC
END-PERFORM
SUBTRACT 1 FROM K

Which gave two syntax errors "Token invalid in this context:
END-PERFORM" followed by "Token END-PERFORM expected, inserted."
So I added "CONTINUE" before END-PERFORM, but got a bounds violation on
INPUT-DATE(K:1). I redefined the input field as an array and ended up
with

PERFORM VARYING K FROM 1 BY 1
UNTIL K > 8 OR INPUT-DATE-CHAR(K) NOT NUMERIC
END-PERFORM
SUBTRACT 1 FROM K

This works, and I suppose I should be happy with it, but shouldn't Cobol
have some construct to do this?

OOH! It's good to be chatting with someone who knows A Series. Let me
tell you my background: I worked for Unisys in the A Series Languages
department. I fixed bugs and even added some features in COBOL(68),
COBOL74, RPG, and the product now called CMT (Cobol Migration Tool).
I reviewed the Gamma revision of the A Series spec for the department.
In retrospect, the thought that no one from Newp, Algol, and Pascal
reviewed the spec is scary.

IOW, yeah, I know it *ought* to be reentrant.

› I'm curious about the COBOL85 compiler.  My A series clients still use the
› COBOL74 compiler, and I would like to encourage them to move up.  However,
…[4 more quoted lines elided]…
› appear to be reliable now?

AFAIK, COBOL85 is pretty stable. But let me add that I've only been
using it for two weeks. Gregory A Series Journal says the main
complaint is speed: COBOL85 compiles take three to four times as long
as COBOL74. The resultant code is as efficient, but when you're waiting
on a compile of your 60,000 line on-line transaction handler (which is
currently down), a slow compiler does not make friends.

My problem is a design conflict between ANSI and reentrant code. ANSI
Cobol-85 requires that data in a nested program remain in its last used
state, unless you specify INITIAL in the PROGRAM-ID. In A Series
pragmatics this means the data is declared at lex level 2. But if the
program is a code library (confusing A Series term) with SHARING of
SHAREDBYALL or DONTCARE, multiple callers can run the exported
procedures at lex level 3 on their own stacks, while referencing the
same data area at lex level 2 on the frozen library stack.

So I added INITIAL to all the PROGRAM-IDs, and I added the $LOCALTEMP
compiler option, and I already had LOCAL on every 01 and 77 declaration,
but no good, the compiler still puts the local data at lex level 2.
Unisys support is not sure if this is a bug or a feature, but informs me
multiple UCFs have been filed on this issue.

The syntax diagram for exporting a library leaves out the SHAREDBYALL,
as though the author knew that it was an invitation for trouble.
Unfortunately, the diagram includes DONTCARE, which is now its synonym.
Once upon a time, DONTCARE meant share by address space. Perhaps now it
means you don't care whether your data is right.

There's a $LIBRARYLOCK option to cause single threading which only works
on a "Cobol74 style" library (a non-nested program which exports it's
one and only PROCEDURE DIVISION). They tell me there are multiple UCFs
saying that this option ought to apply as well to a "Cobol85 style"
library (a nested program, where the outer block exports some programs).

Meanwhile, the answer is to lock out simultaneous access manually.
In WORKING-STORAGE in the outer block, declare a lock

01 MY-GLOBAL-LOCK LOCK GLOBAL.

(Note that the LOCK data type is far more efficient than the EVENT data
type if you don't need the other features of EVENT.)

Then in every exported procedure LOCK MY-GLOBAL-LOCK at the beginning,
and UNLOCK MY-GLOBAL-LOCK just before you EXIT PROGRAM. There's an
unfortunate side-effect of this, none of the exported procedures can
call each other. In my case I exported three procedures, where two of
them called the third. I had to add a jacket procedure for export.

BTW, Judson, is there an A Series newsgroup? I've never seen one on the
newsgroups of a cross-posted message. If not, can we start one?

Your experience with the A Series is more intimate than mine. I 'cut my
teeth' on the Burroughs Medium Systems (Unisys V Series) starting with the
B2500/3500 in 1968. I worked for Burroughs as a senior software tech in
'72-'74. I helped my last V Series client convert to the A in '93. Two of
my primary clients have A's, and I've been programming on the A Series for
about 10 years, though I was exposed to the B6700/7700 a bit when I worked
for Burroughs. I'm one of the few programmers around here who can read an A
Series memory dump. The A is the only computer I am familiar with which has
never (to my knowledge) had an assembler, even back to the B5500. As you
probably know, the original B5500 MCP and ALGOL compiler were cross compiled
from another system.

It's interesting to note the difference in philosophy between the V and A
series computers. Like COBOL, the V series (actually, the B2500/3500) was a
product of the 'practical' school, and developed specifically as a COBOL
machine. Everything about the V was simple and practical, and the customers
loved them. Numbers and addresses, even instructions, were in decimal, so
reading memory dumps was a breeze. Too bad the architecture ran out of
steam. The A series was a product of the academic community, and it shows.
It is much less simple and convenient than the V, though more powerful and
flexible. I like COMS and DMSII, though a DASDL reorg (like most things) is
less convenient on the A than the V. Do you remember having to call DMS with
a COMPUTE because the compiler didn't have DMS syntax? :-)

I suppose you have already considered and rejected running multiple copies of
the same program? In my experience (all COBOL74), as long as you are careful
with your record locking, you can make that work fine on the A, though we
usually only have two or three copies running at once.

I don't know of an A Series newsgroup, but that would be nice.

Essentially correct, there has never been an assembler on A Series, but
even the cross-compiler was written on an assembler-free system. The
Burroughs B5000, built in 1961 was the first computer built for a high
level language: ALGOL. The very first ALGOL compiler was self
compiled. They wrote the compiler and then spent weeks compiling it by
hand and punching a binary deck. Then they tried to self compile it.
Of course it was buggy, so they spent months patching that object until
they finally produced an ALGOL which passed triple compile.

Many years later the B5500 was developed as a successor to the B5000.
The first MCP and the first ALGOL compiler for the B5500 were compiled
with the XALGOL compiler on the B5000. The modern A Series is directly
descended from the B5500 just as IBM S/390 is descended from S/360.
