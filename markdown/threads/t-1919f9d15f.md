I originally posted this message to ibm-main@bama.ua.edu (can bee read
as newsgroup bit.listserv.ibm-main) in response to a thread on
outsourcing to India and other countries.  The only response I got was
from Canada agreeing with me and noting that the privacy laws are
already in effect for financial institutions.  Since this is an
international forum for application programmers, I would be interested
in comments on these issues, especially the one about test data and the
whole issue of confidentiality.

As a United States citizen living and contracting in Canada, in one
sense I would be on both sides of the fence since I might be
undercutting my fellow United States citizens while in turn being
undercut by those from other countries.  Rather than going into the
those issues there are some other issues that long term are of great
importance and being overlooked in the debate I have seen thus far. 
There are issues of balancing market and production, differing laws on
privacy, on intellectual rights and on contract rights.  The concerns
apply whether we are talking about work being outsourced from the United
States to India or India to the United States.  In the area of privacy,
European Union citizens might well be concerned about outsourcing to
United States because of the lack of privacy protection.  This
discussion is meant to stimulate thought and the examples will be given
in terms of United States and Canadian legal framework purely because
those are the areas with which I am the least unfamiliar.  

For people to decry IBM or Microsoft setting up major centers of
production in India is to ignore that India is a major market for both
companies and there is a corporate need and responsibility to have a
local presence.  In addition companies that broaden their base by
integrating various cultural backgrounds are increasing their abilities
to expand intellectually.  

When I note that the legal system in a foreign country is different and
that there may be unpleasant surprises, this is to note the obvious. 
People in the United States have been unpleasantly surprised by the
differences between the states.  The majority of the states of the US
and provinces of Canada have legal systems based on British common law
but at least one state - Louisiana and 1 province - Quebec have laws
based on the French legal system.  The interaction between national and
regional laws can be a minefield in many federal countries.  Of special
concern is the protection of intellectual rights.  This can be a
minefield in many countries including the United States.  While various
toy companies that outsourced their production to China have complained
about their products being copied, I suspect that this is not just a
first world/third world issue.  In addition, many courts are aggressive
about claiming jurisdiction over actions in foreign countries when
citizens or companies within the court's jurisdiction are involved.  The
United States and Spain both come to mind.  Even without this factor
agreement on what rules apply can be tricky.  In addition, with data or
information, it may be an unpleasant surprise in any country to find out
what information the government has access to.  This is equally true of
the United States exacerbated by the Patriot Acts as it is about any of
the other countries. 

Privacy may actually become murkier with call centers outsourced across
borders even if the data bases reside within the outsourcing country. 
Which country's privacy laws apply?  Does the country of the call center
have the right to access the data to enforce their laws?  Given the
cross border scams that are reputed to originate in Canada aimed at the
United States as reported by the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation and
the Toronto, Ontario Canada newspaper Globe and Mail, this again is not
just a first world/third world issue.  If software maintenance is
outsourced, how is the test data created?  If there is a production
problem is the relevant data anonymized before being given to fixing
company?  Can a system that deals with sensitive data be maintained
without the maintainers having access to sensitive data?  I remember an
excerpt from the hilarious appendix to the Westinghouse Fast Dump
Restore manual about their problem determination section getting a large
(then paper) dump of an abend which contained data regarding the sending
company's secret project on fillet of old gnu.  While the example was
stated that way to show the principle rather than identify specific
companies, the problem is real.  Abends may involve or expose sensitive
information.  The problem exists even if both the outsourcing company
and the company doing the work are in the same jurisdiction.  It gets
trickier when state or provincial borders are crossed and even more so
when national borders are involved.  Of course, if the data is for a
company that is operating in both jurisdictions anyway and is accessed
in those
jurisdictions (Visa cards anyone?), then this would be a non-issue. 
Note this may be a case where your data is safer in Canada or the
European Union.  A privacy activist is reported to have purchased the
social security numbers (default national identifiers in the United
States) of key legislators on a California legislative committee.  He
then published parts of those numbers (4 digits for each number if I
recall correctly) to point out the problem and make the point about lack
of protection to these legislators in a way that hit home.  The
legislators' response apparently is to have him investigated for trying
to intimidate the committee.  There will be privacy laws in effect
January 1, 2004 in Canada.  The safeguard and requirements are better
than those in effect in the United States.  However exceptions for
government access carved out by laws enacted in response to the New York
World Trade Center attacks makes data as vulnerable to quiet government
access as it would be in the United States. 

I believe that much of the outsourcing that has been done may have
exposed companies to unanticipated risks even if cross border issues
were not involved.  The risks are far less when you are just purchasing
hardware and software because you are not exposing company information
to outside parties. Of course with the call home capability in much of
today's hardware and software, there are potential security issues. 
Should the military establishment of India trust hardware and software
produced and maintained in the United States?  Given the history of the
relations between the two countries, I would be leery if I were in their
shoes and if I were a member of the United States military, I would have
an equal suspicion of software and hardware from India.

Outsourcing of anything increases risk because control is not as great. 
Doing it across borders increases the risk because the legal systems
differ and the responsibilities to governments differ.  Both parties
have a greater risk.  Just read the Wall Street Journal (major United
States business newspaper) for the perceived risks to business in some
states because of the court system.  For those who own shares in
companies, it might be worthwhile asking pointed questions of your
managements about these issues.  My personal belief is that much IT
outsourcing is because the communication between IT departments and even
between say systems and applications was poor or stilted anyway.  Of
course outsourcing in general may in part be due to many businesses
being divided up into little fiefdoms that don't work together well
anyway.
