### Cobol, the legacy

```
Back in the early 80's when I did my coding in
Assembler, I had the attitude that Cobol is not
a real computing language simply because it
has to go through many other translations and
it lacks the cryptic mystery of a binary machine.
With the shift of the U. S. economy, the proliferation
of PC's and its graphical user interface, and the
more accepted practice of structured programming,
Cobol has become the only computer language
that closely resembles its human counterpart of
English and is the most frequently used computing
instructions that direct business information in many
larger shops in America.

Some people complain that Cobol can be, at times,
verbose and it lacks the robust efficiency of lower level
machine language. The absence of arcane and esoteric
flavor allows non-practitioners to wade into the field of
exclusive association among computing brahmans,
thus polluting the environment, and adulterating the
professional ethics of real artists and practitioners.

Call it a legacy or call it a dying dinosaur. I call it a
practical approach to making a living. It's much easier
than working under the hot tropical sun and under the
guns of the Khmer Rouge in Cambodia. Since the early
80's, Cobol has provided me a steady flow of income to
pay my bills. Through the medium of Cobol, I've come
to learn the different concepts of business applications
such as insurance, banking, finance, and distribution.
Just before last year, during the Y2K bridge to the new
millennium, Cobol had been re-discovered by mainframe
enthusiasts and big time decision makers that it has
remained the main driver of many complex applications
in American business and industry.

With the financial collapse of e-commerce and the
reluctance of investors to throw in new money for
dot.com companies to expand their business operations,
Cobol has once again learned to survive the onslaught
of pc's novelty, glittering glamour of the web, and its
own abend-aids. With its new interface to relational
database, its usual coupling for online transactions of
CICS, Cobol is still the language of business computing
in most larger shops in America.

The last time I had the opportunity to use EVALUATE/
END-EVALUATE, in-line performed paragraphs, and
above the clouds storage allocation, I felt the glimpse
of eternal happiness to see that God had breathed new
life into Cobol over the years. In spite of the bad rap that
Cobol has got from its new brethren that used to be
window washers, coffee makers, and garage mechanics,
it's the computing language that has the semantics of
rich flavor and long tradition of values and proved ethical
practices.

I've made my living with Cobol. I've paid my bills,
supported the family, and raised the kids. But most
certainly, I've learned the business of living in a more
complex society such as America. Throughout the
years, I've come to meet interesting people in different
departments of the corporations, hobnob with business
managers at many levels, and exchange several long
life-threatening conversations with production control
operators. As of today, several thousands of executable
Cobol load modules are still running the business of
America. Contrary to the point of views of its opponents,
Cobol will not be dying in any immediate future. Some
20 years ago, I was told that Cobol was dying. As I left
the office this evening, I saw people were fine tuning their
Cobol codes and migrating their load modules to the
production environment. 
 
Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, we're here to share
and exchange the information about the vehicle of our
survival. I know through conversation with consultants,
head hunters, and simple folks across the streets in
America that Cobol programmers are out of work.
After putting some long years into the practices of
Cobol coding, people are getting laid off, summarily
dismissed, and being treated as a useless piece of
furniture.

Today, after more than 20 years later, I can assure
you that Cobol is still alive and kicking. The realities,
however, are challenging. Cobol is facing competitive
pressure from all sides. Just like someone has
suggested in this newsgroup, bring down the price
of Cobol compiler, borrow some features of Visual
Basic, and make it more portable across various
platforms, then we can be sure that competitive
threat will only create more challenging opportunities
for Cobol folks over the long run.

It has been an outstanding legacy. In Richardson,
Texas, roads are named after the legacy. Let's work
to make it last. Just like an old wine, I hope that
Cobol will continue to improve its flavor and taste
with the passing of time. Have Cobol will certainly
travel. And that's not the end of story.

Chim
```

Consider me a Y2K baby.  I was a network administrator and totally PC
oriented when our organization ran out of programmers and began
recruiting.  I'd always been captivated by the idea of being a
"programmer" and jumped at the chance and learned COBOL fast and on the
fly.  When the dust settled hundreds of people went elsewhere, not
necessarily goodwhere; I was fortunate enough that I'd done well enough
that I'm still working and learning and I really give a shit but also
don't give a shit about what people say about good-bye to COBOL, because
it's here and lasting.

Last week I was transferred.  Now I'm learning PowerBuilder and Sybase
and all this cool stuff, and it's really exciting and back to PC, so I
feel like a duck just landed, but  the tradeoff is the user front-end
for the programmer's backside.  The "English-like" aspects of COBOL will
never be equaled by any other language, and its (COBOL's) power remains.

Now we do open server, and guess what? after all the GUI and shit for
syntax, the gateway falls upon COBOL.
