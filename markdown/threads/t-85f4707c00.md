a tent with plenty of Gin) in India and tried to make sense of it all.

heretofore asides were taken into consideration:

1. The kingdoms of Israel and Judea started their years at different
times: one at Rosh Hashanna (in the fall) and the other at Passover
(in the Spring).

counted as a 'year' and sometimes not.

In Truth it can be argued, and I emphasize, has been argued, that zero never
existed, does not exist and will never exist. And 'though such anti-nihility
seem infinitely silly to us in our culture, as diverse as our intillectual

Most modern text that will get into the issue seem to indicate that the Greeks
in fact had encountered the notion of zero, or nothingness, but they did not
trust it.

Which I suppose should be logged as the greatest of paranoia - to be affraid of
nothing, you see.

The gist of the historical arguement, per the Greeks, was that they could
easily have had a zero-cipher, but felt no particular need for a symbol to
represent that which they did not believe to be real.

All the exploiters of the Semitic alphabet system which led to the Greek
alphabet, actually did have a zero-cipher even though moderns and ancients
would insist otherwise! They used the Semitic Alphabet in a positional
notation. This is not so obvious at first since the set of nine ciphers for
each non-zero value would be different as you increased in magnitude (until the
27 had been exhausted, and then they would recycle the characterset).  

You see, zero would be represented by the absence of any cipher at all in the
position. The blank spot would be closed up (a kind of kerning if you don't
mind the light-hearted treatment). Thus the positional absence of the cipher
_WAS_ the symbolic representation of zero. And it was the same for each
potential position.

It is not so strange, really. Consider modern marketting moniker like
"bellsouth" and "earthlink". Or the easily grokked middle node of this web
address
 www. economictechnology.com
In all of these the characted-nil, or blank is vacumed out of there, and we can
still get it.

Naturally, Judson's, original comments are much broader than my meager focus on

So the concept of zero was surely there quite early, as transparent as the
