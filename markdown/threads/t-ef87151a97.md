Wendy:

I don't know what validating technique problem you are referring to,
so you will have to be more specific. The only obvious ones I see
are the hard-coded date range limitations. Since I am well over 16
years of age, it choked on my birthdate!

As for your problem with writing only the one record you speak of, the
problem is that you do not issue a CLOSE to the file before issuing
the STOP RUN. You are loosing the last record because of this. You
should PERFORM 300-END-OF-JOB-RTN prior to STOP-RUN.

I was curious about your use of STOP-RUN. This is treated as a data
name, as the correct use is STOP RUN. A typo, perhaps?

Mike Dodas

David, David, David:

You are incorrct on every point you make.

1. STOP-RUN is not a valid Cobol verb. IT IS STOP RUN.

2. Placing statements in Margin A when they should be in Margin B
produce only warning messages, at least for most "non-lame"
compilers. A positional problem is nothing more than esthetic.

3. The program, as it is supplied, does write the record only one
time. Some of the code was commented out and I was not about to
second-guess her reasons for this. The program, if I remember,
terminates after entering 1 record. If you took the time to read the
question from the the poster more carefully, you might realize this was
not the problem. Not closing the file before issuing a STOP RUN will
cause problems. Perhaps some compilers on some platforms deal with a
missing CLOSE. The fact that some do not (IBM mainframes), you should
ALWAYS close all file.

Did you just make a quick, visual opinion of the source code Wendy
submitted. I didn't. I compiled, linked and ran it through my debugger
to come up with my conclusions (after I changed STOP-RUN to STOP RUN).
If you're going to give advice to other people and criticize someone
else's remarks, particularly individuals who are trying to learn, do
everyone a favor and know a little bit about what you're saying.

My recommendation to you: Go back to school and take a refresher course.
