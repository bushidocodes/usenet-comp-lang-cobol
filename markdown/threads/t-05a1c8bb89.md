the compiler to anyone who wants it. Binaries are available, from the
FAQ:

----------------------------------
1.5   Are there pre-built OpenCOBOL packages

Yes. Debian APT, and RPM packages exist. Packages for NetBSD. Many.
Google opencobol packages for any late breaking news.

A Debian Advanced Package Tool binary package exists for OpenCOBOL 1.0
as open-cobol and lists dependencies of

    * libc6 (>= 2.7-1),
    * libcob1,
    * libcob1-dev (= 1.0-1),
    * libdb4.5 (>= 4.5.20-3),
    * libdb4.5-dev,
    * libgmp3-dev,
    * libgmp3c2,
    * libltdl3-dev,
    * libncurses5 (>= 5.6+20071006-3)

Thanks to the gracious efforts of Bart Martens, bartm on Debian's .org
domain.
1.5.1   kiska.net repository

Also check out kiska.net for binary builds on various platforms.
Thanks to Sergey Kashyrin.
-----------------------------------

language, that was not intended for free distribution. (c.f.  Java, C#, 
etc.). A (small) team of enthusiasts devoted thousands of hours to providing 
a free Open Source version of a language they loved and are committed to. 
With the tiny resources available (compared to other languages and 
platforms) they simply can't provide everything for everybody. The hope is 
that others will seize the torch and add value to their efforts. 
Unfortunately, this doesn't seem to be happening, but that is nobody's 
fault. Not yours, not theirs, not the rest of the COBOL community's.

It is a simple matter of commercial reality. And the reality is that nobody 
has a pressing need for a free version of COBOL, apart from hobbyists, and 
nostalgic Old Timers.

The people who are using COBOL commercially have been spending money on it 
for so long that they are used to doing that and have no problem with 
coughing up for the latest version of the compiler (whether they actually 
need it or not - I am staggered by the mails I get from people who are 
unhappy about paying for a .NET COBOL compiler, but buy it anyway (because 
they are told they should). Invariably, they don't need it, because they are 
moving off COBOL and they can run their legacy code perfectly well on .NET 
by adding a dozen or so lines of code to it...Surprisingly (or maybe 
not...), there are no refunds or even credit against other products/tools; 
you buy it, you got it.)

Small wonder that the COBOL market is shrinking. (Apart from the fact that 
it implements an outmoded paradigm based on centralized processing, it is 
probably the most expensive language to develop and maintain computer 
systems in, so more and more people are looking for alternatives. At least 
there ARE alternatives now, and some of them are far better than COBOL...)

OC is an attempt to make a boutique version of what was once the world's 
most popular programming language. It is a noble effort, but for people like 
yourself who have chosen a "minority" platform, it simply can't accommodate 
you unless you make a lot more (directed) effort than you have shown to 
date.

I can understand your decision to drop it.

QUOTE:

"I can only conclude that COBOL users have no interest in getting new users 
to learn the language."

UNQUOTE

Why would it be the responsibility of COBOL users to encourage you or anyone 
else to use the language? They are not on commission. People use a computer 
programming language because it meets their needs, not because everyone else 
is using it or it has a high "friends" count on Facebook. Sure, people who 
share an interest often like to discuss things (this forum is a case in 
point) but if you were expecting to be wooed and courted into using Open 
COBOL that seems a bit unrealistic. Most of the sites still using COBOL are 
doing so because they need to. Nobody likes being in a position where they 
need to do something (we prefer to have options...) So the people working on 
these sites have employment guaranteed for the immediate future, but there 
is a shadow over it and, in the long term these jobs will disappear. Would 
you encourage a young person looking for a career to get into COBOL? No? 
Neither would many of us.

BOTTOM LINE:

1. You were looking for a free version of COBOL that would run on a platform 
which has less than 6% of the market.
2.  While this was not impossible, there was a degree of effort required to 
make it happen.
3.  You decided the effort involved was more that you were prepared to make. 
Fair enough. Your call. But don't blame the COBOL people for not persuading 
you or specifically trying to meet your needs.
4. In the broad scheme of things (and this is the most important thing of 
all, more important than choice of platform or difficulties with Unix, or 
any technical problems...) your students will NOT suffer if you don't cover 
COBOL in your course.

Pete.
