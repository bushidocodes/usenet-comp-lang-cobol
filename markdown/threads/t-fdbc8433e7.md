Personally, I think the demand for packages, as opposed to in-house
programmmers, will accelerate. Management will think "if these COBOL
programmers missed something as simple as two digits, what else is
wrong?" Doesn't matter if the reasoning is rational, management today
are more "human resource service" types and doesn't want to be
bothered with technical jargon. Thus they don't want to deal with
technical people. They want "customer relations."

When I first work at my present position ten years ago and built the
department's application, I was part of a three-man crew consisting of
a technical manager, a co-worker and myself; all focused on the
mainframe. Over time, I've seen the manager position disapear, the
co-worker position split off to another department, and my position
redefined and marginalized to just keeping the mainframe application I
built afloat until the LAN-based replacement package takes over
completely (been trying to for almost three years now). When I talk
to current management to explain a mainframe feature, I almost always
see smoke comming out of their ears because they really can't relate
to the mainframe with its green-on-black VTAM screen. Management
brings in an outside director (with no data processing experience or
the business the data serves mind you) whom I see once a week
complaining about the "lack of progress" I and the LAN administrator
is making in the conversion process (never mind the data was keyed in
the replacement package without any external validation for
correctness in the first place). The LAN administrator is learning
the data in the replacement package and making system corrections to
make the package's totals more like the mainframe's. And I just sit
here and take it all in.

Why haven't I quit? Loyalty I quess. It's rare for a programmer to
stick to an application throughout its life cycle. At least I can say
on my resume I did so.

Just my two cents worth; adjusted for inflation,

Boyce G. Williams, Jr.
