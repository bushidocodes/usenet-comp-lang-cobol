After having been in this business for better than 25 years, I have
(like many of you) seen a lot. One thing that comes to mind of late is
the thought that, while we may be raking in (as an industry) big bucks
to clean-up code/files to accomodate the new Millenia, what happens if
it doesn't work? What happens if you "screw up" ? What happens if you
didn't screw up, but it is perceived that you did and now the client is
either wounded badly, or worse, fatally by the failure? I would advise
anyone who is entering into a contractual agreement with a client, to be
certain that they have had a lawyer look over the contract and, if need
be, amend the contract to limit the burden of liability on the
consultant/contractor..
For many years I developed Financial Process applications such as TX,
GL, AP, AR, FA and PR. In one of my many conversations with my clients
(generally corporate controllers or VP's), I casually asked about
liability. What would happen if your company were to be audited and
found to be so far off in tax withholding that the penalty and interest
would cripple the company? You would be sued, I was told. Not a very
comfortable feeling. Ever since, I have taken steps to limit my
personal liability - Generally under the shelter of a corporation,
sometimes by negotiated contract.

Just a thought...

Later Gators.. Randy

Being incorporated helps in limiting your liability. General
liability and Errors and Ommissions Insurance also help.

But, in the 18yrs that I've been programming, the last 13yrs as a
contractor, I've never seen anybody sued for writing bad code and
because of this, never seen a clear use for GenLiab insurance or E&O
insurance.

I've seen recruiters, consultants, and consulting firms get sued for
breech of contract in terms such as working or hiring someone who had
a "no compete" clause in the contract of their prior business
relationship. I've also seen consulting firms sue clients for
non-payment. But I can't think of anytime that a client has sued a
consultant or consulting firm for not fulfilling a contract (but, I'm
sure there are such lawsuits).

Most consultants, consulting firms avoid fixed bid contracts, and
fixed bid contracts are probably more furtle ground for lawsuits than
non-fixed bids. In fact, the consulting firm that I know of that sued
a client for non-payment, was mainly over whether the relationship was
for a fixed price or not. The contract specifically said that it was
NOT a fixed price arrangement and detailed how and why the stated
price could change. And being one of the contractors on that
contract, the 50 programs to change quickly mushrooms to over 200 and
that simple db change proved to be impossible and had to be
re-analysed. The consulting firm won the suit.

But lawsuits are very unpleasant for all involved, and both tend to
try and avoid lawsuits. The press coverage for a client can be very
embarrasing. Which is also why many employee embezzlers are only
fired and not charged.

If a conpany goes to the extreme of sueing a consultant or consulting
firm, they might be hard pressed to find other consultants to work for
them for fear of similiar treatment.

Blacklisting and blackballing are very common practices for bad
programming and probably will always be the unwritten law of the
industry.

For several years now, several of my clients have required that I carry $1M
of general liability insurance. When I first approached my agent about
this, he told me that the company would not insure me if I called myself a
"consultant". However, they would if I called myself a "computer
programmer/analyst". The difference is that the PA writes code according
to the clients specifications (technically supervised), whereas the
"consultant" might be relied upon to recommend business strategy or
solution.

Like you, I've never heard of a contractor being sued for writing bad code.
But that doesn't mean that it couldn't happen. I would be more fearful of
such if my client were a small, family owned operation instead of a large
corporation.
