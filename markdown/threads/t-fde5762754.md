Short answer is $75/hour W2 is fine.  Maybe a little low for a high cost
of living area but decent.   

The long answer is more interactive.

Is it just batch COBOL or does it include DB2, IMS, CICS, VSAM, etc?
Are you turning the crank on a remediation mill, looking for dates, 
ISPF'ing the source, and F3'ing or does the job include business logic 
analysis and an understanding of MVS as well.

I once spent a horrid two weeks figuring out how CA7 works, this was 
after attending a 3 day class on CA7.  What is it about mainframe 
software that the documention always answers questions that I don't 
have... the exceptions are the Principles of Operation, the PL/I 
Language Reference Manual, the Assember VS manual, and a couple others.

Turns out that CA7 is poorly documented.

Are you expected to have puzzled out these life mysteries?  It didn't 
sound like it in this case but sometimes it makes a difference if the
programmer's background is a match for the environment.   ...as always, 
it's better to have a sharp programmer than a dull acronym collector.

Is the rate W2 w/ benefits or w/o benefits.  Some people have benefits 
packages that are worth tens of thousands of dollars a year, or more.

If it's w/o benefits, that's still an OK rate, just not as good.

What do you mean by short term.  There's short term, long term, and 
perm.  I think of short term as 2 weeks to a month.  

I have a long term contract at slightly higher rates 1099.  If you know
the gig is going to last a few years, you can provide a break on the
rates.

For short term contract work, high intensity and with hard deadlines, 
the Dee Cee area is running well over $100/hour.   

I know dozens of people who are billing over $150/hour for long term...
well, I don't actually know the people, I know the guy who's paying
them... and the people don't get $150+/hour, they get a good salary and
their employer, the middleman, pockets the difference.

As always, getting these good rate gigs demands substantive
expertise and a demonstrated track record.  Keeping the gig 
requires performing the work, in spite of management, technology 
limitations, infrastructure failures, conflicting user requirements.

Are you paid overtime?  

Local conventions make a big difference in rates.  As far as mainframers
go, silicon valley was a depressed area until a year ago.  The guys in 
Fremont were always griping about MVS rates.  It looks like it's coming
back.   This summer, they dialed their rates to $200/hour but that's 
short term and very high intensity.

It's hard to project out more than a few months.   Are we in a spiral
into a depression?  Will Y2K trigger a massive rebuilding of the IT 
infrastructure, with ever increasing rates for real programmers.

I don't know; this is one of those things that could go either way.

There's another article above... about Motorcycles...

--------------Quote-----------------------
A tiny bit of research will turn up all sorts of doomsayers, but little
real "information". I didn't read the wired article in question, and
possibly there are real concerns. What I *do* know is that all the crap
I've read about the problem that relates to anything about which I have
my own first hand objective knowledge is crap. The same is true with
everyone else I know who works in hardware/software industry; they've
mostly already fixed the critical (ie the systems will just lay down
and die) problems *if there were any* and will have most of the time
wasting/feature reducing bugs fixed long before the end of next year.
 
Or as one guy put it, the unix guys are sure they are okay, but fear
the mainframes. The mainframe guys are sure they are okay, but fear the
embedded systems. The embedded systems guys seem to think its no big
deal, but fear ... etc. etc. etc.
---------------End Quote----------------------
Not correct for the Dee Cee arena...  Here, there's an incredible 
reality disconnect.   If you ask the Y2K Project Office, you get the "on
schedule for completion by December 1998, leaving all of 1999 for 
testing." line.  If you ask the code-head in the trenches, he'll dart 
his eyes around and tell you that they haven't started yet... 

what! but they're telling us that System-X is tested and in production..

not started yet,  Oh, someone did a study about a year ago and we've 
identified a technical approach but no one has touched a line of code.

It may not be fair to extrapolate from my specific experiences in Dee 
Cee to an indictment of hornhaired management around the country but
that's the story from the potomac.

Some companies are close to completion by December 1998 but this is not
the general rule.   Several firms have reported substantive progress to
WDC Y2K and their IT people have spoken on the effort, the cost and
commitment.

Unfortunately, reps from other firms report that they are not in good
shape.

who's with me?!!" 

...as the mob rushes for the Conagra or ADM facility, you slip away...

boys....."
