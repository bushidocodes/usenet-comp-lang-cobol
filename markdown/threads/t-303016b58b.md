You can find the syntax and nuances in the manuals. Just a simple
example: Suppose there is an input field containing "Steven X Mustard".
You can unstring it into three fields First, Middle, Last. If you
'delimit by space', it will put "Steven" in First, "X" in Middle, and
"Mustard" in Last. That way you can store the three name fields
separately on your database.
Conversely, suppose your database has three fields, First, Middle, and
Last. You can String them into a BIG-NAME field for such things as a
'Dear Mr. BIG-NAME' (I.e., Dear Mr. Steven X Mustard) heading for a
letter, etc.

Initial use was for message handling-I think. You could accept free
formatted character strings, and pick the data items out of it
(unstring), or put a line together for transmission without describing
the output as a bunch of PICs (string). We used them for this and
even included the addressing control characters for terminals that had
this capability. One of the terminals we used could be programmed with
up to 80 fields per line, and the fields could be set to do some of
the editing (alpha, numeric, left/right justify, space fill,...). The
programmer could strip out the controls on input, or insert them in
the character string being output.

The most recent use I made of the verbs was to write a cross-reference
program for a brain-dead freeware assembler. I read the source code,
parsed it with several unstring verbs to extract the label field,
opcode, and multiple operands. After sorting an intermediate file, I
read it back in and wrote the cross reference listing using a few fixed
length PIC items and stringing the line numbers into the remainder of
the line. Got it running in about two hours! Still haven't tried to do
it in C/C++!

Gary
