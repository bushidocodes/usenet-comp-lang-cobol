I feel someone who has learnt to program is "a coder".
  A "programmer" needs to understand systems analysis and design.

  My son's course includes the subject "Computer Programming" in
which they learn C.  Java is not a subject in its own right, but
a by-product of the course "Network Programming in Java".

  COBOL is the by-product of courses like "Systems Engineering
Life Cycle and Systems Maintenance".  It may sound pretentious,
but it seems to emphasise that the programs written in COBOL are
components in a system rather than as some coders seem to think
the program they have written is the be-all and end-all.
  This course includes the lovely definition:  "Existing systems
which must be maintained are called legacy systems". (From the
lecture notes distributed by Roger Tagg (based on Ian
Sommerville Software Engineering, 5th edition, 1995).)
  "must be maintained" doesn't mean they fail and need fixing,
of course, but that they are the systems which must run or the
company is out of business.

I've been reticent to jump in on this one - BUT - just for the hell of
it !

When Noah was still collecting wood for the Ark, and after Alan Turing
et al. had done their thing on ADP, around about 1960 it became viable
for the new magic boxes to be used commercially, at a reasonable price,
(still dear, but at a reasonable price).

In the experimental stuff you had 'boffins, beards and Jesus boots
(sandals) who loved playing with ones and noughts. (Jeepers - when I
first got into this racket there was an article in the UK papers, 'Ford
Motor Company were employing a nurse to look after the well being of the
'freaks' who were programming") So when it came to staffing these new
computer installations - from whence were you going to draw staff ? Well
of course, take a bunch of bright young kids, mathematically inclined,
(most definitely NOT yours truly), train them in machine code and then
assembler - hey presto - programmers ! Whoa there - waddya gonna program
? Who can 'think' how to get debits and credits into one of these
'funny' boxes

So we now have a Systems Analyst, (as the title originally meant).
People who could 'analyse' the business problem and by God and by
guessing, attempt to translate it into some meaningful program
specification that those youngsters could use as a base. The source for
these analysts, not totally, but primarily people in accounting who had
done their penance pushing around sheaves of paper - and were thinking
there's got to be a better way.

It's not an exaggeration to say that early analysts were systems
designers, politicians, psychologists etc. No they couldn't produce you
a diploma to prove all these things, but they sure as hell could show
you the bruises ! OK, so they all had different qualities going from
excellent to lousy. The young guys doing the programming, "What do those
jerks know ?". Well most did know - and it invariably was the 'long
history' of getting an application off the ground, often doing a lot of
fighting with users - including in those days numerous Luddites.

Of course it's changed now, with a mixture of job titles,
analyst/programmer, application programmer etc., (take your pick), and
no longer being in the 'big commercial world' I get the impression the
original concept of a 'systems analyst' has disappeared. Certainly by
melding the two job titles, it offers a more attractive career path to
the young upcoming programmers.

Maintenance - Y-U-C-K !  Fortunately I only have to change my own goofs.
But Paul, tackling 4 to 6 different applications per day - my deepest
sympathies. Eileen, one application that she has got her teeth into, and
is learning it back to front and upside down.  I think Eileen's case
represents an interesting situation - if she is solely devoted to
maintaining this particular 'beast', isn't this a case where perhaps
somebody should say, "We've got to bite the bullet and rewrite the damn
thing. It's costing us a fortune just maintaining it, our corporate
marketing philosphy has long since changed, and a review and rewrite may
well be cheaper, in the long run".

Not knowing how the game is currently played, i.e. analyst v programmer
role - just how many of you, if your efforts are primarily programming,
have the opportunity to get to comprehend the total business problem.

In the cae of those who have branched out on their own, using titles
like 'consultant etc., it is understood. They have had to, successfully
or otherwise, embrace both analysis and programming. But the rest of you
folks...........?

Jimmy, Calgary AB

Tech Watch by Bob Weinstein of Infotrack Inc., March 2001
(St Paul Tribune ?  - not sure)

COBOL PROGRAMMERS ARE BACK IN THE GAME

You're wrong if you think COBOL programmers are doomed to go the way
of the Edsel.
The world kept turning after Y2K.  The job market for COBOL (COmmon
Business Oriented Language) programmers suddenly plunged - and so did
their salaries.  But the declne has turned around, thanks to the Net's
transition into a shopping mall selling everything from toothbrushes
to tractors.
A couple of years ago, Bill Lockhart, 63, a veteran COBOL programmer
from Los Angeles, was hard-pressed to find a company that would take
advantage of his 30-plus years of experience.  His experience as a
COBOL programmer dates to 1966, when he worked as a systems
programmer.  More recently, as a database specialist, he worked on
several assigments for IBM writing in COBOL, PL/I, REXX and assembly
language.
"The tables have turned," said Lockhart. "IT [information technology]
industries need my COBOL skills to get going on the web."
"Most of the world's business data, approximately 75-85 percent, is
written in COBOL," said Bill Payson, head of Senior Techs, a Net-based
job bank for IT professionals in Campbell, Calif.
COBOL is used in almost all Fortune 500 companies.  Many of these
firms have a large pool of COBOL-based applications that are primary
business systems.  E-business requires these systems to be integrated
and connected to the outside world.
Paul Halper, director of traditional development solutions at Merant,
a Web-enabling training company in Mountain View, Calif., said: "If
all the COBOL programs stopped working, the U.S. economy would
collapse.  Nine out of 10 of the top internet brokers use COBOL with
CICS [Customer Information Control Systems].  Chances are when you use
an ATM card, you are starting a COBOL/CICS process."
About 1.5 million COBOL programmers are available worldwide.  "The
challenge is not just finding veteran COBOL people, but finding COBOL
developers who know the internet," Payson said.  "Our strategy is
based on the belief that it is easier to train veteran COBOL staff in
the Internet than it is to teach dot-commers in the complex business
rules of COBOL.  For the most part, young dot-commers don't know
COBOL, nor do they want to learn it.
