First let's take the ones you DON'T need or want to read - some of them 
you have mentioned  :-

- Raymond Obin (An excellent introductory book written by Ray when he was with M/F and was one of the 'promoters' of OO COBOL. It was a paperback summation of the COBOL OCTG (Object COBOL Technical Group ?), published by M/F back in '93 (?) showing the basic syntax. Useful back then, but we've got beyond it. Also it may well be out of print.

- Robert Levey - a new name to me. When was it published ? If back around '93, probably way out of date - UNLESS - he has made some attempt to provide source code examples.

- Andrew Topper - it just didn't turn me on.  Lots of nice graphs and diagrams. Just took a look at the samples. 'Playing with a deck of cards' and the *usual* ATM machine. Looking at the brief source at the back he was obviously using an M/F early version of OO, (perhaps VISOC)..

Ned Chapin - same as above - give this one a miss.

Doke and Hardgrave - a very shortened re-vamp of what I mention below. It wont really give you any pluses.

Murtach - or is that Murac and then Carol Bernoulli (or some such) - 
both their respective books cover mainframes and touch on OO - but 
probably not to the depth you want..

AND THE WINNER IS :-

'COBOL Unleashed' - don't buy unless you want to update yourself on 
COBOL generally, (particularly mainframe), but it does have some good 
chapters on OO and GUIs.

Always on the lookout for OO COBOL Books, but considering that the 
mainframe world has tended to dismiss OO COBOL - there really isn't a 
market for folks to publish on OO COBOL. It just so happens these two, 
PC-oriented and both using Net Express , to my mind at least, are a 
couple of 'winners'.

Will (Wilson) Price - definitely !  - contact him at 
will.price@objectz.com or via cobolreport.com.
It's set out in tutorial style developing a particular theme. He totally 
avoids the issue of GUIs showing how applications can be developed using 
ANSI display, (avoiding the gimmickry of nursing GUIs).

Ed Arranga & Frank Coyle  - excellent if you can lay your hands on a 
copy. My starting point and EPIPHANY before I  read Will's book. Again 
avoids COBOL file handling (tables in W/S) and no GUIs.. What really did 
it for me was their sample In-House Library application at the back of 
the book. (Reading the source - 'Jeez ! Is it really that easy ?'. It's 
the APIs(GUIs) and the Web which are the stumbling block and where you 
get into the true learning curve with OO). The book is probably out of 
print - perchance Ed Arranga *might* have some copies if you contact him 
at cobolreport.com. Alternatively  you might find some second-hand 
copies for sale at amazon.com (search on 'Edmund Arranga').

As you already have a handle using C++, Java and C# - your interest 
should be focused on the COBOL concept of a Class containing FACTORY 
methods (creating instances) and the OBJECT SECTION containing the 
'doable' methods for the instance.  Note that data can be global at 
either the FACTORY or OBJECT level).

When you feel bold enough to tackle code - go to the Fujitsu and Micro 
Focus websites for source code examples using OO COBOL, plus their 
on-line manuals covering OO.
Compiler-wise, F/J have their V3  (from memory they are up to V 7) - if 
you've got Thane Hubbell's 'Teach Yourself COBOL in 24 Hours' that comes 
with the V3. M/F for about $100 offers a University Edition of Net 
Express. You can 'play' with it but can't distribute 'real' applications.

Good luck !

Jimmy, Calgary AB
