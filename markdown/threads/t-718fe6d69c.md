Bill,

    My opinion is that it sounds like you're up to your neck with
commitments. Yes, it is very easy to get in over your head. I'm sure you

    As far as getting people to know you're available, other than word of
mouth and advertising, your local Techno-Pimp is an alternative. But, be
careful with these people.

    If you're going to do this the right way, I would suggest setting
yourself up as an S-CORP for IRS purposes. The cost is about $300-$500
through a lawyer. Make sure that when you take on a project, you sign a
contract with the company and agree upon the time-period for payment, which
is usually in the contract. You may want to seek the advice of a CPA as
well. Past experiences specifying 'DBA' with an SSN could possibly raise a
red-flag with IRS and could cause you some grief. However, if you go the
Techno-Pimp route, you may be able to work something out with them.

    Please note that I am a full-time independent consultant. Your mileage
may vary....

    HTH....

>During the day, I am a web application programmer (no COBOL there) and would

>

Telecommuting part-time as a moonlighter is very very difficult to get
hired for.  My moonlighter guy, has specific application skills that
got him the work.  

Perhaps Y2k work might be a possibility.

But, I'd also look at COBOL/CGI and/or NetExpress considering your
backround.

>
>How do you drum up business (other than word of mouth)?

I've gotten my work through experience and reputation.  However, I
also send out pocket calendars with my business name address info, and
they have gotten me business.  It cost me $300, but netted me $10,000.

>
>Did you need the assistance of a lawyer?

No, but a good bookkeeper or accountant helps alot.  When I got
started, I kept my previous sub-contract agreements, got blacked out
copies of contracts from other contractors, and Quicken Lawyer
(originally I used It's Legal).  I make my own contracts.  And with
high grade paper and company logo, you'd think a lawyer drew it up.

BTW, I decided to go this route and bypass the use of a lawyer because
of the no more than 20 minutes of work a lawyer spent on me when I
incorporated 10yrs ago for $500.  All she did was fill out a
"fill-in-the-blank" incorporation form, put it in a fancy binder, and
call the state and register the corporation name.  Quicken Lawyer will
do much of the same for contracts.

However, if I was in business with others, I'd use a lawyer.

If you do use a lawyer, accountant and/or bookkeeper, don't use the
yellowpages to find one.  Get one, based on referrals.

>
>What TAX considerations did you need to make?

For moonlighting, 1099 (or more accurately, sole proprietor) work is
probably the easiest to get started.  But, I'd never hire you as a
sole proprietor to do work for a client of mine, because of Section
1706.  I'd hire you as W2, unless you would incorporate.  If you work
direct to a company/client (ie. not a 3rd party arrangement), 1706
isn't a problem, but some companies won't use sole proprietors.

Talk to an accountant or bookkeeper.

>
>How do you "collect" on finished projects?

Invoices are due upon receipt.  Don't wait until the project is
complete to bill.  Either work hourly and bill weekly or twice a
month, or if the price is fixed, then set up project "milestone" due
dates and assiociated payment dates.  All of my clients are large
companies, I haven't have collection problems, but others have.  What
I would probably do if it becomes obvious that I'm being stiffed, is
to follow up with delinquency letters with due dates and penalties to
apply if not paid.  Then contact a collection agency.

>
>Do you find it easy to get in OVER your head?
>
Sure!  Even after 10yrs of doing it!  But once you get things setup,
it's just a matter of routine.
