About 3 years ago I wrote an article about the relevance of COBOL.

Quite a bit has changed since then, including my own views, which have 
exanded in the light of experience.

This article is about to be presented to an audience of people who have 
COBOL as part of their organisations and the group will consist of various 
Analysts, Managers, and even Programmers...:-) from various companies.

(The companies are mainly Small/Medium businesses and they are in various 
parts of the World).

The document is not finalised yet but I'm releasing it to this group as a 
preview... Exclusive!! You saw it here first! :-)

I am conscious that I am close to the subject and I want to make sure I 
treat it fairly.

My goals are as follows:

1. Make a fair statement about the future of COBOL. (This, of course, is 
entirely a matter of opinion and completely arguable; nevertheless, I 
present my own thoughts on it.)
2. Examine some of the options that are available for dealing with the COBOL 
legacy. Obviously, I would like people to use the PRIMA Toolset, (which 
allows you to retain COBOL as your development language if you want to, 
although I cannot, in all conscience, recommend that) but that is not the 
primary thrust of the article. It is much more important to get people 
engaged in thinking about legacy and discussing it.

The article does not deal with moving to Solaris or Linux, although these 
are also increasinglyy important target platforms. It is aimed at people who 
are committed to the Windows environment and .Net.

If you think there are points I have missed or anything in the article is 
unfair, please post here. Send the article link below to people you know who 
have an interest in COBOL.

If good points come back, I'll amend the article. Remember you saw it here 
first...:-)

As always, I have checked the web coding in Chrome, Firefox, and IE (Chrome 
moves the 'Home' link but otherwise renders OK...) so if you have any 
problems viewing, please let me know about that too...

I can promise you that no attempt is made to track anyone accessing and all 
accesses are completely anonymous. No cookies, no adware, nada...

Not quite. A COBOL layout CAN be quite efficient on a database but it is 
when OCCURS, REDEFINES, Group Fields, and DATE types are included that 
things get a little sticky. I spent months manually transferring COBOL 
records into RDB and refining and improving the necessary DDL until I could 
get a set of rules that was programmable. I then encapsulated that 
experience into the Toolset. In practise, it doesn't matter whether people 
use the PRIMA toolset or do it manually, normalization still leads to a 
better Database.

My clients do know about Normalizing RDBs and if they don't, I provide 
documentation which clearly and simply explains it and have run the odd 
afternoon seminar covering it.

(The COBOL21 site also has such information available and over 3000 people 
have downloaded or browsed it. In fact, the SQL part of the site is 
something I should update as there is a steady stream of hits on it.  There 
is a paper on RDBMS Normalization at:

http://primacomputing.co.nz/cobol21/SQL.aspx

I just looked at this page and realized it is not rendering correctly in IE; 
I'll fix it later today.)

When people start to get interested in a "new" (to them...) technology, they 
usually do some homework and the concept of normalization is part of that. I 
don't believe everyone using a RDBMS needs to fully understand the 
mathematical implications of Codd's Relational Model, but Normalization is 
not hard to cope with.
>
> 4. If your clients jump to use the toolset then they may be faced with
> excessive immediate maintenance burdens as they learn the new
> codebase.

There isn't a "new codebase". There is an existing Legacy codebase that has 
been transformed to have all ISAM access replaced with invokes of a DAL 
object. The Legacy code continues to run exactly as it always has, but now 
it has a new datasource. Instead of the old ISAM files, it uses the newly 
defined RDB.

It would be quite feasible (although no-one has actually done this because 
it hasn't proved necessary) to keep the old ISAM version of your legacy 
programs (people normally archive this anyway) and continue maintaining that 
just as you always have. Change a program, run it through Transformation in 
the Toolset (a matter of seconds) and your new changes are reflected in a 
program that is accessing the new database. The Toolset generates working 
code in seconds and the generated code has already been debugged.

The DAL objects are generated by the Toolset also and they enable the legacy 
to use the new Database. New development (which can be in any OO Language 
(including COBOL) MAY use the DAL objects as a convenient way to manipulate 
legacy record formats, but it doesn't HAVE to.

I have deliberately kept the DAL methods similar to what COBOL programmers 
are used to when they write programs to access ISAM files. There is a DAL 
programming handbook which shows how you can use these objects to get access 
to your legacy COBOL record formats from the new RDB and it means you can 
access your existing COBOL data from a web page or a desktop just by 
instantiating the appropriate DAL object. (and invoking its methods... :-))

http://primacomputing.co.nz/cobol21/PRIMAToolset.aspx  (click the link near 
the bottom of the page to browse the DAL programming manual. The 
introduction explains what it is.)


Legacy programs require to have the COBOL record, but new programs may 
access a subset of columns or a join or a view just as is usual with 
Relational DB programming. ALL of the access, old and new, is separated to 
the Data Access Layer.

Usually, when people are looking at conversion and migration, they get the
