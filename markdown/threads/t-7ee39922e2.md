Fair comment. This is a COBOL newsgroup.

However, over the last 10 years, and currently, a growing number of COBOL 
programmers have been expanding their skill set to include Java, scripting, 
and Web Development. (I have helped some of them start by delivering help 
and advice with OO COBOL...) And then there are the Java guys who have been 
working on COBOL.

It is possible (and increasingly likely) that some of the people (certainly 
not all...) who frequent this group could be interested in these positions.

If this group is limited entirely to COBOL it will die. (It is already 
moribund...)

The days of lively discussion on COBOL, new features in the Language, 
newbies coming here requesting help, posting of sample code and discussion 
on approaches, standards, etc. are largely a thing of the past.

The group needs to expand its horizons to EMPHASIS on COBOL, but programming 
in general..

We have had a recent long thread on Fortran.While this is of no interest to 
me, I welcome it if it enlivens the group.

For some time now the OT posts have been much more fun than the COBOL posts.

For myself, I have always said I would stop posting here when it stopped 
being fun.

It has been some time since any posts here made me chuckle. (HeyBub manages 
to do it quite often and Doc Dwarf has flashes of genuine wit, but generally 
there seems to be a cloud of gloom and seriousness hanging over CLC.)

Is everyone depressed by the decline of COBOL?

Don't be.

According to IBM and Micro Focus it is about to rebound into the spotlight.

(They simply don't seem to understand the reality of the paradigm clash and 
nobody apart from our company seems to be addressing it, but hits on our web 
site are increasing, more people are viewing the videos, and private mail 
encourages me that people are at least asking questions before racing off to 
buy a new .Net COBOL compiler.

COBOL code that is essential to the business can and should be salvaged. It 
isn't expensive or difficult to do this (PRIMA is becoming pretty facile at 
it now...) but it does mean you have to do some homework and learn enough to 
understand why simply recompiling COBOL for .Net or converting it to Java is 
NOT a solution.)

Despite the necessary talking up of COBOL by people who want to sell 
compilers, the figures do show a slight increase in interest in COBOL over 
the last year.  It has moved from 30th position to around 27th in the table 
of most used languages.

I still have some components that are in OO COBOL. Every so often I have to 
maintain them or extend them and I swear a lot and promise myself I'll 
rewrite them in C#, but I never do. (I have already converted all the "easy" 
ones...) As they are OO COBOL they play nicely with the C# code and are 
generally not problematic. For some time now I have had C# talking to COBOL 
and COBOL talking to C# using the Component Object Model (COM) interface, 
without any problems at all. (The advantage of this model is that these 
components can run on Web pages, phones, slates, and  servers or clients, as 
well as the desktop. It is all made possible by object technology.)

Why don't I rewrite the remaining legacy?

Like most people trapped in legacy, the risk is just too high. They have 
been working for 15 years. During that time they have had new things added 
and old things changed or extended. Some of it is complex but even with the 
controlled approaches to encapsulated maintenance, there is a risk if I 
rebuild it. The Code is robust, efficient, and it works correctly. Why rock 
the boat? (the answer to that is that the rest of the fleet is comprised of 
much more modern boats which are more seaworthy, faster, and have more 
capacity... If you have a fleet of container ships it is irritating to know 
that there are still some old tramp steamers which are essential to your 
operation...)

I console myself with the thought that I can spend my time better adding new 
functionality in the new languages.

Pete.
