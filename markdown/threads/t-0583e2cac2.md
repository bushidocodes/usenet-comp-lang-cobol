Hi:

Recently, in another thread Mr. Dashwood accused me of calling 
him a 'liar' and of 'denigrating' his COBOL code.

To get the facts behind the 'liar' accusation, read the thread
'Dear Mr. Dashwood' posted some time ago.

Now for the next topic.

Mr. Dashwood accuses me of 'denigrating' his code. What I actually 
said was 'Mr. Dashwood's post gives us all an opportunity to compare
a structured program with one written in a sensible manner. . .'.
(see foodman123.com/post.htm)

As I said somewhere else, I considered this to be mildly sarcastic
rather than denigration. Mr. Dashwood also made the point that if
one was going to post code to the forum, one had better be prepared
for the consequences, or words to that effect. 

Anyway, since I am accused of denigration, I will go ahead and denigrate.

Mr. Dashwood described my code as an 'eyesore'.  

This suggests to me that Mr. Dashwood judges COBOL code primarily
by what it LOOKS LIKE rather than whether it is efficient, simple,
compact, sensible, easy-to-comprehend, and so on.

I think the reason Mr. Dashwood thinks my code is an 'eyesore' is
because it LOOKS SO LIKE OLD-FASHIONED COBOL, which is what it 
is, of course.

On the other hand, Mr. Dashwood's code is markedly different - upon
a casual glance it might even be C or Java or something with
its nested construction and mostly lower-case style and lots of lines
and wasted space.

I think that there is is a conscious effort on the part of the modern
COBOL programmer (as exemplified by Mr. Dashwood) to try to make 
their programs not LOOK like COBOL.

It seems that the trend is to try and make them look like programs
written in those other languages. To accomplish this goal, the code
is written in a nested style to take up as many lines as possible 
and to contain really useless stuff. 

A good example of cluttering programs up with useless stuff in order
to make them seem, perhaps, more stylish, is Mr. Dashwood's 
use of 'binary-fields', indexing, COMP-5 and SECTION.

It is categorically impossible to justify the use of these things
on TECHNICAL GROUNDS, i.e., that the compiled program will be
faster, smaller, better, to a measurable and worth-while extent. 
Maybe in the olden days, when indexing was introduced, there could 
have been some justification for some of these features, but 
today?  Why bother?

The only time the use of COMP-5 would be justified would be if 
you had to interface with something which required the parameters 
to be in that format. In the Dashwood case, this does not apply.

It follows that if it is categorically impossible to justify the use
of something on technical grounds, then its use must be in the
name of style, pretence, ostentation, or something else.

How would you justify the use of indexing or COMP-5 to a new student?
How do you explain to the student that using COMP-5 doesn't really do
anything of significance but use it anyway, because it looks good
and is part of the language?  If you use COMP-5, will your program 
be faster, smaller, more efficient, or what? What is the technical 
advantage? There is none - so why bother?

(I have no problem with the existence of things like COMP-5
when they have been included in the language for compatibility
with other stuff. Of course, why that other stuff required
the invention of COMP-5 is another question.) 

Why bother with indexing?  It doesn't provide any technical
advantage so using it can only be a matter of style. Why is 
'set x1 up by 1' better than 'ADD 1 TO X1'?  Would you encourage 
a student to use indexing, and, if so, on what grounds?  All
students are going to learn about tables and subscripting, why
bother teaching them indexing, because it is part of the language
invented in the olden days for some purpose or other? The 
attitude of use-it-because-it-exists is pointless if there is
no technical advantage. Why waste time typing it?

In his original post Mr. Dashwood pointed out that he had used
SECTION and did not wish to get into a big discussion over it.
This is good because it is impossible to justify the use of
SECTION on technical grounds today. It follows that use of
SECTION is stylistic.

I am probably the only person in the entire world who increments
level numbers by 1. Just about every COBOL program I have ever
seen incremented them by 5. Unusually, Mr. Dashwood increments
his level numbers by 12. 

The comparable code to move all the spaces to the end of the
string, as some wag said, requires 23 lines for Mr.Dashwood
and 9 lines for me. This is style over substance, if you ask
me but it sure makes his program look more C-like or something
and not like that horrible old-fashioned COBOL like I write.

On the other hand, my code is totally devoid of any unnecessary
frou-frou, is utterly simple, compact, easy-to-read, neat,
efficient, and, although one could change it, it would be hard
to make a significant improvement. My approach to programming
is to do what I need to do with the utmost economy both in terms
of the time it takes to write and the efficiency of the code.

Execution-time efficiency is totally irrelevant these days but
I personally enjoy reducing something to its essence rather than 
decorating it with useless stuff and trying to make it look
like something it isn't in the name of style.

I don't really care if people write inane programs full of
ostentatious stuff, but I do care when people criticize me for
not doing the same thing. 

Thanks

Tony Dilworth

To me, the real issue of COBOL style is whether or not an "experienced"
COBOL programmer can "pick up and maintain" a program that I have written
WITHOUT knowing "my style" or conventions.

This comes from working in LARGE mainframe shops and I can see it is
(usually?) irrelevant for those doing the development/maintenance of their
applications in a one or two-person "shop".

I believe that I have read something of a "consensus" (but certainly NOT
unanimous) on Mr. Dilworth's style, that it REQUIRES you know "his"
conventions to easily maintain it.  I have not heard similar comments on Mr.
Dashwood's code.  This, to me, is the reason that I "prefer" the latter
style.

There is no doubt that the more complex any single program/application is,
the more "time" will be needed in understanding it - no matter what the
style is.  However, I believe that there are general principals of
programming that CAN make picking up someone else's code easier rather than
harder.  On the other hand, whatever YOU are used to, is always (or at least
almost always) easier for YOU to maintain.

I think you are not seeing the big picture. ;-)

In COBOL, all requirements for decimal arithmetic are handled
by the run time. This is without regard to the representation of
the numeric items involved. [Both binary and packed decimal
representation require decimal scaling to accommodate
implied decimal points. The difference is in the method used
to scale the number.]

With 64-bit registers, newer processors easily accommodate
the arithmetic operations and scaling required for COBOL. For
data items with up to 18 digits, no more than two 64-bit registers
are required for an intermediate result.

Older processors, with their smaller registers, could not
accommodate large intermediate results, as easily. This,
sometimes, made packed decimal a more efficient
representation. This is, in part, because decimal scaling by
shifting is more efficient that decimal scaling by multiple
precision binary arithmetic.

There is no deficiency with either COBOL or the newer
processors. The newer processors, with their larger registers,
have, simply, outgrown the need for even the most basic
decimal arithmetic instructions; and, COBOL never required
decimal arithmetic instructions to get correct results.

Slightly tangential:

Many years ago, Burroughs seriously considered adding decimal-arithmetic
hardware to the Large System line.  There were lots of discussions among the
machine and language architects on the subject.  Clearly, converting from
display or packed to binary and back again was costly, and how better to
avoid that conversion than by doing the arithmetic directly on the data?

One of the counterarguments was that fast BINARY arithmetic was relatively
easy to achieve, and the best way to get fast OVERALL performance was to
speed up the conversion process, not build a new hardware box to avoid the
conversion altogether.  If both the arithmetic itself and the conversion
processes for the arguments are blindingly fast, who needs decimal hardware?
That counterargument won out.

Unisys MCP/AS systems do not include decimal arithmetic processors today;
they do their arithmetic in binary.
