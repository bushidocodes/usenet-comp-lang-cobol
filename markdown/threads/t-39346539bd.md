The company I'm working for is going to change language from PL/I to Cobol.
Before they used PL/I mainly and Cobol sometimes. Now one of the goals of
my departement is to produce reusable and multi-platform software. Just
trying to make a Cobol program I found out that on our IBM mainframe
programmers are using apastrophes(') around text literals and those
on our Tandem mainframe are using quotes(") for the same purpose.

You know, MOVE "Text" TO IBM-whatever or MOVE 'Text' to Tandem-whatever.

I know I can make the IBM Cobol II compiler accept "literals", but
is the Tandem compiler ready for 'literals' ?

I have read the ANSI/COBOL-85 standard specifies "literals"

I know our case tool is generating 'literals'

I know our PL/I to Cobol translator is generating 'literals'

Question, what to use? Does it matter? Is it worth to try to force
everybody to use "literals"/'literals' ?

Tandem mainframe? Har de har har! You mean tandem cheap hardware
minicomputer-mainframe wannabe... 8-)

All real computers are Big and Blue, and support comp-3.
Not only that but COBOL should be coded in uppercase.

No wire hangers ever!

Well, try the compiler directives in the Tandem and/or IBM cobol manuals.
QUOTE for the IBM compiler causes " to be the string delimiter.

For laughs, go into the Tandem and use the ANSI compiler directive
to wreak havoc, since Tandem allows (perish the thought) COBOL
coding to begin in column 1. ANSI makes the comments start in
column 7.


By the way, be sure to code IBM cobol programs to contain
block contains 0 records, otherwise the file will be assumed
to be unblocked (as opposed to your typical RECFM=FB)
(whoops).

Also, watch out for ebcdic versus ascii gotchas. On IBM the
9999 trailer record will be greater than the AAAA character string.
On Tandem, the opposite will be true....

The above represents my opinions alone, not necessarily of my
employers.
