There's your problem. If you're comparing it as a string, format it as a string.
You can't depend on internal numeric formats to work as you want when
they are compared as a string. Keep in mind that a display type sign may not
give you the results you want either. You should add an offset to make all the
numbers positive, eliminating the need for a sign, like this example.

    Data-Field:      PIC S9(06)v99
    Compare-Field:     PIC 9(07)v99 DISPLAY
    Offset:             1000000.00
    Code:  ADD Data-Field 1000000.00 GIVING Compare-Field.

This technique will work, no matter what the sign is.

Why would you pass in fields that are NOT going to cause any change?

If you don't, then the whole idea of comparing to find changes is stupid and 
pointless.

Given that you do, I can think of a couple of approaches to this and only 
one of them pivots on the fact that you know what you passed in.

What you need is a "clever" way to compare only the fields that were passed 
in, to their  previous contents, with any packed fields being automatically 
converted for the compare. I understand that the brute force approach 
requires too much coding, but there are smarter ways to achieve it.

The only fields that are problematic are the  packed ones.

If you have 123F and compare it to 123C (with a group compare) you have a 
problem, even though both the fields contain +123.

As long as you don't have Floating Point or Binary fields in the mix your 
problem then distills down to:

How can I transform every nibble that has a C in it, to an F?

There used to be a construct in mainframe COBOL called TRANSFORM that was 
pretty good for this sort of thing.You could use it to translate a set of 
characters into another set, say, 0C - 9C into 0F - 9F, with a single 
instruction. (Much less work than doing hundreds of compares on various 
fields.) It's many years since I worked in that environment so I don't know 
if it is still an IBM extension of COBOL, or whether the same killjoys who 
removed ALTER and EXAMINE have snaffled TRANSFORM as well...:-)

You could do it with INSPECT of course, but it's only half as elegant :-)

I shan't insult your intelligence any further than it has been already, :-) 
by spelling out the code, but it seems to me that if you TRANSFORMED or 
INSPECTED the saved record before the current compare you should be on a 
win...
