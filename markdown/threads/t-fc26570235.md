COBOL in 1978 and wrote it to be callable from any of our mainframe
languages (COBOL, FORTRAN, PL/1, Assembler, at the time).  However, in order
to put this routine into our system subroutine library, the +ACo-powers that be+ACo-

languages as COBOL so I was forced to rewrite the COBOL into assembler.  It
may be more efficient in CPU usage, but I would venture that the
milliseconds of CPU time saved in the last 20 years has still not made up
for the loss in readability (I write very readable assembler just like I
write very readable COBOL, but many people can't read assembler not matter
how readable) and the extra time I needed to get it rewritten.

The word monolithic implies large, as well as single purpose.  But it
has been my experience that OO's strength is in very large, very complex
systems.  What if your function is straight forward?  Why pay the cost
of the extra complexity of OO in that case?  Or what if the only event
you care about is CREATE THIS REPORT.  Event driven code is another
complexity.

Don't get me wrong, we there are many times when we need the complexity
of OO, of event-driven, and of distributed processing.  But their very
advantages, such as hidden and inaccessible code are also disadvantages
(where in that hidden, inaccessible code is that bug).

Sometimes simpler is better.  Sometimes straightforward is better.

Sometimes it is better to have several tools, designed to solve
different needs.

Howdy

Indeed MS discontinued the support for AXR processors but as of today's
market, one can deploy them without much fuss. WHen IA64 becomes de-facto
the 64 bit processor of choice, we can port all WIn32 into Win64 ....

As for the 15M lines of Cobol -> VB, there is a misundestanding about the
business logic of these lines in COBOL. Needless to say that well over 1/3
is UI related and about 1/3 syntax analysis, leaving a miniscule 1/3, or
500K lines of actual business logic, completely obsolete and patched over
the years. The end result is a compressed < 200K lines of actual portable
information, easily packageable onto MTS components in either VB or VC.
Let's face it, COBOL was a good language, such as APL once was, but the
future is dimm. Having a /390 housing DB2 makes sense. Having MVS run COBOL
code to generate reports makes no sense. Neither having COBOL programs
running obsolete business logic.

As an example, I opened an account at Wells Fargo yesterday. The UI was dry,
meaningless, cryptic, and slow as molasses. Much more can be achieve by
having ASP pages running (or any equivalent server side CGI-based code) on a
web server, DHTML or XML pages, and app servers controlling the transaction.
I had the oportunity to port a whole bank from MVS with CICS into Windows
and SQL servers, slashing millions of said COBOL lines, and ending up with a
system that scales correctly and performed 7.2 times faster than the MVS
equivalent. That is not to say that MVS is a bad OS, on the contrary, I
still consider it the nicest OS ever written. And I also once taught COBOL
and Advanced COBOL, but that was in the mid 80's.

No serious CIO/CTO would invest new software money into a COBOL solution.
And the ones that do it have a very bad understanding of modern software
engineering and the level of sofistication quadra boxes have, being them
Intel or Alpha. The technology of clustering quadra processors or
8-processor boxes is real and works like a charm. Nowadays, we can do
whatever was done by MVS on Windows.

To conclude, last year I served as an advisor for a University asking if
COBOL should be eliminated from the curriculum. I voted yes, some others
don't. At the end, it was removed from the core, and placed as an elective.
Last semester NO Students enrolled in the elective ...

Best regards,
Andre Rosenthal - moderator for comp.os.tpf

VB is not interpreted. You can compile the code using VB6 and the result is
independent of the interpreter. Also, these are DLLs and not exes, running
surrogated onto MTS. Now as far as having a decimal representation, that on
itself is not needed because the BL is independent of the internal format
the data is stored. For instance, is of no used debating if a counter should
be COMP-2 or COMP-4 because it is internal to the DLL calculating it, and it
is returned to the caller in a variant type. You can dynamically alter it
w/o loss of generality. That on itself is again useless for FORTRAN apps,
since the precision is tightly couple with the UI representation, and said
apps are monolythic by nature. In fact, there are several libraries for C++
that contain pre-defined classes for high-precision math, but MFC is not one
of them. As far as string manipulation is concerned, C is lowsy, but C++ is
awesome. Using MFC for instance, you can get CString values handled in
various ways, reverse syntax and the likes. The vast majority of business
apps handle simple math types, and setting them as currency and binary
suffice for most cases. To this day I have not seen a hard conversion from
any COBOL type into a simpler atomic value of OLECURRENCY or long/double.

Tables are things of the past. They are all maskarated into recordsets
handled directly by the DBMS or faked under ADO classes. Like I said, one
cannot convert an app in COBOL using its technology, and thus the concept of
tables are non existent. Whenever said concept is needed, a temp recordset
or SQL-table is created and handled by the object model w/o loss of
generality.

I agree with the confusion insofar as Win9x and OS/2 is concerned. No one in
the full possession of their intellect would deploy a system on these
platforms. Windows NT is a superb app server when used in conjunction with

"   There Is a Difference!
One might wonder why mainframes cost up to several millions of dollars when
their raw megahertz (MHz) or MIPS rates are no higher than a PC costing
1,000 times less.  There are reasons.  Firstly, in a small computer such as
a PC, the CPU does almost all the processing.  Unless direct memory transfer
(DMA) is used, the CPU is also involved with getting data to and from the
peripherals, the most time-consuming part of the whole operation.
  A mainframe provides enormous amounts of throughput by offloading its
input/output processing to a peripheral channel, which is a computer in
itself.  Mainframes can support hundreds of channels, up to 512 in some
models. Mainframes also have multiple ports into memory and especially into
high-speed caches, which can be 10 times faster than main memory. 
Additional computers may act as I/O traffic cops between the CPU and the
channels and handle the processing of exceptions (what happens if the
channel is busy, if it fails, etc.).  All these subsystems handle the
transaction overhead, freeing the CPU to do real "data processing" such as
computing balances in customer records and subtracting amounts from
inventories, the purpose of the computer in the first place.
  Secondly, the internal bus transfer rates of mainframes are also higher
than small computers.  A 200MHz Pentium has a data bus that runs at 66MHz,
but a 200MHz mainframe may have a data bus that also runs at 200MHz, three
times as fast.  The multipliers add up. Three times the bus speed, 10 times
the cache speed, perhaps 32 or 64 overlapped data transfers.  Multiply one
times the other, and the combination of fast buses, fast caches, multiple
memory ports and independent channels and subsystems makes a processing
machine unlike anything else.
  Thirdly, much of the hardware circuitry in a mainframe is designed to
detect and correct errors. Every subsystem is continuously monitored for
potential failure, in some cases even triggering a list of parts to be
replaced at the next scheduled downtime.  As a result, mainframes are
incredibly reliable.  The mean time between failure (MTBF) is generally 20
years!
  In addition, mainframes are highly scalable. Based on symmetric
multiprocessing (SMP), mainframes can be expanded by adding CPUs to a system
or by adding systems in clusters.

   They're Here to Stay

Once upon a time, mainframes meant "complicated" and required the most
programming and operations expertise.  That is no longer.  Networks of
client/server-based PCs make mainframes look easy. Nothing is more
complicated than the DOS/Windows environment.  Add NetWare, throw in a
little UNIX for good measure, and you have enterprise computing at its most
complex ever.  With more than two trillion dollars worth of mainframe
applications in place, mainframes are here to stay, and their centralized
architecture, which is easiest to manage, may just be the wave of the
future!"

To put things in perspective, currently, each of the 32 S/370 G6's _main_
processors operates not at 200, but at about 700 MHz, and nowadays the Big
Blue is about of the size of a bookcase. So, size-wise, the roles between MF
and CS have reversed: To build a Intel-based or UNIX server equal to G6 in
sheer computing power (of course, it will never do the job quite the same
way), it will take many times the footprint.

programmer, and SAS, as many know, runs under virtually any OS. I have had
an opportunity to compare the way it operates on a number of them, and given
a choice, will take the big iron any time.
