Here's 20 things Taft doesn't know about COBOL:

1. 85% of all statistics (without citation) are invented on the spot...
2. There may be 2 million people directly involved in COBOL (mainly in 
India, China, and the sub-continent), but there are 60 million directly 
involved in other languages, across the globe.
3. The COBOL 2002 standard has not been implemented by any major (or minor) 
vendor, in its entirety.
4. The "new" standard he promises for "the early 2010s", should have been 
ready 3 years ago. (5 years between standards is a reasonable and acceptable 
period for most computer languages.)
5. Repeating figures from a very old Gartner report (since "qualified" even 
by Gartner), no matter how much you WANT them to be true, does NOT make them 
so...
6. The procedural paradigm COBOL was developed for, is not relevant to a 
world of networked computing. COBOL has been overtaken by events.
7. BCOOL is one anagram of COBOL; BOLOCs is another...
8. The 5 billion lines of new COBOL every year are being written on a 
handful of Government sites. Places where money is no object and 
modernization is anathema. In fact, they are probably being written by ONE 
government...; no prizes for guessing which.
9. "Mission Critical" does NOT mean "fossilized, serial, procedures which 
require archaic batch processing..." Even if that is what mainframes are 
mostly good at...
10. Some simple arithmetic should have given Taft pause... If his claim 
regarding COBOL processing  more than 200 times the number of GOOGLE 
Searches per day could be true, it would mean the ENTIRE POPULATION of 
Planet Earth (men, women, and children, every single person...) causing a 
COBOL transaction every 7 minutes of the working day.  Given that less than 
30% of the planet's population even have access to a personal or mainframe 
computer, this seems extremely unlikely. Draw your own conclusion.
11. Today "15% of all new applications will be written in COBOL..." So, 85% 
of all new applications will NOT be. Twenty years ago COBOL had 75% of the 
market. Today it's 15%. Hard to see a growth trend there...
12. "70% of all business applications are on a mainframe."  I hope they back 
it up regularly...
13. "COBOL is being re-energized."  Unfortunately the same cannot be said 
for most of its practitioners. They are fading away and failing to be 
replaced.
14. "COBOL has been fashioned for execution within framework environments 
such as Java and .NET."  And as soon as programmers learn those environments 
they abandon COBOL for Java or C#.
15. "There are $2 trillion dollars invested in COBOL..."  And that's just to 
keep it running for one Government... Many businesses have found out they 
simply can't afford the price of labour intensive software maintenance which 
COBOL demands. COBOL maintenance is error prone and expensive. Mainly 
because it is verbose and you therefore need more of it than you do of other 
languages.
16. Anyone seriously investing in a COBOL future in this day and age is 
either myopic or stupid. (Supporting legacy is the ONLY case where you can 
justify continued use of it...) There are MUCH better solutions available 
and they cost much less to implement and maintain. Attachment to COBOL has 
to be on emotional/tradional rather than rational grounds; it cannot be 
justified economically, and even functionally, there is nothing you can do 
in COBOL that can't be done (more cheaply) in another language.
17. "Five billion lines of new COBOL are developed every year." And 4 
billion of those are replacing previous COBOL code that has become 
"unmaintainable"...
18. "Dijkstra once said, "The use of COBOL cripples the mind; its teaching 
should, therefore, be regarded as a criminal offense." " He said it as a 
joke. But he was not far off the mark. It isn't that COBOL "cripples the 
mind" it is more that it "closes the mind". It is a bit like the guitarist 
who consistently played a single chord. When his wife gently asked him why 
he didn't move his fingers around like other guitarists she had seen, he 
replied:"They're looking for the place; I've found it."
19. Modern software is comprised of objects and layers. This approach means 
more easy platform interopability, better functional isolation 
(encapsulation), and easier, quicker, and cheaper maintenance (object 
reuse). This approach evolved out of a search for solutions to the problems 
created when you DON'T use objects and layers. And THAT was largely 
predicated on COBOL legacy.
20. COBOL was a brilliant response to the perceived needs of the 1960s. When 
you had a centralised processor that did everything, based on the Von 
Neumann model of sequential execution. But things have come a long way since 
then. COBOL should be allowed to go with dignity.

Pete.
 -- 
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."

Really?  Being a bit of the Technical Type I got caught up with the 
definition of 'transaction'; for me it had the same kind of slipperiness 
as LUW... errrrrr, Logical Unit of Work, the signal for a CICS SYNCPOINT, 
for those who understand such things.

A person puts a card in a machine, deposits money, withdraws money, checks 
a balance, receives an NSF or NOAUTH response to a request and walks 
away... this is a transaction, right?  Or...

A card is inserted into the machine.  The machine verifies *at the machine 
interface* that the card's mag strip is valid.  This is a transaction.

The machine, having recognised a valid mag strip, requests a PIN.  This is 
a transaction.

The user hits the keys... assuming that each key is not verified at the 
pressing the user hits 'all done' and the PIN is captured and formatted by 
the machine.  This is a transaction.

The machine sends the PIN to whatever 'source of good PINs' exists (some 
verify against the card, some elsewhere).  This is a transaction.

The verification comes back as 'good' or 'no good'.  This is a 
transaction.

... and so on.  I'm sure I'm missing a few steps here and there but... 
once it gets to the bank level I can think of, for a withdrawal, six 
COBOL transactions used for a single withdrawal:

1) Open/verify the account.
2) Verify the account is not frozen.
3) Verify that subtracting the amount requested will not violate (rules).
4) Send a 'disburse (amount of currency) signal to the ATM.
5) Decrement the amount remaining in the account.
6) Send an 'all done' signal to wherever it is needed.

So... how are 'transactions' being counted, anyhow?

account exists things continue, if it doesn't (or is marked as 'don't

For what it's worth (nothing), here is how the ATMs that I work with on a
daily basis function...

From the time you enter your card until the time you complete entering your
request information (PIN, function, amount, etc.) there is no "external"
interaction until after that final keypress where the terminal goes in to
it's "please wait" state.  At that time all of the information, including
the magnetic stripe data and the PIN, is gathered together into a single
request message that is sent to the host system that the ATM is connected
to.  The host forwards the request to wherever it needs to go to be
authorized.  When that response is received it is passed back to the ATM. 
Then ATM then sends a "confirmation" back to the host once the money is
dispensed.  That is the end of a "normal" ATM transaction.  

Now of course the ATM does a lot of things interally every time you press a
key.  I have no idea if anything on the ATM itself is written in Cobol,
though my guess is it is not.

Anyway, I wouldn't consider those six steps you refer to as distinct
transactions.  But that's just me.

Interestingly (to me, anyway!) much of the above changes in the "GUI ATM"
world.  We don't have any of these machines, but apparently they do some
"server interaction" as you press various buttons.  For instance, from what
I recall when we were looking in to going this direction, when you enter (or
swipe) your card the ATM does indeed query a server, specifying your card
information.  This allows the ATM to customize your session based on
personal information stored at the host.  It may even be possible for it to
greet you by name, though I have never seen this.

fruition than I ever expected, already...)

Expand your skill set if you intend to be in IT for the long haul. The days
