> How long have you been working in the Programming field?

It depends on the meaning of the word "work".  I've been paid by people 
to indulge my hobby for about 20 years.  With some gaps for other gigs.

> How hard was it to get your first job programming?

Not hard at all.  I went to high school with the son of a small 
businessperson.  She paid some contractors to develop a system for her, 
and they operated it for the first cycle as proof of success.  When the 
second cycle came around the instructions they left her didn't work, 
neither did the software.  Her son got me involved and we fixed it up in 
about two weeks.

> Is there much job security in programming?

That depends on if you are a good programmer or not.  There is a 
tendancy to slow, or halt, new development when the economy is in the 
toilet.  But there are always places looking for skillful programmers.

> What made you decide to go into programming in the first place?

I actually did not want to go into programming.  I feared that it would 
destroy my enjoyment of my hobby.  I spent time pursuing other careers 
like driving trucks, bartending, and a stint in the infantry.  People 
always seemed to be coming to me for help with their computers (unbreak 
the dispatch system, teach the old man how to use his new laptop, rework 
the inventory system, etc).

I decided not to re-up after [coke-addled, draft-dodging, sleeze] Bill 
Clinton made a mess of Mogadishu.  I took a gig with a bank and have 
been programming full time since.

> Do you enjoy your work?

People actually pay me to play with computers -- how cool is that?  Who 
wouldn't enjoy it.

> What languages do you recommend I learn, and which are most widely used?

I won't touch 'most widely used'.

But I recommend you learn at least one assembler, even if you never use 
it.  Knowing how the machine works will make all of your code better.

If all you have is a hammer, the whole world is your nail.  I also 
recommend you learn at least two high-level languages very well so that 
you can always view problems through their intersection.  Knowing two 
languages might not be a great cure-all for the nail problem, but it 
does help you remember that there are more tools in the box.  Very often 
people will get into the habit of thinking that they are "Cobol 
Programmers" or "Java Programmers" and they forget that knowing the 
language does not mean you know how to program any more than owning a 
hammer makes you a master carpenter.

If I had to make a suggestion -- avoid the "visual" things.  You should 
be capible with nothing more than a text-editor and a command line 
compiler before you start drawing windows and dragging buttons around. 

Java, C and Cobol are all widely used in production environments and you 
should get a good exposure to each (Avoid Fortran and Basic, they 
destroy brain cells ;-).

Perl, Ruby, Python, Javascript and VBscript are quite popular for 
scripting and see some production use.  Expose yourself to a bit of each 
and pick one you are comfortable with and learn it will.  There are many 
needs for scripting in the course of development and it will serve you 
well throughout your career.

At last, very incomplete, count, I found a web site that cataloged over 
400 seperate languages -- not seperate implementations, but separate 
languages.  There are a lot to pick from.  Some interesting ones that 
you will likely never see production code written in, yet still have 
something to offer.  Lisp and other functional languages provide an 
interesting view on how algorithims can be constructed.  Smalltalk 
provides a purists view of object oriented programming.  Pascal does the 
same for structured procedural programming.

Don't let the number scare you.  After you have learned one language 
well it should take you about a week with a reference manual and a 
compiler to feel comfortable in another language.  Most of all, learn 
algorithms in a language independant fashion.

> How much of your work is done by group collaboration and how much is a solo
> effort?

Design is usually done by team with a whiteboard, much caffine and much 
discussion.  Implementation of subsystems within that design are done as 
solo efforts with team reviews.

> Which do you prefer, group or solo projects?

Depends on the group.

> Why?

There is nothing nicer than building a software product with a group of 
capible, competent professionals that know what they are doing.  But 
there is nothing more frustrating than babysitting an incompetent 
programmer.
