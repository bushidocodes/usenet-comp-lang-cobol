```
If all goes well, the following is a copy of the message I sent in
response to the information Bill sent out.  I urge anyone with similar

Members of the US delegation to the ISO WG4
committee:

As a regular readers of the COBOL newsgroups, I am aware
that there are lots of  new  features in the draft of the next COBOL
Standard.  However, I am also aware that at least one issue is of
major interest to the future of COBOL. There are some seemingly un-CO BOL
type specifications creeping into the specification.

These specifications allow each vendor to set his level of support to the
spec.  Levels of support historically have been set by the COBOL Committee
representing a collective opinion of all vendor and user interested parties.

Such a leveling provided a means for the objectives of a common language to
be met via adoption of feature sets and not uncommon sets.

Clearly, users can not see what will come in the way of hardware
futures to know how to set levels by them selves.  Likewise, vendors can not
know what the market reaction will be to the idea of uncommon sets of
features.  Together users and vendors can s can create commons  sets.

Should the ISO, and ANSI organizations permit the unilateral setting of
uncommon sets, the usefulness of such standards organizations become suspect
to me.

Many times when someone or some group sees a problem with a requirement,
they only see the road blocks.  They do not see the opportunities.

The fact that a standards group agrees that fracturing the language to allow
non-uniform sets or subsets of COBOL leaves the impression that there is an
agenda that has not been openly discussed.   This makes me feel that perhaps
it was a mistake to
allow COBOL development gravitate to those with a vested interest in the
support or non-support of the language.  Perhaps, it is time for an
open-system sponsorship to be developed again.  Or perhaps it's time for the
current effort to be more forthcoming with their intensions. The current
efforts has done a very good job with
the language carrying on their legacy until now.

I remind all that new hardware can be accomidated by language. Most new
hardware is just a  rehash of old ideas anyway.  It's time to have an open
discussion on why such non-uniform language specifications are necessary.

Perhaps the UN could send in a peace keeping force.

Give us the problems, then we can talk commonality not nonuniform system
support.

Warren Simmons
cc: comp.lang.cobol
```
