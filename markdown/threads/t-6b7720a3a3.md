```
Hello Steve,
A couple things:

1)  does your school have a career center?  many schools have them, and they
    often have lists of companies that the school maintains a relationship
    with.  they may also put on job fairs where you can hand out dozens of
    resumes, and possibly get an on the spot interview.
2)  bug your friends.  if you recently graduated from school, i'm assuming
    you know other people in your major.  find out where they got jobs.  see
    if they can pass your resume along to HR for their company.
3)  if you know some of your professors well, see if they have any contacts
    they can provide for you.
4)  try a head-hunter.
5)  maybe try some temp work with a firm such as manpower who can put you
    into a company that may end up hiring you full-time.
6)  if your school has an alumni association, check with them to see if they
    can help you.
7)  keep sending out those resumes!  i know when i was job hunting, i always
    had at least a dozen with me, no matter where i went, because you never
    know who you're going to bump into that may be able to help you out.
    

    are you getting interviews?  if you are, but aren't getting jobs, you 
    should polish up your interviewing skills.  and if you're not even 
    getting interviews, you probably should polish up your resume, cause 
    CS is in high demand right now.  remember, your resume is the only 
    impression a company has of you.  one last thing- how flexible are
    you in relocating?  that may be a big factor in a entry-level.


good luck.

-ed
```

```
I doubt the approach I initially took will help you much, but I have learned
a few things over the years.

I was a gung ho, patriotic kid at 16.  Though I was college and scholarship
bound, I decided at 16 I'd enter the military service.  By 18, the fervor of
this interest had waxed, not waned, and so I joined.  I was three days from
signing up in the Marine Corps when I discovered an educational program the
Air Force had that was unique to that service and appealed to me in its
structure and process.  I joined the Air Force.

I was in the service for 11 years and never bothered to go to school; this
was decidedly NOT the wisest element of my service time  :-).  Nonetheless,
I got eleven years' experience on a multitude of platforms, in a number of
languages, and held four distinctly different positions in programming,
database design & administration, help desk/tech support/training and system
management, with a touch of operations thrown in for good measure.

Needless to say, experience wasn't an issue when I went hunting for work in
the "real world".  Unfortunately, the lack of a degree was.  Also, since I'd
joined the service directly from high school, I had no experience in looking
for work -- it had always come to me, except for the military, and they
basically take anybody who can pass the test and for whom there is a job
opening -- especially true in those days.  So knowing WHERE to look was the
thing I had to learn.

I landed my first post-military job in the computer industry by way of a
contract position; my operations experience, of all things, got my foot in
the door.  When the company I was contracted to as an operator turned up
needing a system manager for the platform I'd last managed, they already
knew me and my work ethic, and my experience on the resume' was relatively
impressive, and after an interview process that, as far as I could tell, was
a mere formality, I was in.

This won't help you much, because you don't have the experience yet.

What I learned during that job hunt, however, is that you have to go
seriously proactive.  Here's a few pointers:

Almost every public library has a set of books which contain the STC codes
(I think STC is correct, it's been awhile since I've had to look them up)
where all the companies in the country are organized by the type of business
they are.  Find the code for computer programming or whatever and start
jotting down company names and phone numbers, and, where supplied, the name
of a contact within the company.

While doing this, don't forget your local businesses.  Keep an eye on the
newspaper for companies that are expanding and might use computers in their
business management.  Even working as an office assistant on Word under
Windows 95 will get you experience that you could claim in seeking a help
desk job, for example.  Be creative; don't limit yourself to computer
companies.  Almost everybody uses at least PCs for SOME function.

And, though it is the scourge of the business, don't forget to hit the
Yellow Pages<tm> and hit on all the contractor/temp/headhunter type
companies.  If nothing else turns up, they might land you doing something
really boring with computers which, while paying the rent, also gives
you...EXPERIENCE.  And, to be honest, the next time I went hunting for a
job, I did the full proactive search, and the first thing to turn up was, in
fact, a contractor position as a system manager, and after the initial
contract period, I was taken on board in the same job as a permanent
employee.  So don't knock the professional prostitution rings too soundly --
they can still pay your rent, get you experience, and potentially land you a
job.  They get you in touch with people in the business in any regard, and
from everything I've heard and seen, NETWORKING yourself is still the key to
an easy and successful job search.  It may be WHAT you know and HOW you
present it that lands you the job, but it's WHO you know that gets you the
interview.

Never burn bridges.  If you wind up in a hellhole of a job, make sure you
leave with as many clean links as you can.  You never know when the boss
that pissed you off there may drop your name in his friend's hat when you're
looking for work the next time around.  Most people aren't jerks on purpose;
something generally drives the behavior pattern, such as stress from being
in a bad job, for example.

That's about it.  Best of luck in the hunt!
```

```
    Well, just so you'll know (and I'm hoping I'm not beating a dead horse):
the computer industry is almost unique in the fact that, while it requires
very specialized, knowledge-based skills, education is secondary or tertiary
to finding a good job.  In most other professions such as this, education is
used as a yardstick to measure your competence (who would hire a lawyer
without a law degree, no matter how many years of experience he'd had?).
Not so for engineering.  What matters is your experience and you're
ablities.  I've never (I mean *never*) been asked about my education when
interviewing for a job.  All they've wanted to know is what kind and how
many years of experience I've had.

    I know you're probably saying "Tell me something I don't know!", but
this is just the nature of the computer industry.  With that said, here's
some questions for you:

    First, do you honestly only have 6 months experience with VB and C++?
Six months ago, were you completely clueless about these languages?  If not,
then you've probably had more experience than you realize.  When was the
first time you took a C class?  When was the first time you created a
program in VB?  Don't stretch it too much, of course (I'm sure you know you
should never, never, NEVER lie to get a job), but give yourself a little
credit, too.

    Next, what sort of jobs have you had before?  Were any of them
computer-related?  Did you do any programming for them?  Mention anything
having anything to do with computers that you did in any of your previous
jobs.

    Also, who do you know?  Does a friend or a friend of a friend or college
professor or family member or someone (anyone) know anyone who needs an
entry-level programmer/analyst?  Nepotism is a great way to get a job :-)
And if you don't like getting jobs this way, stop worrying about it:  in the
computer field, if you can't do the work, they'll fire you no matter who you
know or how you got in.  It's all a matter of getting your foot in the door.
Once you're in, though, you're on your own.

    Finally... are you sure you're looking in the right places?  Are you
looking through ads or going to headhunters or what?  Headhunters probably
aren't a good idea when you're starting out because these firms usually
don't want to deal with finding people $40K a year jobs.  They make a % of
what you get paid, so they'll probably only deal with junior- or mid-level
or higher.  Also, don't bother with contracting firms since they normally
don't deal with entry-level.  Have you gone through any programs at school?
There's a lot of companies out there who will hire you directly out of
school and pay you decently (and even pay for your Masters program).  Make
sure you check with your school counsellor for these sorts of programs.

    You may have to take some less-than-glamorous jobs in the beginning just
to get experience (building computers, updating web pages, writing reports,
being the office lackey, etc.).  However, it's definitely worth it :-)  I
had a string of crap jobs, including my own business, before becoming a
"real" engineer.  Each of them (all in computers, of course) gave me more
and more experience until it impressed someone enough and I got hired.  By
that time, my big problem was underestimating my earning potential :-)  I'm
still amazed by how much $$ you can milk out of this industry.  It just
takes getting that first REAL job :-)

    Anyway, hope this helps.  Hang in there; you'll get hired and you'll be
on your way :-)
```

```
i've interviewed new programmers for 30+ years and have rarely seen

having said that, it can also be dangerous... some concerns:

 - showing HOW you designed the program and WHY you designed it that
way are more important to me than the code itself -- although the code
is important. if you have code, also have a structure chart or other
graphic to display architecture. be prepared to defend it -- shows you
have confidence.

 - some institutions and employers may have coding standards that are
archaic... (I've seen such...) and the code you display may be
offensive to a potential employer who follows more current practices -
but this can backfire if it's the other way around and your code is
clean, crisp, top-down design, decomposed, structured -- and the
potential employer still believes that GOTO and EXIT are the only way
to code.  few employers want to hire "experts" who are going to tell
them how to do their jobs... so you can easily get into quagmire here,
either from being ahead or behind....  

 - a major issue -- few employers who interview are close to the
programming process. many jokingly comment that they haven't coded in
years -- and are obviously proud of it. in this case, if you offer to
review your fancy piece of code, the interviewer may send you off to
show to a programmer and, bingo, the interview is over. AND -- the
programmer who looks at your code may see you as a threat... kiss off
the job offer.  

 - on the issue of whether you can take code you wrote for a current
employer.... i believe you can 'scrub' the code from anything
proprietary and turn into a generic piece... bring only one component
so the architure itself is not displayed. by doing this - and
explaining to interviewer why you did it -- you exhibit a sense of
integrity and professionalism that can't be determined by any
questioning... points in your favor...  sensitive issue? yes, so play
it that way.
```

```
Get to know as much as you can about the type of application that you want
to lie about. Putting this on a C.V. should get you that all important
interview. Then if you know your technical stuff, you get a reasonably easy
interview and they don't check your references - your in! You would be
surprised how lax most employers are when it comes to checking these things.

For the record I used this technique to get my first programming job. I was
working as an electronics engineer and wanted to become a programmer. I
learned how to program in C, then claimed that I had written an application

I've since used this approach when I've wanted to gain experience with
different technologies.

MY TOP 10 HINTS ARE

1. Know your programming languages. (do you know how to declare function
that returns a pointer to a function C)

2. Don't be over ambitious. (Say for instance that you only coded the
software and that someone else was responsible for the most of the design)

3. Any claimed experience should be with a stand alone system. (Networked
systems or systems with databases are more complex and harder to lie about)

4. Ask lots of questions as this will limit their time for
asking you awkward questions.

5. Be prepared for questions on the process of developing software. Such as
how did you gather your requirements. How did you manage your configutaion
control. Did you use any design tools. You should have covered this in your
course.

6. Always remain cool, even if you suspect that you've been rumbled.

7. Avoid jobs in the same field as you claimed experience. (Strangely most
employers think programming ability is more
important than expertise in the field of the application)

8. If possible get a job using computers as a user. As describing a system
that you've used is easier than inventing one that you haven't.

9. Remember that anyone who interviews you DOES want to give
you the job. If they didn't want to employ you then they wouldn't be
interviewing you.

10. Play the lottery, with a lot of luck you won't need to get a job.
```
