It is interesting to see how wise the new breed of programmers are.
Perhaps they had better teachers than us (or perhaps hind-sight is
20/20 vision).

But let's look at our early COBOL teachers, some of whom incidentally
claim still to be experts today. Although I never see them making
any postings to this news group or the Year 2000 news group.

If you care to look at Daniel McCracken's A Simplified Guide to Structured
Cobol Programnming (1976) you will see on page 50 that he sets an exercise
for students to decipher part of a record layout for a bank loan. The record
layout is as follows:
....
05 DUE-DATE.
10 MONTH-DUE PIC XX.
10 FILLER PIC X.
10 DAY-DUE PIC XX.
10 FILLER PIC X.
10 YEAR-DUE PIC XX.
05 FILLER PIC X(38).
05 LOAN-BALANCE PIC 99999.99.
-----------------------------------
I don't know about America but in Australia most housing loans are for
25 years. Given that the book was written in 1976 and we were using it as
our COBOL textbook in 1978 I think that Mr McCracken wasn't really setting
a very good example.

However, let's not pick on Mr McCracken alone. Let's look at another
contemporary counterpart Messers Orilla, Stern and Stern. In their
book Business Data Processing Systems (2nd Edition 1977). Take for
example page 96 which has a wonderful picture of a punched card with a
date of the format mmddyy.

Interestingly, in neither of these books is there any discussion of the year
2000 problem or four digit dates. But, in fairness, it was 20 years ago and
who would have thought of that back then. Obviously, not these experts
who were teaching the rest of us.

But let's move forward to the 1990's. If you look in Messers Stern and Stern's
latest effort Structured Cobol Programming (Seventh Edition - 1994) and
look at page 218 you will see an example of a date format as mm/dd/yy. Incidentally
this is in the section that talks about 'accepting from date' but there is still no
mention of four digit years and the year 2000 problem. Basically they are
just churning out edition after edition of the same old stuff, in fact, do Messers
Stern and Stern really exist or is it just a name made up by the publisher.

My point in writing all this drivvle, by the way, is that you shouldn't be too
hard on old Cobol programmers and designers, after all, look at the fine
examples we had teaching us. Stern and Stern still hadn't recognised the
problem in 1994.

I am not saying that this excuses us, but I think it is trivialising the problem
to think that Cobol programmers were so dumb that we didn't think of four digit
years. We just didn't think our programs would last that long.

And if you think we were so dumb, I'm willing to bet that our 20 year old programs
will still be around next century while your C++ GUI front ends will be replaced
in the next two years by Java and then by Java**.
