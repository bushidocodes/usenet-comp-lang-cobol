The system I work on is about 25 or 30 years old and, from what I've been
told, was based on an Insurance Admininstration system developed by IBM.

All of our internal dates are:
   YYY      PIC S9(03) COMP-3
   DDD     PIC S9(03) COMP-3

YYY is an offset from 1800. eg X'199C'
DDD are the Julian days

If it weren't for the auditors and certification we would have had
practically no Y2K work to do.

I would be interested in any comments as to why this technique was
(apparently) not used to any great extent.

Lanny

If your going to store dates in expanded EBCDIC/ASCII format you shouldn't
switch to this format just because its easier. Its almost as easy as using a
sliding window for the century or even a constantly updated fixed window.

But the main advantage in adding century characters is that it always
works.  It will work when the requirements change.   Since requirements
do change, why not code the year logic just once.  I can see people
saying that school kids have to be under 100 years old and they will be
usually right.   Make 100 assumptions like that though, and I guarantee
one of them will be wrong.  Or the law or business rules will change.

It's sort of like deciding how large of a table we will need in our
COBOL program to handle future needs.  First, decide how expensive it
would be to eliminate the table altogether, replacing it with logic
which won't break down if things change too much.
