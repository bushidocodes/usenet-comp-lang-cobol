First of all, go where the money is, and right now it seems to be in active
Server Pages, VB, and SQL server.

Just remember that where ever Microsoft documentation uses the word "object"
you can substitute "thingie" and it will make more sense.

Second, on a theoretical level,  I have to say what's easier, 'C' or COBOL,
depends on what you are trying to do. 

'C' is great for small routines that do small logically discrete mathmatical
tasks.  You want a little item to calculate area of a circle and display it,
'C' is the way to go.
'C' is also more vesatile in some respects, and runs less overhead in terms of
setting up data areas and libraries for small tasks.

However, when you go to large systems of logically complex ( or in the case of
many operational systems, irrational) programs and tasks, COBOL is far easier
to work with.  

It's sort of like the difference between a hand-axe and a chain saw: if you
just want
to chop down a sapling and split a little cordwood, the hand axe is a nice way
to go.  But if you want to log several acres, then you go  to the trouble of
getting out that big old chainsaw, the gas, oil, sharpener, kevlar gloves.
goggles,  & all the other things.

And if you have to, you can pound nails with an axe- don't recommend you try
that with a chainsaw.
Nathan Meyer
Berkeley, CA

You've had the various "it depends" answers already so just take that as 
read, as well as the detail included in the other answers.

What is it about C that you don't like?

I know both languages and prefer COBOL by far but that is because I've 
never done mathematics past the age of fifteen. I really don't understand 
mathematicians' banter, nor do I particularly want to, and to see things 
that I know expressed in mathematical terms leaves me cold.

One of the original principles behind COBOL was that it should be readily 
understood by anyone who can read English, regardless of whether or not 
they were technically trained.  So if you can read or write half-decent 
English, you're on a start already.  It's shifted a bit since those days, 
but it can still be reasonably understood by the average Joe.

Like most regulars in this newsgroup, I work in a business environment 
where the problems/challenges are to automate operational business 
processes and adjust programs to cope with new requirements.  Most of our 
programs produce neatly processed data from raw data for various people 
in various forms such as management reports, screens for the desk clerk, 
etc.  It's a far wider variety than this but I'm trying to keep it 
relatively simple.

I understand these processes much more easily because they affect my 
daily life in one way or another and I would say that there is no genuine 
rival to COBOL for programming these systems.

There are quite a few small COBOL shops, and I daresay they are pretty 
friendly places to work in, but most COBOL programmers work in big-ish 
offices with a lot of colleagues and often there is a fairly good social 
life in these places with shop-talk usually "banned" outside the 
workplace.

One other thing to bear in mind, COBOL programmers learn and use a lot 
more than COBOL in their programming, such as application tools.  I'd 
guess that most of know how to work with databases, for example, and how 
to embed SQL or... well the list can get pretty long.  C programmers tend 
to stay immersed in C for ever with maybe an occasional trip into C++ or 
other C-like languages.

There's a lot to learn either way but it's more likely that your first 
program will be to solve a small business process than to display "Hello 
World".  So, the question is, where would you feel comfortable - with 
maths or with English?

Please excuse me for getting off the subject a little...

Regarding the glut of Y2K COBOL programmers on the market, I believe
the glut will be short lived, say, 1 or 2 years.  By then the ones
that have not found COBOL jobs will have re-tooled themselves into
other languages, or else they will be out of the programming business.

Then we will be right back in a state of balance where there will
simply be hiring shortages for *all* languages and technologies!

"So how come there seem to always be unemployed programmers?" is the
next logical question.  My response to this is that most corporations
don't want to train anyone.  They only want people that can "hit the
road running", with many years of experience in the technologies that
the company cares about.   Meeting these hire requirements gets
tougher every year as corporations invest in multiple platforms and
multiple programming languages.

I think the challenge for young programmers today is the ability to
convince corporations that they can learn quickly enough to be of
value to the company.
