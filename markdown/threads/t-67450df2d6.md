November 13, 1998                                      J4/98-0587


SUBJECT: (Quality Review) - Who is the Standard for?

FROM:       William M. Klein (wmklein@ix.netcom.com)

REFERENCES:

     1.   Base document CD 1.4, June 1998

COMMENTS:

During  several  informal conversations both in person  and  via
Usenet  Newsgroups, it has become clear to  me  that  I  have  a
different view of the "target audience" of the Standard than  do
several  others involved (often more intimately than I)  in  the
Standard�s  development  process.  Although  this  may  seem   a
somewhat  esoteric or hypothetical issue for this late  in  this
Standard�s development, I truly believe that spending  a  little
(not  too much) time on this issue can easily save time  in  the
long run.  I know that there are several outstanding issues that
I  would  have very different opinions on than I do,  should  it
become  clear that I have misunderstood who the current audience
is.

The  view  with  which I do NOT agree is that  the  Standard  is
primarily  (possibly  exclusively - but  I  haven�t  heard  that
expressed in so many words) for the vendors and implementors.

Although,  I certainly agree that vendors and implementors  will
(and do) need to use this document, I have never thought of them
as  its  PRIMARY target.  Now, I don�t think that there  is  any
question  that  the  COBOL language itself (as  opposed  to  the
Standard  document) is intended for the "public" -  but  I  have
always  believed they were also the primary target  audience  of
the document itself.

I  know  that many text writers, course developers,  and  cross-
platform  oriented programmers and developers have in  the  past
and  continue to use the existing Standard.  However, it  is  my
belief  that  with  this next Standard, the  built-in  increased
cross-platform  and  cross-language  capabilities  of  the   new
Standard,  and the growing interest in "open systems"  that  the
next  Standard  will be the reference manual  for  significantly
more  programmers.  Of course, the Standard isn't now and  never
will  be  a  "Programmer�s  Guide" -  but  it  is  an  excellent
reference manual - and I think should be enhanced as such.

Should  WG4  give direction that they agree with  me  that  this
document is not and should not be targeted primarily at vendors,
then  I can think of at least two (outstanding) issues that this
might impact:

1)   Should the composite language skeleton be kept (and made as
  accurate as possible)?  Obviously, if you agree with those who
  think  that  the primary target for the Standard  document  is
  vendors and that programmers will only (or primarily) use  the
  reference  manuals  that the vendors supply  (and  that  those
  developing courses and text books will just HAVE TO WAIT until
 some vendor comes out with a document to use), then this section
  of  the Standard does seem to be "nice" but not necessary.  On
  the other hand, if you are trying to create a document intended
  for  authors, programmers, course developers, etc (as well  as
  implementors), then its usefulness seems apparent.

2)    Many  terms  have changed in this Standard  from  previous
  Standards (for example, removal of "verbs;" "headers" becoming "markers;"
"increment and decrement values" becoming something that  I still don�t
understand; "COBOL character set" becoming  "COBOL  character  repertoire").
Again, if  the  Standard  is  targeted primarily at vendors, it is probably
reasonable to let
  them (and their documentation writers) try and find and figure
  out all the terminology changes.  However, if the Standard  is
  supposed  to be useful for authors and course developers  (and
  programmers who try and communicate with others familiar  with
  different  products  and platforms), then  it  really  becomes
  imperative to highlight each and every one of these changes in
  the "Substantive Changes - not affecting" annex.

It  is  my  guess that there may be other issues where I  simply
have not understood the J4 recommendations that would make sense to me,
should it turn out that the WG4 direction is to produce a "vendor-targeted"
document.   (In  fact,  I  think  the  entire
concepts annex could be removed or at least pruned, should  this
be  true.)  Similarly, if clear cut direction is given to J4  to insure
that this turns out to be as useful a document (still  a reference  manual
and not a programming guide) targeted  at  the entire COBOL-oriented public,
then I think this might make  them reconsider some previously made
recommendations and to  reorient some of their current work.

I have lots of problems with the Standard committee (as regular readers of
this NG know) and wish there were more "users" on it (at this weeks J4
meeting, 4 of 12 "members" were represented by users - which isn't too bad).
HOWEVER,  I want to make one thing very clear,

In my experience, there has been little (not none - but truly a minimal
amount) of vendors fighting for features or definitions of features - JUST
because their current implementations support it.

As the person who PROPOSED the addition of "GoBack" into the next Standard
(and later proposed that the function get the name "goback"),  I can tell
you that I have never (and do not) currently work for IBM!

In fact, the biggest problem with vendors and the current work of the
committee is that so much is being made "non-portable" by calling it
"implementor defined" or "processor dependent" (which now basically means
"optional").  However, it was two of the user representatives at the recent
J4 meeting who made a real point of saying,  "I don't think this should be
defined in the Standard, just let my implementor figure out how to do it
best on their machine with their operating system."  (This really shocked
and depressed me - but didn't completely surprise me.)

One other comment that I need to make about the increased "non-portability"
of Standard COBOL is that I understand well the origin of much of it.  One
of the major goals of this Standard is to provide both the functionality of
and inter-operability with the C programming language.   One of the
historical advantages of COBOL over C was that the run-time results of COBOL
were always much more portable than those of C (although the rest of the
world has called C "portable").  Once you try to add these non-portable C
functionality and interfaces to COBOL, you are "stuck" with making them
equally un-portable in COBOL - just so that you can correctly interface with
all the non-portable C implementations.  I am not saying that this "change"
in COBOL is good, but I do see it as (probably) necessary in a world where
most interfaces and APIs are defined for the C-oriented.

they are the ones represented on the committees, so their view prevails.

Much of the problem is that programming language standards are developed
under a system developed for other types of standards.  The earliest
standards were weights and measures.  Though everyone uses them, it
doesn't make much difference exactly how big a gallon or a barrel or a
meter is, as long as everyone can refer to the same standard.  Later came
quality standards (how large is a large olive), manufacturing standards
(plugs and jacks, screw threads), and interface standards (MIDI, SCSI).

into SCSI devices, but I have not read the SCSI standard, not do I intend
to.  The only people who need to read the actual SCSI standard are
engineers designing devices.  Even the engineer is usually happy that
there is a standard pin ordering, and spends little time ruminating over
how a different ordering would make his job easier.

lives in.  When the 1974 Cobol standard said that "ADD A TO B GIVING C"
was an invalid statement, it was not enough that every implementor knew to
give it a syntax error, but a million programmers had to learn (usually
after half an hour of staring in disbelief at an error message) that it
was invalid.  When the 1985 standard corrected this anomaly, they didn't
do it for the implementors, they did it for the programmers. 

It is wrong for the committee to put the implementors ahead of users.  The
paucity of user input in the standard development process is a major cause
of this.  At this point, I want to see the current draft pushed out the
door as soon as possible, even with a few of the existing warts.  But the
Cobol standard development process is flawed; we need to create a new
process for subsequent Cobol standards, and it should be centered around
the users, not the implementors.
