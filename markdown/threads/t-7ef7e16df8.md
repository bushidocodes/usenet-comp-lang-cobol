Macroeconomic Thesis for y2k

This is an essay demonstrating the method and impact of large scale macro
economic collapse caused by the year 2000 computer bug. This is made under
the assumption that a large percentage of government agencies, businesses
and financial institutions will not fully complete repairs before the
January 1, 2000 deadline. At present, this indeed appears to be the case.

It must first be stressed that the year 2000 bug (or y2k) ,is firmly built
in to virtually every computer system in the world (but not every individual
computer). There are an estimated 1 trillion lines of date dependant cobol
computer code world-wide. There are also 25 billion embedded micro chips of
which 1.5% to 5% are not year 2000 compliant. We are now in a world and
society that is heavily dependant on computers. They are literally
everywhere, and are intertwined to a degree that boggles the mind.

In order to understand the macroeconomic scope of the y2k problem, the issue
of systems must be addressed. The modern world's economy is a vast living
system of sub-systems where each component requires each other to function.
There must also be a plentiful input of various critical resources such as
oil, minerals, investment capital, transportation and free flowing transfer
of information. Eliminate any one of these inputs and the "system"
collapses.

Cities are also living systems. They depend on a constant ,tremendous input
of goods and critical resources. Cities are akin to parasites feeding off
the vast country-side around it. City dwellers depend on food shipped from
the surrounding rural areas. The fuel to transport and harvest crops and
produce is found and refined elsewhere. Minerals used to build urban
structures and the vehicles used to transport these items is brought in from
elsewhere. Vast investment capital is required to finance these endeavors.
Finance is now dependant and interconnected through electronic means. It is
a necessity. Every large institution, government, corporation and each of
their subsidiaries are all interconnected and dependant on one another. If
one goes down, the rest are brought down with it.

The y2k computer bug can be likened to a virus built in to all these
computer systems. Any one computer apparatus must be FULLY converted; every
date dependant line of code must be repaired, or will otherwise infect the
remaining code and program structure. If a company is to remain viable, ALL
it's computers must be fully converted in order to function with one
another. The company's survival depends on it. While many are doing a last
minute scramble to fix their systems, many (if not most) just won't make it,
and are faced with over night extinction. (see:'Too Late to Fix y2k')

Entities who are fully converted cannot deal with those who are still y2k
infected. A descriptive analogy is a case of two friends who cannot meet
each other because one has the flu or chicken pox.

The situation becomes infinitely greater and more disturbing when dealing
with larger integrated systems as an economy or world trade and finance. In
order for a large-scale structure consisting of many systems and sub-systems
to function, they ALL must be virus free...Exchange of data must be
congruent and compliant between electronic structures. In other words, we
may be facing a plague that could bring everything down. I mean EVERYTHING.
In the case of the y2k bug, a troubling situation arises. Not only must an
entity (company or government) be fully compliant in and of itself, but it
cannot deal with any non-compliant entity without corrupting itself.

In dealing with an economy based on multi-levels of interconnected systems,
isolation means death. But so does dealing with y2k corrupt systems. Most
companies don't have a choice of refusing business with one another, and not
dealing with companies who are y2k infected entities.....lest they go
bankrupt (which would happen if they went into isolation.)

If many sectors of business ,government and banks don't complete the y2k
conversion, (alarmingly, this will be the case) We are facing a total
system-wide financial and economic collapse of the world's economy. A severe
displacment in the division in labor must occur thereafter.


>From this analysis we can deduce that any 'system' dependant on the y2k bug,
and any other system reacting to or involved (directly or indirectly) will
fail and breakdown. Hence, we are facing not only a collapse of the current
financial economy, but most urban city systems as well.

I wish a different conclusion could be reached. Sadly ,this is not the case.
