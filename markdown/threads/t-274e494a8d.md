I didn't bother finishing the over-long
article that you sent me because it's just
too idiotic. This guy is using selective
information -- typical for an academic with
an axe to grind.

Obviously the out-of-work programmers
he quotes are a bunch of losers.
Everybody working in a professional shop
knows (probably works for!) people who
are holding down jobs who've been out of
the information/skills loop for years!
Eventually these people make retirement
age or get laid off. For the time being
they're still managing to hold on to their
positions as project leaders -- positions
they earned using skills in outdated
products like PacBase and other code
generators. When they do loose their
jobs, they're out for good. Should that be
surprising?

Does this fact indicate that there is not a
shortage of programmers in the market at
this time? No. It indicates that their is a
shortage of skilled programmers at this
time.

Something the author doesn't seem to
understand (or is loath to acknowledge) is
that his university shop environment
(where programs are written per the
curricula and the test data used to drive
them is refreshed at the beginning of each
semester) bears little resemblance to the
industry he's training his students for. He
also doesn't acknowledge that an industry
perception of being able to survive on a
steady diet of newly graduated 'computer
science majors' works to his advantage.

As for the situation of the corporate
recruiter: faced with a choice between a
mediocre 15-year-man with outdated skills
who apparently made his way up the
ladder to his $70,000 salary through
politics and guile, and a grad or foreigner
who has yet to develop the comfortable
work-ethics of the American upper-middle
class, who do you think will get the job?

Besides, if the company represented by
the recruiter is forward looking at all, it
has an in-house training program in place
to compensate for the lack of experience
and range of skills the grad/foreigner
possesses as the result of the inadequate
training he received at whatever
institution he had the misfortune to attend.

If I were a career counselor at a university I
would strongly advise the Computer
Science Degree candidate to take the
minimum of courses required by his major
in the field. Devote all of your electives to
one of the liberal arts. It will increase your
value in the job market much more in the
long run, than taking a 400 level course in
Visual Basic. The training program of the
company that hires you and the
experience of working in a shop who's
requirements are dictated by the needs of
the marketplace, will teach you what you
really need to know about programming.

P.S. The 2% hire-per-application figure
for MicroSoft cited by the author is no
doubt analogous to the situation that
prevails at a large publishing house or a
highly influential technical or literary
journal. Tens of thousands of submissions
are received each year by people who just
don't make the grade.

By the way, what influential journal was
this article published in?
