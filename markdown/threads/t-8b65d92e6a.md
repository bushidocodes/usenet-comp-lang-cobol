If you're like me, you're getting tired of fighting the GOTO statement
in this NG. Here is something else that bothers me as I see it used
frequently and I abhor its use. It is the use of the period after
every statement.

To me, the period is a syntax element of the 1960's when people were
pretending/hoping that COBOL programs would read like a memo.
(Seriously, stop by a library and pick up a text from the time -- such
as a McCracken textbook -- and look at the 'memo format' code with
IF's and GOTO's and god-knows-what, all rambling end-to-end with no
indentation guidelines. It's enough to make you wonder how we ever
survived... ) Pretending programs were composed of sentences and
paragraphs was innocent and wrong.

In today's world, the period is never needed and is the cause of many,
many program bugs. Instead of finding the best place to put a period,
I have for years taught new programmers to not use it at all --
relying instead on their program code for control.

The only requirement for a period is at the end of a paragraph and
many compilers will note this and insert one for you. The period
stops a logic flow -- that makes it a problem. The entire concept of
'NEXT SENTENCE' is problematic. Fortunately, newer versions of COBOL
give us the CONTINUE statement.

Now, you're NOT going to convince me that periods are necessary
but I felt it might be at least a different topic to consider.
Thoughts????

note: my technique is to place periods on a line by themselves, as
with all other verbs. This eliminates the problem of rearranging
periods when introducing new logic to a program module.

<>

Whereever did you get that notion? Not true. OS/VS Cobol & Cobol II pgms
can (and in my application, do) LINK & XCTL to each other without regard to
the other's arrtibutes. CICS keeps (from CICS/ESA 3 or so and on) COMMAREAs
in 31-bit storage between quasi-conversational tasks. When your pgm is
running it has addressability to the 31-bit COMMAREA if you're AMODE(31);
otherwise, CICS makes a copy of the COMMAREA into 24-bit storage and passes
this to your pgm. Absolutely no problems at the CICS level mixing the two
Cobols; HOWEVER, it's up to the programmers to make sure that 31-bit values
aren't passed to pgms in AMODE(24). So, if you want to pass a record
address from Cobol II (let's assume it's AMODE(31)) to OS/VS Cobol in the
COMMAREA, make sure you GETMAIN storage below the 16MB line. It's not very
hard to do - just sounds that way. Also, so no feels the need to correct
me, the two COMMAREAs need to be defined correctly, i.e., USAGE POINTER in
the Cobol II pgm and PIC S9(9) COMP in the OS/VS Cobol pgm.

Hope this is helpful,
Bill Lynch

symbol is called a "period" in the USA.

have something to do with the language difference between over here and
over there. "Period" merely implies a pause, whereas "full-stop"
specifies a termination.

make COBOL be like other, less-suitable, programming languages?
