I have a some writeups on some of the issues that I plan to put up

Basically, the trick amounts to this: if supply is tight, everyone
who has some will have theirs bought at virtually any price.  If
you build a modern, efficient plant, you can sell its output at
the same price -- or on lower-load days, just below the price --
commanded by an old, inefficient plant.  The modern, efficient
plant costs less than half as much to run as the old one.  The old
one is of course fully justified in charging exorbitant rates,
because they have to pay a great deal just for the fuel to run that
plant.  With wholesale rates like $300/MWh (.30/kWh), a 500 MW
plant run for 10 hours produces $1.5 million in revenue.  Subtracting
operating costs leaves the modern plant with a profit of over
$750000 every ten hours (vs some tiny profit at the old plant).

As soon as the new plant goes up, of course, the old plant stops
being able to sell at $300/MWh on low-load days.  But it costs
them almost $300/MWh just to run, so they stop being able to make
any money at all.  Hence, the old-plant-owner is very much against
having the new plant go up (and put them out of business).

Fundamentally my personal opinion is that the FCD, as for each of the
standards before it, is of enormous value, regardless of how many vendors
actually implement compilers 100% compliant with it.  For example, "In-line
comments" and "compiler directives" would be welcome additions to *any*
COBOL compiler  as an extension, regardless of the standard to which that
compiler (unextended) complied.  Should a '74 compiler "flag" inline
comments?  Well, yeah, arguably, but how many users of that feature would
actually *object* to its very presence in the compiler?    The vital,
critical importance of the standard is not how many *vendors* produce
compilers 100% compliant with it but rather how many *users* make use of
some of its features in production code.  The latter does *not* require a
100%-compliant compiler, only one that is compliant for the subset of syntax
