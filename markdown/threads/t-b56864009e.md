```
I foresee the time when we do not call phone numbers.  We won't even
call phones, we will call people or their electronic agents.

But your calling software has to be smart enough to know who you are
wanting to call, and certainly your receiving software (agent) has to be
very flexible.

The agent will look at features of the caller and call, check to see
what you're doing, and then decide whether to buzz, ring, leave voice
mail, leave e-mail, ask the caller to phone back later, report the
caller to the authorities, or tell the caller to bug off.

When you get back to your computer, you browse through messages and
listen to the voice mail you desire, or read your agent's description. 
Getting phone calls will be better.

Sending calls?  I suppose this might be harder to set up.  Once you
establish a nick name, you would use it, but you might have to say "call
my wife as soon as both she and I are back at our desks with our
computers on and my boss isn't in my cubicle with me".

So what will we save in our business databases?  We don't want to be
tracked as spammers in a customer's database.  Our database will
probably use an agent to talk to the customer's agent periodically to
check on data currency.  Our customer will have privacy rules which have
been customized for different callers, but he might be willing to let us
query on how much soap his rest rooms have.

Don't you hate it when a salesman for the newspaper you already
subscribe phones you to offer you a great deal (which isn't offered to
existing customers)?  Why don't they KNOW you are a customer?
```

```
However you implement it, make sure this is a variable (easily changed,
too). I'll bet the Cleveland arrangement (no "1" prefix) will change
when the demand for new numbers gets to a certain level. IIRC, we had
the same setup locally (NYC) when TPC first broke NYC into multiple area
codes, 212 & 718 the first time around. Originally NYC was all 212, then
Brooklyn, Queens & Staten Island were split off into 718, then The Bronx
was split off into 718, too, leaving just Manhattan using 212. Next TPC
added another area code, which is pagers & cell phones (my cell phone &
my son's cell phone (he lives in Brooklyn) are both 917), my best
friends' cell phone (who also lives in Brooklyn) is 646). NYC is an
extreme situation because of the explosive demand for pagers, cell
phones, fax machines, etc. for business use; but it's the direction
other areas are headed, IMHO. On the bright side, the calls amongst
these area codes are still local calls.
```
