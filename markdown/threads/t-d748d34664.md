As a corporate consultant myself, Howard I have great sympathy for this
argument.

It is difficult to persuade corporates to take risky steps. Your credibility
is on the line and if they fail (for whatever reason) possibly your job too.
(While I don't have a career to worry about, I do have a track record to
consider. At least if I want to keep working (and I do for a few years yet).
I suffer from the same anxiety as anybody else in this situation, namely, if
you screw up they only remember the one you got wrong and immediately forget
all the stuff you did right...<G>. I guess this keeps us honest, but it can
also tie us into not taking risks when we arguably should.)

However, there ARE demonstrable positive benefits from bringing in OO. Even
OO COBOL. While it is wordier and more unwieldy than Java, it DOES retain
all the accepted benefits of COBOL, plus it provides the accepted benefits
of OO in general (which Java programmers take for granted and which we have
debated at length in this forum...). There ARE risks (the learning curve can
be horrific) but, provided the risks are evaluated and managed, the rewards
can be well worth it.

I think the answer is small, low risk, pilot projects to get programmers
around the learning curve. Once a few of these are under your belt and
people see how maintenance can be eased by Class extension (for example) it
is possible to generate some enthusiasm for the new paradigm.

If it doesn't start somewhere it will never happen.

(In this event many people will say, "So what...we don't need it. We get
along OK with what we're doing..." This fails to take into account the fact
that the technical and Business environments are in the process of radical
change. If you close your eyes to these changes there won't BE an Enterprise
to continue doing what it has always done, ITwise...)

The clincher is that if your vendor doesn't encourage OO COBOL, and it looks
like IBM are definitely in this category, then you are shovelling shit
uphill before you start.

If there is no compiler available then there is no argument...
