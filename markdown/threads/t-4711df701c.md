If your thinking of taking courses on Cobol just to get at the
Y2K problem in that language, wake up!
Reality is, no-one is going to hire you, you're wasting your
time and money.
Any course that doesn't give you hands on Mainframe Cobol for
compling and de-bugging on a mainframe IBM is not going to get
you onto an IBM mainframe doing Cobol Y2k changes. The same
applies to all other Mainframes and their languages.

These people pushing correspondance courses or courses at
Joe Blow's Business College on a PC/LAN are selling you a
dream and laughing all the way to the bank.

Reality is:
You MUST have a minimum 6 months hands-on work experience in
that language and on that platform before you will be hired
for anything as critical as the Y2K problem.
There is no time for errors, the deadline can't be extended.

Almost all I.S. jobs go through a placement agency, most of
these will not give you the time of day unless you have 2 yrs
hands-on work experience. Phone an agent and ask them.

A course must be from a University or Poly-Tech College for it
to be recognized by employers or agencies as worth anything and
most of these courses take 2 years or more.

I've been around this industry for 25 years, I know these things.
Your just too late, sorry.

Bill H.

"A company with half a brain....". If companies had 'half a brain', and
behaved like a responsible adult; they would not be I this fix in the
first place, wouldn't you agree? :)

I tended to express this behavior on the part of corporate America prior to
around 1985,(probably worst in other parts of the world) as most likened to
a human who is half brain dead. However, since 1985, I would now liken
their behavior as being totally brain dead - not just in regard to the Y2K
problem that has now gained wider recognition but in most matters regarding
C.S. .

I spent 2 years early in my career working for a computer hardware vendor,
CDC, providing professional training to their internal staff and to their
clients on virtually all of their product lines - which included training
on using all their compilers, using all their operating systems, all
management courses at all levels - and all their 'guts' courses on the
internals of all their operating systems.

In addition, because of my extensive professional training, I was also used
as both a internal consultant on CDC matters and as a consultant to CDC
customers.

I could do all this because I was professionally trained in all this stuff
- and worked my butt off to fill in the large gaps in knowledge that these
courses left out. My usual work week back then averaged 60 hours per week
- on a salary. CDC got it's money worth with me.

But I was not the only one who was given this professional training. There
were five of us who were trained in al this stuff. Each of us were in
charge of one of the five regional areas CDC split the USA into at that
time. My region was the entire North East region - all of New England, all
of New York State, and the Eastern half of Pa.

All CDC courses were provided based on a 8 hour lecture day.
This was unique since IBM's lecture day was about 1.5 to 2.0 hours per day.

A majority of our students had education levels of BS and up. My point
being that I know a little something of how professional training is
provided at the corporate level.

In general, the professional training level of our corporate clientel -
including internal to CDC was nothing short of deplorable.

IMHO, based on my experience in those two years and the over 27 years since
then, most corporate 'professional' level people in corporate America had
no special train or received the usual 'on the job training.

In short, and in general, while corporate America insists that it's
'professional' level personnel have tons of formal education prior to
being hired by them; they show little indication of valuing any continuing
professional education for their professional employees.

Such professional training as they might provide on occasion is usually
directly related to the widgets they build or sell; and not usually of a
nature that one of their employees might be able to bring to another
employer.

Training in Cobol - will it be valuable for the Y2K problem in general -
yes. But which Cobol and on what operating system, on what computer
hardware - and where will people get the books they need and access to the
hardware and operating systems necessary to become effective?

Why just focus on Cobol language? How about RPG and PL-1 and all the 3rd
party packages companies purchased over the last 35 years.

How about the C language since around 1983 and C++ since around 1990. How
about Pascal and assemblers used on a lot of computers.

My point is that in order for any programmer to be effective they must also
understand the operating systems being used since all operating systems
since the mid 1960's use central I/O - meaning all application code must
request I/O through the operating system.

For Cobol alone on a IBM S/360/370/390 a programmer would have to have
access to hard copy reference manuals that would fill up a small bookcase;
similarly for other computer languages and computers and their operating
systems. And we all know that vendor reference manuals are not tutorials.
Reference manuals do not teach.

"Get training in Cobol", is that 'good', sure! Now tell them where to get
effective training in Cobol - and the rest of the stuff they will need.

If it is true that 60 percent of the programmers in the world are still
doing Cobol; and there is this huge shortage of such programmers that may
allegedly pull C/C++, Pascal, etc programmers into it - after they get some
training somewhere - that seems to mean that a whole lot of trainees will
be doing Y2K stuff.

Like you, I also program in assembly language on IBM S/360's through the
S/390's and about 30 other assemblers as well. Sure, like you I did some
machine language work and machine language patches - on many different
machines.

How many of us do you think are still around who know how to do this.
Enough to deal with ALL the Y2K machine language./assembler problems ?
Somehow I do not think so.

In general, "supply and demand" is suppose to determine hourly rates or
salary. The rarer the job skill set, the higher the demand and pay scale
is for that job skill set.

I just thought I would do a bit more extrapolation on some of what you have
been reporting.

May I re-iterate. My national rate surveys for business programmers were
done in May, 1997 and periodically over the last 2 years more recently. I
am still not seeing significant rate increases nor a demand for large
numbers of business type programming positions - with 900+ days left to go
- and the clock is counting. This does not bode well IMHO.

Knowledge of:
The operating system:
Text editor
Compiler
Batch mechanisms
Interactive stuff (CICS, etc)
Sort-merge utility
Database services
How to use the terminal/network/e-mail

IS in general
Accounting basics
How to use & write documentation
Systems analysis methodologies
What an end-of-month/year/quarter cycle works
Testing procedures & how to automate them

The business:
Where the bathroom/copier/etc are
What the real (not stated) priorities are
How to get support (an extra PC for a few days, etc)
Who really knows what is going on

probably. Some will be OK and some won't. Then tell yourself (softly)
