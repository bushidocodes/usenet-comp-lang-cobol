(First, a disclaimer: is there a newsgroup dedicated specifically to
COBOL on MVS platforms?)

My impression is that IBM OS/VS COBOL, VS COBOL II, and IBM COBOL for
MVS and VS (formerly known as COBOL/370) all provide somewhat different
means for programs to obtain current date/time. Command level CICS
adds a wrinkle, as does a Y2K PTF for COBOL II.

We currently don't license COBOL for MVS and VM, although I suppose
it's somewhat inevitable.

Anyway, here's the question: does it make sense to write a routine
(probably Assembler) that returns the current date & time (maybe in
your choice of 6 or 7 popular formats) and make that routine the
"standard" for the entire shop? My rationale is that this "standard"
could be used with existing and future COBOL compilers. If we modified
existing VS COBOL II programs now, to use this routine, it might be one
less issue in a future migration to COBOL for MVS/VM...

Is this how other sites handle the issue? Does the voice of experience
wish to comment?

I'm personally frustrated by the lack of intrinsic DATE, TIME, and
TIMESTAMP functions in COBOL II (ala DB2). I think some vendors have
included such functions (Microfocus? Wang?), using a base date of 1/1/1600
or something like that.

To the best of my recollection, all of the IBM COBOL compilers since COBOL
D had a DATE special register (PIC 9(6) yymmdd), accessible via the ACCEPT
verb.

Your idea makes more since to me than the scalar approach that my shop is
using.

At my shop we use the common assembly language date routine approach,
but not for Y2K reasons. We have an architecture implemented in
assembler that does almost all of the non-business logic
(Checkpointing, restarts, scheduling, workload balancing, report
distribution, and lots of utility type service functions. Actual
application programs are almost pure business logic. Date retrieval
and calculations (increments, differences, validation, formatting) are
a part of this architecture. We are also lucky that our designers
standardized on four digit centuries throughout all of our data bases
over ten years ago. But having lucked into this, I can tell you that
it is a great relief that Y2K and the various COBOL compiler upgrades
are trivial changes in this system. As a bonus, performance is
outstanding. I would encourage you in using a standard assembly
language date utility.

It's probably heresy for all the "one language does everything"
sector of our community, but I think that there's nothing wrong with
utility routines such as Larry mentions.

To my mind, a series of tight date routines - rather than a single
all-singing, all dancing routine - is preferrable. That way, you do
not call a routine that has to make choices based on passed
parameters every time that it is called.

Once you have these routines in place, they can be called by any
language, or language variant, that you care to be using at any given
time, thus giving you an element of independence - other than
platform independence. You don't have to worry about the next
compiler writer's foibles or the next Standards Committee's ruling.

One other advantage of this approach is that you are in control of
the situation though some may see this as a disadvantage...
