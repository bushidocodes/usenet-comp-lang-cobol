Bill Gunshannon wrote:
> 
> No, it does not.  Tenure is something offered to Full Professors by

At most US universities, you do not have to be a Full Professor to be
tenured. Most US universities tenure professors at the Associate
level; promotion to Full comes later, if at all. Tenure is often
granted in conjunction with a promotion from Assistant to Associate,
but there's no necessary connection between the two.

Tenure (again, at the typical US university) is a status that may be
awarded to "tenure-track" professors, as opposed to fixed-term and
adjunct professors. Tenure track is a feature of the professor's
contract; obviously, most professors consider tenure-track jobs more
desirable, because they offer better job security, as well as more
prestige and typically better benefits. In many institutions,
fixed-term and adjunct faculty can't hold various administrative
roles, can't sit on graduate committees, etc.

Tenure-track professors must apply for tenure (the usual idiom is "go
up for tenure"). Typically this happens in their fourth year at the
Assistant level. Most institutions have a "tenure clock" for
tenure-track faculty that requires them to go up for tenure after a
certain number of years on the job; the clock can be temporarily
halted for reasons such as medical leave, and faculty who perform
particularly well can go up early.

You go up for tenure by submitting a file of your qualifications to
the Promotion and Tenure Committee of the department where your tenure
line resides. (Some people have split tenure lines and have to go
before two committees; some people have tenure lines that are owned
elsewhere in the university hierarchy, eg by a non-departmental unit
in a college.) The file - we're probably talking a banker's box or two
of material - demonstrates your work in three areas: teaching,
research, and service (administrative work, work in the field such as
editing journals, etc). The mix of work is expected to hit targets
that vary from university to university and unit to unit; research
universities require more research than do teaching universities, for
example. The file also goes to a couple (or more) outside reviewers,
people in your field at other universities (or retired, etc). They
write letters supporting (you hope) your tenure case; those go into
the file too. And you write your own letter explaining why you deserve
tenure.

The P&T Committee - a handful of people from your department,
typically elected on an annual basis - evaluates your tenure case. In
the old days this would have taken place in solemn secrecy in a
smoke-filled room, but in our enlightened modern age smoking is
prohibited. If they like what they see, they recommend you for tenure.
Your chair conveys this, and your whole freakin' tenure file, to the
college housing your unit. A committee *there* reviews it and if so
inclined pass it on to the Provost's office. The Provost says yea or
nay. At some universities the President also gets a say - notoriously,
at Harvard the President can override everyone else either way, and
sometimes does.

So, 'round about the end of the academic year, you'll find out
(typically back-channel, with a formal announcement a bit later)
whether you were tenured.

Tenure basically means you can't be dismissed from your job for
anything less than a major infraction of university policy or
conviction for a major crime, and even then only after a pretty
complex process is followed. I've seen tenured professors be fired for
sexual misconduct and then successfully sue for their jobs back
because the university didn't follow process. The idea, of course, is
that tenure protects intellectual freedom because tenured professors
can't be threatened with job loss for political reasons; and in fact
it does contribute to the quality and breadth of research. There are
reasons why the US has the best university system in the world, and
the tenure system - for all its flaws - is one of them. (It also helps
universities retain faculty, because getting hired with tenure at
another place is a complicated process.)

If you don't get tenure, there are a couple of possible outcomes.
Sometimes you're allowed to go back up for it next year. Sometimes
your appointment is terminated (typically after another year), and you
have to look for another job. Sometimes you can transition to a
fixed-term appointment or a full-time administrative position, or some
other employment category.
