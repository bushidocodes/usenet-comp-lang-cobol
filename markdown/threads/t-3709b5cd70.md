I think that you will find that Alan Turing Way is in Manchester, not
Birmingham - he was working at Manchester University and committed
suicide in Manchester in 1954.

As far as I am aware, the first stored program computer (ie. the
forebear of todays computers) was built at Manchester University in 1948
to test the William's Storage Tube and was nick-named 'Baby'. This was
followed by a larger machine known as the Mark I. I believe that the
concepts of paging/virtual memory were developed here as well.

A replica machine was built for the 50th aniversery and is on display at
the Manchester Museum of Science.

Shortly afterwards, EDSAC was built at Cambridge (England) University
using mercury delay line storage (core for the mark 2) - this is the
line from which the LEO machines were derived.

We are in the 50th aniversery year of the Pilot ACE (built for the
National Physics Laboratory - Teddington), again Alan Turing was very
involved with the design. A meeting was held at the Science Museum,
London, sponsored by the British Computer Society - Computer Consevation
Group a couple of weeks ago, with speakers who were involved with the
project and worked with Turing. A full scale ACE was later built and ran
for many years. Again, I think that a replica is being built.

Donald Davis CBE FRS the pioneer of packet switching (without which the
internet wouldn't function) died on May 28th. He worked with the ACE
group at the NPL with Alan Turing. His design was used by the NPL
Network and Arpanet.

Simulators of (some of) these early machines can be found on the British
Computer Society - Computer Conservation Group Web Site
http://www.cs.man.ac.uk/CCS/

The British certainly come up with the concepts and ideas even if we
can't seem to exploit them commercially, as can the Americans (and it
seems most other countries).

Brian
