My educational background is in physics and math, so I'm not totally
unfamiliar with the ideas discussed here.  Unfortunately, there simply
isn't enough sunlight falling on any reasonable amount of area that
could be practically utilized that would produce the needed energy.
Though the sum total of solar energy falling on the earth is awesome,
we can't cover a significant percent of the earth's surface with solar
collectors.  Wouldn't the greenies love that!  Perhaps some future
method to extract heat from the ocean, or some such, would allow us
to harvest energy of that magnitude, but that's only hypothesis.  The
only practical available energy source we have today, other than
fossil fuels, is nuclear.  Geothermal is too localized, and has only
been used successfully in a very few places.  Wind power, a variant
of solar power, is great, but like direct solar power, can only be
used successfully in certain places.  We have already utilized most
of the potential hydroelectric power available.  About 20% of the
power in the U.S. is generated by nuclear plants.  In my opinion,
the only other possible source of that much power, besides fossil
fuels and nuclear, is if thermonuclear power is made practical.  It
is not clear yet if fusion reactors can be made practical.

Unfortunately, many people have a knee-jerk reaction at the mention
of "thermonuclear" (Wow! "Nuclear is bad, "thermonuclear" must be
much worse!).  However, the truth is actually the reverse.  Fission
(nuclear) fuels are radioactive, fusion (thermonuclear) fuels are
not.  Fission reactions are inherently dangerous, because you must
keep the reactor core cool and contained at all costs.  If the
cooling system fails for any number of reasons, you could have a
meltdown.  But the difficulty with fusion reactions is keeping them
going in the first place.  If anything were to go wrong, it would
shut down.  The byproducts of a fission reaction are naturally
radioactive for thousands of years.  The byproducts of a fusion
reaction are not naturally radioactive, and 'cool down' in a short
period of time.  In fact, they are useful substances that could be
safely used for other purposes once the residual radioactivity was
gone.  All of the fission fuel is present in the reactor core, but
in a fusion reaction, fuel is fed to it a tiny bit at time.  Even
if there was a catastrophic failure, such as loss of containment,
there would be an explosion (heat, not nuclear), but there would be
only a relatively small amount of radiation released, and it would
shut down.  A fusion reaction could not 'run away', as can fission
reactions.  I don't know if they will ever make fusion reactors
work, but we should all be hoping and praying that they do.  For it
would truly mean virtually limitless power, with *no* pollution,
and safety risks comparable to conventional coal or oil fired power
plants, with no radioactive fuels, no radioactive byproducts to
store forever, no danger of meltdown, and fuel from seawater that
would last virtually forever. :-)

The byproducts *include* a lot of neutrons. ;-)  As you (should) know,
fusion is the combining of two light atoms at the lower end of the
binding energy curve, resulting in the formation of a heaver atom, plus
various subatomic particles, plus energy.  In hydrogen fusion, the
primary byproduct is helium-3 and neutrons.  For the very light elements,
particularly hydrogen, fusion releases enormously more energy than it
takes to initiate the fusion.  For each heaver element, the energy
released is less and the energy required to initiate fusion is greater,
until you soon reach a point where more energy is required to initiate
fusion than is released by the reaction.  In practice, the energy to
maintain the containment field must also be considered (see the Lawson
criterion).  But, back to my earlier statements.  The quote below is
from Microsoft Encarta 2001:

  "If fusion energy does become practical, it offers the following
  advantages: (1) a limitless source of fuel, deuterium from the ocean;
  (2) no possibility of a reactor accident, as the amount of fuel in
  the system is very small; and (3) waste products much less radioactive
  and simpler to handle than those from fission systems."

Roach research results:


I do have a story about small brown bugs (Small Brown Bugs are very

are at least 138 distinct varieties of insect colloquially known as
"roach," but I am speaking generically of course.)  The whole idea
makes me somewhat queasy; perhaps I should explain.

You surely know how disconcerting the wee beasties are.  They are
nasty looking, nasty acting, and (probably) nasty thinking.  But did
it occur to you that matters could be worse?

Science tells us that these creepy creatures have remained unchanged
for millions of years.  Not much else has.  What might have happened,
I wonder, if the repulsive roach had evolved?  For example, what if
the rapacious rascals had developed eyes on the ends of stalks, like
snails.  How unsplendid.  To have those little eyes following you
around the room.  Perhaps, instead, they could have grown in size much
like the primitive horse.  If the roaches (the French have a word for
roaches, but I don't know what it is) had increased in bulk in
proportion to the horse, I compute the contemporary critter would be
about two and a half feet tall and weigh about eighty pounds.  Just
think about meeting one of those in a darkened room!  Or what if they
had developed vocal cords instead?

Consider, you are sound asleep in your little bed.  One of the buggers
crawls stealthily to your ear and screams:


  B O O G I E W A H H H ! ! !


Anxiety attack!

On the other hand, what if these fey fellows had gotten smarter
instead of physically changing?  Imagine, you awaken one morning to
find, once your vision clears, thousands of them spelling out "YOUR
DOOM IS NEAR" in college-band script on the ceiling.  Unnerving, to
say the least.  Or what if one peeks out from beneath a chair daring
you to chase him.  You do.  He runs under the lamp where, unbeknownst
to you, he has a confederate in waiting.  So, out from under the lamp,
two roaches.  You, somewhat befuddled, continue the pursuit.  Over to
the rug.  From under the rug, Four Roaches!  Zip to the picture of
Aunt Hissey on the wall.  From behind the picture, EIGHT roaches.  You
would be a incoherent before they reached the TV.

The mind boggles at the though of it all!

What got me thinking on this was a call from a customer in Austin.
This call in turn engendered certain reflections about my college days
and the "War to End All Wars" waged with winged waifs.  As I think
back, one of my biggest concerns was the safety of goodies from home.

From time to time my mom would send me a CARE package and I learned,
from experience (experience being a great teacher, although,
admittedly, not nearly as neat as a detailed instruction manual), to
stuff myself immediately for that which remained uneaten would, in the
morning, be either eaten or inedible.  But I devised a plan: I built a
ROACH-MOAT.  Oh, the idea was simple enough, a number-two washtub
filled with water, a pedestal in the center and, on this artificial
atoll, the leftovers from the day's mail-sent manna.  I still remember
going to bed that night, a gleeful grin on my face, certain beyond
certain that all was well in the world.  Now I don't know exactly what
happened while I slept, but I can imagine.  In my mind's eye, I see
hundreds of little roach-faces gathered around the rim of the tub.  I
can see the tears in their little roach-eyes and can almost hear a
rending roach-sob or two.  Then, it happens.

One roach, perhaps younger than the rest or perhaps imbued with an
attenuated sense of familial loyalty, stands up on his four hind legs,
screeches in roach-talk "One small step for roachkind!" and throws
himself into the murky moat.  After the initial shock, others take up
the cry and before you know it, there is a ROACH-BRIDGE to the
victuals thought to be safe beyond their grasp.

They were still at their rapacious roach-repast when I casually came
into the kitchen the next morning.  I hope you never have to
experience the profound sense of loss I felt at seeing my dreams
destroyed, my logic exposed as ludicrous, and my goodies gone.  I can
still remember the little roach-chuckles as they waddled their obese
brown bodies off to their roach-lairs.

In a perfect frenzy of loathing (there have been only four perfect
frenzies since records have been kept of that sort of thing), I sped
forthwith to the corner store and garnered a gallon of "Granny
Goshen's Roach Rectifier" (not to be used in an aluminum utensil -
rubber gloves recommended) and, returning to the apartment, doused
everything.  All this frenzying, speeding, and dousing  rendered the
place uninhabitable to everything BUT roaches for almost two weeks.
The roaches, in a frenzy and with speed, but without dousing, bred
like crazy, way beyond, I might add, the Malthusian projections.

At least the Aggies were happy.  We had to swallow our pride and ask
for help (beg, actually) which was gleefully provided in the form of a
small, tactical nuclear device detonated right in the middle of all
the frenzying and speeding and asking and begging. Three square miles
of Austin, just southeast of the campus, remains uninhabited to this
day.  Well, almost uninhabited.  It is rumored that in the rubble
lurks an eighty pound, knee-high intelligent roach that not only
screams when the moon is full, but can actually swim.

For reasons that should now be apparent, I no longer live in Austin.
Watch for the next report on how, exactly, chickens spit.
