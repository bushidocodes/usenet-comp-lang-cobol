If you are having problems simply because part or all of your work area is
larger that 64K and your compiler breaks down at that limit, then get a new
compiler.

But more likely you are falling victim to one or more of the subtleties of the
ODO clause rules.

In a group move from a either a variable-length source or from a fixed-length
source into a target that has an ODO clause. The value in the target area can
determine
(in part) the length of the move. The object of the ODO clause will determine
the length. Even though you perhaps think that a group level move will overlay
the target ODO objects and there by control the length of the move.

If you move at the 02 level in your example you will not control the length of
things in the target at the time of the move by the new values! Instead it is
with the old values in the target group's ODO objects.

But there is an even worse strangeness in ODO. If there is an area A-F-T-E-R
an ODO (say a fixed length trailer, after a varying length mid section),
compilers will use the MAX LENGTH of the target for a group level move if the
target in the group level includes the varying area and the fixed fixed length
trailler section.

In most such situations, folks are moving from some variable length source to a
variable lenght target. If done at high enough level old compilers would
simply do a move for a length corresponding to the current length of the
source. That is NOT the new COBOL standard. This fact blows people away
sometimes.

It is the length in the target's ODO objects that conrol the length of the
total effect on the target.

Keep in mind that nothing more is move from the source than the full current
length of the source (fixed or variable). The extra stuff jammed into the
target is filler at the end (and for group moves that means spaces).

If the target's controlling ODO objects have garbage then strange lengths can
be used. For those converting from old COBOL to new with COMP-3 fields in the
target ODO objects, very surprising data exceptions can occur on the ODO object
data element.

If a group level move is the means of initializing targets that have an ODO
where the object of the ODO is within the group, then the initialization will
go wrong.

Several other reponders have recommended that you isolate the controlling ODO
objects outside of the target area. This helps if you initialize them
distinctly and always intialize them before any group move under their control.

But you are fighting an uphill battle since you have an area or so defined
after the varying length area. You will engage the max length part of the
standard on account of that. If I am not mistaken, the presence of two ODOs
within the group also leads to some max length rules.

Another way to fight the battle is to not move at the highest level. Move at
the level of the varying section. Your layout supports this. Many copybooks of
old, never having had this problem, may not have an appropriate level to target
between the ODO object and the ODO subject.
And the solution there is to insert the level (renumbering levels if
necessary).


This fun problem can effect really unexpected things like the INTO clause of a
READ statement! Wheee!
Aren't standards changes nice?

This weirdness also is bringing havoc to the odd CICS READ command involving
variable length layouts with ODO clauses, wherein a preprocessor deploys a
LENGTH OF phrase which bonks right into an uninitialized or stale ODO object.
