> My specifications state the ASCII should take up 2 characters (bytes).

Then they are expecting to find the data as "packed" decimal (the COBOL 
spec probably says USAGE=COMP3 as well as giving the picture).  This means that two 
digits are squeezed into each byte (except the last, which has one digit and the sign 
bit). This results in characters which get converted as if they were the equivalent 
one-byte text character when you IND$FILE or KERMIT them to the EBCDIC machine. When 
they get broken apart in COBOL on the EBCDIC machine get don't get the numbers you 
started with.

My ParseRat product handles this going from EBCDIC to ASCII but you might have to write 
something to do it going the other way.

Just out of curiosity, the source system wouldn't happen to
be a WANG VS would it? If it is, do yourself a favor and
just put the file out to the mainframe in binary. On the
mainframe do the following:

//STEP1    EXEC PGM=IEBGENER
//SYSPRINT DD SYSOUT=*
//SYSUT1   DD DISP=SHR,DSN= <<Fully qualified name of
uploaded Data Set
//SYSUT2   DD DISP=(,CATLG,DELETE),DSN=&&TAPE,VOL=SER=
<whatever your shop dictates
//SYSIN    DD DUMMY
//*
//STEP2    EXEC PGM=?????  <= Your program for processing
//ddname1  DD DISP=(OLD,PASS),DSN=&&TAPE,DCB=OPTCD=Q
//ddname2  DD DISP=SHR,DSN= <<Same as SYSUT1 from STEP1
        .
        .

Now, if you set your COBOL program to use the same copy
books as were used on the WANG VS, then here is what happens
when you read from ddname1 & ddname2 (keep both reads in
sync): the area holding the record read from ddname1 will be
translated from ASCII to EBCDIC. The record read from ddame2
will have all the packed decimal data read for you to put
where ever you'd like.

I used to do this kind of thing when converting from a WANG
VS to IBM systems back in the '80s.

Otherwise, why don't you try changing the picture to "SIGN
SEPARATE FOLLOWING" (I think that's the correct clause) on
the ASCII machine?
