For the benefit of the rising generation who may not have heard it...

An engineer, an industrial chemist, a priest, and a computer programmer are 
all travelling in a car when the engine conks out and the vehicle comes to a 
halt.

"Sounds like a big end bearing has failed." says the engineer.
"Far more likely that the fuel is either contaminated  with a foreign 
substance or doesn't have a proper octane rating." says the industrial 
chemist.
"The ways of God are most mysterious and this trial has been sent to help us 
build our characters." says the priest, smiling cheerfully.

They all look at the computer programmer, who thinks for a moment, then 
says:"Why don't we all get out, then get back in again?"

Then, with all due respect Fritz, you haven't had a broad enough experience 
of mainframe programming :-)

(And this behavour is not only confined to Microsoft staffers/programmers... 
:-))

I worked on a site in Auckland (in the late 1960s) where intermittent 
problems with an IBM 360-30 were solved by cold booting it. This went on for 
several months until an engineer replaced a board in it. After that it was 
fine.

As mainframes moved into supporting online systems in the seventies and the 
new display screens were being driven from foreground partitions  (32K) it 
became standard practise to switch a screen on and off when it stopped 
responding. Later, as teleprocessing monitors like CICS and IMS/DC were more 
widely implemented this became unnecessary as these monitors were good 
enough to control things properly.

In Germany, a major insurance company who were experimenting in the 1980s 
with document scanning and storage on their IBM mainframe used to routinely 
power down the scanner and restart it when it failed. If this didn't solve 
the problem, a request was made to Operations for a warm boot.

In the UK (in the 1990s) I saw PL/1 programmers on a mainframe site who 
would routinely get stalled printers to work by making it SOP for Operators 
to switch them on and off. It turned out that the problem was caused by some 
APF functions which were incorrectly parameterized and simply hung the 
printer (sometimes). Nevertheless, it was actually a programming problem.

Today, with modern networks and decent drivers, we don't just reboot as 
often as was once the case. My personal network gets cold booted once a week 
(and never to solve a programming problem... :-)). The various real and 
virtual machines I use are simply hibernated when not in use. However, I 
must say that since switching to a new broadband provider recently I have 
needed to reboot the wireless router a little more often than was previously 
the case. (This is a warm boot carried out remotely; I haven't actually 
switched the device off for about 9 months :-))

Never mind. It was just a joke. :-)

From what I have seen in this forum recently, there seems to be a real 
danger of programmers taking themselves far too seriously.

It's only computer programming, not life and death...

Pete.
