Maybe I've been immunized by exposure...

I like AGILE principles (although I wouldn't necessarily observe the
letter of the law when managing an AGILE project.)

I became disillusioned with the Waterfall some years into my career,
when I saw several major projects fail because of it (with the business
and the developers blaming each other) and one that was actually
delivered according to spec. but was of no real use to the business
because it had been overtaken by events.

The system I will now describe I figured out based on my own analysis
and experience, before AGILE was formalized.

The 2 key things are ITERATION and INTERACTION. This means the customer
(usually a department in the business) has buy-in throughout the
process, contributing through prototype screen assessment and discussion
of dynamically changing requirements (INTERACTION), while developers are
adding prioritized levels of functionality during separate builds of the
code (ITERATION).

The end result is a system that has "evolved" through ITERATIONS to meet
the current requirements, with useful functionality being "trickled out"
along the way.

This compares very favourably with the "Waterfall", where the end result
is something like what the requirements were 6 - 12 months ago (when the
"Requirements Gathering" phase was going on) with changes frozen during
the development. This leads to: "It's lovely, but can we just have this
one small change..." on Day 1.

The only danger for AGILE style projects is that they must be tightly
controlled so that the "evolution" does not just continue ad infinitum.

I use Timeboxing to manage this and have found it to be extremely
effective. MOSCOW priorities are decided with the client, iterations are
timeboxed, and anything can be changed along the way by agreement, on
the understanding that something else may be dropped for that iteration
if the change would cause it to exceed the Timebox.

Although this "Dashwood Dynamism" :-) is much less formal than proper
AGILE methodology, it is based on the same principles and I have found
it to be very useful. (I have never failed to deliver when using this
approach.)

I see very little difference between an AGILE scrum and a JAD workshop;
they both achieve the same results.

The reality of traditional IT projects is that they are NEVER right
first time. So why even try for that and break your heart when you can't
achieve it? Instead, let the system evolve towards a comprehensive
result, with most important functionality being delivered along the way.
It can be flexible with dynamic changes being no problem (as long as
they are made in the context of agreed priorities and the current Timebox.)

To people raised on the Waterfall, who don't deal well with constant
changes, AGILE may well seem like a deathly virus.

I don't see it that way.

Pete.
