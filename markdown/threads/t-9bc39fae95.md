Many people here will have been in IT for a long time.

I believe most of us have had the experience of learning something that
was going to be "hot" and finding a few years later that the effort was
pointless, as "hot" item is now obsolete (Segmentation of programs, x86
or Motorola Assemblers, Amiga and Sinclair Spectrum Basic, etc.), or
used only in a limited niche market (COBOL (increasingly), BAL, Fortran,
Algol, PASCAL, etc.).

For myself, I take comfort when this happens (and it has happened many
times in the course of my career) that there is no such thing as
"pointless knowledge" and the experience of learning makes the
experience of learning easier for the next thing that comes along...

But, every so often, something comes along that is SO revolutionary and
SO "hot" that it looks like upsetting the existing applecart and
introducing a complete new paradigm.

What would you say to a currency that has no actual value and exists
only as bit patterns?

Or, a new way of transacting financial business that crosses borders and
systems, leaves an audit trail that cannot be erased, and is many times
faster than existing systems (including SWIFT)?

Or, a new transactional approach to data storage that will obsolete
Relational Databases?

I can't speak for you, but I am decidedly skeptical about these things.

(I am reminded of the famous Editor of the London "Times" newspaper in
the early 20th century, who refused to install telephones. "Why would I
need that when I have Copy Boys?")

Nevertheless, I read the following with interest and share it with you
in case you are thinking about a new way to work from home...
(Some learning effort may be required and YMMV)

https://www.computerworld.com/article/3235972/it-careers/blockchain-jobs-continue-to-explode-offer-salary-premiums.html

Pete.
I used to write COBOL; now I can do anything...

Pete. I like this comment.

I am also skeptical about new things and have tried to steady the boat and keep a straight course without deviating to every new fad. Pick out what is best and I have found that COBOL is a chameleon language that keeps adapting and is not dead in my books.

You prompted me to recall those HOT items that come along and stay the course of time.

My shortlist which is probably incomplete is:

The Spreadsheet
Word Processing
Graphics Editing
Video/movie production
The internet
Google everything
Apple everything

I recall going to see the first first spreadsheet running on what I think was an Apple II. One had to go to Brisbane city to see it demonstrated and stand behind a crowd.

I came back to my work desk and just pondered how one programs such a thing. Formulas in cells, Huge rows and columns. Also in those days memory was very limited and processing power was slow.

It became the Accountants tool and also for a non-programmer.

Another hot fad was 4GLs and some were quite successful but did they overtake 3GLs?.

A personal opinion is that I'm not sure Microsoft invented anything. They copied or stole ideas, bought and marketed better. That includes Apple's GUI. They didn't even invent Terminal server and RDP and I give that credit to Citrix whom MS kept a 50% share for letting them change source code. The first invention was Citrix Winframe and this enabled Windows to be Multi-user.

MS Word was a fail for many years and WordPerfect was the standard. After years of improvement, MS Word is quite good today and the same goes for MS Excel. MS has dominated the market but is losing to Google, Facebook and Apple in new areas.

It is flooding back some memories so bare with me. First of all OS/2 was not multi-user and that was a disappointed. It was a fail and meanwhile UNIX/LINIX were winning multi-user commercial market share.

When I first heard about Citrix Winframe on Windows I got quite exited. I adopted and recommended it to customers. However, mainstream distributors were ignoring it. I contact a National Distributor in Brisbane to suggest they take on Citrix Winframe. They said their techs looked at it and it was not going anywhere.

This leads to my next experience and repeated over many years. Techs and IT Networking experts just do not understand main business Applications and transaction processing and the loads on a network. Maintenance of user access is centralized. Going to each PC to maintain apps and more is a thing of the past.

The following best new fad is possibly Virtual Machines. Multi-OS on single hardware with central admin. Not sure if this is clear but hosting Cloud Servers is one example. I have my website on a Windows cloud server and can access it as Administrator. The hardware is shared with others. Load sharing across multiple servers is something that boggles me but it can be done.

Today, Windows Terminal Server or Citrix Winframe seem the best platform to deliver main business and transaction oriented applications and remote access. All my (small and limited) users either of these methods and I can do support from my remote desk and work from home. I could say much more.

I still say that COBOL can be the one-stop language for the main business oriented language for commercial transaction oriented processing.
