Actually I found the article quite encouraging. It indicates that three
years from now, or five years after its introduction, that OO COBOL will
become the dominant client/server programming language. This is exactly
comparable to C++, which was a bleeding edge technology from its introduction
in 1986 until about 1991 or 1992 (it is still mutating to the point where
textbooks written in 1994 are obsolete, not covering the STL, RTTI, template
member functions and other sweeping changes). The author does not mention
that anyone looking for a C++ job in 1988 would have been just as
disappointed as an OOCOBOL programmer is now.

One thing that I saw in that article and mentioned in this newsgroup is that
C++ programmers make a lot more money than COBOL programmers. The author
tried to claim that it is because of demand, but I would modify that. The
reason COBOL programmers make less is because any reasonably intelligent
person can become proficient at COBOL in a short time -- thus the supply
of programmers is high relative to the demand. On the other hand, it takes
a genius to write C++ code that isn't full of time bombs, or as is usually
the case, to fix the tangled mess left behind by the average skilled
developers who didn't realize all the potential dangers of C++ syntax. The
supply is therefore limited relative to the demand, so C++ programmers will
always earn more. If you don't believe that C++ is really that bad, do
what I just did and read the book "More Effective C++" by Scott Meyers
(Addison-Wesley). He is an ultra-genius and yet in many of his 35 discussion
topics he ends up with a solution that works most of the time, under most
conditions, etc. In other words, there are rarely fool-proof (i.e. manager
under schedule pressure-proof, overworked programmer-proof, just out of
college programmer-proof) solutions. COBOL isn't perfect by any means,
but there are fewer ways to hang yourself.
