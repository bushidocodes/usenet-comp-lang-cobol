The following was just sent to the CICS list-server.  I have only glanced at the

Hi All,

This is a newly published Whitepaper that I have just finished writing with the 
help of Tim Sipples. It is aimed at information technology (IT) architects, 
project mangers, and other decision makers who are considering their options for 
new IT projects. It focuses on the merits of deploying new applications to the 
System z platform, and in particular, to an environment managed by CICS 
Transaction Server (CICS TS).

The whitepaper does not assume any prior knowledge. In this regard, it can also 
serve as a good introduction for those who want to learn about CICS TS and the 
IBM System z platform. Additionally, it can be a useful document for existing 
clients who are not aware of (or taking advantage of) the unique capabilities of 
a modern CICS TS for z/OS deployment.

It is available from this link :
  http://www-01.ibm.com/support/docview.wss?rs=1083&uid=swg27013865

Any feedback would also be much appreciated.

Cheers, Andy

I read this paper with interest.

Mixed reaction.

Some of the arguments presented against alternative approaches are specious 
and show a lack of knowledge about other products on the part of the 
writers. It is also possible they have simply bought into the Company's 
party line about things like sysplex. Virtualization and coupling are 
available on other platforms.

Arguments about centralization versus distributed forms of processing have 
raged for decades and the truth is there is merit in both approaches. They 
are not mutually exclusive. I was amused by the description of "sprawling 
server farms"... they neglected to mention that such a farm can be set up 
for less than one tenth of the price of a z/OS system. I use a server farm 
for my web server and it is extremely reliable and performs very well. The 
authors of this paper should realise that not all "sprawling server farms" 
are poorly managed, or ineffective.

Still, we can forgive them some Company bias provided they give us the good 
oil on the actual facilities available. This they delivered pretty well, in 
my opinion. Certainly, CICS transaction server looks like a useful approach. 
And no-one would question the performance capabilities of z/OS. It is 
PRICE/performance that most people are interested in. I saw no comparative 
figures.

I kept getting interested then having a bucket of water thrown on me. They 
were talking about facilities in CICS and extolling the virtues of them, 
then they say it is still pseudo-conversational. (As if that were a Good 
Thing...).  They extol DB2 (superior to other RDBs) then tell us that VSAM 
might be faster and better for some applications. Same old same old... SOA 
and WEB services, good stuff, but hardly unique to IBM.

Overall I got the impression that nothing has changed. They still cling to 
1960s approaches and architectures, but wrap it in 21st century technology. 
("superior" cache and chip design for z/OS...).When they talk about using 
"components" they are talking about program "modules" not objects. I saw no 
commitment or support for OO as a paradigm. No grasp of why this is 
important in today's world; instead a white-knuckle grasp on yesterday's 
approach. "Let's centralize everything then we won't need objects". They are 
perfectly happy to continue development using PL/1 and COBOL (they threw in 
C/C++ to let us know they are "hip" with the flow... :-)), support CISC 
computing even though it has been discredited for decades now,  and dismiss 
Unix's use of RISC processors (which have completely trashed CISC in every 
benchmark I have ever seen...)

Having said all of that, it would be churlish to throw out the baby with the 
bathwater. CICS Transaction Server does look like a very good transaction 
monitor and it has come a long way since Hursley Park in the 1960s. There 
are facilities in it now which make it better than it was in my day, but it 
still hasn't got the edge that TaskMaster, Shadow, and even IMS/DC had in 
the 70s. (The fact that you have never heard of the first two is 
attributable to IBM's marketing skill, which buried both of them when they 
were seen to be eroding the CICS customer base. Sadly both of these were 
superb products technically, but such is the way of the 
World...(VHS/BETAMAX...etc.))

The problem is with pseudo conversational processing. Using terminal 
dependent buffers to maintain state for re-entrance, while you multi-thread 
the same processing code, is OK up to a point. It will never be as fast as a 
separate dedicated thread for each user, but this requires far more system 
resource. If you can simply instantiate another instance when you require it 
(as you can with objects) you get a dedicated thread, don't require a 
terminal dependent buffer (and all the internal overheads of maintaining 
it), and can win this battle. I'd really like to see a benchmark between CTS 
and an OO transaction processor, running on hardware of equal power.

Overall, I thought this was a pretty good article (I may have appeared to be 
hard on it here, but these are IBM professionals and I'm sure they can fight 
their corner). As a Project Manager or CIO I would require a lot more 
convincing before I would recommend commiting to this approach. The days 
when you couldn't be wrong for buying IBM are over; products have to compete 
and be seen to compete. That means hard figures and benchmarks.

If the purpose of this paper was to inform and suggest approaches, it 
succeeds very well. If it was to persuade me that centralization using CICS 
and z/OS, and going "back to the 60s" for my IT system architecture is 
viable, I'm afraid a lot more work is needed...

Pete.
