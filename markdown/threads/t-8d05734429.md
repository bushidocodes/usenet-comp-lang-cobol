Since the reserved word FILLER is optional in the 1985 ANSI COBOL 
Standard, why do you consider it bad form to omit it?  (If it were 
simply an extension put in by a compiler writer, I think I would feel 
differently, because it might make a program less portable to another 
compiler / platform.)

What advantage is gained by coding FILLER?  Whether FILLER is explicitly 
coded or not, the data item has no name, and cannot be referenced 
(except by reference modification).

Many programmers intentionally introduce white space into COBOL programs 
as a readability aid.  If it were your practice to align all the data 
names in a record description, the lack of anything on a line would tend 
to catch your eye and tell you "here is some FILLER".  Or, if you were 
coding a literal whose purpose was to help you find your way in a dump, 
you could get more of the literal ona single line by omitting the word 
FILLER.

> Since the reserved word FILLER is optional in the 1985 ANSI COBOL
> Standard, why do you consider it bad form to omit it?  (If it were
> simply an extension put in by a compiler writer, I think I would feel
> differently, because it might make a program less portable to another
> compiler / platform.)

The very fact that you rarely seen it done is sufficient evidence that it
is poor form. In design there is an important but often neglected rule
called "the rule of least astonishment." The rule of least astonishment
says essentially that the use of any device/feature/etc. should be as
intuitively obvious as possible. IOW, in a way that causes the least
astonishment. This rule has broad application to any type of human
interface, even that of computer listing/human eye-brain. Since you
rarely see FILLER omitted, it is clearly most intuitive that FILLER be
present for best clarity and least astonishment. Q.E.D. :-)

theory tenet proposed by Marshall McLuhan that "data has value according to
its unexpectedness".  It has nothing to do with coding style in COBOL or any
other Language.  Richard has pointed out many times here that what we
perceive as "familiar" in code is what we consider "good". As "familarity"
must depend on each individual's experience, "good" code is a purely
subjective assessment and must come down to "style".

In fact, all of the "Religious Wars" we have seen in this forum over the

entertaining...<G>). We can agree on points of style (and that is what Local

descision. For example, If the cost/benefit of implementing a solution is
extremely attractive, but I can't afford that solution anyway, it really
doesn't matter HOW much cost benefit there is...).

Coding isn't necessarily JUST about benefit in "efficiency" terms (because
the "efficiency" definition varies depending on circumstances and
environments), and to assume a lack of knowledge because someone chooses to
do something a certain way is just presumptous.

I am very well aware of the status of FILLER, yet I continue to code it, and
will do so in the future.

It's because FILLER has a very particular meaning for me.

It doesn't just mean "I can't think of a name for this bit of storage and I
don't think I'm likely to reference it...".

I have never used the forms described here of:  Filler01,  rec-filler02, and
suchlike, because I don't consider that is proper use of FILLER.

Neither have I ever had a problem with INITIALIZE not moving spaces or
anything else to FILLER.

FILLER in my code means: "This is a piece of storage which I am CERTAIN I am
NEVER going to reference, but which I should be aware of, because I may
REDEFINE it or its higher levels, and I may expect it to have a certain
static value that will never change. (As in table definitions,
f'rinstance...)".

Yes, I know you can do exactly the same and just omit the dataname FILLER,
but there are many things in COBOL which give you an option and the
individual use of them constitutes one aspect of "style".

For me the "benefit" in coding FILLER is that I can immediately see what
unreferenced storage is allocated in my program (FILLER entries stick out
like sore thumbs) and, if I want to use any source code processing (and I
have several  processors that I wrote many years ago), they will work
correctly.

(When I wrote these processors I operated on the assumption that a data
definition would contain a dataname somewhere in it, normally following the
level number, but a quick reserved word check makes it definite... FILLER is
a reserved word; any other dataname is not.... I COULD go back and update
them but I have neither time nor inclination to do so. I can't help
wondering how many OTHER COBOL Source processors expect FILLER also...)

I don't think people who don't use FILLER are wrong; any more than someone
who codes ACCESS SEQUENTIAL rather than ACCESS IS SEQUENTIAL is "wrong".

I DO think it is wrong to make assumptions about what people know, based on
how they code (or any other indirect evidence, actually).

And I DO think both...

AND

do with imagined "cost benefits", "efficiency", "rules of least
astonishment", or perceived lack of knowledge about COBOL standards.

And whatever they decide is not "wrong".
