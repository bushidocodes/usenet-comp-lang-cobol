Infoworld's Stewart Alsop).

It's true that C/S has grown leaps and bounds, and mainframes haven't

detailed a strategy to bundle hardware and software to access
mainframe data from a Web browser, called SNA Surfer.

by a 390.

My two cents...

Although less of a mainframe person, I do have 16+ years using VAX/COBOL and
COBOL/400 (my CICS skills are so old, they're virtually useless). I've also been
using VB for the last four years, mostly in side projects on my own.

Thinking that COBOL is slowly dying, I was pushing my VB knowledge lately. Ha!
Just got me a year long contract position at Ryder in Miami because I know
COBOL/400. As it turned out, most people weren't even interested in the fact that
I know VB.

As for C/S. One of the existing 'green screen' application systems being
rewritten at Ryder, is being redesigned from the ground up as a C/S application.
Thing about C/S applications is that they are predicated on the fact that there's
gonna be a PC somewhere in the middle of the equation (the client side...) This
was all fine and wonderful, until someone, somewhere (probably in accounting),
pulled out a calculator and multiplied 900 (sites) by 3 (PCs per site) by $3,000
(cost per PC per site). They were talking about an $8 million capital investment!
Suddenly, all them existing 5250 dumb Telex terminals didn't look all that bad
(who needs a GUI when all the user is gonna be doing is entering data?!?). Final
resolution? Ryder is going ahead with the C/S development, using C/C++ and
PowerBuilder on the client side... but, they're also going to duplicate, at least
part of, the on-line portion using COBOL/400, so the company won't have to
install PCs at every site by next year, when the project comes due.

None of this has anything to do with that Year2000 whatchamaycallit thing. Since
DB/400 allows for date fields (8 byte binary fields that store both the date and
the time), there shouldn't be any problems at Ryder.

So, is COBOL dead? As far as I can tell, as more and more people either move away
from COBOL, or don't bother even learning it at school (how many new programmers
come out of school knowing COBOL these days??), I'm finding that I'm becoming
more and more valuable. Can't tell ya what that does for my self esteem... or for
the rates I charge... :D :D :D From what I've seen, COBOL isn't only still very
much alive and kicking, but, with all the latest extensions to the language, it's
being used to develop many new and exciting applications.

Don't get me wrong! I *did* take time out to teach myself Visual BASIC, as well
as how to use the Windows API (never quite liked C/C++ or Pascal though). My
point is that knowing at least something about other languages/systems, besides
pure IBM mainframe, can only broaden one's professional horizons, and it never
hurts your resume. But, no, I don't think COBOL is quite as 'dead' as some
so-called 'experts' would like to have everyone believe.

Hmmm... two cents?... that was more like two dollars worth... :o

This letter says it all. In todays market you cannot limit yourself
to any one set of skills. It seems to be very important to have
several sets of tools in your kit.

I grew up speaking SPS (Symbolic Programming System) for the 1401,
then moved to Autocoder then to Assembler then to COBOL. For those
first 15 years I maintained an ability to design and program in all
the languages, converters, JCL and interpreters. During this time I
also learned and used RPG in several flavors on several different
machines.

Nowadays, I have learned C, C++, Visual Basic, Foxpro, dBase,
Powerbuilder and UNIX command language. I have done projects for both
Windoze clients and X-Terms. At present I am deep into a system that
uses the capabilites of both the mainframe and an HP9000 server. We
are using both COBOL, DB2 and IMS on the mainframe and Oracle and
Powerbuilder on the server. The system uses an enterprise connection
between the server and the mainframe.

My advice to anyone in this profession who is not ready to retire in
the next few years, is to keep up to date with the new technologies
and languages. If necessary, upgrade your home PC and write a
shareware application in a new language. Who knows, it might even
sell.

Wes
