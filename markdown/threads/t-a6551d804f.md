```
I have been preparing some video tutorials for the PRIMA Toolset.

This has brought me into the new (to me...) world of video toasters, video 
formats, and video codecs (code/decode).

The problem I have is that a 15 minute video in its raw .MOV form takes 
around 1.6 GB. So, the 45 minute session that describes Transformation of 
COBOL code (this is where your existing applications have all the indexed 
file access removed and replaced with invokes of an Object Oriented Data 
Access Layer), is heading towards 5 GB...

I converted the .MOV files (I made the video in sections) to be .AVI files 
and that brought it down to 4 GB, then I Zipped that and finished up with 
1.4 GB. (Video in 2 parts of around 25 minutes each)

This is still a pretty heavy chunk of data to be passing about. And it is 
stretching the friendship with my web hosting SP for me to park it on the 
PRIMA web site. (I COULD cut it to a DVD and mail it to individual clients, 
but at least one of them needs it urgently and so I need to have it 
downloadable by Monday. Besides, I need it to be public so it can be browsed 
by interested parties who may then become prospects for the toolset.)

I had a look around the web and found there are a number of places where you 
can park stuff (apart from the obvious YouTube) and some of them are even 
free (with conditions), but the most likely one (which allowed uploads up to 
5 GB) would take around 23 hours to upload it, at the rate it ran when I 
tested it with a smaller file...

I sought help from an ISP who should know about these things and he pointed 
me at various software but none of it seemed to really make the files much 
smaller. He claimed I should be able to get 4 GB into around .5 GB without 
too much loss, but so far, I haven't been able to achieve  anything like 
that.

I tried 9 different codecs (these are the "engines" that encode and decode a 
video file into a compressed format). Some were good (DivX/Xvid) some were 
not. I tried converting the .AVI files to .WMV (for Windows Media Player). 
This was the worst of the lot... certainly it cut the size significantly, 
but it also rendered the file almost unusable due to data loss which left 
text unreadable.

In this tutorial I walk through COBOL code and show exactly how a legacy 
program in standard COBOL can invoke methods on objects that deliver data 
services. There are a number of places where transformed code is shown 
alongside the original so that the COBOL can be compared line by line, and 
there is a fair bit of jumping around in code. This means the  video 
definition has be high enough to render program source clearly.

If any of you have been playing with video (maybe toasted screens, or even 
family movies) and have found a favourite video manipulator 
(Editor/converter) that allows video to be compressed to a manageable size 
for streaming or download, could you please comment here?

Cheers,

Pete.

PS If I do manage to crack this and get the current 1.4 GB into something 
more manageable, I'll post how I did it.

Maybe a video tutorial... :-)
```

```
OK, we finally got there. (Many thanks to all who responded.)

We tried direct FTP to the client and it would have taken around 23 hours...

VPN couldn't work (although I managed to sign in successfully) because of 
security keys and subnet address ranges on both ends. We probably could have 
got this working but there was no guarantee it would transfer any faster 
anyway so we abandoned it.

Finally, with help from my long-suffering web-host ISP, we got the 1.4 GB 
onto the PRIMA site, and the client downloaded it from there. I shouldn't 
think many of you are interested in the actual video but, in case I'm wrong, 
here's the link:

http://primacomputing.co.nz/Downloads/Tutorials/PRIMATransformation.zip

(I probably won't be leaving it there too long and will put it somewhere 
else as soon as I have a smaller version. It will probably end up as a 
series of files - see point 4 below.)

The feedback from the client has been glowing. He said that, thanks to the 
video he was able to do the process on his first attempt. He said a number 
of other things which made me blush but I won't repeat them here, suffice to 
say, he is a VERY happy customer and will be a reference site for PRIMA.

Here's what I learned from all of this:

1. Video tutorials are a really good way to document processes for training 
(they don't completely obviate the need for documentation, but they 
certainly allow you to impart a large amount of information in a quicker 
time than people could read it), and they are actually fun to do.
2.  Video is a resource hog and it doesn't matter what codec you use, it 
will take HEAPS of space.
3. Most of the video editors and converters you will see advertised simply 
don't live up to their advertising. (I ended up trying 8 different video 
editors. Most of them were very good and let you do neat stuff like 
transitions, but the huge plethora of legal and illegal codecs out there 
makes it a jungle. The link that Fred posted about codec comparison should 
be required reading BEFORE you start...) The disappointment is that none of 
them REALLY do a good lossless compression (at least, not good enough for 
something like a program listing), and some of them take hours to render a 
file into a different format. I liked AviTricks and HandBrake, AVS suite is 
probably worth buying (I did), GOM was hopeless (although the ogm codec gave 
the best compression, the GOM reader couldn't read its own files and kept 
complaining that the codec was missing. In the end I uninstalled it.)
4. Keep videos short and split them into parts which should not exceed 
around 10 minutes. You don't want your .AVI file to exceeed 300 MB as that 
is the limit for an upload in photobucket.
5. .AVI files can be loaded as a playlist for Windows Media Player, so if 
you have a series of short videos, they will play as one contiguous file, 
without you actually having to convert them to such.
6. There are a number of free video toasters on the Net, and all of them 
seem to work fine.

Pete.
```
