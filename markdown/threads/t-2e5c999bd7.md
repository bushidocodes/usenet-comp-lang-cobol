(Semi-related to current and past threads)

I just "closed" a problem ticket with "Ameriprise Financial". This is the 
company that I use for both my investments and my "checking" account.

The bottom-line is that I entered an "enhancement" request to get their 
online system working the way that it "used to".

A few months ago, they announced and made a change for how they 
work/interface between their "online" reporting system and "downloads" to 
Quicken.

Before the change, "new" transactions appeared in the online system the day 
(date) for which the "posting" date appears. (Usually these appeared a 
little after 9am in the morning. I don't think new transactions ever 
appeared in the middle of the day). When one used Quicken to "update" PC 
based tracking, these transactions "posted" as soon as they were visible 
online.

Now (since their "update" to the Quicken interface), the online system still 
shows the transactions the same day. HOWEVER, when you try to "update" your 
Quicken transactions, there is a 24-hour delay (i.e. only "yesterday's" 
transactions post).

Working with my "financial advisor" and Ameriprise online support, the 
bottom-line is that this new behavior is

"working as designed"

As I told the support person (when entering my enhancement request) - Can we 
say

"Working as MIS-designed"

Interestingly enough, this happened just a few months before Ameriprise 
stopped offering "overdraft" protection (so the delay in showing what is 
already posted is a "bigger problem".

* * * *

So why don't I post this as "off-topic".

It is possible, that this really WAN an intentional decision to change their 
interface.

However, my GUESS is that something is now being processed "in batch" 
(probably because of an increased load issue).

Do I think it is in (old style) COBOL or some other procedural language? I 
can't guess - but it sure LOOKS like it.

Rattle?'... perhap the financial colossus of which Quicken is a part has

As for the difficulty Mr Klein has I'd say that the need for immediate 
reconciliation of debits and credits is directly proportional to the size 
of the sums involved.  Keeping track of a day's interest on a number of 
$USD which contains two commas might be sufficient to dedicate a full-time 
equivalent to the task.  Digging the lint-dusted pocket-change out of my 
sofa cushions is best left to a batch process.

I was thinking about the nature of batch processes the other day when I 
was in the supermarket.  I take something off the shelf and there's a 
decrease in the inventory available for immediate sale.  Usually the store 
is notified of this when the item gets scanned at the checkout: 'Store 
123, at yyyy:mm:dd:hh:mm:ss.nnnnn, sold a blue widget.'

Now... in a world where batch does not exist this signal would be 
transmitted oto the stock-clerk whose direction has been determined to be 
the most probable one for passing the warehouse area in which blue widgets 
are kept so that said clerk could grab a widget (or signify 
unavailability, causing recalculation and retransmission) and re-stock the 
shelf.

This would be chaos.  Checkout-scanned items get sent to a list, the list 
is sorted by size of item and location in the warehouse and when a 
transport-item load (hand-basket, wheeled cart, oversized-item dolly, etc) 
is reached the list is transferred to a clerk who then tours the 
warehouse, grabs the items and re-stocks the shelves.

It might be helpful to think of a shipping-container on a dock as a 
manifestation of the need for some kinds of batch processing.

No it wouldn't. (It could, but that would be stupid.)

The on-line inventory processing I have been involved with simply enqueues 
the request on the warehouse server. (if there are a number of outlets being 
served by the warehouse they will each have their own queue. There is a 
single entry in the queue for each product and it has a datetime on it )

You can call that "batch processing" if you like but there is no iterative 
processing of a file of transactions; instead, there is an action triggered 
by something happening in real-time.

When it reaches a pre-determined activation level  (there are enough 
products enqueued to make it worth the walk or a specified duration has 
elapsed, or the queue contains a high profit item which should be in stock), 
a picking list is produced and a stock clerk does the biz. The truck is 
loaded and the delivery note shows what couldn't be filled or has been back 
ordered. Truck delivers to the outlet and they re-stock their shelves.

Usually the warehouse doesn't walk for a single product and there is more 
than one stock clerk involved.

Also the outlet system has re-order levels which are not usually zero and a 
re-order quantity which is also not usually 1.

In the old days, the truck would have made deliveries a number of times each 
week to each outlet. Some places still do that and they simply clear down 
the queue when the truck is making a delivery, so it is driven by the 
delivery schedule rather than the requirement. The system supports both 
views.

Add to this modern replacements for the old exponential smoothing prediction 
system using Brown's "alpha factor" and you get a pretty good overall 
inventory control system, that even has some capability to predict demand.

It does not REQUIRE batch processing, but sometimes people can't think 
outside that..
