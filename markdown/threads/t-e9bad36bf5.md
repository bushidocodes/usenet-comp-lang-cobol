```
Thank you Buddy, for your reply.  But I understood all of your points
before I posted my complaint.  Let me respond with a few facts in
counterpoint.

First, the *only* value that Fujitsu COBOL, or any other product, has
for me, or any other user, is how it serves my needs.  When a product
fails to meet my legitimate needs, it is irrelevant to me, in a very
practical sense, why the vendor failed to meet them.  Even if I have
sympathy for them, I'm not going to spend my money unless the product
serves my needs.  No consumer would or should feel otherwise.

Second, I received a NetExpress 3.1 update last Fall.  But I have only
had it running for about a month.  The primary reason is problems with
the upgrade involving licensing issues.  I lost months of use of an
upgrade that I needed, and had paid for, because somebody stumbled in
the implementation of the licensing module, or the update install.
This fiasco cost me thousands of dollars in lost time and energy.  So
it *is not* only concern for the total failure of MERANT or Fujitsu
that bothers me.  And don't scoff, for I have received updates from
Fujitsu that required my uninstalling the old version before installing
the new version.  Lots of extra trouble for no benefit to me, and that
is *without* the extra complication of the licensing function. ;-)

Third, there is great pressure on the entire COBOL community right
now, to "move away from the archaic platform" to something "nifty"
like VB.  Though I *do not* share those sentiments, as a consultant
in a free market, I have to deal with them.  It does not help when my
most important compilers' prices soar through the roof, and all the
while the vendor plays more and more hardball about licensing, and
I don't know when and how I am going to be shut down because of a
licensing module or computer failure, and I am not free to install
the compiler on a backup computer without a lot of hassle and delay.
What good does it do for my compiler vendor to stay competitive if
I cannot?

Fourth, though I understand the reasons why it is so, I can buy a
copy of Visual Studio, install it on all my office PCs (nobody but
me touches them), and a computer failure is but an annoyance.  I am
also a few thousand dollars better off.  True, it takes longer to
write applications using VB or (shudder) VC++, and they won't be
as reliable as my COBOL apps, but my development speed and the
reliability of my systems is already so far above what the community
is used to that it won't really hurt me.  It grieves and aggravates
the daylights out of me, but it won't hurt me.

Fifth, though Pascal lost out in the long run, because it was being
used for things it wasn't designed for, it is doubtful to me if
Pascal would have been more than an academic blip if not for Turbo
Pascal.  A high quality, very fast Pascal compiler at a very good
price made Philippe Kahn a very successful man, and put Pascal on
the map, big time.  Even more so, by putting *free or cheap* BASIC
on virtually every computer sold, Bill Gates sold the world on BASIC.
Microsoft *still* gives away a very good BASIC interpreter with every
copy of Windows 9x/NT (don't know about 2000).  Is there any doubt
about the financial viability of Microsoft or Bill Gates?  Instead
of fretting about COBOL compiler thieves to the point that you drive
away your legitimate users, why not make limited versions that will
compile EXEs that can be distributed, but limit the size of the
programs that it will compile, and practically give them away?  A
'student edition' for $100?  Why not sell anybody who wants one a
fully working copy (limited program size) for $39?  This principle
is called "planting seed".  Right now, nobody is planting any COBOL
seed.  I grew up on a farm, and I can tell you that if you plant no
seed, you will harvest no crop.  Apple and IBM have spent billions
doing this in schools, and have made it back many fold.  Just don't
stop with schools.  I realize that Fujitsu, at least, understands
this principle.  Just keep it up, and do it better.  Another point:
get humans to write your manuals, and make sure they understand both
COBOL *and* English grammar.  Well.  Another reason Apple initially
made it big; their manuals won awards for clarity.

Sixth, never but never play hardball with your customers.  I was part
owner of a microcomputer store in 1979-1983.  In 1979 Apple started
playing hardball with dealers and customers, and listening to their
own inner voice (Steve Jobs) rather than their customers.  I said then
that Apple would pay dearly for it, and they did.  The Apple III and
Lisa died on the vine, and the Mac came very, very close.  Apple would
have been dead had it not been for the almost fanatical loyalty that
Apple had engendered with previous policies, and for Woz' amazing
Apple II, which Apple sold many years after it was obsolete.  And the
final insult: for Bill Gates to bail out Apple with $350 million was
to Apple about like it would be for the Pope to accept a bailout from
Satan (or vice versa :).  The bottom line is, if you irritate people
enough, they're not going to buy anything from you, even if it's to
their best interests to do so.  To have purchased a compiler, but to
be physically denied the ability to install it when and where you need
it to make a living, rates a 10 on the 1-10 irritation scale.

Seventh and last, if enough of Fujitsu's and MERANT's customers become
as concerned about the above issues as I am, it really won't matter
in the end whether anybody steals your software or not, for nobody
will be buying it.  In my opinion, if MERANT and Fujitsu are looking
toward the long haul with COBOL, they should be very concerned about
keeping as many people using COBOL as possible.  Higher prices and
irritating licensing policies will not do this.
```
