You have given me an excuse to leave lurk mode and share my wisdom gained
over the last 20 years of programming ! The rambling that follows
expresses no opinion other than my own. My methods work for me and are
subject to change without notice . Unlike at the workplace, tact mode
is turned off, strong opinion mode is turned on. If you are squeamish,
stop reading now!

Um, program design is so darn simple that you don't need a method. If the
system design has been done (and the system might end up being only one
program) the program design is trivial. Choosing a design methodology for
a system based on the artificial constraint of an implementation language
is like shopping for a car with the sole criteria being the color green.
Many makes and models are available in that color, and it is a relatively
(to the cost of the car) low-cost retrofit to make any car green in color.

The best design methods for a system are independent of the language used
to implelement the designs. However, once you have identified the WHAT
needs to be done, you have to figure out the HOW. If you do a top-down
structured design of the steps needed to accomplish the WHAT, you identify
the HOW and the language used to implement is still largely unimportant,
although you may choose slightly different steps to avoid the weaknessess
and exploit the strengths of the implementation language(s). Now that you
have the HOW, you implement it in the language best suited to the specific
tasks, available, or required by some pointy-haired moron (yes, I have had
to do bit manipulations in mainframe COBOL for the latter reason, despite
the availability of ALC and PL/I). The choice of language balanced with
other implementation details such as performance or the crappy data stores
designed by the intern three cubes over (new business reality is there is
always time enough to do a shoddy job, never enough time to do it right,
and it will never get done over because that is re-inventing the wheel and
would interfere with the revenue stream we get from keeping our square
wheel rolling) and the pointy-haired imbecile will be the factors that
influence which pieces of the HOWs are to become a program modules.

I have written single programs to implement a HOW, including an online
screen with an input transaction file that updates a data store, extracts a
subset of data, reports on the subset, and performs archive/purge actions
on the data store. I did this not because it was a sensible way of doing
it or was required by any technical constraint, but because a pointy-haired
yo-yo insisted it be one program because maintaining one large program
associated with the data store would be more cost effective than
maintaining five small programs despite all empirical and experiential
evidence to the contrary.

My personal favorite is to do Entity-Relationship and High-Level Process
diagrams to identify data and the tasks, and then do good old fashioned
top-down design using hierarchy charts for the programs necessary to
accomplish the tasks. Sub-trees of the hierarchy may eventually become
program modules or be identified as being common to other tasks and become
sub-programs.

COBOL lends itself very well to top-down program design, and if you hit
pieces lower in the hierarchy that are too cumbersome in COBOL, linking in
a proggie in another language better suited to handle that specific
lower-order task is no major difficulty.

In COBOL perhaps a little moreso than some other languages the structure
and characteristics of the data are the key to writing a solid and
maintainable program. Some of the larger shops have been doing OOD for
close to thirty years now! We just didn't know that is what it was called.
We do data hiding and encapsulation where needed. The parameters are put
into the linkage section, the CALL ... USING invokes the sub-program
(either static or dynamic depending on coding, design, and maintainability
choices). Because there is more work involved to do a full OOP in COBOL,
use sub-proggies to OOP where OOP provides the benefit instead of the
shotgun approach of modern OOPLs that make it easy enough to OOP that
everything is OOPed, mostly without cause or reason.

There has been no paradigm shift in programming or design, just in
semantics and approach. Imaging the programmer of twenty years ago brought
to our time. All this talk of tables and rows and columns and objects and
classes would surely confuse him and cause him to think he was going
insane. He'd figure it out, and say 'Oh, you mean files and records and
fields and entities and programs!' at which point he'd be poo-pooed for
thinking procedurally and not understanding that what is happening today is
fundamentally different from what he is used to doing. At this point he'd
realize that there are no differences and that we have let the availability
of tools to support solid design methods, the change of semantics for more
precise specifications, and the desire to be somehow different and superior
to those who have come before us to delude us. Poor guy would be the only
sane programmer around, in a relative sense.

The bottom-up program design encouraged by most OOPLs and OOPers is a
little like putting the cart before the horse. It boils down to figuring
out a specific implementation of a lower level task and then forcing the
higher levels to conform, hoping that your experience will allow you to end
up at the correct objective. By designing top-down you will by default
identify the functional primitives and their general requirements, you can
then build them and plug them in where needed and have a good idea of how
to implement them instead of the guesswork and constant recoding iterations
required otherwise. The hierarchy chart is your friend! After a while,
you'll find you are really writing the same programs over and over to do
different tasks. Everything boils down to pruning from and grafting to ye
olde hierarchy chart.

Note all references to pointy-haired things inspired by Scott Adam's
Dilbert cartoon strip. The kicker for me was noticing an increase in the
cost of running a system I devised a plan of performance and data tuning
that estimated out to three man months of effort and would save a client
about twenty grand a month. It never got done because the client was
having a hard enough time justifying their budget because the new system
was already so much cheaper to run than their old system. And that my
friend, is why Dilbert and Wally and Alice and the Pointy-Haired Idiot all
still have jobs and why Pointy-Haired Idiots can get jobs - they have shown
they can manage a large budget. Make the budget smaller and they are less
marketable for employment purposes and less important. Their own warped
survival skills will prevent them from doing any such thing.

Have a great day and good luck dealing with the pointy-haired in positions
of power.
