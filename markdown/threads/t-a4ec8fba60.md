These tools and fixit programs work, they really do. Perhaps not as
well as the marketeer claims but the programs do work in some cases.

A claim might be is "finds and fixes 90% of Y2K problems". OK, I'll
accept that but what about the other 10%? The hard to ferret out,
nearly impossible to find 10% that is triggered by an obscure
combination of data, environment, subtle interactions of Y2K and
processing volume.

The last 10% is the trick, to find and fix those, you need a Time
Machine, a full-house production sized data center running production
volume data.

Or, you can accept the hit when 20 years worth of "Darn, Feb 29, 1996,
Dec 31, 1996, Jan 1, 1990" as well as all the master file changes you've
ever rolled into production, occur at once.

With a Time Machine, you'll catch 90% of the last 10%. But you can't
get all the Y2K problems. You still don't have a way to simulate the
confusion of thousands of users, operations, and data
entry people, who are trying to type in January 1, 2000 all at once.
This is something they've never seen before, you will be surprised at
how imaginative users can be.

That class of problem will take real pro's to find and fix. And guess
what, we pro's know that and we're not going to be available, not at
any price, not at $100/hour, not $300/hour, not at $1,000/hour. We'll
be on retainer and working for the very select clientel that paid us on
time every month for two years before Y2K-Day, and have support
contracts extending two years after Y2K-Day. We will take care of those
that took care of us.

So how many system destroying problems are left? You know in all
the excitment, I've lost count, but seeing as Y2K is the most powerful
threat to software made, you gotta ask yourself, do you feel lucky
today? Well, do you, punk?
