I was reading one of the articles about the government addressing the Year 2000. I work for a
Year 2000 company that sells software and services that automate the finding and fixing of the
Year 2000 in COBOL applications on any platform. I have tried to get the government interested
for several years and for the most part they haven't been willing to do anything. This is a
pretty big problem both in the government and in the commercial sector.

If anyone is interested, many of the Year 2000 problems are not only in fixing the problems
found in the applications but in other areas. For example, when we go into a site for the first
time, in many cases, we find that the customer has either lost a portion of their source code or
has old source that no longer matches the object they are executing. This type of problem needs
to be addressed before any corrections may be taken. We have software and services that help
them address this but it still takes time.

In other cases, there are PC's that need to be audited to find out what may go wrong with the
hardware, OS type software and various application software running. Other issues revolve
around software running on the mainframe provided by the manufacturer themselves. We have also
seen customers with languages that they don't even know they had or didn't realize they were
components of mission critical applications.

This is the problem that I see with the people who have waited and are still waiting to address
this problem. The problem is that "they don't know what they don't know" and by the time they
find out it may be too late.

On a side note, if anyone has a problem with the Year 2000 and would like to discuss it with
someone, I would be happy to help. Aside from the COBOL tools we sell, I have set up
relationships with software and services providers all around the world that have addressed most
of the major issues that need to be addressed. Usually these have been addressed in some
automated fashion.

you are interested in Y2K. Our's is www.allgnt.com and another is www.mitre.org.
