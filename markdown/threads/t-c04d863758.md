A poster to another thread expressed concern that, if he was fired from his 
present job, he would have great difficulty getting re-employed, as the new 
employer would take up a reference and find he had been dismissed.

This made me think about the whole question of references. I'd be interested 
to hear the views of other forum members, from both sides (employer and 
prospective employee), as to how much weight is actually attached to these 
and how much should be...

Here are my thoughts on it... (comments, disagreements, agreement, all 
welcomed...)

For the EMPLOYER:

1.  Nobody is going to show you a reference that says they suck.
2.  Nobody is going to refer you to someone who is likely to say bad things 
about them.
3.  The very worst people will get their friends at the old job to write 
them a reference (or will do it themselves...)

Given the above, how much credibility can a reference actually carry?  Even 
with genuine good ones, there is no guarantee that the prospect will fit 
into your environment as well as they did at the previous place. Every 
workplace environment is different; every 'corporate culture' is different, 
and these factors can greatly affect performance on the shop floor.

Even assuming you believe the reference and employ the prospect, what good 
does the reference do if they turn out to be different in your 
environment...?

"Er... Mr. Jones, IT manager? You remember you gave a reference on Peter 
Punchcard who was one of your employees a while back? You said 'he is an 
ideal employee, calm under stress and produces work of high quality.'"?

"Yes, I remember Peter. He was solid and reliable."

"Well, he wrote some code that was severely criticised in the review 
walkthrough. He didn't take it well."

"I'm surprised at that."

"He stormed out of the meeting, went to his desk,  pulled out a gun and shot 
three co-workers dead. He then defiled the Lead Business Analyst with a 
rolled up program listing, urinated in the corner cubicle (while it was 
occupied), and is currently holding the operations manager hostage. You can 
possibly hear the sirens in the background; we are waiting for the SWAT team 
to arrive."

"Well, that's terrible! But why are you phoning me?"

"I intend to sue you for punitive damages, given that you were completely 
wrong in your reference."

"Ha! I don't think so...That reference carries no legal weight or 
responsibility whatsoever, it is an opinion not a fact, and has no value of 
any kind in or out of a Court of Law. I stated my opinion on how Peter 
behaved when he worked for us; I made no guarantee as to his future 
behaviour. Sue away if you want... you'll end up incurring legal costs on 
top of your current problems." Click! The phone goes dead...

If you are an ex-employer and someone phones you for a reference on one of 
your ex-employees, be VERY careful what you say... see the next point, 
below.

On the occasions when I am required to recruit people, I NEVER ask for or 
require references. If they have them and offer them, I will do the polite 
thing and look at them, but they cut absolutely no ice with me.

I have employed people who were supposed to be "unemployable" (according to 
previous managers they had, and who were shocked to hear I had taken them 
on), and some of them (not all :-)) were the best people I ever worked with. 
Human Beings are very complex creatures; we respond completely differently 
in different environments.



For the EMPLOYEE:

1. A future employer is (probably... - more enlightened companies are 
dropping this practice and relying on their own assessments... and, in some 
cases, tests...) going to ask for a reference. Given that you're leaving 
because you are pissed off with where you work, what can you do about a 
reference? The company is legally (in all the jurisdictions where I have 
worked) required to provide a 'Certificate of Service'. This states the 
dates you were employed from and to. They are NOT required to make any 
comment as to your suitability or otherwise. If they do and it is bad, you 
may have a case for libel.  If you refer someone to them for a telephone 
reference and they say bad things about you, you may have a case for 
slander. Makes the whole reference thing pretty pointless, doesn't it?

2. If your new prospective employer is pretty serious about having a 
reference (usually they are driven by SOP which was formulated in some ivory 
tower in Xanadu by Human Resources people who have no idea what life in the 
trenches is like), you will need to get SOMEONE to say nice things about 
you. If your manager isn't likely to, (and more often than not, he's the 
reason you are moving on...) you will need to get a colleague or a drinking 
buddy and invent a title for them like "Team Leader" or "Senior DB 
Administrator".  If you feel that this is verging on the shady, remember 
that the whole process is pointless anyway, and it is just like ticking 
boxes on a Tick IT form, or writing 8 hours on a timesheet  (you did turn up 
for work today, right?), or charging the time you spent learning how to 
write JCL and play online Backgammon, against the Testing budget for the new 
application that you were employed to help develop... :-)

Very occasionally, you will be moving on regretfully, due to exterior 
factors. Take this opportunity to get a genuine and glowing reference from 
your Boss. (You can intersperse a few of these amongst the ones from point 2 
and it gives the whole collection a bit more credibility...)

In GENERAL:

I have had some amazing references. "His personal work is of the standard 
one would expect from one whose job it is to improve the work of others." 
(loved that, treasured it for years. Written by a mentor (sadly, now 
deceased)when I was a young programmer... ), "His unfailing enthusiasm and 
capability is an inspiration to the people who work with him."   (I never 
knew it until they told me... :-))... the point is, that no matter how many 
you have, and no matter how glowing they may be, ...they are worthless.

The only ones that MAY have some value are telephone ones, where you have no 
idea what they are likely to say about you, and you have very little 
comeback if they denigrate you.

For the last contract I did in Auckland the company took the most extensive 
references of anyone I have ever worked with. They wanted males and females 
who had worked for me and for whom I had worked. It was all telephone and 
involved 8 referrees on 4 continents. I passed, even though I had no idea 
WHAT the people might say and had never asked any of them to be referees 
before. Did I do the job any better because of this? No. Did this provide 
any more real credibility for me than my actually doing the job? No. Did it 
provide my prospective employer with any kind of guarantee as to my worth? 
No.

Pointless. It is simply an HR time waste.

DISCLAIMER:

For the last 30 years I have worked only as a free lance and on a contract 
basis. Normally, with contractors, companies don't seem to require 
references.

I think the theory is that if they take you on and you obviously can't do 
the job, they can fire you PDQ and get someone else; it ain't so easy with a 
permanent employee.

The Auckland company mentioned above was a notable exception. Maybe it's a 
Kiwi thing, I haven't worked here for a very long time and NZ does tend to 
be pretty conservative.


BOTTOM LINE:

Job references are a pointless waste of everybody's time. Prospective 
applicants should be employed on their merit, based on what they showed in 
an interview. Prospective employers should hone their interview skills and 
back their own judgement.

Pete.

equivalent of your "certificate of service".. about all you can get from any

reluctant to say anything negative so the 'downside' evaluation was more 
difficult than the 'upside' evaluation. But when conducting interviews, I 
always felt I could get a pretty good idea of what really happened at a 
previous employer simply by observing body language when I asked, "So why 
did you leave that position?" (You can safely infer a lot from a squirm).

Well I have to weigh in on the side of "References have limited value".

No one dares give a bad reference here in the USA for fear of a
lawsuit.

When hiring I prefer to hire someone I know or is known by someone I
know well.

If I don't have either, then I carefully monitor the new-hire's work
and terminate quickly if a problem surfaces. One of my college
professors once said "If an employee needs terminated after the
probationary period, it is usually management's fault for not doing
their job".

Fortunately in our business we can tell very quickly if a new-hire has
the right-stuff.

I haven't been an employee (except as a legal fiction for a temp agency, 
stuff that has to do with intricacies of tax laws in the USA) for decades 
now; the job-hunting drill, for me, usually consists of:

1) I come down off a contract and send off updated brag-sheets to various 
head-hunters, some that I've worked with in the past and are still in 
business (body-shops can be rather... transient) and others which are 
freshly-sprung from the ground, like mushrooms after a storm.

2) Folks at either sort of place, new or old, will sometimes ask me for 
references.  I ask if they have an actual slot in mind or if they're just 
filling in check-boxes... I don't like my references to be bothered with a 
pimp's 'look, I'm busy!' activities.  If there's a live one on the line I 
give names and numbers of folks I've worked with.

3) Sometimes they call the numbers and find someone I've worked with... 
and sometimes not, folks in this business can be a bit transient, as well.

4) The people they manage to reach give glowing evaluations of my 
capabilities and skills... something along the lines of 'Wonderful 
programmer... great programmer... singing programmer'.

5) The bodyshopper is either satisfied with these reports or dissatisfied 
('Singing programmer?  Too bad... the client is looking for a singing *and 
dancing* programmer.')

6) The person who supplied the reference gives me a call, lets me know 
that folks are looking into my background and tells me that I should send 
off the usual payment... oh, all right, the last part I made up.

7) The bodyshopper calls the client and says 'Got a good one here, 
relevant experience, good refs... let's set up an interview.'  The 
client-contacts responds with something along the lines of 
'zzzzZZZZzzzz... huh? whuh? oh, OK, send him in/let's set up a phone 
tech/sorry, the slot's been filled/hey, we were running a blind ad to 
determine rates.'

... and day passes into night, summer into autumn, the planets dance 
through their epicycles and 'twas e'er thus.
