```
Yea though I walk through the valley of death.

I am a clueless COBOL newbie.  I am a C and Perl programmer, 
an Oracle SQL geek, a mid-range Sun hardware geek, who has 
been thrust in a very dangerous situation.

My input can destroy the careers of many COBOL programmers
in my company, and I wish to avoid that.  A failed implementation
could destroy the company.  I like that even less.

The corporate MIS direction seems to be throw away COBOL,
move from the MVS on the mainframe, and redeploy the world 
on the Linux partitition of the mainframe, and possibly
move to Linux or Solaris elsewhere.

Since I know many of what they consider the target 
technologies, I've been put in a research and recommend
position.

We have 30 years of flat file and VSAM COBOL code that has
been twisted and tortured into place.  We use CICS.  Our 
actual data quantity is pretty small, ie: few hundred GB.

We've got about 100 users, customer service types, plus
inventory tracking and specialized accounting code. 

The buzzwords of the moment are Linux on the Mainframe,
IBM Websphere, and Java.

What are my alternatives?

My goals are:

1) Don't kill the company.

2) Keep as many current COBOL programmers as possible.  
Their institutional knowledge value is much higher than
the cost of their lack of skills in any new technologies.
As long as they attempt to learn the new stuff, we don't want
to lose them.

3) Move forward in limiting use of the mainframe and COBOL.
The cost of running an mainframe far exceeds the cost of any
alternative technologies, and the alternatives are far faster,
which means we can accomplish more.  Same with COBOL compared
to something like PERL.  

The problem is, it took years of discipline before "structured" 
COBOL took off, and the concept of "structured" PERL doesn't 
exist, so most PERL written by newbies is hideous.

The problem with COBOL within my company currently is that
it might take a month to do something in COBOL that would take
2 days in PERL.  So the COBOL is looking pretty bad.  But it
seems that people who coded in COBOL for years do not easily
move to PERL.  So I'd like some alternatives to research and pitch.

4) Move flat file data to a SQL based repository system.  DB/2 or
Oracle are most likely.  We have too many copies of the same data
in too many places.  Some layouts are only defined in the COBOL as 
opposed to copy books.  Different programs accessing the same
files have different layouts, due to them not agreeing on
the filler.  It is OK for the programs, but they can't be trusted
to document all the data.

Since you people know COBOL, what would you prefer to happen
in your company if this directive came down?  What language
would you prefer, and why?  What environment, tools, etc?

Note:  Please don't shoot the messenger.  I told them if they
wanted to get off the mainframe, go to a big Sun box, using 
UniKix, and keep the COBOL.  They didn't want to hear it.
Said it was getting harder and harder to find competent COBOL
programmers, and needed an alternative.
```

```
What follows is my opinion. There are a number of "right" answers to the

They have to do SOMETHING with the existing code. If they are adamant that
they don't want to maintain it, then it must be converted to something they
feel more comfortable with.

PERL does not fit this category.

While the modern incarnation of Perl (Perl5?) has come a long way from its
roots as a "Practical Extraction and Report Tool", best suited to tricky
little code generation within the CGI or ASAPI on a Server, and despite its
OO support, to a COBOL programmer, it smacks of RPG <G>...

surprised that the COBOL Programmers have difficulty adapting to it. They
will have difficulty adapting to anything remotely Object Oriented (with the
possible exception of Java, see below) and that's why OO COBOL has not
enjoyed the user acceptance it deserves...yet.

Java on the other hand can be relatively easily generated from COBOL (I'm
sure LegacyJ systems will be in here shortly...making their pitch. I stress
I have no connection with them and have never used their product, but, based
on feedback from reliable sources, I WOULD use it if I was in the situation
you describe...) and it is easily fitted to the Web and IntraNet. It also
deploys nicely on the mainframe and the Client/Server systems.

No Management in their right mind would reject your suggestion to convert
the existing code to the Language of the Moment. (Especially when there are
very powerful tools available to do so...). The COBOL Programmers get to
keep their jobs (and the company gets to keep their application knowledge)
while they are learning Java, as they convert using the LegacyJ (or
whatever) tools.

Learning OO in a Java context is certainly the best way I have found (easier
than Smalltalk and MUCH easier than with C++) for dyed-in-the-wool COBOL
Programmers. (This is based on personal, first-hand experience; others will
have different stories. Nevertheless, the good COBOL programmers I know
personally, who have embraced OO, came by way of Java.)

So the "conversion to Java" strategy looks like a game where everybody wins,
and the Management can be reassured that all the Legacy code is being
leveraged into a universal, cross platform language, with in-house expertise
remaining on-site.

This means a huge exercise in deciding priorities for conversion and
planning, but you will have that with any strategy you decide on. (Apart
from the status quo, of course, but we have determined that that is not an
option for Management.)

Personally, I'd do the Data conversion first, and THEN the code. I recently
consulted to a software house in Melbourne where we did such a conversion.
60 PC ISAM files to RDB. The COBOL team had no experience with SQL or RDB
but the data conversion went like a dream. (I wrote some tools to assist it
and one of these takes a COBOL FD and SELECT and generates a 3NF RDB,
removing the repeating groups to attached tables, maintaining referential
integrity, and implementing the same primary and alternate keys as were
defined in the original COBOL, without intervention by the user.) Having
built the RDB, I then wrote some templates to load it, and the local team
modified the templates as required and loaded the RDB directly from the ISAM
files. With defining and loading the new database almost fully automated,
data conversion was achieved in 5 working days. The real taskof modifying
the existing COBOL programs to access the new database has proceeded in the
background and I believe is now complete.

I mention the above, not just to publicise the fact that I have tools to do
this (although that doesn't hurt...<G>), but really to assure you that the
conversion can be simple.
```

```
over 30 years, but it is time to drop it... (I'm not advocating it should be
removed from the language; that would be stupid. I'm simply saying it should
NOT be the repository of first choice when we come to design new systems.)

Anchoring COBOL in this file system isolates COBOL from the other tools
which exist in the market place, prevents COBOL systems from exchanging and
sharing data with systems developed in other Languages, and consequently,
makes Management suspicious that if they go COBOL they will be faced with a
major data conversion within a few years. All of these perceptions are
contributing directly to the decline of COBOL.

Fortunately, the converse is also true. Showing that "standard", "open" data
can be manipulated by the Language best suited to manipulating data (COBOL),
that other tools and systems can interact with the database directly (even
concurrently, if required), that ad hoc reports/queries can be called off in
minutes, then there is much less anxiety on the part of Management.

Whatever their perception of COBOL, at least they know their data won't be
locked in to a system that REQUIRES a language over which there is a
question mark, in order to be accessed. If the COBOL system does the job
well, they can be confident in using it for more projects, secure in the
knowledge that there is no risk to the Company's data resource.

Personally, I'd like to see COBOL programmers being trained in RDB design as
part of their course, and the file system only mentioned in passing, with
maybe one or two exercises to access an indexed file randomly and
sequentially (dynamically) just so they know how to maintain the old stuff
<G>.

Before we are deluged with posts screaming about the efficiency of the file
system and the horrific inefficiency of databases let me just say: "Give it
up."

Modern, properly designed RDBs, implemented and administered by DBAs who
know what they're doing, will not suffer in performance comparisons with
equivalent flat file systems. Not even on huge volumes. If you find
differently, the RDB needs attention. Simple as that.

The only possible exception to this would be large volume sequential access
on primary key, where the COBOL files can employ a huge blocking factor. The
RDB must necessarily compromise its "blocking" (paging, actually) to support
both random and sequential access.

I don't intend to debate all this again. Use whatever you like, but realise
that continually isolating COBOL is not helping its survival.
```
