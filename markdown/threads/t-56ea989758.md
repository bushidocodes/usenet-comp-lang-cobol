Assuming you are on a Windows or UNix platform with one of the major COBOL
vendors...

In the SELECT statement in the FILE-CONTROL paragraph, try the following:

      SELECT file-name ASSIGN ...
          ORGANIZATION BINARY SEQUENTIAL

Then in the data division, make sure that the record definition is 300
characters.  One way to do this is:

      FD  file-name.
      01   a-record-name   PIC X(300).

Then in the procedure division:

     READ file-name INTO some-working-storage-area  AT END ...  END-READ.

There are two types of sequential on PC and Unix systems:
    LINE SEQUENTIAL - useful for text editors and printers and such, but
doesn't conform to semantic rules of COBOL and also does not have data
transparency (your problem), and
    BINARY SEQUENTIAL - does not work well with text editors and printers
and such, but DOES conform to the semantic rules of COBOL and DOES have data
transparency.

A further subtlety is that binary sequential files that are defined as fixed
length tend to be viewed as contiguous chunks of record-length characters,
which seems to be what you want.

Hope this helps.

That's easy. You have to tell your program to read 300 bytes ignoring
control characters (X'0C' is a form-feed to those I/O routines looking for
that sort of thing).

You specified the organization of the file improperly. As to what the proper
definition is, I can't say.

Review the documentation of your compiler. Each compiler vendor has - and is
allowed - their own rules for file organizational definitions. Find the
definition that allows 300 bytes to be read irrespective of their contents
and use that. It may be "X" in

ORGANIZATION IS "X"

or in ASSIGN TO "X"

who knows? But that's what you have to discover.
