One of the significant _advantages_ of Cobol is that there is no need to
'lash out' to comply with a new standard or a new language.  Businesses
are there to run their business not to employ programmers.  Computing is
an _overhead_ not a purpose.

While it may seem important to you to use the 'latest new language'
because it is interesting, it is not important to most businesses where
this would be simply throwing money away.  If their 30 year old system
still sends out the invoices and gets the accounts done then what
advantage would acrue from spending millions to have it rewritten into
something that doesn't work ?

Cobol can get passed down from one generation of programmers to the next
while continuing to work on newer compilers.  This gives continuity to
the business.  It is modular enough that parts can be replaced without
throwing out the rest.

Cobol is about business needs, not about programmer's trendy wants, or
computer companies annual revenues.

Ummm, something everyone shoul know about the COBOL standard:

We work VERY hard to keep the language "upwardly compatible".  As a
general rule, most programs written in the 1968 standard will compile
unchanged with the 1985 standard compilers --  and the 2002 standard
compilers.  A few things are deleted in each standard (but usually
there are TWO standards of notices: First a language feature is made
Archaic, in the next standard it is made Obsolete, and THEN, in the
next standard, dropped.)
Therefore, as a general rule, no changes must be made to make existing
programs conform to a new standard.
However, compiler makers sometimes add features based on early drafts
of a standard, which are not the same as the final syntax, or their
extensions are ultimately adopted, but not quite the same way that
they had implemented them.  These features are gradually dropped by
the implementors, and these features are 99% of what is usually
involved in "updating" COBOL programs to conform to a new standard.

Users should NOT have to rely on COBOL programmers to produce reports. End
of Story.

It doesn't matter how "way cool" the tools are if it is Programmers who get
to use them.(I am NOT suggesting that programmers should NOT have "way cool"
tools...just that where programmer involvement in a User Interface can be
avoided, it should be...the ultimate arbiter is the User.)

I don't doubt the latest incarnation of RW is excellent (actually, I thought
the previous ones were pretty good) but that is NOT the issue. The issue is
the perception of COBOL as a CLOSED system.

file system for reporting, I simply send the report data stream to MSWord so
the User can manipulate it however he likes (Fonts, formatting etc are
handled by styles in a standard template and if the User doesn't like it he
can easily amend it.) More and more users are becoming proficient in using
tools like Word Processors and are very happy to manipulate what they
perceive as THEIR data... This gives them a sense of ownership and
involvement which is just plain good for all concerned (including IT).

It is NOT the programmer's job to decide how data should be presented. It is
the system designer's job, in conjunction with the end User. The User should
decide ultimately how he wants screens and reports to be presented. Tools
which enable him to do this (Like Crystal Reports and QBE) are infinitely
preferable to tools which require a Programmer in the loop (like RW...).

Yes, I know there are many sites where they still use green lineflo and
produce reports from COBOL programs as they have always done. The difference
is that they no longer HAVE to; they have a choice. Our encouraging them not
to make that choice is simply not good for the long term future of COBOL.
