That's exactly the wrong way to look at it. First of all it's not 5
instructions *per* record. It's 5 instructions per PERFORM in a program that
processes millions of records. Everything you can do to reduce the overhead
of processing *per* record is a big savings because everything is multiplied
by the number of records you have to process. Removing as many instructions
as possible from any looping path while still doing what you have to is a
win, no matter what fraction of time they require.

Take a medium size program that does 100 PERFORMs to process every record.
100 PERFORMs * 5 excess instructions is 500 excess instructions. Like you
say, not much. Now consider a 10 million record batch run. 500 x 10 million
is 5 billion. Are you telling me 5 billion extra instructions aren't
significant? BTW we are paying for MIPS in most cases so it's time *and*
money. Of course the biggest savings would be to process less records  but
you can't do that. You have to be conscious on cutting down everything you
can in *per* record processing because that's your biggest opportunity for
saving time overall. And all of this is aside from the benefits of writing code
that's easier to understand and work on and has less impact on paging etc.

If you're devoted to minimizing clock time, you write the routine in 
assembly language.

That said, I can tell you that 5 billion instructions are virtually 
insignificant. At the simplest level, where one integer arithmetic 
instruction equals one clock tick, and running on a 3GHz machine, you get
  5,000,000,000 / 3,000,000,000 = 1.6 seconds.

Here's a typical benchmark:
Integer arithmetic - 45,000 - 1 clock cycle
Data transfer - 32,000 - 2
Floating point - 15,000 - 2
Control/Transfer - 8000 - 2

Total instructions, 100,000, total execution time on a 40-MHz machine: 3.87 
ms.

correct business decision. The decision came about after a year of 
disastrous floods and fires in Queensland. Prior to that, nobody was rocking

You may think it is a long way from changing PERFORMs to GO TOs, to the end
