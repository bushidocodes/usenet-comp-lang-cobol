```
All righty... I was talking with someone today and I said 'Well, we can 
change the lookup paragraph so that the lookup code is commented out and 
it flags all records as valid.  The advantage is that if we need to back 
out the code is already in there; the disadvantage is that a perform can 
cause a page to be swapped out of core and that's a cost in time and 
IO...'

... and before I could say '... but core is cheap and IO is cheap so it 
really doesn't matter' the fellow said 'Ummmmm... what do you mean, 'a 
page swapped out of core?'

So I smiled and began to explain about the Oldene Dayse, when any coder 
worth his paycheck didn't need 16K and how a SECTION had to fit into a 
page for the sake of efficiency...

... and instead of going into eyes-glazed mode the fellow said 'Wow... 
that's fascinating.  Do you know of a book or a website I can learn more 
about this from?'

... and I said 'Nope, no idea, most of this came to me word-of-mouth from 
instructors who said 'Now you really don't need to know this but...' and 
that's where the class became interesting... but I know of a place where I 
can ask some folks for suggestions.

So I ask... anyone have any suggestions for websites where this fellow can 
surf and learn the Anciente Wisdome about how the Sys360 architecture 
dictates the way the present-day IBM MVS operating system deals with... 
stuff?

Thanks much.

DD
```

```
It could be that knowing the history of virtual storage might be a little
misleading as far as todays processors are concerned.  In the past we used
to worry about "locality of reference" i.e. keep your data near the code
that processes it.  Recently on the assembler list, some who know much more
about todays processors than I do, have indicated that this may no longer
be ideal.  The latest processors have both an instruction cache and a data
cache and if your data is within 256 of the instructions you can actually
experience reduced performance due to flushing of the cache.  Here is an
excerpt cut and pasted from one of the emails:

"We have noticed that one of our programs takes significantly longer on
our z/900 then the older (and slower?) es/9000s.  The CPU time for one job
on the z/900 was 20k seconds and 14k seconds on the es/9000.  Has anyone
else encountered this problem?  For the moment we are using the es/9000s
for
the overnight run as we are tight on time.  Both machines are running
OS/390
2.10 and the code is an inhouse assembler written program.  In both
cases the CPU time was aprox 70% of run time."
 
 
" You could be running into a caching issue.  The z/Series significantly 
 changed the caching architecture, splitting into data and instruction
cache.  If you modify data <= 256 bytes away from a series of executing
instructions,  every modification causes a instruction cache buffer flush. 
One of our
 software products was a victim of this; the solution was to add 256 bytes
to  a GETMAIN'd area to separate the data and the instruction.
```

```
The course was my first COBOL course, taught in the evening; most of the 
folks who attended had Other Jobs during the day... some were even in 
Operations, looking to, by education, improve themselves to the dizzying 
heights of... A Programmer!  One day he asked the class two Zen of 
Programming questions; the first was:

'What is the job of Operations?'

... and he got a slew of The Usual Answers, from the Ops crew 
themselves... maintain computer and job function, mount tapes, fix things 
when they Blew Up... and his response was a steady stream of 'Nope!' and 
some head-shaking.  When the class finally gave up he taught:

'All right... the job of Computer Operations is to come in at five 
o'clock, start the daily backup job running and then go out back and smoke 
hashish.  Write *all* of your batch jobstreams with this in mind and Ops 
will *love* you.

After the laughter subsided he continued with:

'What is the definition of a User?'

... and there were the usual attempts... but the class had wised up and 
they didn't go on as long.  When the silence returned he taught:

'A computer user - and remember, as programmers you will be users, too - 
is someone who, when prompted for a 'Y' or 'N' response, keys in the 
numeral '8'.  Write *all* of your online jobs with this and mind and the 
Users will love you.'

He was also the fellow who taught us about being fired for demanding that 
the manager buy a mainframe with 16K of core... and how The Consultant 
managed to earn US$5,000 for nine business days of assuming the 
Programmer's Position (feet on the desk, cup of coffee in one hand, 
cigarette in the other, mapping out memory-locations on the ceiling's 
acoustic tiles) and then one day of coding a single statement to many 
SELECTs... but those are other stories for other times.

He was a good man, knew his stuff and taught it well... but out of mud one 
cannot make diamonds, the best one can hope for is a good brick... and so 
I try to float along in my usual brick-like manner.
```
