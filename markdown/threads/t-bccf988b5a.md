I have received a response from Bayer regarding my questions on their
involvement with heroin.

I should state that, having been involved with this company, it is only fair
that I should declare an interest.

In my experience they are one of the most ethical of the Global Corporations
(sadly we can't say the same for some of the other Pharmaceuticals who won't
even publish their accounts because they know the numbers would provoke
outrage around the World...) and I have been amazed at the responsibility
and goodwill shown by both their Management and Shareholders.

(I recounted elsewhere the incident I witnessed at one of their AGMs in
Cologne where the shareholders voted for foreign workers (in their own
Countries) to be paid the minimum German wage (in some cases a princely sum
by local standards), and the Company had already provided decent canteens
and medical care (without being required to) to these workers.)

Bayer are a good Company. (and I mean the word in every sense we have
discussed <G>).

Here's the response:

=======================  Begin quoted response
=============================
Dear Peter Dashwood,

First let me say thank you for your e-mail and your request regarding
Heroin.

Yes, it is right that the active substance diacetylmorphine was introduced
to the market as a pharmaceutical product in 1898 under the trade name
"Heroin." The substance was welcomed by the medical community due in
particular to its qualities as a pain-reliever and cough remedy.

Over the course of the 20th century, "Heroin" went from being a registered
trademark for the active ingredient diacetylmorphine to a generic term - a
fate suffered by a number of well-known trademarks. Whenever the abuse of
the drug diacetylmorphine came up in the past - and the topic goes back a
long way - people falsely used Bayer's registered trade name rather than the
difficult-to-pronounce chemical name.

Until roughly a half century ago, Bayer made repeated efforts to combat the
improper use of this trade name so as not to be brought into association
with the sale of illegal narcotics - but to no avail. In 1940, Bayer ceased
its production of Heroin, which had been taking place on a limited scale
anyway. The registered trademark has since expired.

In our 100 year celebration of our Chemical Research we published an
overview of our compounds - and Heroin was mentioned. Here in Germany it is
well known that Heroin was produced by Bayer. We also supported for example
a book written by Michael de Ridder "Heroin - from a pharmaceutical compound
to a drug" which was published by Campus. As you could see we are not trying
to hide it.

All the best from Germany,

Christina Sehnert

________________________________________

Bayer HealthCare

Corporate Communications

Bayer AG

Geb. Q 30

51368 Leverkusen

Tel.: +49 214/30-66102

Fax: +49 214/30-71640

E-Mail: christina.sehnert.cs@bayer-ag.de

Internet : http://www.bayer-ag.de

=============================== end quoted response
============================

So, Robert was "right" about heroin ("wrong" about opium <G>). And this is
yet another company that has had its trademark converted into general use.
(In this case a rather unfortunate association...)

(I believe that is the point that the thread was addressing anyway.)

Pete.
