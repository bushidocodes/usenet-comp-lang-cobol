Well, no.  At least not where I work.  The customer gets credit from the
bank before the check even clears the issuing bank.  Anyway, all checks are
basically assumed to clear.  That is, all the non "on-us" checks that are
deposited are given credit to our customers.  These checks are sent on a
cash letter to the Fed or a local clearing bank, and at that time we get
credit for all of them.  The Fed then sends cash letters to each of the
issuing banks (or another Fed branch, etc) and gets credit from that bank.
If, finally, the check ends up not clearing it goes back through the chain
on a returned items cash letter.  When it gets back to the depositing bank
it is then charged back to the account of deposit.

This is somewhat of a simplification, and may not be true for all banks, but
I bet it's true for a lot.  Even in the case of an on us check (one drawn on
our bank) there is a one to two day delay between the day a bad check is
deposited and the day it gets charged back.  As an example.

11/22/99:  You deposit a $100 check to your account.  You get credit on this
same business day.
11/23/99:  The debiting account on 11/22 did not have funds to cover it, so
it "bounces."  The earliest we can charge it back is this day.  Often times
the chargebacks don't even run until the *next* day (11/24/99).

No, I am not trying to teach people how to kite checks!
