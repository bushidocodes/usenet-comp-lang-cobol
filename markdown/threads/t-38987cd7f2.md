Conclusions.

1. It looks as though Richard doesn't have to worry very much about ALTER's
although of course it just takes a second to check the program.

2. Everybody appears to write according to their own consistent style,
either one way or the other, except for 3A using both SECTIONS and
paragraphs (sort I/O routines not counting).

3. A legitimate concern is whether PERFORM .THRU is used, or not,
consistently.  I am quite confident to state that I ALWAYS do, but given the
amount of writing I've done it's not impossible that I missed a THRU at some
time or another.  A utility to check the text of a program for consistency
either way would be very simple to write and could be used either in the
writing phase or the maintenance function.

4. Apart from that, based on this tiny sample, I think Richard exaggerates
the difficulties engendered by using GOTO's & their attendant paragraph
names.  In the context of maintenance, which is where his remarks mostly
seem to apply, Job 1 will always be to identify the area of code that's of
importance.  A compiler listing with a paragraph-name cross-reference will
immediately identify all usage of paragraph names within this code section,
EXCEPT for fall-thru's.  But since you can't fall thru line 1000 of a
program anywhere except from line 999, again the problem isn't as extensive
as he makes out.

 I would add that if  a PERFORM of a "THRU'd" paragraph (if I may use the
term) is identified, then there may well be a problem.


 I have to admit I was initially thunderstruck by the identification of the
"falling into a THRU paragraph" conundrum.  Certainly one does usually fall
into it, by the usual definition, of executing one statement after another,
in order.  But equally that doesn't violate the concept of NOT falling
through into a PERFORM'ed  paragraph..  The very definition of the language
permits and validates it!   Therefore we have to draw a distinction between
a paragraph that's the target of a PERFORM and one that's the target of a
THRU, and accept that falling into the first is not acceptable but falling
into the second is.  This smacks of speciousness, and those who would make
it an argument against using THRU will no doubt do so, but it gets us out of
the dilemma - if dilemma it is!

Thanks all
