Okay, here is how it works.
Your account is 7-digits with 6-significant digits and a check digit.
           123456c
           3418217

Each odd digit is accumulated into SUM-PROD
         3 + 1 + 2 = 6

Each even digit is doubled and the resulting high and low digits
are added to SUM-PROD
        2*4=8, 2*8=16,2*1=2
         0 + 8, 1+ 6, 0 + 2 = 17 + 6 = 23

Divide SUM-PROD by 10 and retain the remainder
        23/10 = 2 remainder 3

Subtract the remainder from 10 to get the check-digit
        10 - 3 = 7


Does this make it plain enough?

OK OK seems to a lot of misinformation around here.

This is the formula for Mod 10 check digits for credit card numbers.  
Starting at the right hand side of the number, ignoring the check digit, 
if you already know it, multiply every other digit by 2 and the digits in 
between by one.  Then add the digits from these multiplications together 
to arrive at the check sum.  Subtract the digit in the singles column of 
the sum from 10 to obtain the check digit.  This works the same on 
Visa/MC, Amex, etc. as well as most private label cards.  Try it on your 
own credit card.  Be sure you start from the right side.  On a typical 16 
digit credit card number start with the 15th number and work your way back.

Here is an example for an 8 digit card # (7 digits + a check digit)
1   2   3   4     5     6     7   C
x   x   x   x     x     x     x
2   1   2   1     2     1     2
2 + 2 + 6 + 4 + (1+0) + 6 + (1+4) = 26

Check digit = 10-6 = 4

If the sum of the numbers ends in 0, ie: 10, 20, 30, ... 
then the check digit is 0.  you can't compute that, it is an exception 
that has to be built into the code.

Another exception is if someone enters all 0's (00000000).  if the sum of 
the numbers =0, then the card is bad.
