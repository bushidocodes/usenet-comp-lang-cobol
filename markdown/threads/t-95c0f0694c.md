I do a field merge on a postscript file to get form printing.  Using
'impress' (no not the OO.o one) to draw the form putting
<!%fieldname%> where the data is to be merged, print this to
postscript file.  A program then takes a data file of the field values
and the PS file and merges the data into the fields.  There are a few
tricks to this, but is quite easy.  With Linux CUPS the output can be
sent to any printer via a ghostscript driver - directly connected, or
via Windows using smb.

I hate printers and me providing advice on them is like a blind man helping
to choose the colour of the wall paper.

I have no idea what you mean by keeping the form as a "macro" on the
printer, so I'm really totally ignorant of what you are doing.

Since this is a Cobol forum, I will assume you have some programming skills.

If I were doing it, I'd pretend I was dealing with PostScript printers.
Then once I figured out how to do it, it would then work on any postscript
printer.

If you have a look at "ghostscript", it will let you pretend that your
Lexmark printers and InkJet Printers of whatever brand are PostScript
printers.

(Half a day stuffing around downloading GhostScript, half a day to read the
documentation, 10 minutes to install and test)

So now all of your reports have to be generated as a text file full of
PostScript commands.  But since you have already written all of your reports
and they aren't in PostScript commands, what do you do?  Depends on how your
reports are produced at the moment and I don't know this?

(Whatever, this will be another day stuffing around getting everything to
come out PostScript)

So given that you have found a little utility to get all your reports into
PostScript format...

Now the problem now becomes, "How do I print a form and then print an
arbitrary report on top of it".

I would attack the Unix Printer Interface Script and add a single line of
code to it to call a filter that I would then write.

If my Form was stored as an image, then all my filter would do is to add a
single line to the front of each report with an "image" operator.  This
image would then be printed by the first "showpage" operator to come along -
and lo and behold, that will be at the end of the first page of your report.

(Half a day to read the PostScript tutorial, half a day to write and test
the shell script)

But what if there is a multi-page report?
Then my filter would need to insert the image just before each showpage.
(Change the half day to a full day to write the Shell Script)

But since I am now dealing with Postscript, I wouldn't leave the Form as an
image since its easy enough to do something better now that we are playing
with PostScript :)
(Another couple of days stuffing around, because once you start doing this
you won't be satisfied until the client tells you to stop stuffing around
and just give him what you have done so far.  He'll be happy, but you will
always wish you could have spend just 5 more minutes on it).


On the other hand, if you have absolutely no sense of pride and are willing
to deny you ever wrote the stuff in the first place, you could just forget
the PostScript stuff and...

Try attacking the printer interface script and see what happens if you throw
a few reverse linefeeds in.  If you are lucky, the printer might recognise
them and let you overprint earlier bits of the report.  In that case, you
just "cat" the form to the printer, send 66 reverse linefeeds (or whatever)
and then carry on printing your report as if nothing happened.  If you are
not lucky, you might have to open the printer's manual and spend an hour
reading it.

(Entire job done in half a day - as long as you can live with the fear that
one day your quick-n-nasty will surface).

And if you have multiple page reports?
What the hell, if it works on page 1, then you might as well do it whenever
you find a "\f" or reach 66 lines.
(Add another half a day and practice denying it was you who did it).


Now you know why no-one ever asks me about printers :)

Jim Morcombe
