Well...my thoughts on this is that they're wanting to get some
awareness out in the general market as to their existence. So they're
giving away their product, so people can see what it is.

As far as it being legal, companies give away their product all the
time -- companies with products that are quantifiable in value, and
those with products that aren't.

It's not helping Fujitsu in any way to give this compiler out in terms
of the pocketbook. But obviously, they see some value in it for
later. My guess is that the next time people want a COBOL compiler,
maybe MAJOR IS DEPARTMENTS, that they'll consider purchasing Fujitsu,
because one of the individuals in the IS department got the free copy
and liked it really well. Also note, that Fujitsu does have a license
fee on condition of public production clause in the license agreement
for these free compilers.

Giving away your product is perfectly legal. For example, I got a
free (legal) copy of Quicken when I bought my tape drive. Is Quicken
doing an illegal act? Not necessarily.

The law states (at least in the US) that this would be illegal if it
had the intention of driving the competition out of the market to the
detriment of the customer. As far as I see, all Fujitsu is doing is
trying to get their foot in the door of a market largely dominated by
IBM and MicroFocus...and I can't see an argument where the customer is
getting free product where you could say the customer is not
benefiting..

useful, reliable, compatible etc) they will come back for upgrades
and new versions at full price.

and the COBOL community benefit.

Robert:

I have a feeling there will be more individuals who will respond the
same way as you. If Fujitsu's offer stirs up the competition to take
notice and do a better job with their own products and get pricing in
line and more competitive, then the people who buy and use these
products will benefit. In my opinion, Fujitsu has made the first step
that has been long over due in the Cobol market: An effort to make
Cobol competitive with non-Cobol solutions. If giving away the product
helps promote Cobol and the advantages of continuing to use Cobol as a
language of choice, then bravo.

The PC Cobol market has done a terrible job, in general, of promting
Cobol, either through overly-expensive pricing or simply by catering to
only large corporate accounts. Fujitsu's offer will give individuals of
all levels (novice, experienced, corporate, non-corporate) an
opportunity to have access to a non-trivial development software
package. I applaud this move as positive and only wish it was done
sooner.

No one can predict if Fujitsu will continue or withdraw their offer down
the line. But for know, it is a great offer. I think that if Fujitsu
holds to their stated licensing agreements and does not do something
totally contrary in the future, they can build user confidence in the
product and Fujitsu's intent. If they don't--well, that will be a whole
other problem.
