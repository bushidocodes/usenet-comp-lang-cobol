IBM defines the comp-1 type as 4 bytes long and the comp-2 type as 8. The
format of the internal floating point item is :

The sign of the fraction(mantissa) is the leftmost bit ( bit 0) in either
format.

The exponent appears in bits 1 through 7

The fraction (equivalent to the mantissa) appears in the rightmost bytes:
Comp-1, the fraction is 3 bytes long.
Comp-2, the fraction is 7 bytes long.

A slight correction to your post - comp-1 and comp-2 are internal
floating-point types, external FP have USAGE DISPLAY and take the form
{+/-} mantissa E {+/-} exponent.

I don't know if these types comply to IEEE standards as they are IBM's and
may have existed prior to any standard. It would be hard to say if they
are compatible in their internal formats with the internal floating point
representations used in langauges like C or Basic. In the C language
along the FP data types created by the MS C compiler are not compatible
with those from Borland so, again, it would be hard to say.
