... told me, long ago, by one with greater familiarity about such things 
than I.  As with many jokes, though, there might be a grain of truth to 
it.

A group of rabbis were once arguing about a manifestation of Divine Intent 
in a bit of Scripture.  The old one argued that it was one way, the three 
younger ones were in agreement in their disagreement with him.  Finally 
the 'leader' of the younger set said 'Look, you say it is one way, we say 
it is the other; there are three of us and only one of you, we must be 
right.'

At that moment the sky grew dark... and in the clouds there appeared a 
hole through which came a single sunbeam which illuminated the old 
rabbi.  From the sky came a deep, peace-filled voice which stated 'The old 
one, truly, is right'...

... and the sky clouded over and there was silence.

... and in this silence the 'leader' of the younger rabbis said 'But 
*still*...!'

DD

On the lower East Side, an elderly Jew is at prayers:

"Baruch HaShem, Master of the Universe, all my life I've been a good Jew.
I've raised my children to be good Jews. I attend shule, I keep the
commandments. I've never asked anything for myself. Now I'm asking. In
return for a lifetime of obedience in your service, I want one little thing:
I want I should win the New York state lottery. I know you can fix it.
Amen."

The next day.

"Baruch HaShem, Master of the Universe. I didn't win! Maybe you forgot...."

This sequence repeats for several days, the demands becoming more intense.
Finally:

"Baruch HaShem, Master of the Universe. I'm tired of waiting. You can fix it
so I win the lottery. I want you to do it now! I insist!!!"

The room darkens. A slow, sibilant, low voice is heard:

"Moshe. Don't you think, first, you should buy a ticket?"

----
Jewish jokes are great. They deal a lot with food, relationships
(particularly with God), and poke fun at their own stereotypes.

Marvin and Benjamin meet at their high-school reunion.

Marvin: "So, how's things?"
Ben: "Last year we had a fire at my furniture store. But, Lord be praised,
we had insurance. Everything is OK now. And you?"
Marv: "As you know, I moved to Florida. Last year we had a hurricane and it
wiped out my wholesale kitchen utensil business. But, Lord be praised, we
had insurance. Everything is OK now."
...
Ben: "So, tell me, how do you have a hurricane?"
