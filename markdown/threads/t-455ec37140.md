I have been hearing the term "waterfall" used a lot recently, and in 
general it has a bad meaning.  But I don't really understand how
there can be any methodology other than waterfall.  Probably, I
do not understand the meaning of the term.  
Looking at Ron Jeffries's site on "Xtreme Programming," he 
describes Waterfall as the Development Life Cycle:  1. Analysis
2. Design   3. Code   4. Test 
It is a "waterfall," because you can never go backwards.  I.e., once
the third step, Code, is begun, you cannot go back to Design, let
alone to Analysis.  As an alternative, he suggests "Test First" 
development.  I could not find any example of Test First on the website.  

My problems: 
1.  What other way is possible besides Analyze, Design, Code, and Test? 
Although there are frequent jokes about "in this shop, we code first and
design later"--meaning that the designer did not think of everything--whether
good or bad, the design is always done before the coding. 
Quite aside from corporate-sized COBOL projects, I follow exactly the 
same steps if I am writing a hobby-level Java program, or making
a new Excel spreadsheet to figure my gas mileage, or even cooking 
breakfast in the morning.  

2.  I don't think the idea that there are clearly marked Points of No
Return is a valid concept.  Suppose a test reveals improper coding?  
Do you say "Sorry, the test failed.  Trash the project"?  No, you go
back and fix the code.  If the test reveals that some basic principle
was overlooked you go back to Analysis and make the corrections.  
If a coder finds himself in a cul de sac, he might go back to the
designer and say, "You can't get there from here--let's find a different
approach."  Regardless of what's written on a document, there is
a constant interaction among the various phases. 

3.  Just because a program goes into production doesn't mean that
it is complete.  Otherwise, why would software vendors release a new 
version every year?  I don't think a program or a system is like a 
chair, in that you build it once and use it forever.  It is more like a
garden, in that you build it and then spend the rest of your life
maintaining and enhancing it.  

4.  In 1980, where I started programming, we did not follow any 
explicit methodology.  You just talked to an accountant, tried to
figure out what he was asking for, wrote a program, installed it, and
went to the accountant and asked, "Is this it?" and then made the
changes.  This process was ongoing for varying lengths of time.  
If it was a simple process, maybe the first try was acceptable. 
In a multifunctional accounting system, the process went on
forever, as new functions were added, old bugs were identified, and
revised definitions appeared.  We were told that this was very bad--
such fix-it-on-the-fly programming was very costly.  We should use
written test plans, specifically identified milestones requiring 
someone's signature, and so forth.  It seems to me that every
recommendation to avoid the Waterfall consists of an attempt
to return the days of happy chaos. 


Will someone who opposes "Waterfall" development explain his
reasons for opposing it? 




Ross
http://community.webshots.com/user/ross_klatte

You have not quite got the whole picture -
Waterfall development, along with the others, is a PROJECT MANAGEMENT methodology,
not a programming technique. ;)

In a project oriented environment, you often are required to get from the start of a project through delivery in a very specific
amount of time, and so you try very hard to never go 'backwards.'  For one things, changes in the requirements for a software
delivery are MUCH more expensive if they occur in say, the testing phase, than in the design phase. It is also a way to control
cost.

There are other methodologies out there, usually applied in corporate settings where the management views IT as an evolving process,
and not a hated cost center. One is cyclic development, which is pretty much what you describe yourself as doing back in the 1980's.
You get the requirements, design,code,and test the product, then go back to the users for feedback and do the whole thing over
again. Sounds inefficient, but it is actually very efficient in some settings.

It really *is* all in your point of view- at the lowest level, your point of view is always going to be to figure out how to do
something, do it, test it, then revise it. At a much higher level, a software project's cost can be much better controlled by
specifying and controlling those steps on a project wide basis.

-Paul

I recommend the article,
< http://www.eng.uwaterloo.ca/~msci738/clippings/00000059.pdf >.
It discusses different methodologies, including Waterfall,
and presents the Spiral Model.

Waterfall applies, generally, to all development; but has
a specialized meaning, as well. In this specialized sense,
the opposite of waterfall is RAD, XP fits within RAD with
an emphasis on OO.

The ultimate in RAD is that users do their own analysis,
design, code, and test. For example; spreadsheets,
macros, etc. A user or small group of users may do
their own analysis ("This is what we need."), but may not
have the technical expertise to make it work. This is
when a programmer gets involved.

As the focus shifts away from a known set of users, it
becomes necessary to assign a surrogate user, an analyst.
As the project increases in size, a team of analysts is
assigned, along with designers, coders, testers, etc., and
the distinct (and formal) characteristics of waterfall emerge.

It is not a question of whether waterfall, in the general sense,
will be used; it is a question of how formal, in the specialized
sense, it will become.

While it is true that water falls, the analogy to a waterfall
only applies to the primary flow of the process. Each
step has a secondary flow "upstream" to the previous step.
This allows for clarification and correction.

I am not certain what "Test First" means; but having looked
at XP I would not be surprised to find that it is the first of
two steps.

1. Test what the user likes or doesn't like about
    (some or) your program.
2. Code or modify the program to present to the user.

Repeat these steps until the user is satisfied. This works
because the user is the ultimate tester. It works well with
OO because it is relatively easy to refactor classes to
shift 'behavior', to inherit from existing classes to modify
'behavior', and add classes to create 'behavior'.

Other's have said it already, but the classic waterfall
approach does not precluded you from going upstream.  It does
however, in contrast to XP, presume that you "mostly" complete
one phase before moving to the next.  There's nothing
intrinsically wrong with the classic approach, but it has a
hard time reacting to environments where the requirements are
_not_ relatively static.

Another classic approach was to scale your project proportional
to the set of requirements, much like you would double or
triple a recipe.  Although this is not really part of the
waterfall method, it's often lumped into the "standard methods"
that are contrasted with XP.

The relieve the project under these conditions, XP suggests a
couple of techniques:

1)  Drop the presumption that the project's progress is
essentially linear, and consider it more of a to do list.  When
your list is complete, the project is.  There's a bewildering
flowchart at that tries to give an overview of a typical
project:
http://www.extremeprogramming.org/map/project.html

2)  Use smaller teams with small, but well-defined, sets of
requirements.  Larger projects are harder to maneuver, so XP's
working theory is that a large project should be decomposed
into a group of smaller project with achievable goals.

3)  Design your tests and your application at the same time.
What's more, design your application such that it can easily be
tested.  As you are implementing the design, repeatedly
validate your work with those tests.  A well-written suite of
tests should validate most, if not all, of your project's
requirements.  I think this is what's being referred to by
"Test First."

It is a bit of a joke that XP is a legitimization of
developmental sloth, and it can certainly be exploited in that
manner.  It appears to me, though, that it's an attempt to open
up the structure of project management, not obliterate it.

Do a macroscopic project plan that segments your application
into relatively small, but testable units.  Within that unit,
do lots of iterations through the analyze-design-implement
loop, with testing being considered/executed at each step in
the process.  Ultimately, the loops become thinner and faster
as the requirements, design and implementation "evolve" to the
most adapted solution.

In spite the the chaos that was present when you started
programming, you probably miss the responsiveness of that
"system."  XP attempts to preserve that ability to react,
without sacrificing the project's long view.

the team than the project management style.
