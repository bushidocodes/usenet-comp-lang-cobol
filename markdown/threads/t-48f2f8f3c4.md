Jeff,

A lot of this depends on how you want the application to behave. From
the outline you have provided there are still many questions that
affect how I would approach this problem.

Just one of the things you should consider: how big are the tables, and
what would the impact of a join be? If you have 1,000,000 records in
table 1, and only 10% of them have the flag set to retreive data from
tables 2-4 then using a "joined" cursor would not be ideal here.

In the (rather large) application I develop here, I actually use both
methods you are describing depending on what the most practical is for
the task at hand. Joins can be a great asset when used in an
appropriate situation - but they can also be extremely detrimental if
you use then where they really don't "belong."

I know this isn't very helpful in addressing your exact situation, but
from the generalized post you've submitted its the best I can do for
now. There are a few of us here that have extensive experience working
with Pro*COBOL, so with more details about what you are trying to
accomplish, we may be able to offer better suggestions/tips.

Jeff,

Based on what you are describing, I would attack this using a
combination of a "joined" cursor and a secondary cursor.

I would use a joined cursor between MEMBER and MBRSEPMSTR, since I
would assume that each member must have a name, and this relationship
is almost certainly going to be one to one and not one to many.

Then I would use a secondary cursor into DEMANDHIST or MEMBERHIST
depending on the value of demandacct returned by your "joined" cursor
above.

Unless you are calculating the SF-TOTREV field used in the sort
statement, you probably want to try and shift that sort to the cursor
declarations - since a) Oracle is much faster at sorting than the COBOL
sort verb and b) you can also create an index in Oracle that will make
the sort that much faster.

Again - this is speaking in generalizations - but I hope you get the
idea. If not I'm always available.

Good luck.

Chris
