```
thought that as programmers, you guys may be interested in this.  in my
shop, we have an "on-line" parts catalog to look up exhaust parts.  what
do we use?  the old-fashioned book.  at the warehouse i call for brake
parts, what does the manager use?  same thing.  i have a complete set of
raybestos and wagner catalogs to find part numbers.  the computers (how
much money spent on on-line catalogs?) are relegated to looking up
prices, invoicing, and bookkeeping.  i was taught this method by those
who have been in the business for 30+ years.  they and i can locate the
correct part in the book in almost the same amount of time it takes
anyone else to find it in the computer's on-line catalog.  i'm not
trying to insult anyone, but in the automotive business all the on-line
parts catalogs i've ever seen totally suck.  and i'm sure that this
phenomena is not unique to the auto repair industry.  maybe y2k will not
be as catastrophic as some say.
have a good night, y'all.  see you tomorrow.
```

```
We are wholesale distributors of commercial lawn maintenance equipment.

Ninety percent of our illustrated parts lists have been put on CD by a
third-party vendor in Wisconsin.  The software that displays the pages
(quite clearly, I might add) also includes the ability to print pick lists,
cross-referenced parts, and other inventory functions.  It also has hooks
to tie in with various retail point-of-sale systems available to the retail
dealer.  

These catalogs on CD are quite useful at both the wholesale and retail
level.  While paper manuals are still in widespread use, even our 67 year
old service manager still goes to the computer first to look up a part. 

Perhaps the difference being the number of parts catalogs we have to
deal with.  Literally dozens and dozens.  Just the Kohler IPLs take up
6 feet of shelf space.
```
