```
Perhaps it is the way you have been presenting the concepts.  A person
must be presented with new concepts in a way that relates to their
current knowledge.  Not everyone can make the conceptual leap to new
ideas presented with little relation to what they already know.  How
could a COBOL programmer not understand a presentation of pointers, if
the instructor said something like "Imagine all of memory REDEFINED as
one table, each entry having a PICTURE X, with OCCURS the number of
bytes in memory.  The subscript into that table would be equivalent to
a pointer."?  How many COBOL programmers would give you a blank stare
at that?  Remember, we're talking low-level assembly-type pointers or
vectors here (pure addresses), not C-type pointers, where a length and
data type are associated.

I don't suggest that every programmer must learn assembly language as
a first language, only that assembly language is a very good starting
place if you want to *really* understand what's going on.  I think that
every 'good' programmer should know at least the basics of assembly.
Donald Knuth says in the preface to "The Art of Computer Programming":
"A person who is more than casually interested in computers should be
well schooled in machine language, since it is a fundamental part of a
computer."  Knuth is one of the real giants in our field.  I have yet
to meet a programmer that I thought was *really* good, who did not have
a very good knowledge of assembly language.  How many top notch race
car drivers do you think there are who do not have intimate knowledge
of how engines and drive trains work?

As far as C being better than COBOL to teach basic concepts, I would
disagree based on significant experience teaching programmers.  C is
far more difficult to master than COBOL, so the 'typical' C programmer
may be more skilled than the 'typical' COBOL programmer.  Understand,
I program mostly in COBOL myself, and am not knocking COBOL programmers.
But all of us know COBOL programmers who simply could not master C.  It
is simply that COBOL is easier to use, and there are those who can be
productive in COBOL, but who could never do so in C.  In my opinion,
that is a strong point in COBOL's favor.  And yes, you must learn about
pointers to use C.  In fact, you *better* know pointers, because you
are going to be spending a lot of time being shot in the foot and head
by pointer problems! :-)  But pointers are not everything, and there is
more to memory allocation and management than pointers (C programmers
often don't realize this).  When a COBOL programmer creates a record,
the process is very close to how you would create a record layout in
assembly or machine language.  C is very weak in this.  Struc and union
are very clumsy and kludgy by comparison.  I have taught a fair number
of programmers over the years, and have never found it difficult to
convey low-level hardware concepts to COBOL programmers.  They are all
used to the concept of subscripts, which is easy to relate to pointers.
Most COBOL programmers are also familiar with record numbers, also easy
to relate to memory pointers.  I can remember classes where some of the
students were what I would call marginal COBOL programmers; comfortable
enough in COBOL, but who would find it difficult, if not impossible,
to master C.  None of them ever had any real trouble understanding low
level hardware concepts, or assembly language.  To me, that is a clear
indication that programming in COBOL had given them a solid perceptual
basis upon which they could assimilate the new concepts.  I have yet to
see that so well displayed in any other language than assembly.
```
