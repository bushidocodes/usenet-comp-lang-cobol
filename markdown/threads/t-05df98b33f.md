As you'll recall years ago I told you we had bumper stickers for cars

Doggy-people, met a bank employee, head office not a teller. "Rubbish",

It makes sense for me to give up on computing when I did. Worked friggin 
hard and earning zero.

I gave you the details years ago and a reference I made in here CLC, you 
commented "Ahh the killer application that we all dream about".

Well we had it, not complicated program-wise but associated with 
Engineering you  had to understand the buzzwords and formulae. A branch 
of NDT (Non-Destructive Testing) it was an ultrasonic probe application. 
By law, in the oil/gas/chemical, engineering world, probably 
universally, governments both local and federal demand that on an annual 
basis you submit returns on the state of production equipment - 
specifically vessels (like you would visualize a home boiler, pipes 
going into and out of tanks with bends in the pipes. The agency in 
Alberta is called the Boiler's Branch. You are particularly looking for 
corrosion in the pipe bends; sulphur content automatically comes to 
mind, but just plain water is the main killer. (The water builds up a 
'sand-bank' like in a river and the contents of that sediment is what 
causes the piping to corrode.

Let's call him by his name starting with "K". He was a very smart young 
man, I dubbed him a "computer freak". He was always very pleasant but 
very demanding, looking forward light years ahead of where I was 
currently programming. He visualised the day he could transmit the data 
from his van in the boonies via satellite back to his office in Calgary, 
so it could be worked on while he was still out in the field. Give me a 
break ! Me, a one-man outfit, concentrating on getting a comfortable 
handle on OO - which I have now achieved - I didn't even have time to 
concern myself with programming for the Web. That would have come later, 
and hopefully his satellite idea 'Never'.

The actual application - way, way north of Edmonton, K is sat in the 
back of his van powered by a diesel battery. He sits in the van, an 
office area, ultrasonic screen and PC in front of him. Communicates with 
the helper where to go next. The likely corrosion spots on a pipe are 
already marked (called points). The tecchie holds a probe against each 
point, which transmits an imperial 99.999 inches reading back to his 
ultrasonic screen in the van. The graph can be a bit iffy so he may 
adjust the reading slightly - that he keys into the dialog on the PC. 
Dialog is two-dimensional 10 columns (allowing for 10 inspection dates, 
usually 12 months apart, unless a user requests more frequent readings 
on dodgy equipment); 25 rows/lines allowing for a maximum of 25 points 
on a pipe - averages around 10 points and sometimes goes to 15.

Having stored the data, do a standard set of calculations so that 
reports can be printed showing the Life cycle of Vessels ( indicated by 
the Items in the report, i.e. individual pipes ) comparing one date set 
of readings to the next, showing the gradual loss due to corrosion until 
it hits Nominal Critical level (pre-defined from engineering tables or 
formulae in the program). The reports in terms of Life cycle are 
ASCENDING by number of years calculated showing the first likely Vessels 
to fail. (If they get a Vessel go kaput, oil companies can be talking 
$millions, daily, until the Vessel unit is fixed/exchanged to bring it 
back on stream in the production process).

First engineer K ever approached said, "Of course this is written in 
FORTRAN ?". That's what they used to teach them back at school :-)
Unfortunately K used to keep the tech stuff close to his chest, only 
feeding me the tech detail as necessary - I suppose thinking I would go 
off and do my own thing. Well off and on through two companies and the 
period 1980 - 2000, working on Datapoint's DATABUS, RM/COBOL on a Tandy 
Radio Shack Model II, and having 'plugged' the Tandy machine to a Comapq 
portable we got it into DOS using a a PC-based version of RM/COBOL.

The money I was getting was iffy - it was his lawyer advised him 
ethically he should be giving me a regular monthly sum. K the 'Mr. 
techno-freak', was always looking to the future and got into the Web. 
"Try this, try that.....". I had between times switched to MS/COBOL and 
then Bill G pointed us at Micro Focus - who were they ? So bought M/F 
and and started re-programming the application in text mode. Not content 
with using the Web as a general source, he's looking at COBOL, 'cos he 
wants "Winders". Very first one, look at CA-Realia, then while I'm 
absorbing that, Fujitsu - couldn't possibly tell you which version - it 
was segmented into two parts and the compiler progression was 
illustrated by a clock thinggy. The manuals, when you printed them, said 
you could use any Editor. No luck there, e-mailed them and got back zip. 
Then he spotted some outfit he didn't know about called Micro Focus - 
they had a beta (VISOC) which did "Winders". I liked and was comfortable 
with their DOS version so that's the route I went. OMG ! Object 
Orientation ?????? The rest is history.

Snag is, all the while I am absorbing the various compilers and trying 
to get the hang of OO and GUI-ing, he's off on another tack - here's an 
enhancement he wants or claims a user 'demands to have it, like 
yesterday'. Put on the brakes, turn your head around 180 degrees and get 
back to the RM/COBOL all UPPERCASE (Uggh !), no END terminators - we are 
talking COBOL 74 - and no slick Animator to Debug. Sure you could get 
into DEBUG mode with RM, but in machine code or whatever - not this 
Johnnie's scene. And this process of switching from M/F back to RM for 
quick fixes just went on for years and years. I had to cycle re-setting 
my brain into RM or MF mode as appropriate. We both got pissed off, he 
was paying money getting no result and I was truly frustrated with the 
constant switching. No haranguing, but we parted company in 2000 after I 
came back from J4 held at Newbury. Never spelled out but I think he 
objected that I took that first holiday in 20-odd years !

So yes at this point I did do my own thing, continuing to write the 
application in OO. There were always factors floating in the back of my 
mind - no technical contact, how was I to know if there were any 
significant changes in NDT, plus a guess and probably very likely, the 
collection techniques have changed using new equipment. Example, oil 
pipelines trundling across the countryside getting oil say from Alberta 
to Chicago. They too have to be inspected for corrosion - but they have 
used for years little mini-carts/trains/machines traveling automatically 
along the pipes taken thousands, perhaps zillions of readings. I'll bet 
the equipment used at static oil/gs plants have adapted to this 
technology, certainly making a considerable change to the input to a 
computer system, plus the volumes and volumes of additional readings 
generated. (For years K has been using Kraut-Kramer instruments from 
(Sweden or Denmark) when it necessitates taking the top off a huge 
storage vessel - becomes a bit 'destructive' as opposed to 
'non-destructive' at that point - set the machine on the rim of the 
vessel top and it automatically moves around taking a considerable 
number of readings and from the results does calculations, graphs etc.)

No engineering expertise or backup, then reality really sets in. Be OK 
if they don't meet me, (i.e. did the marketing via the Web), but 
inevitably someone would ask me to come downtown. In walks this 
white-haired old gent of 77 years; even worse the poor old sod is using 
a walking cane, because of the hip surgery. We can't deal with him, 
he'll croak anytime soon !

I've just recapped above and see I missed the point about the 'killer 
application' - Way back as I switched to MF OO, K had a very detailed 
enquiry from Singapore about NDT software. Needless to say K wanted to 
jump in. I had to put him off, we just weren't ready. If you do a google 
on NDT I think you might *perhaps* find one reference to Ultrasonic 
software relating to production plants. K and myself, we are both aware 
of some applications written in DB access languages and they are 
dreadful - systems design - not programming - they have no concept of a 
hierarchical structure on identifying keys and require a lot of 
explaining before you can 'nurse' the applications. Can't remember 
whether it was Chevron or Pan-Canadian - they brought in an office 
worker just to feed data into the system because it was so complicated. 
Not necessarily $millions but I think we could have both been content 
with $150 - 300K per annum :-)

There's really no joy in programming just as a hobby - not unless you 
are an idiot. You young 'uns out there in your forties, be glad you have 
a job in the corporate world.

Jimmy

They do very nicely. NZ is one of the most efficient lamb producers in the 
world thanks to over a century of study, applied science and hard work by 
Kiwi farmers. The first shipments of frozen NZ lamb were sent to the U.K. on 
sailing ships in the 1880s, so we've been doing it for a while.

We can raise lambs, slaughter them, snap freeze them, and ship them anywhere 
in the world for less than the cost to local farmers in most of the world.

However, the sheep (particularly wool) industry has declined from a peak of 
over 70 million sheep to somewhere around 40 million today.

We have the right terrain for sheep (both mountain and plains) and careful 
selective breeding over decades has ensured that the sheep we raise are 
ideally suited to the environments where they will live.

We even have Halal slaughterhouses facing East to provide for export to 
Islamic countries like Iran, which are a big market for our lamb. 
(Originally, they wanted live exports so they could slaughter the sheep on 
arrival, according to the requirements of their religion. It was decided 
this was too cruel as the journey by sea was several weeks and it was pretty 
hard on the sheep. The solution was to have them ritually slaughtered here 
then frozen and shipped. Much less hard on the sheep...:-))

As for prices, we have noticed a steady increase in the supermarkets here as 
most of the production is exported. Today, I would expect to pay around 
$US15.00 for a decent leg of lamb. I can buy half a sheep (butchered, packed 
into chops, cuts, leg, and frozen, we call them freezer packs and they would 
feed a family for several weeks) for around $US20.00 but that is not lamb; 
it is mutton.

It hasn't quite got to the point where lamb here is considered a luxury (as 
it certainly has in Europe), but it is edging away from the budget of most 
housewives.
