While laptop drives have substantially higher *operating* shock
tolerance than desktop drives, the non-operating tolerances are
usually only a little higher.

For example, the (desktop) Seagate Barracuda's have operating shock
limits of 63G for 2ms, and non-operating limits of 300G for 2ms.

The (laptop) Seagate Momentus have operating limits of 350G for 2ms,
and non-operating of 1000G for 1ms.

So long as they're powered off and in a bit of padding, you're not
going to see much difference in their ability to survive transport or
shipping.

Laptop drives have the advantage of being much lower power, so you
usually don't need a power brick with a laptop-drive based external
hard drive, and are physically smaller and lighter, but are
substantially lower in capacity, substantially slower, and
substantially more expensive.

IOW, use the cheaper and faster desktop-drive based for backup, unless
you really need the portability of the smaller drives, or need to
actually use them in a mobile situation.

I don't think it is quite as black and white as that, Rene.

What has changed is that it is much more difficult nowadays to get by with 
just one language. For many years COBOL was the only game in town for 
commercial data processing and it is still very good for that.  It was quite 
enough to know COBOL and maybe a bit of Assembler for the platform you were 
working with, and that was about it really.

The technology was centralised and there was separation between most people 
and the computers that processed business. It was all mysterious and arcane 
for the average man in the street, who had no idea about how these machines 
worked or what they could do.

What changed was that the technology became so advanced that it was possible 
to put computing power onto the desktops where it was needed so that 
branches of the Business had immediate access to data instead of having to 
wait for the overnight batch to produce a listing and look at it tomorrow. 
People started connecting up the toy computers and discovered a synergy that 
was almost unimaginable. New ways of doing business started to emerge and 
they were not constricted by having a central processing point. At the same 
time advances were made in software technology and new paradigms emerged 
which were ideal for the idea of networked computer power. "Computer 
Science" evolved and spilled over into Business. Communications took off and 
it was expected that remote branches of the Business could be contacted and 
connected to without it costing an arm and a leg.

Although some very alert and progressive vendors added the new facilities to 
COBOL the community at large rejected it because it wasn't what they were 
used to and they simply didn't get why it was important. COBOL got left 
behind. Even those of us who did see the importance of things like Classes 
and objects soon came to the realization that using the COBOL version of 
these things was way less productive than using the same facilities in other 
languages. The final nail in the coffin was that COBOL became increasingily 
expensive and everythig else got cheaper.

That doesn't mean it is worthless, but it does mean that there are other 
languages which are better for modern development.

It isn't about emotional attachment to a Legacy language, it is about the 
most cost-effective tools available. These days it isn't enough to be a "one 
trick pony". You need skills with languages and scripts for the Web, for the 
computer, and probably soon for the pocket devices which will become the 
computer wave of tomorrow. For some of these things COBOL can be used, but 
it is far from the best tool for the job.

COBOL is experiencing an upsurge in demand because the "knowledge base " for 
it is dying off or retiring and it is not generally being taught.Although 
there is still need for people to maintain their legacy while they look at 
what to do next, there are very few development shops that see COBOL as 
being where their IT future is. However, it is a market place and like all 
markets, the laws of supply and demand drive the costs. As the rates go up 
people become interested in the language and some of them decide to learn 
it. (I have two people currently who are in contact with me because they 
want to learn COBOL and it isn't on the curriculum at their respective seats 
of learning. These are bright people who are proactive about their 
education. One of them is planning making a niche business in modernising 
COBOL, or migrating people off it. He seems to think that the market for 
this is increasing (I hope he's right, but things are pretty quiet at the 
moment... :-))

Still, past experience has shown me that sometimes very interesting 
challenges drop suddenly out of the Blue so I'm still hanging in there at 
the moment.

I think when it comes to COBOL it really isn't too important what the 
general consensus is. (It would be if you were a young person seeking to 
start a career and needing to be employed...) but if you are programming on 
your own account you can pretty much use whatever you feel comfortable with. 
If COBOL works for you (and you can afford it), use COBOL. If you are 
concerned about overall cost of ownership you might want to look at 
something else.

As I said earlier, it isn't just black and white, there are shades of grey 
in there.

Pete.
