There are two sides to this, of course, and I have spent time on both
sides.  But I would have to say that if I was paying a programmer $15
and charging a client $80 for his time, I couldn't sleep.  'Fairness'
depends a lot on the specific contract, and who the programmer is.
For 1099 work around here, a contract is considered 'fair' when the
consulting firm gets 20%-40% and the programmer gets the rest.  I
usually get about 25%, sometimes only 10%, but never over 33%, when a
programmer works for me.  Even 25% may sound high to some people, but
consider that the programmer doesn't have to hunt down the business,
and takes virtually no legal risk.  Believe me, it is a serious thing
to put your neck (reputation, home, etc.) on the line for many hundred
thousand dollars, and know that it could be chopped off by a contract
programmer doing something stupid that you couldn't help and didn't
even know about.  When real estate sales people sell a house they
typically get only half what their firm is paid, and they do all the
work, even finding the sellers and the buyers.  I think the programmer
should get certainly more than half, but 90% is probably too much.  I
would have to think about taking on a contract again that I only got
10%.  The risk and trouble is just too great to make it worthwhile.
It would have to be programmers that I knew well and trusted, and even
then it would be 'iffy'.  If I subcontract to somebody else, I don't
care what they make, as long as what they pay me is reasonable.  If I
did not help get their contract for them, then I have no claim on that
money, other than to pay me what I agreed to work for.  What they get
is between them and the client.

not take the contract.

The bottom line is that unless you are in you own subject domain with a
customer that you know well with systems that you know well you just
cannot do this.

between 3 and 9 months.  If you say 6 months then you are wrong, as the
details become clear then the qoute will go up or down.  Once you have
generated the functional specification from the user requirements you
are in a better position to refine the quote.  We have used a month
doing the analysis and we need between 10 and 14 weeks to complete the

out.  Going into to much precision to early can be a fatal mistake.

can accurately see whether there is a problem with the estimate or just
a problem.  If the tasks cannot be handled quickly by developers then
you may be in for surprises.

is fully complete before proceding to the next.  90% of the time is
