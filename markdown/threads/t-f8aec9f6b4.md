I promise you, no other language will "kill" COBOL, any more than
Esperanto has killed English!

This question is repeated a few times a month on this newsgroup.

I am very good at C programming, but prefere COBOL in many cases. If all
you're doing is processing fixed-field data, it is FAR SUPERIOR to C, and
quicker to develop and debug, and you can sit the customer down beside you
and THEY CAN READ THE PROGRAM. Try that in C! COBOL is also very
portable, and you can now get an Object Oriented COBOL compiler for $99
from Micro Focus for your PC.

I love working in C, and the code runs much faster for some applications,
and you can do all sorts of bizzarre stuff in a few lines. You can
also hang yourself very quickly, and take hours figuring out what has
happened! I love Perl for text analysis, and shell script for job
control with minor data manipulation. Different languages are good for
differenet things, so the competition is not an either/or thing.

But there's virtually nothing you can't do in COBOL, including such new
applications as client-end GUI control. This is very definitely a living
language, and is the world's predominant vehicle for business programming
among other things. I expect to see it still quite dominant 20 or 30
years from now, while many other languages rise and fall in its shadow.

I will try to NOT comment on this subject again for a few months ...

Roger J. Timberlake wrote:
›
› Is COBOL dead yet?
 
› Not even close.
 
› Will C\C++ ever kill COBOL?

Roger, stop being so myopic! I hate to tell you this, but there are many people out
there using languages such as RPG, Ada, Assembler......etc. because those languages
provide specific and REAL advantages over other languages FOR THE SPECIFIC TASK AT HAND.

C was originally developed by Bell Labs as a "systems software" language. COBOL was
developed as an business application language. If C kills COBOL, then there are a lot
of people using the wrong tool for the job at hand.

Roger, will hammers ever replace screwdrivers? Will wrenches ever replace pliers? Will
Lear Jets ever replace Boeing 747s? The message is... use the right tool for the job at
hand. If you need to write systems software such as communications drivers, video
drivers, printer drivers, etc. then use C. If you write business applications, then use
the language which was designed for writing business applications, COBOL!

We use C and C++ to write our software, BUT we're writing systems software. If we ever
decided to write a financial application, accounting or banking application, then there
is no question that we would write it in COBOL.

› 10 years from now what will be the dominant language?

Roger, the world is not nearly as black and white as you suspect. There's a lot of grey
out there and more than one solution to a problem. Despite what you read in magazines,
COBOL isn't dead. Because writing about Java and Delphi sell more magazines,
journalists will write about those languages. I don't believe everything I read in
magazines, because most journalists don't have to deal with real data processing
problems. Their biggest problem is meeting deadlines for magazine articles.

Based upon what I've seen, most corporations are loath to discard
anything that may have 'book value' from an accounting standpoint. To
wit how many dumb terminals and typewriters still linger around your work
site? The miliions of lines of COBOL code supporting today's enterprises
still have 'value' in the eyes of the non-MIS leader (unless they happen
to be one of those types who've read a COMPUTERWORLD and reckon
themselves experts in the latest technology).

In closing, many of these companies will not discard this substantial
investment until they are convinced it will help their core business.
When I worked for one of the Big 3 Automakers, we in IT were constantly
reminded 'this is a car company, not a computer company', the implication
being we would never be technologically current unless it helped build
cars more economically. Until this mind-set changes, the phrase 'Not
while we're alive' would aptly describe the impending demise of COBOL.

Even if some other language becomes the dominant one for developing
new applications, the more time passes the more, not less, difficult
it becomes to rewrite the billions of lines of COBOL out there that
are humming away in production every day and night.

As management becomes more cognizant of the real costs of
client/server, stupid old COBOL and the (usually) big iron it runs on
look better and better.

A recent Gartner Gorup report estimates that it will cost $0.60 per
line of COBOL code to make current production applications Year 2000
compliant -- a huge price tag, but still a bargain compared to
rewriting from scratch.

If a mishmash like C++ ever becomes the "dominant" language, heaven
help us . . . .
