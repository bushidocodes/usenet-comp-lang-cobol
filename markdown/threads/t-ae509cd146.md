```
Well, gang, since the situation at work has slowed to a point where
the motion of chilled molasses seems vigorous in comparison (MOVE
THUMB TO NOSE. PERFORM WATCH-THE-CLOCK UNTIL IMPETUS-TO-LEAVE >
BEARABLE) I have occupied my time with an exercise in speed and
elegance. Permit me to submit the problem for general bemusement and
amusement; I'll give the solution I came up with in a later posting.

The problem concerns reading a file, checking valid data- fields and
counting the errors for display at the end. Transaction volume is
fair-sized; in round numbers the input flatfile contains 46,000,000
recs (FB, LRECL=300). In the record there are three fields defined
S9(9) COMP-3 which may or may not contain valid data... hence, may or
may not cause the S0C7s discussed here a bit back. There are, then, 8
possible conditions for each record: (note: for those unfamiliar with
the acronyms AOK = All OK, NFG = No Fornicating Good) 1) All three
fields AOK 2) All three fields NFG 3) FLD1 AOK, FLD2 & FLD3 NFG 4)FLD2
AOK, FLD1 & FLD3 NFG 5) FLD3 AOK, FLD1 & FLD2 NFG 6) FLD1 & FLD2 AOK,
FLD3 NFG 7) FLD1 & FLD3 AOK, FLD2 NFG 8) FLD2 & FLD3 AOK, FLD1 NFG
(those of you familiar with matrix algebra are welcome to draw your
own diagrams)

To make life a bit easier let us assume that a healthy majority of the
records fall into two categories; approximately 16,000,000 recs are
class 1) (all flds AOK) and another 16,000,000 are class 2) (all flds
NFG). Using, then, the COBOL of your choice I would ask: what would
be the most efficient way to code this? The only restrictions I
placed on myself are adhering to modified Yourdon structuring (GO TO
permitted only when directing execution to the paragraph which
contains it or the exit of said paragraph) and keeping the code simple
enough for a two-year programmer to maintain; feel free to exercise
artistry and elegance.

DD
```

```
I presume the "solution" is not to use nested IF statements. I don't
have my manual here but as I remember EVALUATE FLD1 FLD2 FLD3 does not
allow NUMERIC checks. If it does just put the WHENs in order.

So here is a way to do it without nesting IF statements. It is based
on a book on structured COBOL I read about 15-18 years ago and I've
used the method many times. The general solution is number of fields
and number of values the individual fields can take.
4 fields
1 2 3 4 Field 1 can have 4 values 'w' 'x' 'y' 'z'
0 4 8 12 Field 2 can have 4 values 'a' 'b' 'c' 'd'
0 16 32 Field 3 can have 3 values '1' '3' '27'
0 48 Field 4 can have 2 signs +, - (no 0)

So 4x4x3x2 = 96 = 4+12+32+48 "cases"
The method is
line 1 always starts with 1 and is incremented by 1
line 2 always starts with 0 and the LAST value of the 1st line
and incrementing by that value
succeeding lines always start with 0 and "next" value that would
have been calculated for the previous line
Sounds complicated but once you've done it once or twice, it's very
instinctive. And it's maintainable if you know the method,ie Comments
or Remarks.

SO 1 2 FLD1 GOOD, BAD
0 2 FLD2 GOOD, BAD
0 4 FLD3 GOOD, BAD

77 EDIT-PNTR PIC 9.
..
..
PERFORM B000-EDIT-MASTER THRU B099-X
..
..
..
B000-EDIT-MASTER.
IF FLD1 NOT NUMERIC
THEN MOVE 2 TO EDIT-PNTR
ELSE MOVE 1 TO EDIT-PNTR
END-IF
IF FLD2 NOT NUMERIC
THEN ADD 2 TO EDIT-PNTR
END-IF
IF FLD3 NOT NUMERIC
THEN ADD 4 TO EDIT-PNTR
END-IF
GO TO
1OK-2OK-3OK
1BAD-2OK-3OK
1OK-2BAD-3OK
1BAD-2BAD-3OK
1OK-2OK-3BAD
1BAD-2OK-3BAD
1OK-2BAD-3BAD
1BAD-2BAD-3BAD
DEPENDING ON EDIT-PNTR

1OK-20K-3OK.
.
.
GO TO B099-X.
1BAD-2OK-3OK.
.
.
GO TO B099-X.
.
.
.
1BAD-2BAD-3BAD.
.
.
GO TO B099-X.
B099-X. EXIT.
```

```
Why do I do this? I check the fields 'backwards' because it is an old
CICS technique for placing the cursor at the first field in error which I
like to practise, nothing special there. I check ALL-OK and ALL-NG first
because... I stacked the deck that way; in giving the problem I said a
majority of records would hit those two conditions and since those
conditions are checked by the most 'expensive' statement (the PMAP/LIST
will show a CLC, Compare Logical Character) they are at the top. A
single-character condition check compiles to a CLI (Compare Logical
Immediate); if I recall correctly a CLI is one-third as 'expensive' as a
CLC... so one 'loses' in the verbosity/readability of checking two
single-character conditions one 'saves' by generating two CLIs vs. one
CLC.

Mr Rains - your use of the GO TO DEPENDING ON brought a smile; it reminded

client wanted me to write an IO module; they were too stodgy/lazy/afraid

the limit. I put together some code and had the following interchange
during the walk-thru:
Management Weenie: Hey, you can't do that... you use a GO TO DEPENDING ON
to direct program flow!
Me: What's wrong with that? It is a valid imperative not slated for
obsolesence and it is *blindingly* fast.
MW: But it's an ancient technique not readily understood by a younger
programmer and *all* our code must be understandable by a two-year
programmer!
Me: Then I am Even More Correct in using it... because you do *not* want a
two-year programmaer maintaining your IO modules.
```

```
I infer from a line elsewhere in your post that you are not using CoBOL
II. There is a useful feature in CoBOL II that would serve you well
here:

SET ALL-OK TO TRUE.
IF FLD3 NOT NUMERIC
SET FLD3-NG TO TRUE.
IF FLD2 NOT NUMERIC
SET FLD2-NG TO TRUE.
IF FLD1 NOT NUMERIC
SET FLD1-NG TO TRUE.


In addition to being useful, it is also more efficient. MOVE LIT-X TO
FLD3-FLAG generates a six-byte, double-mr MVC, whereas SET FLD3-NG TO
TRUE would generate a four-byte, single-mr MVI.
```
