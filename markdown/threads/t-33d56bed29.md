In 300-TABLE-LOOKUP, it looks like you could use the SEARCH verb
instead of inline PERFORMS to find stuff in the table you are using.

In addition, consistently indenting lines inside of statements like
READ and PERFORM by two to four spaces can improve readability, which
can ease debugging.  No indenting, or indenting only some lines, makes
it harder to read the code.  Also, when END-* explicit scope
terminators aren't lined up with the statement they are terminating,
that also makes the code harder to read.


In the following code block . . .

>            300-TABLE-LOOKUP.
>                MOVE "NO" TO WS-FOUND-PRODUCT
>                MOVE "NO" TO WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE
>                PERFORM VARYING WS-INFO-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
>                    UNTIL WS-INFO-SUB > 10 OR WS-FOUND-PRODUCT =
"YES"
>                IF I-PRODUCT-NO = WS-PRODUCT-NO (WS-CUST-SUB)
>                   MOVE "YES" TO WS-FOUND-PRODUCT
>                PERFORM VARYING WS-INFO-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
>                      UNTIL WS-INFO-SUB > 10 OR WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE =
"YES"
>                   IF I-UNIT-CODE (WS-INFO-SUB) =
>                        WS-UNIT-CODE (WS-INFO-SUB WS-CUST-SUB)
…[13 more quoted lines elided]…
>                END-IF.


In the deepest IF statement, the MOVE statement is indented two
spaces, but the PERFORM below it is not, making it appear that it is
not a part of the IF statement, however there is an END-IF below the
PERFORM, including it.  Further distracting the eye is and END-PERFORM
directly below the END-IF, making it look like it belongs to the
PERFORM, although it can't because the PERFORM isn't inline.  This
END-PEFROM actually belongs to a PERFORM five lines up and two spaces
to the left.  Two lines down, there is another END-PEFORM, it also
looks like it could belong to PERFORM 400-GOOD-REPORT, etc.  However
it belongs to a PERFORM 13 lines up and two spaces to the left (more
on this later).  So, there are two END-PERFORMS (arguably starting in
column 14), that belong to PERFORMS starting in column 12.

However, looking at it again, I realize that that second END-PERFORM
is a bit odd, because two lines down there is a third END-PERFORM, and
we only have two inline PERFORMS in this paragraph.  Even allowing
that PERFORM 400-GOOD-REPORT can be successfully terminated by
END-PERFORM, I don't see how that termination can happen across the
END-IF statement that comes directly after PEFORM 400-GOOD-REPORT.

I am not sure whether the following is correct, but it is how I would
have indented it:

 300-TABLE-LOOKUP.
     MOVE "NO" TO WS-FOUND-PRODUCT
     MOVE "NO" TO WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE
     PERFORM VARYING WS-INFO-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
         UNTIL WS-INFO-SUB > 10 OR WS-FOUND-PRODUCT = "YES"
         IF I-PRODUCT-NO = WS-PRODUCT-NO (WS-CUST-SUB)
             MOVE "YES" TO WS-FOUND-PRODUCT
             PERFORM VARYING WS-INFO-SUB FROM 1 BY 1
                 UNTIL WS-INFO-SUB > 10 OR WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE = "YES"
                 IF I-UNIT-CODE (WS-INFO-SUB) =
                    WS-UNIT-CODE (WS-INFO-SUB WS-CUST-SUB)
                     MOVE "YES" TO WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE
                     PERFORM 400-GOOD-REPORT
                 END-IF
             END-PERFORM
*END-PERFORM
         END-IF
     END-PERFORM
     IF WS-FOUND-PRODUCT = "NO"
         PERFORM 500-NO-PRODUCT-ERROR-REPORT
     ELSE
         IF WS-FOUND-UNIT-CODE = "NO"
             PERFORM 520-NO-UNIT-ERROR-REPORT
         end-if
     END-IF.

Notice how all the terminators now line up with their statements, and
all statements inside IFs and PERFORMs are indented.

At the end of the paragraph, I also put the PERFORMS of 500 and 520 on
the same line as the paragraph name.  Putting two statements on the
same line isn't against any rule I know, but I don't do it personally.

I added the small-case end-if.  I'm not sure how your compiler would
have interpreted a missing terminator, but since it was the last line
of code in the paragraph, it may not have mattered.  If there had been
more code, it most certainly would have mattered.

Note the line of code that is commented out.  This is where I'm not
sure what will happen.  Since there was an extra terminator in this
paragraph, I can't see how the program compiled, but I don't know the
compiler.  It would not have compiled successfully under VS COBOL II
(at the least it would have given a RC=4 and a warning, and I think I
might get an RC=8 with a "END-PERFORM was found without matching
PERFORM, the statement was discarded." etc.


ECS already responded about the problem in 100-LOAD-PRICE-TABLE, so I
won't duplicate his work.

Try putting the suggested fixes in and see if they work, then come
back and tell us what happened.

hi,
try using some displays to see where you are at and what you expect.  (Assuming
mvs environment) .  I generally place a display inside 000-main to start.  Egs.

 DISPLAY 'INSIDE MAIN RTN'. 

start from there and add more displays to follow the logic of your program. 
That is how I learned cobol.

You have nested performs and 2 dimensional tables weird things can happen 
given the limitations of the human mind.

Recommend eg.
for   WS-PRODUCT-NO (WS-CUST-SUB)   right before or after

DISPLAY ' '.
DISPLAY 'WS-CUST-SUB=' WS-CUST-SUB.
DISPLAY 'WS-PRODUCT-NO='  WS-PRODUCT-NO (WS-CUST-SUB).

You can also display an entire record:
eg :
DISPLAY 'I-ORDER-FILE-REC=' I-ORDER-FILE-REC.

If you have a large input file try the displays with say 5 records and follow
the logic.

Try and use periods in your coding, you may sometimes be surprized at what
defaults you may get from the compiler.
