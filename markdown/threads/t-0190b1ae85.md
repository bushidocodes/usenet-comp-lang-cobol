The blurb is about OO :-

" Craftism - the combination of pragmatist and empiricist practices in
software engineering. Those who practice this combination in software
engineering are Craftites. The term Craftite is apropos because most of
the adherents of pragmatism and empiricism in software engineering see
themselves and software engineering in a way similar to how most of the
guild craftsman of the Middle Ages saw themselves and their work
activities. Latter day Craftites, just as the craftsman of yore, see the
work of their profession primarily AS AN ART CARRIED OUT BY THE GIFTED.
While this may have been true for the craftsman of the Middle Ages
this does not characterize the nature and organization of present-day
software engineering activities."

I was browsing OO books this afternoon in Chapters, mainly Perl and
Java, JavaScript etc. but found one titled "Fuzzy System Design".
Thought, ahh that's the book for me - only to find the author had put an
entirely different meaning on the word 'fuzzy'. But his book did start
with one truism, quoting his old school teacher, "Learning is doing".

Ib,

It started out as a joke to rib Michael. But I see the 'learning is
doing' touched a raw nerve for you. Back in 1963 there were no courses, 
no nothing (or rather anything). First project I was on as an analyst
went from mid '63 to Summer '66 before it went live. We were learning as
we progressed. In to-day's terms an horrendous timeframe for something
as simple as printing invoices ! But when it went live, it went live
without a single major cock-up.

There is nothing wrong with formal training, as it became available -
but I still insist analysis is somewhat of an inherent skill, just like
those who have skills for sports, or the various branches of the arts
and sciences - and no amount of reading tomes is going to turn you into
a good analyst, you just have to think, think, think ..... and keep
re-thinking. You can take all the courses you want - it still finishes
up with applying the book learning to doing it, and sometimes getting
burned in the process.

Exactly the same applies with programming. Certainly an understanding of
how instruction sets etc. work can be a beneficial background. But even
with learning and doing you could still finish up as a 5 on a scale from
1 to 10. When I first got my Radio Shack and started learning RM/COBOL I
was working with a programmer who had his MSc in Computer Science. (His
basement was full of example listings which he had squirreled away over
the years). He insisted that he should write the first program to serve
as a model. I didn't object, made perfect sense. Some six months later
he told me I wrote better COBOL programs than he did - I still don't
know what he was referring to. 

Until such time as all our COBOL compilers have an OO module we are left
in the doldrums of treating OO as an abstract topic. As and when OO
COBOL becomes universally obtainable - it will get down to the doing to
learn, is OO viable/relevant and  what are the best approaches,
regardless of all the theory that has been written.

> .... but I would never spend money on a book with quotes like this.

I didn't <G>

PS: Haven't got the source, (picked it up through comp.obj), but from
memory the author of the 'Craftite' quote is an Earl Cox - has three
papers on his website.
