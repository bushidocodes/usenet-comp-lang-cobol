```
Hi, I just found this group last evening, but it has taken me awhile to
get through all of the messages. I've been programming mainframe cobol
for a very long time, and I am just amazed by the back and forth
discussion on GOTO's. Eons ago, as soon as I got to a shop that allowed
structured programming I never went back to GOTO's. I pulled many, many
all nighters debugging someone elses code that was riddled with go to's
(even out of sections, that one was real fun), alters, and switch logic.
Why regress back to 1970's way of coding. Yes, at times, you can save
code by using go to's, but why? Memory is cheap now days, and the code it
self sure shouldn't be taking you more then a few minutes to write, but it
will save days during future enhancements. I've seen some programmers
overly abuse the performs, but since CobolII allows instream Performs that
problem should go away. Sorry, I didn't mean to write an essay on this
subject.

What I really wanted to write about was, why companies arn't interested in
mainframe telecommutting??????? With all of this wonderful tech. at our
fingertips, why are companies complaining of the they can't get
programmers in their cities, when all they would have to do is hire
consultants from all over the country to telecommute. I could work 2 or 3
contracts at the same time if I was doing it from home. There are to many
times, when the project I am working on has down time, ie; computer
resources backed up, waiting on other peopel etc., that during that time I
could be working on another sites workload, etc. I'm sure I am not the
only one around that has been doing cobol so long, that we can program it
in our sleep. And the advantage to the clients is that they do not have
to supply onsite equipment and supplies.??? I am really confused why this
isn't becoming more of the practice instead of the rare exception. The
companies looking for internet programmers are starting to take this
approach, but why not the mainframe shops? Any one have an answer??

A.S.
```

```
Well, as a former manager/director I would disagree with the above (at least
slightly ).

While I've been in places where they don't trust contractors to do anything
out of sight, most are more reasonable. I did a project at one place where
contractors had to sign in when they got to the floor where all the IS staff
was located. Not only did you have to sign out for lunch but you had to sign
out whenever you were going to be off the floor for more than five minutes!
Whenever you signed out you also had to indicate when you expected to be
back and then sign your "actual" return time. The sign in/sign out book sat
right outside the director's office. Needless to say, this shop (run by this
anally retentive guy) was an absloute disaster.

I have convinced some clients to allow telecommuting over the years. First,
if I needed to work a weekend I would tell them that I had the capability to
do COBOL/CICS/DB2 stuff on my machines as easily as their mainframe. Then
I would say, "look, if you authorize me to work at home (or at my office) over
the weekend I won't bill you for the time it would take me to drive here and
back" (get them in their pocket books). That probably carries more weight here
in Los Angeles than some other places perhaps. I've never had anyone turn this down.
Of course you need to know the client. After that it wasn't a big leap to say,
"I think it would be more effective if I do this at home (or in my office)".

On the larger point of telecommuting. I'm always amused (and irritated) at the
reluctance of managers to accept telecommuting. They always wonder "how do
I monitor progress. I always look at them as if they just spoke to me in
Sanskrit and say "well, when I was a manager we had a project plan with
tasks and deliverables and I used those to monitor progress. Why would it be
any different if I were looking at people or not. They either complete their
assignments or they don't. What's the mystery?" Usually, if I'm in a conversation
like this I try to say this in a way which implies, "why haven't you figuered
this out yet"?

I happen to think that what we do is very suited to telecommuting and over the
last fifteen years I've heard a lot of talk about telecommuting but have seen
little of it. After the earthquake many companies alowed telecommuniting
because some people were facing three hour or longer commutes and the phone
companies were giving big discounts. Most of that telecommuting has gone away.

Sorry for the length.
```
