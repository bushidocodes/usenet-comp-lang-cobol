I was going to wait until this got posted a few more times before 
responding... but I guess I'm just impatient.

If you have reason to conclude that your system and its historical data 
will never be ported to another compiler/platform or that there will never 
be a need for your system to exchange with another system based on a 
different compiler/platform then storing the numerics as anything other 
than DISPLAY format is, I would say, a matter of taste or preference.

Absent these absolutes - and Life as I know It is full of uncertainties - 
then it would most likely be easiest, for you and those who come after 
you, to store the numerics without any sort of compacting, compression or 
editing... and writing data which might go Someplace Else as edited 
numerics, especially S9(n).99 SIGN LEADING/TRAILING, might well make Life 
easiest for all.

COMPing of any sort - unless you are dealing with *very* large quantities 
of data and *very* tight schedules - made more sense when machines were 
slower and core/disk/tape more expensive than they are now.  Before taking 
any steps which will require a lot of work to un-do I would suggest some 
serious benchmarking, involving an LRECL of, say, 5,000 with at least 500 
numeric fields of varying sizes and between twenty and thirty million 
records.

(year-end processing for inventory or suppliers, breakable to a 
store-by-store or individual item level ('Is it worth carrying Size 25 
left-handed widgets any more?  Let's see how many we sold' ... so no, it 
isn't that much)

I would like to add that -while you hit the top of the nail regarding
storage of binary data items- it won't hurt to use binary data items
for internal representation (e.g. working-storage and -linkage
sections) in case those items are regularly used in computations. The
storage saving aspect not really being important nowadays (except in
some special cases) but it saves many instruction cycles for conversion
of data formats.

Reminds me of the first program they gave me to increase its performance
(being a junior programmer). A very intensive computing program with
really large tables for that time it used to run more than 60 hours on
a 512 KB mainframe in 1971 in single task mode (no OS, only an
Executive). Analysis of the object listing of the compiler instructed
me to change some data elements from USAGE DISPLAY to USAGE COMP-3
(packed decimal). The result being a runtime of about 24 hours.

No wonder many asked me later on to solve their performance
problems. :-)
