Today I had to extend a C# routine that parses COBOL, so it could detect if

INVALID KEY
DISPLAY "INVALID KEY WAS FOUND ON PRODUCT MASTER FILE: "
PROD-KEY

COBOL INSPECTs?

PERFORM... VARYING?

slicing or UNSTRINGing the string?

Today it is a couple of regular expressions (1 for the required key word and
one for the dot) in 2 lines of C#. They check if the required key word
(or dot) is preceded by a single " or ' and return true if it is.

Time moves on.

I had to do something similar for the COBOL style checker I wrote. I
got an idea from reading the dragon book:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_Compiler_Design

So I wrote a lexer (not a parser) in COBOL to examine the input file
character-by-character to break it out into "tokens" as:
1. INVALID
2. KEY
3. DISPLAY
4. (quoted literal string)
5. PROD-KEY
6. DISPLAY
7. (quoted literal string)

That makes the "INVALID KEY" and full stop invisible because they are
inside the quoted literal character strings. Plus the lexer created a
record for each token with metadata. So that would be three COBOL
reserved words, a literal character string, a user-defined name
(PROD-KEY), another COBOL reserved word, and finally another literal
character string.

I didn't attempt anything like parsing out complete COBOL statements,
and the lexer still took a LOT more than 20 lines of COBOL code. I only
had to "emit" a report on the COBOL source file, rather than translated
code in assembler or machine language, but it was fun coding it and
testing it.

I don't know anything about C#, but I would have a healthy respect for
any program that reads COBOL source code and translates it into
something else.

A 1900lb warhead is plenty big! And there's nothing wrong with Amatol
as an explosive (and if there were, the Germans would not logically
have used it on a particularly expensive delivery system). Just
exposure to the overpressure wave of that size explosion will result
in 99% human fatalities out around 80-90 ft (~10 PSI). And most
structures will fail at much lower levels - you'd expect almost any
building to be destroyed at 5 PSI, and most residential structures at
1 PSI or less. Heck, at 5 PSI you'd expect serious damage to very
strong structures like bridges.

It's not that humans are all that tough, rather they're fairly compact
- a large wall will absorb and transfer a very large amount of energy
to the building's structure - the limited cross sectional area of a
human rather limits that.

But explosions do weird things. Perhaps the incident in question had
the warhead landing in a nice deep basement under the butcher shop
before exploding, or just embedding into the ground. Or it fizzled
(which might well be due to poorly manufactured explosive).
