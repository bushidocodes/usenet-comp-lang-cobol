Interesting problem.

I did something similar once in COBOL/400, where the people in accounting could
roll-up one or more 'real' accounts into a temporary account. Then, they could
roll-up the temporary accounts, along with other 'real' or temporary accounts
into even higher temporary accounts, and roll those up into yet higher
temporary accounts. What I was trying to do was create an MS Excel spreadsheet
'thing' for the users on the AS/400, written in COBOL. Trouble would be when
they tried rolling up into a lower temporary account, thereby, possibly,
creating a recursive situation.

The whole thing was created using DB/400, with indexes pointing to ancestors
and children (index fields containing zeros indicated the end of the 'genetic
line'). That way, I could 'walk' any temporary account's 'genetic line', and,
if I encountered myself along the way, I'd know that this temporary account is
invalid and I'd disallow the user-created formula. In your situation, however,
the 'genetic line' is more complex in that there are always two ancestors and
any number of children. And (!), you want to display the recursion in a
user-friendly manner (GUI? Or does it *have* to be character oriented???). In
my case, I simply issued a message to the user indicating that the roll-up
formula was invalid due to recursion, and I left it up to the person to figure
out where the error existed (no problem, since I was dealing with end users who
did the high level design in the first place, so they all had a detailed
understanding of how the application worked).

Has to be done in COBOL, huh? But the DBMS can be something like DB/400 or MS
Access, right -- not necessarily VSAM?!?

Hmmm... ::putting on MTV RAD Thinking cap::... ::pity COBOL doesn't support
dynamic arrays the way VB does... but (!), if I designed it in COBOL/400, I
could use DB/400 logicals to...::... ... ... ::where did I put the source code
to that stupid COBOL/400 program I'd written???::...
