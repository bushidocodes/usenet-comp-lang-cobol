I've been interviewing some real mainframe veterans for new
positions. These guys go back 25+ years and they are some of the finest
people I have ever met. They all share a real love of programming and
have seen the good times and the bad. These guys are certainly not
greedy, but I think they should maximize their income without getting
jacked around by all the BS artists who are jumping on the the Y2K
bandwagon.

Cory had an idea a while back - " Five Guys who do Cobol" - I've been
thinking about it ever since and its making more and more sense. This
weekend we roughed out a new website to market these mainframe warriors
and I'd like to get everyone's opinions. Here's my idea:

1)Y2K capable programmers will be able to list their skills, experience,
salary requirements, and availability in a simple grid.

2)To keep things simple, you must be able to report for work within 10
business days.

3)All candidates will retain confidentiality to potential employers -
only valid offers will be forwarded - no BS/hype/etc...

4)All fees will be paid by new employer - candidates to contact us to
verify employment - one time employer fee equivalent to 1 week gross
wages after 30 days of contract completion.

Let me know what you think.

Thanks,
Steve Wray Recruiter - "A good listener is not only popular everywhere,
but after a while he know's something."

My Five Guys COBOL Inc. is a more formal concept. The Gang of
Five incorporate (Consider using a $19.95 incorporate yourself
kit rather than paying a lawyer $750 to fill out a few pieces
of paper.), elect one of them president, pitch in a couple grand
a piece. They laser-up some hand-outs, send them to all the local
companies they know and work as a corp.

They keep their current job and use voice mail, cell phones,
faxes, email, beepers, a web page to market themselves and look
like a full production corp. If one of them has a stay at home
spouse with a nice phone presence, that spouse can man the voice
mail and even answer the phone, "FGC Inc, Steve is meeting with
a client this afternoon...." on occasion.

As the work comes in, they decide who gets to quit and work the
job.

The downside is about a day's worth of paperwork a month. The
upside is they are their own master's. They clean up over the
next 5 years and either retire (perhaps retire again) or in 2002,
2003 shift their FGC Inc. to doing JAVA front ends to Object
Database 2.

As a corp, they pay themselves as they earn the money. Keep some
of the funds in the corp, set up the 401(k) as they wish, be very
selective on who they hire. Use a law firm or a CPA partnership
as the financial model, but not as exploitive. They clear a
million bucks apiece EASY by the time the Y2K dust settles in
2003.

That's when they light the cigars and pour the brandy. Until
then, it will be work, work, you never pay attention to me, work,
work, are you listening, work, work....

It will take an understanding spouse, they are building a better
and more secure life for their families. Compare part ownership
in an FGC Inc with a couple contracts, their own P/390 or OPEN/370
(http://www.funsoft.com), a half million in the bank, a couple
hundred grand in each person's 401(k), 25 employees, to being an
employee and the 2004 RIFs are looming. FGC Inc can propose to
outsource all the IT work from their clients. Save them even more
money.

An employee has a contract for another 2 weeks and that's it.
Being an employee is less secure than being an owner of an FGC Inc
with a couple contracts.
