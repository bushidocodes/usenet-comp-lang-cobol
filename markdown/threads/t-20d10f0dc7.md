### Is the Standards process broken?

- **From:** "Chuck Stevens" <charles.stevens@unisys.com>
- **Date:** 2001-05-16T15:40:31-07:00
- **Newsgroups:** comp.lang.cobol
- **Message-ID:** `<9duvkt$krr$1@mail.pl.unisys.com>`

```
Various people at various times have asserted that the process by which the
COBOL standard is being developed is "broken", that the people responsible
for "breaking" it should acknowledge their failings and strive to repair the
damage they have done.

Some of these have pointed the finger at ANSI and demanded that that
organization be held accountable.

ANSI bore responsibility, as I understand it, for the development of the
current "American National Standard" COBOL ('85) and its amendments.  This
standard (and its predecessors, I believe) was developed in cooperation with
the various national standards bodies of other countries, but was developed
and published first and foremost an *American* standard.  Basic
responsibility both for the *development* process and the *content* of the
standard lay in the United States; any other input, however carefully
considered, was advisory.

But the FCD is a different matter.  The FCD is intended from the very
beginning as an *International Standard*, with "one country, one vote" over
the technical content of the document.  It is true that the International
Standards Organization through its various subcommittees has delegated
responsibility for producing the document itself to NCITS/J4, but it is also
true that ISO/IEC JTC1/SC22/WG4 maintains responsibility for its technical
*content* and exercizes significant oversight into technical matters.  J4
may *recommend* changes, enhancements, revisions to WG4, but it's WG4 that
ultimately decides, and the United States position on any topic carries no
more weight than that of any other national member body, as I understand it.
ISO/IEC procedures, at least as they apply to this standard, seem to be
based on the premise "one country, one vote" from all I can gather.   The US
contingent has no more say than the British or the Danish or the Japanese
contingent.

I believe this change in responsibility, and the changes in process
resulting from that change in responsibility, stem ultimately from the
perception that (however cooperative X3J4 may or may not have been during
the development of the current and prior standards), the "American National
Standard" was fundamentally a 'Murkin product developed by 'Murkins for
'Murkins and while the 'Murkin developers might listen politely to other
views they were under no obligation to incorporate those views.   Yes, I
know, that's probably an exaggeration, but ...

I believe the ultimate authority for the *approval* and *negotiation*
processes -- including these  "one country, one vote" conventions associated
with the CD-to-FCD-to-DIS-to-IS processes -- lie with ISO/IEC.   Whether I
think ISO/IEC should use those procedures is not a whole lot more important
or more influential than my opinion as to whether Great Britain should
retain or abolish the monarchy.  I have no standing in the latter case, only
a little in the former.

This change in process, authority, and focus were introduced as a response
to a perceived need.  I believe that perception was legitimate.  And if
enough interested parties in enough member countries felt neglected enough
by the ANSI process to cause ISO/IEC to issue an issued an ultimatum to the
effect "Turn technical content responsibility for the next COBOL standard
over to us, or we won't approve it like we have its predecessors", I can
certainly understand why they would have done so.

With this change in authority, made as the result of a perceived need, comes
acceptance of the rules by which that new authority operates.   ISO/IEC has
a right to its "one country, one vote" conventions, with all the
negotiations that entails.  They have every right to maintain those
conventions whether I think they should or not.

Moreover, I believe the division of authority (J4 for developing the
standard, WG4 for its contents) makes  the process of developing the
standard somewhat more ponderous than it would be were authority not divided
and input from other bodies deemed advisory, as was the case in the past.

It seems to me that each of these issues -- the fact that the United States
for many years decided what COBOL should look like and the international
community was largely pressured into accepting their juidgment on the issue,
that users in other countries found that unacceptable, that the members of
ISO/IEC agreed with those users and took over content authority for the
International Standard while leaving development authority with J4, that
ISO/IEC procedures allow each national delegation an equal say in any issue
regardless of the number of COBOL users that feature would impact (in or out
of that nation), and the fact that individual national bodies have sometimes
taken opposite positions on the same subject, leading to lengthy
negotiations -- has contributed to the fact that the process of developing
the FCD has been slower than it might have been.

It has been nigh onto sixteen years since the promulgation of the last COBOL
standard, and the draft that is being proposed has a GREAT many new
features, some of which are perceived as already obsolete.   It may well be
"too much, too late", and in hindsight I can certainly see that the COBOL
community would likely have benefited from multiple revisions introduced
more frequently.

But the current standards process *is* a replacement process already, and
each of the differences from its predecessor process is evidence of a change
intended to fix something within that process that was perceived as
seriously "broken".

Does the fault lie with the US-centric nature of the earlier standards
processes?

Does the fault lie with those who lobbied to have ISO/IEC take over
authority for the next COBOL standard from ANSI?

Does the fault lie with those member national bodies of ISO/IEC who demanded
equal votes to their more populous brethren in the process of setting up
that organization?

Does the fault lie with those member national bodies who insisted on the
inclusion of certain features in the FCD?  Or insisted that they be omitted?

Does the fault lie with those who submitted a plethora of "better
mousetraps" for inclusion into the standards, and lobbied their national
bodies for the inclusion and integration of those features?

Does the fault lie with the consensus of WG4 for insisting on the inclusion
of such features rather than deferring them to a later revision, whether J4
or J4-tag felt it was appropriate?

Does the fault lie with J4 for attempting to be all things to all nations?

And if so for more than one of these, to what degree do we apportion the
blame among them?

While the current process is arguably ponderous, I'm not convinced it's
*wrong*.  I believe international standards arrived at by formal consensus
are a good thing in and of themselves, and I believe there is no way to
avoid intrinsic limitations to any process by which such international
formal consensus can be reached.  But I do not believe the processes should
be abandoned because their limitations or even their intricacies have in
some cases slowed or otherwise restricted progress toward that final
consensus.

    -Chuck Stevens
```

```
delegated and one which retains responsibility over the ultimate contents --

to raise and answer the question formally at *two* levels of authority than
it does to do so at *one*, even if everybody voting is in agreement.

listen to your suggestions and maybe we won't.  Is that process responsive
to the needs of programmers in Japan or Denmark?

And I'm trying to figure out how a process can be "accountable".   The
individuals involved in the process are accountable to their national
standards organizations, and the joint committees formed by those national
standards bodies are accountable to ISO/IEC.  To what *formal* organization

each implementor using Humpty Dumpty's   approach to the definition of the

appropriate for *some* formal body to be in charge of any given standard,

process is broken because it is *un*responsive to the desires of the
worldwide COBOL community; in fact, I am inclined to believe that, if the
process is flawed in that department, it is because it was *too* responsive
to the desires of the worldwide COBOL community.  The various committees
have tried to accommodate just about everybody's desires.   Now, when
they've cried "Enough!  Time to get the standard *published*!", there's a
hue and cry because they're not adding this last little feature or that last
little adjustment!    That's how it looks to *this* observer, anyway!
```

```
The standards process is NOT broken. And it doesn't need much in the
way of an overhaul. What COBOL DOES need is more people to do the work
of developing the standard. There are only 12 of us now (with
occasional help from non-members). It is not enough.

After the Corrections Ammendment to the 1985 Standard (ISO 1991, ANSI
1993), the target date for the new standard was 1995 or 1996 (I
forget).  Two MAJOR projects have delayed it all this time.

One was Internationalization, which involved MOCS (Multiple Octet
Character Sets) such as Japanese, Locales (which would set a number of
default features, such as Sort sequences, date and time formats,
currency symbols, etc), and different "Roman" alphabets (e.g., in some
alphabets, and accented E comes after unaccented E; in others it comes
after Z. And then there are all the different letters that are not in
the English Alphabet.),  and some other geographical considerations.

Part of the problem with internationalization was a lack of immediate
expertise on the committee, requiring inquiries and external reviews
of the proposed standard.  Additionally, there are MORE THAN ONE
standards committees working on computer representations  of
alphabets.

Lest  anyone complain that this is a waste of time and it will be a
totally unused feature of COBOL, let me say that this was/is a hot
item for both the Deutsche Institut fuer Normung and the Information
Technology Standards Committee of Japan.

The second feature which seriously delayed the standard was Object
Orientation.
OO development was mostly handled by an "ad hoc" subcommittee of J4
(at that time, X3J4), because not enough of the regular members had
sufficient expertise.  Meetings were held in conjunction with J4 (and
sometimes alone).  But since there were no meeting fees, no NCITS
fees, and no commitment, companies and individuals were willing to
participate on this limited basis.
At one time, the new standard was scheduled for 1996. However, about
this time, X3J4, in consultation with the OO ad hoc, decided that the
syntax/organization of the OO features was flawed and "too much out of
synch with the developing OO techniques."  A conscious decision was
made to make the COBOL OO format match the CORBA model, even tho that
meant a major re-write of the features, and a major delay in the
standard.  CORBA was chosen over COM because CORBA was not
proprietary.

The third feature which delayed the standard was "Binary, Bit and
Boolean".  These are really crucial to bring COBOL into the modern
computing paradigm.  Unfortunately, there awere a lot of unexpected
side effects:
What is the padding character in a Bit Field (or a National field, for
that matter)? A MAJOR change to COBOL fundamentals.  How about the
padding character in a group which consists of all Bit Fields (or
National fields)? That took months of argument: One of the oldest
rules in COBOL is that all Group Names are alphnumeric.  We finally
came up with a new syntax feature: GROUP-BIT and GROUP-NATIONAL, where
the padding characters are binary zeroes and "National" spaces,
respectively.  There were other ways to do it... hence the lack of
initial agreement.

There were also "minor" features which took up a lot of time. For
instance, "Dynamic Tables" was high on the list of desired features. 
There were several proposals (I wrote one of them), but when we
finally adopted it, several compiler makers advised us that there was
"No way we can implement this efficiently."  Ultimately, it was backed
out of the standard.

Work was still being done on OO, Internationalization and BBB just a
few months before the FCD was isssued.  A STANDARD has to be done
RIGHT! If it is full of vagueness, incomplete specifications and
conflicting rules, It is not a standard, it is a recommendation or a
guideline!

While work was being done on these, the other items on the "desirable"
 list, and even the "nice, but not important" list were done. 
Depended on who had the interest to write up the proposal.  But I do
not think that these efforts slowed down the standard at all.  A few
times the meeting even broke up early.

Even now, the OO ad-hoc is working on a Class Library, to be issued in
a year or so as a Technical Addendum to the Standard. (See, there WILL
be changes before the next complete standard!)

In Particular, the committee needs representatives of the COBOL USER
community to work on the language. We need more programmers, and also
more support from the COBOL compiler "community". Realia (Computer
Associates), Ryan-McFarlane (Liant), AccuCOBOL, Tiny COBOL, GNU COBOL,
DEC (Now part of Compaq, which IS represented on the committee by the
Tandem Compiler guru, but that still isn't DEC), and ICL are NOT
represented on the committee.  Membership on the Committee would be a
GREAT assignment/opportunity for a Graduate student in Computer
Science, or a great source of publishable papers for a Computer
Science Faculty member.  There might even be grant money for THOSE
folks!

It's not the process, folks, it's the amount of work and the lack of
help.

YOU can be a member of the COBOL Standards Committee, and make a
difference!

Stephen J Spiro
Member, J4 COBOL Committee
```
