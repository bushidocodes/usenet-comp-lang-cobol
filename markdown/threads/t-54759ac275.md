The mathematical definition of integer was formed in a field named Set Theory,
which is generally credited to Georg Cantor, who invented it in 1874. 

In set theory, a 'thing' cannot be defined in isolation, only as a member of a
set. A number in isolation is simply a whole number. In order to be an integer
(singular), it must be in the set of 'all integers'.

The COBOL standard follows that definition. It calls numbers "numeric data
items" rather than integers. The word integer is used for operands and function
classes, which in all cases but two _does_ mean all numbers without gaps. The
exceptions are ACCEPT .. FROM DATE and FUNCTION DATE-OF-INTEGER. Since there is
no definition of 'numeric operand', those two should have been alphanumeric.
They _can_ be written as such, thanks to weak type checking.

Set theory is *a* foundation for mathematics, including the theory of

among those who care, but it is not the only foundation.  (And glossing
"foundation for the theory of integers" as "mathematical definition of

simply means "axiomatic basis sufficient for deriving the remainder of
mathematics, with the usual Godellian completeness/consistency caveat".)

Peano arithmetic is both a foundation for mathematics and arguably
closer to being a "definition of integer", since it's first and foremost
a theory of integers.  Frege's theory of integers is apparently
essentially equivalent to Peano arithmetic, but I'm not familiar with it
myself.

Apparently there's some opinion that category theory is also a
foundation, and not just (just!) a superstructure built on set theory.

Some folks seem to feel you have to start with first-order logic as your
foundation, and everything else is built on that.

(It might also be worth noting that there are now multiple competing set
theories, of essentially equivalent degree of logical consistency, and
hence suitability for defining integers.)

In short, I don't think the appeal to Cantor is going to help support
your argument.

I'm hardly a mathematician.  My degrees are in CS and English.  I get by
with typical non-specialist math that might come up in my work (enough
calculus to integrate polynomials, take partial derivatives, and so
forth; discrete math through basic group theory and a little category
theory; basic linear algebra; basic probability and statistics), and I
occasionally poke into number theory or game theory or what have you for
amusement, but I know some mathematicians, and I'm not one.

I don't think introducing more definitions of integer into the
discussion is useful at this point - and indeed it may never have been.
  Whether you use a cardinal definition like Frege's or the typical
set-theoric approach (the natural numbers are the cardinality
equivalence classes of sets or other collections - "3" is the class of
all collections with three things in them and so forth), or an ordinal
definition as in number theory (where each natural number is the result
of the successor function applied to the previous number), or some other
kind of definition entirely, you're still sidestepping the original
question, which is whether a member of a set with "holes in it" could be
an integer.  Obviously it can.  Other posters have pointed out that
membership in arbitrary finite sets (eg {1, 2, 4}), with or without
"holes", does not preclude being an integer.  We might also refer to
infinite cardinality and the equivalence of aleph-null infinite sets to
note that, say, the set of all even numbers *is equivalent to* the set
of all integers, even though clearly the set of all even numbers has
"holes" when compared to the integers (ie if E is the set of even
numbers, and Z is the integers, then Z - E is not empty).

But even this distracts from the actual issue, which is whether
something like "20030430" (I've forgotten the exact example that was
originally posed) is an integer, possibly with the qualification "when
treated as a date".  Of course it is, and it is not, depending on what
assumptions you which to make.  It is more precise to say something like
"under the system commonly used for representing numbers in writing
using the numeral characters found in the Latin-1 alphabet, the series
of characters '20030430' outside any special context represents an
integer".  Obviously "20030430" is not *in itself* an integer, any more
than any string of numerals is.

The question then becomes "when 20030430 is interpreted as a date, do we
have grounds for stating that it still represents an integer?".  No
amount of mathematics solves this problem (except for a most basic
understanding that an integer is a whole number or negation thereof).
Certainly we have *some* grounds.  "20030430" is identical to the string
used to represent a certain integer (20030430) under the
widely-recognized representation system described above; thus we have
syntactic grounds.  It is reasonable to argue that these are not
sufficient in themselves.  Consisder then semantic or functional ones.
The "add one" argument, which I believe you (Robert) introduced, goes
like this: integers have a function for adding one, which is a bijection
Z->Z; that is, add 1 to an integer, and you get another integer.  Adding
1 to the integer 20030430 produces another integer, 20030431, but
*considered as a date* 20030431 is invalid.  Thus the members of the set
"integers considered as dates" do not have the "add one" bijection, and
so are not integers.

However, as has been noted, being a member of "integers considered as
dates" does not preclude being a member of "integers".  "20030430" is a
"integer considered as date" but also an "integer".

The distinction you're trying to draw would be better established in
category theory, I think, where collections are associated with
functors.  There you could say that the category D, consisting of the
set "integers considered as dates" and the bijections upon them, is
distinct from the category I, consisting of the set of integers and
bijections upon them.  And then you could get to whatever your point
about integers-considered-as-dates was going to be.  Um, maybe that "add
  one" wasn't a bijection on them?  (Of course, D does include a
bijective successor function, and that function can be defined in terms
of the bijections defined for I.  It's a funny old world.)

But IANAM.
