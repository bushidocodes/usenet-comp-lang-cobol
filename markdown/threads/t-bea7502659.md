One suggestion.  Try to stay away from "percent complete" reports.  You'll find that 
every item spends 80% of its time "80% complete".
Try to arrange your work packages so that they are between a week and month in duration. 
 Nobody can fudge whether they have started something, or finished it.  This way you 
will always have a better handle since everything is "not started", "in progress" or 
"finished" and nothing can ever "run-away" too far.

(I know that sometimes you are forced to present "% complete" reports - I even provide 
for them in PlanBee - but only because nobody would buy it if I didn't).

1. You can not deliver a baby next month by impregnating nine women
today. Which means that you will not do much to speed an ailing project
along by throwing people at it (in my experience it will slow a project
down because the guy in charge (me) spends too much time bringing extra
bodies up to speed).

2. If any thing can go wrong it will. Also means that if you are not
worrying about something then you should be worried about the fact that
you are not worried about something (MACLEAN'S LAW, before anybody
claims copyright to it!).

3. Don't trust any one, especially users. They lie. Don't trust your
programmers when they tell you they have completed their work and it
works (it will fall over in testing). I've had programmers (with
degrees) tell me that their program works, simply because the compile
did not work.

4. When some one tells you they are working flat out it usually means
that they are reading Playboy (or the local equivalent). I once watched
an experienced programmer spend a year doing that which a trainee took
two weeks to re-do. 

5. When you estimate that it would take you a wee a job, double it up
for any one else.

6. Assess risk seriously and adjust project duration accordingly. OVER-
ESTIMATE rather than be optimistic. Most projects will over-run because
we all tend to under-estimate with undue optimism. I remember one
project I worked on where I doubled my best estimate because I knew the
project manager would halve it....
