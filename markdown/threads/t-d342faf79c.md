I really enjoyed that :-).

The first machine I ever learned to program was an NCR 500, in 1965. It had 
a "four address" instruction format... do this to this, store the result 
here, then go here for your next instruction. So, in effect, EVERY 
instruction was an implied GO TO. There was no assembler and no high level 
language; we worked in machine code, just like Mel.

I think I did all the things that Mel did (mainly because I could :-)). It 
was great fun making the program modify itself as it ran... and the most 
popular program I wrote for it was one which picked horses every weekend. 
You fed in the form for the race, and it gave you what it considered would 
be the first three runners  (It used least squares with an alpha factor to 
analyse the trend for a given horse. :-)) Nearly everyone in the company 
used to get the predicted results for their favoured weekend meeting (Kiwis 
were then, and still are, pretty fanatical about horse racing. I advised 
people to back the program's picks each way so the risk was hedged and 
nothing too bad would happen, but one guy bought me dinner when he placed a 
large wad on the double at Avondale and both the computer's predictions came 
in. On the whole, it was probably no better than a pin through the race 
book, but people liked it and it was fun. One of my colleagues decided to 
program it to come up with a betting strategy that could be used in 
conjunction with my picking program. I never used it, but I remember him 
going ashen when he had a week's salary riding on the nose of a horse he had 
never heard of, with a mediocre jockey, over hurdles, in the rain... I don't 
know whether he won or lost (never had the heart to enquire) but not long 
after that he left the Company and married a rich American... :-)

Pete.
