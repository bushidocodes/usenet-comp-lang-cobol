This one is worth answering (I think).

If you check comp.lang.cobol, the "COBOL" programming language is my love and
the way I used to make my living and still enjoy myself.  Hopefully, those
(with at least a minimal bit of knowledge) in this (comp.software.year-2000)
NG know at the rumors of the WHOLE *Y2K* problem being the fault of that
"old - now dead" language COBOL were (and still are) false!!!

On the other hand, there are lots and LOTS of "mission critical" business
applications written in COBOL (many of these still-in-use programs were
originally written before 1995, many before 1990 - and a surprising number
before 1980).

Now, since about the mid-1980's (when the last official ANSI/ISO COBOL
Standard was approved) the fact that COBOL is a HIGHLY portable programming
language has been exploited by many companies.  UNFORTUNATELY, the native
COBOL language - as defined by ANSI/ISO did NOT include any way of getting a
4-digit year (for the current date) until it was ammended in 1989.
Furthermore, many compiler vendors did not introduce compilers that supported
this "4-digit year" enhancement (via Intrinsic Functions) until well into the
'90s (and even then their "new" compilers had other "inhibitors" for existing
programs and programmers).  A final enhancement was made to the draft of the
next COBOL Standard in the last few years which made it even easier to get a
4-digit year.  (ACCEPT from DATE/DAY enhancements).  When these were added to
the draft Standard there was every expectation that this draft would have
been made "official" long before Dec 31, 1999.  UNFORTUNATELY (for reasons
too many and too painful to go into here), it still isn't official.
Therefore, COBOL (the "new and improved variety" - for those of you who still
hear it is dead or dying) which includes this enhancement won't become
official until well into the 2000's.

The bottom-line is that programmers who wanted "portable" code (as defined by
the ANSI/ISO Standards) - but to stay in the COBOL programming language have
had REAL problems getting the 4-digit year for the current date.  This was a
FAULT in the language, not in the programmers.

It is remarkable to me how many programs written in COBOL have been fixed AND
FIXED CORRECTLY  by "sliding windows"; assuming that "today's date if it
began with a "9" must be in the 1900s"; and various other "programming logic"
solutions.

Again, don't blame those programmers who used a "flawed" tool (for whatever
business reason they continued to use that language - and there were and are
many such reasons). Just thank them for "getting around it".

Most of the Y2K projects I have been aware of have actually spent
most of their time looking for problems and testing whether there
were any problems. The actual remediation was usually only a
minor part of the cost.

Given that you probably have to test anyway, even if the code is
supposedly Y2K compliant, the savings may have been quite minor. 

And in addition, most of the old code *has* been retired, though
not all of it. In some cases, too, you just may not have been
able to fit the program in memory if you used 4 digit dates. You
look at the old source files and they often have number of bytes
for each routine in the comments, which shows what an issue it
was. Try running a bank on a 64k machine.

Bill is right though; providing support only for 2 digit years in
COBOL 85 was not COBOL's finest hour!

Actually, an analysis of this was done by a columnist in Computerworld (I
think) several years ago.  His numbers seemed to be top-of-head stats, and so
are somewhat suspect, but he came up with a a net savings of using 2-digit
years and remediating and testing the code (I agree that testing was much more
expensive, particularly building adequate test environments for non-trivial
systems!) of 1.5 times.  That is, the savings in storage cost was 1 and half
times the Y2K project costs.  That may be wishful thinking, and within the
limits of error of the numbers, but still, seems like a good deal, particularly
when we, the currently active programmers, had no choice in the matter!
