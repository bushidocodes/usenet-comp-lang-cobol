Folks:

In a DB2 table ZZG.TZGAZZ01 with about 3 million rows I have, among others, 4
columns A, B, C, and D. They can assume about 500 to 1000 distinct values each.
I need to create a simple report listing all unique combinations of A, B, C, D
in the table. My first impulse was to have a cursor with a query like, say,
select distinct A||B||C||D ordered by this entire concatenation, count
occurences for every combo in a control break, and unstring the cluster before
printing. Any thoughts about doing it more efficiently? In COBOL, that is.
(Someone in the meeting mentioned like 'you can do it in 80 or so lines of SAS'.
As usually with SAS, this was probably quite a bit exaggerated, plus we do have
SAS here but in our group nobody programs it.) 

Any advice?

Thanx
Jacob Elgart

Jacob,

Your COBOL plan looks fine to me, even though COBOL gurus may have sharper eyes.

As far as the SAS brevity goes, your esteemed colleague did exaggerate. Here's a
_complete_ SAS program accomplishing this task, together with JCL (less the job
card):

// EXEC SAS OPTIONS='MEMSIZE=100M ERRORABEND'
//SYSIN DD *,DLM='$$'  
PROC SQL;
   CONNECT TO DB2 (SSID=DB2P);
   SELECT * FROM CONNECTION TO DB2 
   (CREATE VIEW ABCD AS SELECT A,B,C,D FROM ZZG.TZGAZZ01);      
quit;

PROC FREQ data=abcd;
   TABLES A*B*C*D / LIST;
run;
$$
 
Replace DB2P with whatever your subsystem ID is. Statements in lowercase are
optional. In addition to the frequencies, cumulative frequencies, percents and
cumulative percents will be printed also. If you do not want them, add the words
NOPERCENT NOCUM after the slash. If you have nulls on the DB2 table and want to
list them as valid values, add MISSING after the slash. Default output DD is
SASLIST; the report goes there. If you do not want the output to go to SASLIST
but rather to an output file whose DD is OUT, add it to the JCL and then before
the PROC FREQ, add the line

PROC PRINTTO PRINT=OUT;

Watch the semicolons!!! Your colleague must have meant '8 or so lines of SAS'.
Either way, one who is equally good at COBOL and SAS, is guaranteed to have the
SAS report on his boss's desk long before he would start debugging his first
compile should he decide to code it in COBOL (or anything else, for that
matter).
