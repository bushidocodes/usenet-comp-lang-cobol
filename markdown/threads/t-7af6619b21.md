I think this is one more scare tactic. Whatever the alarmists said would
happen hasn't happened.

The Y2K alarmists have been proven so wrong that they are fast losing any
credibility that they ever had. Just to give a few examples:
1. They said the stock market is going to crash in 1996- It never did.
In fact the market had a bumper year. Some alarmists said it is going to
crash by mid 1997. It hasn't happened yet.

2. They said there will be a shortage of programmers- It hasn't happened
yet. I am reading reports from more and more programmers from different
parts of the world in this very same newsgroup that they are not seeing the
kind of demand that is being depicted by the alarmists. The salaries have
gone up, but not significantly. There are more ads for Visual Basic
programmers in the newspaper than for COBOL. I recently forwarded resumes
of 2 dozen COBOL programmers to several New York based companies. I
haven't heard from any of them. This is hardly a "shortage" situation.

3. They said there will be a panic in the market by 1997- The only people
who are panicking are the Y2K tool vendors. They have put so much money in
developing the tools and nobody is buying. Stocks of Y2K vendors have
crashed because there are no revenues. (Yes, some major vendors have
signed a few multi-million dollar contracts, but the total amount is
ridiculously low compared to the $600 billion figure oft quoted by the
alarmists.)

4. The alarmists said that the "smart investor" will dump stocks and buy
gold- The fact is that gold prices are at an all time low. The "smart
investor" is apparently unmoved by the gloom and doom stories. On the Wall
Street, Mike Tyson is a much bigger story than Y2K.


I am not denying that the Y2K problem is huge, I am just pointing out that
the alarmists have been wrong up until now. It also goes to show that
predicting the future is a lot harder than you think.
