You may be confusing citizen and resident here, Jerry. There is no 
requirement that the bearer be resident in the country issuing the document.

Your South African may have been issued a British Commonwealth passport, 
(which looked exactly like a British passport except that page 3 of it did 
not have the words: "Holder has the right of abode in the United Kingdom." 
on it.). These passports allowed Commonwealth citizens to travel under the 
protection of  Great Britain, but did not allow them to live and work there. 
Many people from the sub-continent , and previous African colonies, held 
these passports and fought to get them upgraded to full British nationality, 
so they could work in the U.K.  (The passport reflects citizenship. In this 
case, "British Commonwealth citizenship").

Since the British Passport has been replaced by the standard European one 
I'm not sure what the status of Commonwealth passports is now. The rules 
change. There was a time when Australians and NZers could travel freely and 
live and work in either Australia opr NZ, without any kind of travel 
document being required. Passport control has had to be introduced because 
many immigrants were using one of these countries as a "back door" to get 
into the other one. (If your immigration to Australia was declined due to 
quotas being full, for instance, you could apply for NZ and then simply move 
to Australia. If you couldn't make the requisite points threshold for 
immigration into NZ you could apply for Oz then move to NZ, and so on. 
Nowadays it is very much tighter and passports are required for trans-Tasman 
travel.

(I remember an Australian Immigration official having a good laugh at my 
expense when I arrived in Sydney on one occasion, only to find that my NZ 
passport had expired a few days before. I then produced a current European 
one and it was found to have no Australian visa in it. The NZ one did not 
require a visa for Australia but the UK one did. He was pretty good natured 
about it as there was no doubt I was a Kiwi, but he ribbed me about being an 
"honorary Pom" for the duration of my visit. They issued a visa on the spot, 
which was pretty decent of them really.)

Generally, passports must reflect the citizenship of the bearer, 
irrespective of where they reside. I have dual British and NZ citizenship so 
I can hold a full British (European) passport as well as a NZ one. It means 
I can reside in Europe or NZ and, for tax purposes I must state where my 
primary residence is. (It is now NZ,  but for some time it was the UK and, 
for a shorter time Germany, and Spain.)

Obviously any kind of effective border control needs proper documentation 
that should be difficult to forge. "Smart" passport technology is a step in 
the right direction.

The old trick of getting a passport issued in the name of some child that 
died at a young age (Stonehouse MP in England was a famous example), no 
longer works in NZ. But with a population of just over 4 million it is a lot 
easier for us to implement these kinds of cross references and controls, 
than it is to do so into a population of 350 million.

Pete.
