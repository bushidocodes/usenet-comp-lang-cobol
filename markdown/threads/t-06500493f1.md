"Paul Pigott" <ppigott61@hotmail.com> wrote in message news:<E7Z77.53375$C81.4597507@bgtnsc04-news.ops.worldnet.att.net>...

It comes down to determining how sensitive the data *REALLY* is.  When
it is determined how sensitive the data is then you determine how to
secure the data.  "General principle" is pretty vague.  What would
happen if the data does get into the "wrong hands"?  How determined
are the wrong hands to get at it?  It is one thing to secure the data
from employees who can not resist temptation or just stumble upon
it(quite simple) and another when one is bound and determined to get
at it (let us say a foreign govt conducting deadly serious espionage).
 Most security situations fall into the former category and are
usually dealt with readily acessible security utilities (RACF has been
mentioned) or system functionality.

Is it possible to get the data out of the VSAM dataset (yes I know
that perhaps 2000 programs read that VSAM and it would take a major
effort to get the data into DB2 or whatever) and perhaps into a
Relational DB?  It is possible to get
real fine control on who/what can read data out of a relational DB.

It is my guess that existing system functionality will probably take
care of you.  Does your company run its own hardware or is it farmed
out?

Anyway
KA

Let me give  Mr. Pigott a head start on this project.

Start now, writing the VSAM data access as a dynamically called module,
using a syntax something like,

  CALL VSAM-ACCESS-PROGRAM USING ACTION,

INPUT-BUFFER,

RETURN-BUFFER,

RETURN-STATUS


 At this time,VSAM-ACCESS-PROGRAM will use conventional OPEN, READ, WRITE,
REWRITE, CLOSE, etc

Perhaps at at some point in the future, VSAM-ACCESS-PROGRAM will use EXEC
SQL CONNECT| SELECT| INSERT| UPDATE|DISCONNECT  END-EXEC.

The thing is, once all programs have been converted to use this syntax - on
whatever schedule is convenient - it really makes no difference "how" or
"where" the data are accessed. He'll be able to change the
VSAM-ACCESS-PROGRAM to read a DB2 database, an Internet URL or even another
VSAM file.

First stop, RACF.

If it is "sensitive" it should be password protected.

Both Thane and the Doc have made points which are valid regarding who should
access it and how you might encrypt it. I am assuming you want access only
from those who are authorised, whether they are inside or outside the
organisation?

In this case physical password protection has to be the first stop, closely
followed by serious encryption...

I have dealt with similar problems in a major American Bank (based at the
time in South London) and the one inescapable conclusion I reached was this:

Dashwood's first law of system security:

"It is IMPOSSIBLE to totally protect a data asset from unauthorised access,
so long as there is LEGITIMATE access to it."

(The best you can do is make access to the stable as cumbersome as possible,
and close the door after the horse has bolted...At least know that the horse
has been taken, who had access to the stable at the time it disappeared, who
was responsible for locking the stable door, etc. ...and then look around
for someone riding a newly acquired horse...<G>)

Pete.

I would hope that your security people would have the case covered. It's
normal to protect datasets at the generic HLQ level. If they have not got
this or something similar in place for your production files then your file
should be the least of your worries. If security is so bad at your site that

eyes can't get to the CICS log file as that has all your updates on it (and
probably your screen input output images as well, I may stand corrected, I'm
IMS not CICS). Also make sure that when people carry your system over into
the test environment they make their own independent file, and that they
don't just take a production image copy. (That is far more common than one
would hope, no matter what the data protection people say).
