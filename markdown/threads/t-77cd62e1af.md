```
I was talking to a friend of mine who is on his way to becoming a local
'expert' in Year 2000, since he is now a project manager for a consulting
company. While I have dabbled in fixing the occasional bug and run a few
scans, I have not yet been drafted onto a Y2K project and my new company
sells software which they believe is Y2K compliant in the version with which
I will be working. This means that professionally I am an outsider to the
issue, although I have been tracking the problem since reading an article
about the problem in a Software Maintenance Association magazine in the
latge 1980's.

Anyway, we were discussing a recent poll of CIO about their view of the
compliancy of their companies. The results were not very good. So I asked
my friend on a scale of 1 to 10, how bad he thinks it's going to be.

I want to throw the question out to my fellow grunts. On a scale of 1 to
10, with 1 being almost no effect and a 10 being the end of civilization as
we know it, how bad do you think it will be for the US and Europe?
Remember, the third world countries are not as dependant on computers for
day-to-day operations. Just post a number unless you want to add some
comments.
```

```
I have been keeping in touch, since the problem affects me as a human being
living in one of the most technologically dependent countries in the world
(Japan might have the disadvantage of being number 1). As a member of AITP,
I attend some of the Y2K meetings of our local Y2K SIG. I have been reading
all of the articles in Computerworld from doomsday to head-in-sand, and I
have a personal opinion. I have just not gotten professionally involved.

In my opinion, it's in the 4.0-5.0 range. This includes some small and
mid-size bank failures and partial disruption of transportation, power, and
communications. In essence, the same kind of effects which in wartime would
be caused by a coordinated campaign of sabotage or airstrikes on command and
control and transportation facilities. In effect, we may be doing to
ourselves something which none of our enemies in the 20th century has ever
been able to pull off on a large scale.

I don't think it's at the grab-the-guns-and-gold-and-head-for-the-cabin
point, but it is definitely not the slight sneeze that many head-in-sand
"consultants" predict. I have also been hearing about hospital equipment
issues. Will there be a body count? Definitely. Even something like
malfunctioning traffic signals or a power blackout in winter could kill a
few hundred people nationwide. Even small things have an effect. The
question is will it be a few hundred or tens of thousands.

But hey, maybe it's just me.
```

```
Overall rating: 7.8

Business mainframe mission critical systems, especially batch
operations will be fixed on time. Secondary and support systems will
show flaws.

The real problem will be when different institutions depend on one
another for data. One company's mission critical system, could be
another company's secondary system. Or when there are dozens of
companys that are in the mix working within the same system. Case in
point: The credit card industry. Just considering MC and Visa cards,
on the banking side there are 3 major service bureaus, and banks that
do it themselves (Citibank). On the merchant side, there are many
times more servicers. And right in the middle is MC and Visa bringing
the two side together to post and process. About a year ago MC and
Visa reported problems processing cards expiring in "00". Not to be
out done, Discover reported the same problem 6 months ago.
Distribution in retail and manufacturing could suffer.

IS operations that run on shoestrings. The small shop that hasn't
upgraded it's compiler since 1979. Using 360 architechure, CICS
version 3.3 or less. And let's not forget the midrange System 36
which isn't Y2k compliant and IBM's solution is to upgrade to an
AS400.

This survey will fail to include another problem area. Those who have
moved to client/server and have rewritten the code, changed to a
different language, but left the DATA in it's prior format. Or the
fact that it's still possible to write new code in any application
language and be non-compliant to Y2k.
```
