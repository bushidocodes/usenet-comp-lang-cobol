It's my understanding that the *workshop* (not a seminar) that Don mentions
is being seriously considered as part of a much larger COBOL-related event.
Don Schricker's private message to the J4 committee (as a member and as
chair of that committee, not as an employee of Micro Focus) was published in
this forum without any indication of prior permission from the author (a
surprising act, in my opinion, as I have been told it is Extremely Rude to
post a private mail message, or a message received from a limited mailing
list, in a public forum without express permission and without any
indication that that express permission had been granted!).

Moreover, if the plans for this workshop do gel, it is intended as a forum
in which the COBOL user community has an opportunity to discuss their wishes
for new features in the next COBOL standard with members of J4 and WG4, face
to face.  I for one think that's a Really Good Idea.

COBOL Expo 2003 is tentatively scheduled for June 23-25, 2003 in Las Vegas
Nevada, and Don has been in communication with the organizers of that event
on the possiblity of the workshop in question.

Moreover, Don has offered, on behalf of Micro Focus, to host the next WG4
meeting in Las Vegas, Nevada, on June 26-28, 2003.  This date has been
chosen precisely to allow both J4 and WG4 members to attend this workshop --
if the COBOL Expo organizers agree to host it -- so that these members can
discuss the suggestions from the user community given at this workshop
directly into the WG4 meeting.  The decision as to whether Micro Focus'
offer to the United States to host this meeting at this location on these
dates is accepted is, I believe, in the hands of ANSI, and the decision as
to whether to accept that offer in the hands of ISO/IEC JTC1 SC22.

Note that there is much that is tentative about this.  It is not clear to me
that COBOL Expo 2003 has formally agreed to hold the workshop, it is not
clear that the process of asking permission to host the WG4 meeting has gone
through all the right channels, or that the offer has formally been made to
ISO/IEC JTC1 SC22, or that that offer has been accepted.

I also don't know where Don's estimate of fifty participants in the workshop
came from, but my best guess is that it comes in part from the experience of
COBOL Expo 2002 and in part from estimates from the organizers of COBOL Expo
2003.

I believe Don's planned participation in this workshop is as both chair and
member of J4, and as convener of WG4, not as an employee of Micro Focus.  I
expect to participate in this workshop by *listening* to the user community,
and I expect to do so as a member of the US delegation to WG4, not as an
employee of Unisys.  I strongly suspect the same is true for the other
members of these two committees, whether they are sponsored by corporations
that happen to produce COBOL compilers for sale or not.

Thus, it strikes me that boycotting this workshop whose purpose is to help
guide the future of Standard COBOL, on the grounds that Micro Focus' pricing
policy is displeasing, could be considered a bit short-sighted.

Don expressed his concern to J4 that the interest in this workshop in a
country that has a population of nearly 300,000,000 indicates a potential
for fifty or so participants, and interest in a workshop with a much
narrower focus in a country with a population of some 130,000,000 resulted
in over four hundred participants.  I tend to agree with Don, that this is a
surprising and frustrating estimate.

What can Don do to get the user community to participate more in determining
the future feature content of standard COBOL?   This workshop is an effort
in that direction; does boycotting it help the future of COBOL?

Most (not all, I'm sorry to say) participants in comp.lang.cobol participate
because they're interested in the future of Standard COBOL and wish to see
it improve.

I hope the readership takes this discussion as an opportunity to consider
feature content that will assist both in the functionality and the longevity
of Standard COBOL, rather than as an opportunity to bash the standards
process or the people who have done their best to make that process work.

    -Chuck Stevens
