I would like to address some of your questions and give you some
realistic expectations.

1) You can learn COBOL in two semesters, but you need to back it up
with classes that teach you about logic and how to construct it. Also
classes in data structers is helpful. Knowing a computer language
does not automatically make you a programmer. You must also have the
ability to think logically and then translate that tought into a
program using the language you know. I benefited in this regard by
taking a minor in math when I was in college.

2) A company will not send a junior or inexperienced programmer
overseas to work. Having been an ex-pat for 6 years, I can tell you
that it is very rewarding but also very expensive for the company.
Companies only send their best to work oversears. Your only chance
might be if you pay your own way overseas and get hired by a foreign
company. Of course you won't be able to get things like living
allowance, transportation allownace, hardship pay etc.

3) You may be able to get into a company with a degree in C.S. and
work on a Y2K project, but remember you will have no programming
experience or knowledge of the particular industry you are going to
work for, so don't expect a windfall salary. You might be able to get
25-30K a year if you are lucky.

Good Luck.

We have a self study course, "COBOL, Just in Time" that teaches the basics of
COBOL. It's being used by government agencies, corporations and educational
establishments to train entry level programmers.

The materials are very easy to study and learn so we even have people who are

The course teaches the basics of COBOL. We recommend students study "COBOL,
Dates and the Year 2000" after they have completed the CJIT course above.

As any programmer will tell you, programming is one of those professions where
your studies are never done. Once you have completed this course you will be
able to program to an entry - intermediate proficiency. Practice this. If you
have to, write programs for friends to gain experience. You will need to study
the materials for the particular working environment you will be going into,
and more advanced programming books. But with the basics well under your belt
this is not such a difficult proposition.

The course itself takes about 30 days, 6 hours a day. I have had a professor
tell me it is the equivalent, if not better than a college level COBOL course.

The course costs $350.00 + shipping. If you'd like to let me have your address

I have seen many opportunities for work overseas. However, I do not know what
kind of background is being required for this kind of work, and whether
overseas employers will want to go to the trouble of getting working papers for
entry level people. Maybe they would for experienced programmers. Another
factor in this is that some other countries are producing programmers who are
willing to work for a lot less than US programmers are accustomed to, so they
would be more inclined to perhaps "import" programmers from these countries.

As regards to salaries - yes and no. It depends upon a lot of things. Highly
skilled COBOL programmers are pulling $100 + an hour. Consulting companies are
charging clients up to say $250.00 an hour. But it depends on a lot of things.

Salaried programmers generally earn less than contractors. However, they also
usually get benefits. An entry level programmer really should be in a salaried
position as he really does not have the skills and experience which would
validly make him a contractor. He also needs to be in a supervised
environment, where what he is doing can be checked and he has the support and
guidance from experienced programmers that he needs to become really competant.

It also depends upong the area of the country you are in. There seems to be
lots of entry level work in San Francisco and very little in Los Angeles, - so
rates are higher in SF.
