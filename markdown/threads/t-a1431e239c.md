Hi, Bill,

My 2 cents' worth, having been involved in a couple of startups over
the years:

1.  Don't put all your money into a startup, no matter how convinced
you are that it's going to fly.  The principals are always far more
optimistic than outsiders.  Which, incidentally, is why your bank is
being somewhat recalcitrant about advancing funds.  Bear in mind that
most startups, fail within a couple of years.

What would you do if a stranger came to you with a similar idea and
wanted YOU to hand over some $$$ to get started?  Would you?

We were once involved in a joint venture where we provided hardware
and technical stuff and the other J/V partner provided the facility
for an educational venture.    He had a great looking business plan,
profit projections, etc., etc.  On paper it looked like a gold mine,
so we negotiated a contract and got to work with him.

The portion we were involved in was a huge success, but the partner's
business plan called for "hitting the ground running" so he cashed in
virtually everything he had to get the thing going.  He rented a huge
amount of space, put in lots of expensive furniture, spent tons on
advertising... and diverted the profits from the J/V into keeping the
non-J/V portion afloat.  He also talked a few other businesses into
extending rather large lines of credit for rent, furnishings, and so
on.  We came way too close to losing about $100K on that debacle.

Moral of the story?  Even the best-conceived startups are still
extremely risky, and there are always unanticipated problems.
Furthermore, your assumptions just may not reflect reality.  Don't
blow all your money on starting up; you might lose it all. 

2.  Do you have a real product, or just an idea?  I can tell you from
over fifteen years of proprietary software experience that it's one
thing to dream it up and quite another to actually put a product
together, complete with good documentation, tech support, etc.  

You also need to deal responsively with bugs you never dreamed of once
your product hits the street.  Beta testing never flushes them all
out, and you need to fix them - fast - once you start taking money for
your product.

Not to mention marketing, though that has become much easier via the
Internet, than it used to be.  It takes many talents to bring all of
the pieces together.  

3.  Is your idea unique?  Is it new?  Is it sufficiently complex that
you can be fairly safe from copycats?  Our first product (see below)
was all of the above.  The same market today has a number of players,
and our latest product, even though it's far more powerful, complex,
and sophisticated than the first (successful) one, is doing OK but not
making us millionaires - yet <G>.  

It's far, far more difficult to dominate a market where there are
superficially similar, competing, established products - regardless of
how good yours is.  

4.  Proprietary software can be a very good business.  It's great fun
once it takes off.  You write something once and, hopefully, sell it
many times over.  You get to control the product (within certain
limits).  It's both challenging and interesting.

Our first product, written in the mid-80's was a character mode screen
manager for COBOL on the PC.  It was the first one out there, and when
we showed it to Marc Sokol, president of Realia, he saw the potential
and encouraged us to take it to market.  (At the time, one of the big
obstacles to COBOL development on the PC was the lack of an
easy-to-use screen manager).   So, Marc realized our product could
help his COBOL sales.  

When the product was ready to hit the street, Realia began referring
people to us for the screen management, and sales took off like a
rocket - both ours and Realia's.  They ultimately marketed ScreenIO
under their label.

This was a great thing for us, because we had a symbiotic relationship
with a major player - which made our product profitable within a
matter of months.  We did not have any outside financing, all we had
in it was about 1,000 hours of our (spare?) time, and we didn't need
to spend a ton of money on advertising.  

If you can find a similar benefactor for your product, it could be one
of the better ways to get off the ground without taking a lot of
financial risk.

Oh, yeah, we worked our butts off that first year.  It was not at all
unusual for me to arrive at the office at 8AM and go home at 3, 4, or
5 the next morning - seven days a week.  I don't think I'd be willing
to make that kind of sacrifice today, but I was fifteen years younger
then.

I'm not trying to discourage you.  If you've got a great idea, go for
it by all means.

I would suggest, however, that if you can develop it in your spare
time and find a few customers who will work with you through the
development phase, you'll be risking far less - and if your product is
good, you'll have proof of it when you start going after a mass
market.  You'll have a track record that you can take to your bank or
to investors.  

If you can generate income as you go, you may not even need outside
funds - and, frankly, that's the best way to go, in my opinion...

Best of luck,
Kevin
