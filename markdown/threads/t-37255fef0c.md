```
The US Public Review period ends next Monday, April 23.  If you have any
comments about the "reaffirmation" of the US (not ISO) '85 Standard,
Intrinsic Function Module, or Correction Amendment,  NOW is the time to send
them to:

    Deborah Donovan, email: ddonovan@itic.org
        and
    ANSI, ATTN PSA Dept., email: psa@ansi.org

You do *NOT* need to be a US citizen to send comments on these.  You should
(in your comments) say that they relate to the reaffirmation of:

 Reaffirmation of a Standard.
    X3.23:1985 [R1991]: Programming Language COBOL

    X3.23a:1985/ADD 1:1989 [R1991]: Addendum to ANSI X3.23-1985, Programming
Language - Intrinsic Function Module for COBOL

    X3.23b:1985/ADD 2:1993 [R1998]: Title: Correction Amendment to ANSI
X3.23:1985, Programming Language COBOL and ANSI X3.23a-1989 Addendum 1

  ***

If you are interested in the currently submitted comments, you may look at
them at:
 http://www.ncits.org/archive/2001/it01200r/it01200r.htm

 ***

My *personal* (and this is NOT a very popular opinion) hope is that some
more commentors will indicate that:

A) J4 should spend its time and resources on "finishing up" the revision of
the Standard (and doing work on its planned "technical reports") and *not*
take up (again) the burden of "maintaining" these current COBOL standards -
that are certainly "functionally frozen" and that I think those vendors that
do support - will continue to support whether or not there is an ANSI
Standard.

B) The "perception" about COBOL is *not* impacted ignorantly by whether or
not these "old" Standards are "reaffirmed" - but by when and how the NEW
Standard is progressed (and made as clear and correct as possible)

C) That *if* the '85 Standard and the Intrinsic Function Amendment are
withdrawn (by ANSI - not ISO) that it makes NO sense for ANSI to "reaffirm"
the "Correction Amendment" which will end up as "changes" to a
non-recognized document.

  ***

Having said what I would like to see come in as comments, I would still like
to see as many people comment by the end of next Monday EVEN if they all say
that I am "stupid" and a "trouble maker" and that of course ANSI should
reaffirm the existing Standards.
```

```
Speaking as someone who is called upon to maintain compilers for a compiler
vendor, I can state from personal experience that customers feel much freer
to demand their "pet projects" from compiler vendors when there is no
standard in force to which that vendor can appeal, and the implementors have
much less leverage in striving to adhere to those standards if they are not
in force.

That has nothing to do with what the terms "in force" (or for that matter
"reaffirmed" or "withdrawn") actually mean in J4/NCITS/ANSI
process-description documents.  It does have a great deal to do with whether
ANSI (or for that matter ISO) standards are *perceived* by the customer as
having direct, *current* relevance to the product they're using.   It doesn'
t really matter to me as an implementor whether they are "functionally
frozen", whether maintenance amendments are being produced, or whether
interpretations are promulgated; what matters is not just that the standard
exists but  more importantly that it continues to be designated as having
official standing in this country.    The fact that it has that standing
today is important to me.

Which leads directly to ...

<<B) The "perception" about COBOL is *not* impacted ignorantly by whether or
not these "old" Standards are "reaffirmed" - but by when and how the NEW
Standard is progressed (and made as clear and correct as possible) >>

A number of the public comments in support of reaffirmation received thus
far have explicitly stated that *failing to reaffirm* those standards would
have a direct and deleterious economic impact on them, on their companies,
and on their customers.   Some claim a *possible*, some a *probable*, and
some a *certain* impact, all deleterious.  These are not comments about the
Final Committee Draft, they are specifically comments *supporting
reaffirmation*.    These submitters assert that the public perception that
COBOL is diminished if ANSI fails to reaffirm the standard, and they support
reaffirmation on economic grounds.

Whether the public perception of COBOL ought reasonably to be diminished by
a failure to reaffirm the standard or not isn't particularly important;
these submitters claim they will suffer if the current standard is not
reaffirmed.  What seems to  matter to them, and to their financial
well-being, is how their customers see it, and they seem to believe their
customers want the standard to continue in force.   The submitters'
ignorance isn't relevant, and neither is the ignorance of the customer that
might not be intimately familiar with the standards process.  These folks
claim that if the standards aren't reaffirmed and are withdrawn, they and
their companies will suffer.  Whether the perception is born out of
ignorance anywhere up or down the financial food chain isn't important to
that financial bottom line.

<< C) That *if* the '85 Standard and the Intrinsic Function Amendment are
withdrawn (by ANSI - not ISO) that it makes NO sense for ANSI to "reaffirm"
the "Correction Amendment" which will end up as "changes" to a
non-recognized document.>>

I'll leave this for others to argue, but I am by no means convinced that "NO
sense" is the whole and complete truth.

    -Chuck Stevens
```

```
BOTTOM LINE:

Unless the rules are changed, the Standard will NOT be re-affirmed. (It
cannot legally be...)

It doesn't matter whether everybody thinks it should be or not, it WON'T be.

(Personally, I'm not losing any sleep over this because, the way I see it,
either way will be good for COBOL.)

The ANSI organization and the committees delegated by it to handle the COBOL
standard are the ONLY ones culpable. They have shot themselves (and COBOL)
in the foot.

It is time the procedures and processes implemented by ANSI were overhauled
and brought into the 21st century. The Management Style should be reviewed
and a much more open and responsive approach should be encouraged.

I have some very strong ideas about how this could be done but I won't go
into them here.

It is my sincere hope that at least some lessons can be learned from this
and it will never happen again. Until that is guaranteed, COBOL is best
served by complete dissociation from ANSI.
```
