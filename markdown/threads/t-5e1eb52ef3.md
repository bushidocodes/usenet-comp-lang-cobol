Not exactly right, but close enough for demonstrating extensive
knowledge of U.S. energy needs. For shaming with guilt-by-association,
it misses the mark.

Haliburton (Cheney's former employer) is a service company. They
provide products and services TO oil companies, mostly at the well
site. Much like a company that sells drill pipe, or paint, or radio
equipment/operators, or helicopter commuting.

Haliburton does not explore, drill, transport, refine, or market
petroleum products. From a purely business perspective, Haliburton is
indifferent to the U.S. oil industry (if oil is not produced here, it
will be produced elsewhere - and the need for Haliburton's services
moves with the well locations).

I used to work for Petroleum Information (a division,
believe-it-or-not, of A.C. Nielson). This company tabulated and
compiled the daily output of every oil and gas well in the world (as
best they could). The 'product' was a book, published daily, and
forwarded to some 3,000 subscribers (virtually all were oil
companies). (This was a big data-processing job in that data were
coming in from hundreds of sources - like state regulatory agencies -
in multiple formats and these data had to be massaged against a
massive database, a 2-300 page printout created, and sent to printing
so the book could make the evening FedEx planes).

Despite the name and the client list, Petroleum Information was no
more an oil company than Haliburton. But employees DID learn a lot
about oil, gas, and energy, just by osmosis.

P.S.
Another division of A.C. Nielson counts grocery coupons. The coupons
were shipped from a collection point (somewhere in Iowa, I think) to
Juarez. There, in a room the size of an airplane hanger, Mexican women
with eight-pound tongues sorted these little scraps of paper into
General Foods, Hunt, Carnation, etc. Boggles the mind. Nowadays, the
coupons have barcodes. The Mexican women have sensors mounted on their
tongue-tips.

A.C. Nielson is a division of Reed-Elsiver. Reed is a publishing
company that produces Books In Print, The Physician's Desk Reference,
Marquis' Who's Who, Martindale-Hubble Law Directory, etc. The common
thread, it seems to me, is that every one of this company's endeavors
has a massive cost of entry to a would-be competitor.
