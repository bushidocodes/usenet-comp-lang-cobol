The old 80-column punch cards for each column had 12 places a hole could be
punched.  The 12 punch was the top row, 11 punch was second, while the zero -
9 punches fill out the rest of the column.  After a number was punched in a
card, for example 1234, that field could be negated by punching an 11 punch
over the 4.  The 11-4 punch card code gives the letter M or x'D4'.  When the
number is packed for numeric processing, it turns into x'01234D' (on IBM
machines anyway).  A 12-zone overpunch would give the letter D or x'C4' while
no overpunch would give the digit 4 or x'F4'.  This makes sense in EBCDIC but
less so in ASCII.
