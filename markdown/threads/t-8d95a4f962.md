Danger.

Newbie to group about to open mouth on a non-technical flammable
subject.

About 8 years ago (I had 4 years of Unix/C/low level geek experience
then, some low level database), I went to a Pick User's group meeting
after spending a day in Pick class. My job at the moment was to learn
our company's Pick based system, and make sure the vendor was not
screwing us too badly.

I watched some type of industry awards dinner go on, and almost got
nauseous about the self-congratulatory tone. Everyone was telling
everybody else what great job they were doing, about the strength of
the Pick industry, about the manna from heaven that was being bestowed
upon them. I must have overdosed on testosterone. I actually got up
during a question and answer period, and told them that their entire
industry was going to go down in flames, that there would be nothing
left of the Pick OS, and that their database would be nothing more
than just another application fighting for it's life under the Unix
OS.

Whew. I made a LOT of enemies that night, but also a lot of friends.
I had a crowd of people follow me out the door, asking for suggestions
of what to learn and what to do.

See many ads for Pick expertise in the want-ads? See ANY new Pick
installs?

8 years later, a bit of Oracle/C/PERL/UNIX experience under my belt.

Now you can stop steaming, I have no intention of saying anything like
that about COBOL. It is JUST a language, suited for some tasks, not
for others. It certainly will have a place for many years to come. As
long as the user exits are not written in assembler, it's very
portable, you can run the same code almost anywhere.

That portability, on the other hand, will be the DEATH to the
mainframe. I'm not saying that the mainframe is bad, isn't fast,
can't do batch or back-end database faster/better than most competing
systems. What I am saying, it's TOO damn expensive for the
functionality that it provides. Very few organizations can justify
the expense, when compared to the competing solutions.

Remember the thread about getting the resume's past human resources,
who are tasked with finding the least expensive/entry level capable
solution? High level managers have the same responsibility when it
comes to their hardware!

And please, don't come back with the failed C/S garbage. I agree with
you. What I am taking about is host-based mainframe style terminal
based systems. Very fast I/O (200 MBYTES per second to start), fast
CPUS (faster then the high-end IBM CMOS), very expandable (start with
4 CPUs, go to 64, start with 4GB of memory, go to 64GB), supporting
thousands of users.

Can they compete with a full-blown Sysplex? No. They don't have to.
They have to incrementally satisfy one task at a time. It'll be
stealthy, with no major turnover of personnel except a few
JCL/assembler gurus who refused to see it coming. All the
applications programmers who write COBOL on the MF will write COBOL
there. All the operations staff will still be swapping tapes, just
bigger/faster ones. The disk packs will still be there, as well as
somebody to manage them. The 3rd party apps will be there, they've
been ported to Unix years ago, and are just waiting for you to catch
up to them. The people who have years invested in the 3rd party apps
might feel a little pain as they move to the new environment, but most
will have no problem.

Why would management do this if most people stay the same? Hardware
and licensing costs. Low cost entry to try new things, easy
expandability when things work. As time goes on, they find out that
the new people coming out of school prefer this environment, so the
applicant pool is wider and cheaper. There are more programmers
programming for this environment, so there is more competition from
3rd party apps, too. They are cheaper and have more features. The
hardware itself will get faster and cheaper, allowing more things to
run at the same time at far less cost. Virtuous cycle.

Yes, IBM, Fujitsu, Amdahl and other are getting fast/cheaper. But not
ENOUGH. And the environment is alien to the people making $$
decisions. It'll still be at least 10 years before you see any
massive "Turn off the switch" efforts. There are serious issues that
the Unix systems don't address, but with the dwindling installed
based, the cost to do Research and Development for additional
mainframe capabilities will be spread across fewer customers, forcing
the mainframe price UPWARD, not downward. I'm sure someone will point
out that the mainframe is generating serious $$, but how much in
relation the entire high end market? And for how long? Each time
there is a mainframe sold to a non-mainframe shop, it makes big news
and is a cause for rejoicing it seems. That alone should tell you it
is on it's way out, and that these purchases are aberrations, not the
norm. Vicious cycle.

This is not a Microsoft Blue-Screen of Death against the MF issue. MS
trash has no business in the enterprise, and should not enter into
this discussion. IT IS a Unix VS the MF issue, but does not have to
be an US VS THEM issue. Unless you work for a hardware vendor, you
are quite capable of gaining from this. The only real losers are the
people who refuse to learn new things EVER (and I really don't care
about them anyway), the hardware vendors, and the FEW customers who
truly need the high-end mainframe Sysplex clustering capabilities.
Hopefully they will be rolled into high-end Unix servers, but nothing
is guaranteed.


Please, any comments would be welcome. Show me where my logic is
twisted or colored by inexperience.

Thanks,

Barry Roomberg

PS: Please don't flame me in the future as I ask questions on how to
make my MF COBOL programmer's lives easier in this world. I'm trying
to help them along, not hurt them.

As I mentioned on another thread above, there are still some serious
problems with UNIX variants, like limited file sizes. I don't know what
kind of applications you are doing, but we can't live with two gigabyte
fragments of our files.

The cost of mainframes has come down much further than you seem to think,
while the cost of UNIX servers is going up. We paid $160,000 for our most
recent purchase, $110,000 for the one before that. You can buy a very nice
AS/400 for that kind of change, and even a low end System 390.

You mention that it is news every time a non-mainframe shop buys a
mainframe. I don't recall seeing that much, but there are lots of articles
about how XYZ Company has ported this application or that to a LAN C/S
environment. The real issue, of course, is how often existing mainframe
shops upgrade their existing installations, vs. change over. I think this
kind of situation is called a Markov chain (or maybe it's a stochastic
process...) In any case, it is very easily demonstrated that very small
companies always buy very small hardware, and try to stick to lower priced,
off the shelf software for their various applications. As you point out,
they can't afford much else. However, it is just as easily demonstrated
that those small companies which survive and grow large tend to migrate
their core applications to larger systems. It is not that they can now
afford bigger hardware, but rather that they have, by that time, realized
that they cannot afford to continue fighting the problems of small systems
for what are now their critical applications.

As to the lack of portability of mainframe applications, you apparently
haven't heard about LE. Just as Windows NT has a hardware compatibility
layer, so as to run on Alpha and Mips and Intel, and just as every Unix
system is delivered with a host of shared objects, so as to be able to
compile and link the system to the hardware, IBM is separating the
application layers from the hardware layer. Write your application to run
under LE, and it becomes portable across anything which supports LE. IBM
will make sure there is a version of LE for every one of it's mainframe
platforms, just as Sparc and Mips and Alpha and Intel do for their variants
of Unix.

Actually one of the things which still seems to limit Unix in some managers
minds is the very fact that it is so open. For better or for worse, IBM
has a lock on MVS, and everything associated with it. Because of that
every third party vendor has to match the same standard. Every purchasing
manager knows that the third party stuff they buy has a reasonably good
chance of working on MVS, and that they have a lock on winning the civil
suit against the third party vendor if it doesn't. HP-UX, SCO, Solaris,
Unixware, Linux, BSD, System V...the very fact the list is so long scares
most managers. They will certainly want to bring in some small scale Unix,
so they know what it is. But they won't put the company payroll system on
it until they KNOW that it will print checks on time, every time (lest they
be lynched on payday...)

Then there is the security model. There are two basic levels of security
in Unix, normal and God (named root, of course). Groups and execution
privileges don't give the levels of control mainframe shops take for
granted. Can Unix be taught to do this? I would guess there are already
products which improve on this. Do they come with the base system? Most
managers of MF shops sweat profusely when they discover their new Unix box
didn't come with something like RACF built in.

You mention that the newly schooled applicant pool is larger. I agree that
the personnel department is going to try to find the cheapest person who
meets the requirements (I think I already wrote that in another thread).
However, the most common 'requirement' sent to personnel is three years
minimum experience, give or take two years. Most development and
maintenance managers do not want fresh-out-of-school kids because they
don't have the time or budget to train them. Sure, the big consulting
houses are still sucking up new graduates as fast as they can, but they
always did that. And, since when do new hires tell their employers what
platform they would like to work on? Most newly graduated students spend a
few days trying to find the 'dream' job, doing what they want. Then when
the rent comes due they take the job they can get. On that job they will
get experience in whatever their new employer assigns. Surprise! The plum
assignments on the new Unix system go to the veterans. The new grunts get
to do maintenance on the thing they first wrote in Autocoder, and have been
patching ever since. Unfortunately, maintaining that garbage is how so
many people learn absolutely horrendous programming techniques.

Will those eventually get recoded? Yes. Will they get moved, at that
time, to the new machine which was purchased only for that application
(something has to be first...)? At best a minority of the time.

Lastly, there is the issue of 'assembler/JCL gurus'. There are
comparatively few assembler gurus in most application shops these days (and
