No, because OPEN, READ, WRITE et al are not 'routines' or 'procedures'  at
all... they are compiler directives. You are telling the compiler to include
ITS routines for file access.

Depneding on the compiler and environment, you may or may not be able to
hook onto those procedures. (The procedures are not part of any 'standard'
for COBOL. Only OPEN, CLOSE, READ and WRITE are COBOL-standard)

You want to use alternate I-O mechanisms, you have to CALL those routines
yourself.

You can write a precompiler to do anything.  Some folks say parsing COBOL is
involved.

But if you just want to take over OPENs ..... WRITES. You could just preprocess
for thatl. That is lexical scanner and parser question. And really we would ask
you. Are you able to write a pre-compiler? 

Not a bad plan really, .... as long as the set of I/O behaviors you have in
mind in those existing programs is in fact simple! The issue is not the
difficulty of scooping up the lexical particles. The issue is the semantics of
those COBOL verbs. 

If you really think you have a simple set of requirements in your program
collection then go for it, write a scanner.  If you have a small set of
programs it may be quicker to just change the source code to CALLS manually. 
If you have a large set of programs, that implies a complex system (not an
assumption, just a suggestion, even big systems can be stupid and simple, ...
but not ususually).  

If you have a complex system, and the notion is to try to save you time later,
somewhere in there you will discover that the COBOL verbs are non trivial. From
afar, one can only guess. But if you have a limitted set of modules, then
accept it, maybe you ought to just get to the source code.

Do you have any relative files and dynamic files, or is it all sequential? Is
everything fixed length? Do you really have only reads and writes? Or do you
have any rewrites or deletes? 

You see out here from afar one wonders why you are reluctant to get into the
source code.
If this system is big, I bet strapping your routines on is not going to be
simple, because in the learning curve for COBOL is some detail about those
verbs.

If there is any chance that you can isolate the complicated programs then
consider divide and counquer. If it warrants your time do a scanner to master
the bulk, then do so; and then just go ahead and get your hands dirty on the
subset that is intricate and code the CALLS if you think you can map the
functionality.

Do have a look at the code to make certain that you are seeing what the COBOL
does. This is not meant as opposition, just as support. You said you are new to
COBOL. How many of the source modules have you looked at? Do you have a sense
from what you are seeing that you can achieve a level of confidence as to what
those I/O statements expect? If so, and you eventually do convince yourself
that the full set of
requirements are simple, then do a scanner.

You will need to learn where EOF and file status is stored to fully interface
to that other stuff in COBOL that is not always in the statements headed OPEN
.... READ ... 

If you have modern COBOL on your hands you may wish to state here if in
addition to variable length files, do you have any occurs depending on clauses?
There are FROM and INTO phrases in I/O verbs that will get you off into this 
regrettably esoteric realm. So if you do have ODOs and FROM/INTO, you might
continue to seek COBOL expertise as you port 
this valuable application to your own I/O routines.

Best Wishes,
Stay ambitious, its fun!

The original post is quoted verbatim below.

Rather than thinking REDEFINES, which as COBOL parlance does not really mean
what you are thinking, you might instead begin to think relink. Capturing the
interface
at the point in actual executable code where the 'native' I/O routines are
engaged.

You then would have to understand all of the behavior of standard COBOL I/O
verbs. 

Observers here would be all for your ambitions. But if you are new to COBOL it
is perhaps fair to say that COBOL I/O verbs are rather detailed and have
behavior evolved by committee processes that have occurred over decades of
political process reflecting competitive corporate interests.. 

C has much more basic I/O that has behavior conceived by technical process
reflecting cooperating human invention (originally in a rather short period of
time).

One wants to be positive, but you may find 'basic' I/O in COBOL to be a much
larger time sink than anticipated if you are new to this. But we are all for
you. If you have a set of COBOL programs that actually do pretty simple stuff
you can just interface into them the small subset of I/O functionality they
need. But look before you leap.

As important as SQL is in the market place, COBOL product venfors must escape
it into COBOL in non COBOL syntax. As  important as CICS and EXEC DLI are to
the economic success of IBM they crowbar these vast I/O facilities in with
comparable escapes from the normal COBOL syntax. Perhaps take that as a guide
(why wouldn't they just 'REDEFINE' or relink, taking command of I/O interface).

Instead, consider doing what you say you don't want to. Change those OPEN/CLOSE
and READ/WRITE statement in the source to CALLS to your interface, ... but have
a good look at those programs and how they must actually behave before you let
yourself believe that ordinary simple I/O is going to be enough. 

I am really just amplifying feedback on the one aspect of your excellent and
frank post. If you are new to COBOL, lets talk about those I/O verbs a bit
before you conclude your analysis.

Most vendors will provide some information about naming conventions on I/O
subroutines. And most compilers will give you some kind of assembler map and
you can get a basic list of things just from the linkage process.

Please post further detailed questions with code snippets if you feel the
interaction can help you get up to speed. You can get excellent detailed
responses here, and many generous folks here will go off-line with you also in
detailed exchanges.

Very Best Wishes, and encouragement
to post further details !

Robert K. Rayhawk
RKRayhawk@aol.com
