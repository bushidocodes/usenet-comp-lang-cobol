```
As I recall the "usual" problem - it has to do with how far in advance you
need to "read" and/or what you need to "know".

Consider

  Perform ABC

This could be the start of something like

  Perform ABC
  Display "YES"

where "ABC" is the name of a procedure to be "performed"

       OR

it could be the start of something like

  Perform ABC times
     Display "YES"
  End-Perform

where "ABC" is the name of a variable telling how many times to perform the
"inline" routine.

    ***

Also consider the case of:

   Perform
      Continu
    Display "ABC"

The question is

A) Is "continu" a mis-spelling of CONTINUE - and this was intended to be an
inline PERFORM?
    or
B) Is "continu" the ACTUAL name of a paragraph/section that appears LATER in
the procedure division.

    ***
P.S.  As far as I know, there are NO cases where a "valid" (ANSI/ISO
conforming) source program is actually ambiguous for the PERFORM statement.
On the other hand, what you may need to know may not be something that a
"simple" lex/yacc will provide easily.
```

```
Duy,

the problem you are encountering is probably related to following:

    "PERFORM" paragraph_or_section_reference
    "PERFORM" data_reference "TIMES"

When yacc encounteres "PERFORM A IN B" it doesn't know
which rule it should apply: "paragraph_or_section_reference" or
"data_reference".

The problem is that yacc is not very well suited for building a
Cobol parser. I can assure you that the above problem is not
the only problem you will encounter.

Also, why are you building your own cobol parser? There are
several open source alternatives, and numerous commercial ones.
```

```
We use a full-context free parsing engine to handle COBOL
for our DMS Software Reengineering Toolkit.
(See http://www.semdesigns.com/Products/DMS/DMSToolkit.html).
This gives us an easy way to handle the apparant

and nested IF statements.

I think your problems with PERFORM are probably caused
by YACC's limitation of one token lookahead.
(Because of our context-free parsing engine,
we don't even notice this kind of problem).
To make a COBOL parser with YACC, I think
you'll have to add some weird hack in the
lexer to provide with the lookahead information
that YACC will not.
```
