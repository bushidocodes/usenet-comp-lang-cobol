Nathan,

Honestly I don't think it is a fair question. Firstly are you asking
from an OO COBOL background or experience with other OO languages ?

Much of what can be done with OO COBOL can be paralleled with Structured
COBOL - the 'old hands' have accumulated tons of routines as they moved
down the structured path. OO can't be explained in a couple of
sentences, (only by 'touch and see'  - i.e. coding in OO COBOL). It
starts right at square one - your design approach.

Given a problem in 'Structured' you can quickly put the bits together,
file formats, screen layouts and the business logic.

Before you even attempt it in OO, up front you have to PLAN you design,
with a very deep APPRECIATION of the concepts involved,  recognizing
where you need additional support classes containing reusable code, to
be used over and over again, (same principle applies with Structured)  -
but building in flexibility so that it fits more than one case (object
type), i.e. polymorphism. Accepting that as yet there aren't reams of
support classes written in COBOL, (other than what the compiler vendors
give you, to my knowledge there aren't any !), then that is a task you
have to fulfill. That means at the front end you appear to be doing
'non-productive' design. Subject to getting that up front support coded
and working, the payola is down the road. Might be a year before you see
the benefit - but you'll lick your lips with satisfaction when you
realize how easy it is to draw off from that initial support design.

The real problem I see, (specifically N/E users)  is that many OO
programmers are not designing the up front stuff. Copy and paste from an
existing Micro Focus example - particularly in the area of GUIs. Hey it
works ! What the hell ! But at the same time, they say to themselves,
"It takes up a lot of time, just pasting and re-testing". Some realize
the deficiency, but in a busy production environment they just have to
code, code, code and get it out quick. The benefits of OO only come into
play when somebody can take a deep breath, pause and give time to the
design of those support classes I referred to above.

Jimmy, Calgary AB

It isn't.

But it is never easy to overcome the inertia of learning something new, when
you already have a big investment in learning something that works for you
and that you feel you understand. This is why, in general, COBOL programmers
don't take easily to Object Orientation. It requires effort. And it requires
setting aside the current understanding you have of how computer programming
"must" work.

Newbies to programming, on the other hand, are not encumbered with
pre-conceived ideas about anything and they take to it like ducks to water.

If  it is any consolation I can tell you my own story.

After using COBOL for 30 years (with occasional sallies into  "new"
languages like C and various Assemblers for mainframes and PCs) I decided it
might be good to have a look at OO COBOL, particlarly as I was seeing more
and more use of Objects in the Client/Server arena, and my current compiler
vendor (Fujitsu) had included it into their 4.2 release and based Power
COBOL (which I was using extensively) on it. (Fujitsu did this while the J4
committe were still thinking about including it in the proposed COBOL 2000
standard (which is now COBOL 2002) and we haven't seen yet...). For
PowerCOBOL users it was not a requirement to understand OO, but being of an
inquisitive nature, I wanted to get to grips with it.

Anyway, I got into the documentation and started looking at the OO
extensions to COBOL.

I am not slow when it comes to things computer, but this stuff just left me
feeling like a SCUBA diver who gets 50 feet down and realises he forgot the
aqualung. I was way out of my depth. There were loads of new constructs, the
purpose of them seemed to me to not be readily apparent, and it was like
wading through molasses to try and get anything started. After about three
weeks, I was ready to pack it in.Tried again several times and always gave
up.

I decided to teach myself Java instead. I bought some basic books on Java
and got cracking. Right from day one they started explaining the concepts of
OO. Within two weeks I was into it. Java is a language DESIGNED for OO.
COBOL has had OO "bolted on" (and a very good job of bolting has been done
too, certainly as far as the Fujitsu product is concerned.). With Java I
could use Objects created by other programmers even using other languages.
Fantastic!

Armed with my new found understanding, I went back to OO COBOL. Suddenly it
was all clear. No problem. I started using COM  and ActiveX Objects with
COBOL... started writing my own Objects in COBOL and giving them to VB
people to see if they could use them (they could...). I even had one
application where C++, VB, and COBOL objects ALL co-existed on the same User
form, completely seamlessly and transparent to the user! I got so excited I
wrote an article about
it...(http://www.aboutlegacycoding.com//archives/v3/v30201.asp )

As soon as I acquired this understanding, and started talking to other
developers (swapping components and trying stuff) I realised that EVERYBODY
outside of the COBOL community already took it as a given... VB, Java, C++
all the people who were actually using these languages to make a living (as
opposed to doing a bit of game writing at home) were into objects. The whole
"new technology" is planted firmly on OO.

In the job I do currently, I see people doing things in minutes that would
take days in COBOL. They can do this because they are empowered by OO
technology. Very little actual code gets written. (when it does it is in
VB...)

All presentation is Web Based (XML etc), all data access is via ORACLE or
SQL Server, and the actual "processing" (which really comes down to
"connecting the objects, passing them data, and invoking their methds") is
generated with tools like DTS. Where Business units wish to do their own
processing on corporate data, "Standard" tools like Excel or Access are used
to pass data to Business areas for final slicing and dicing by users to suit
themselves. Objects are constantly re-used. Even the tools we use are based
on OO technology. Procedural coding as used with COBOL is considered
"history", (see the Ferrari/ Horse and Buggy analogy I used in another
thread here recently.)

The most significant litmus test for me is in the scale of the
application.  If the project is very large at all, it's my
opinion that it can benefit from the organization that
object-oriented programming can lend.

The degree if interaction between components is also a factor.
The more interaction, the more advantage there will be to using
a rigorous method of defining interfaces.  (GUIs are a great
example, but not the only application that has a lot if
interaction.)  This can of course be accomplished with
structure programming, but why invent something that's already
foundational to object-oriented programming.

It's easy to say this without any consideration of your
project's specifics.  Some of the more practical issues:

*  Pre-existing code-base.  If you are evolving a project
that's not object-oriented, refactoring it an object-oriented
model may be more time + money that it's worth.  Certainly,
conversion to OO should not be _the_ only goal of a project.

*  Interoperability with other systems.  In general,
component-based environments allow for easier integration.  If
your current environment does not sport this as a design goal,
build that infrastructure for one application will be of little
use.

*  Technology selection can sometimes determine this.  If
traditional tools are simply better than their OO counterparts,
on technical merits, you have to consider this.

*  Staff experience is a realistic factor.  If the team that is
to implement a design is unfamiliar or hostile to a particular
flavor of development, you have to heed those risks.

My apologies if this repeats what others have said -- I just
wanted to offer a bit of speculation from a clean palate.
