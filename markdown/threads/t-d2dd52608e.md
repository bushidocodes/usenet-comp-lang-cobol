Z80 is a microprocessor chip made by Zilog that was more or less a clone of
the Intel 8080 chip.  It was often supported by the CP/M operating system
developed by Digital Research.  There were a number of proprietary computers
put together around the Z80 chip, including an add-in board for the Apple
II+ computer that was manufactured by Microsoft.  That was about mid-1980
IIRC, not too long before IBM got Microsoft to put together PCDOS/MSDOS for
the original IBM PC.  One COBOL compiler I used under CP/M was UTAH COBOL
(anyone remember that one? sort of a toy compiler for a toy computer at the
time, but it seemed to follow the COBOL standard pretty well and produced
usable executable code.)

I can confirm what Carl says: Nevada COBOL was the 8-bit (8080 and Z-80) product
and UTAH COBOL was the 16-bit (IBM-PC 8088) product.

I spent many an hour with Nevada COBOL and a Morrow MicroDecisions computer in
the late 70s early 80s writing a series of business applications for a small
weekly newspaper I published at the time. I developed a good accounts receivable
system, subscription/mailing list  management system, and contact management
system. Still kicking myself for not marketing them.

My copy of Nevada COBOL cost me $29. (Years later, I coughed up $39 for Utah
COBOL.) As I recall, not only did Nevada not support ELSE, it didn't support
nested IFs, which made for some pretty convoluted code. Still, I was extremely
grateful to have it. Here's why (listen up kids):

I had left a large Honeywell mainframe shop to start my newspaper. At that shop
the compile process involved: writing code on a coding pad, giving it to a
keypunch operator, sending the resulting deck of cards by messenger to the
computer room, waiting for a return printout by return messenger. We got maybe
two turnarounds per day.

What a thrill it was to sit in front of my own little $2000 computer, press a
key, and get a complete compile back inside of ten minutes! It was apparent that
a revolution was underway... and it seemed that it would be a revolution that
would empower the little man. (It was really an time exciting time, kiddo.)

I still have my old computers stashed away, along with a Wordstar manual or two
and a BASIC manual by an obscure company named Microsoft. Most all the 8-bit
computers came with CP/M and were bundled with WordStar and MS BASIC. (But it
seemed obvious to me that COBOL was a superior language for a people's computer,
and that some advanced derivative of it [even more English-like] would
eventually dominate programming on the PC platform.)

Now about WordStar... [but I see this thread is drifting a bit]

Just sign me...

--Old and In the Way

Yeah, and it was the last time Intel found itself following, not leading,
a competitor.  But Zilog, like Motorola, did a clean break design when
going from 8 bits to 16 bits; Intel, instead, went for compatibility
(whence
the arcanity of the x86 design).  The short term gains of backward
compatibility
were overwhelming in the marketplace at that time when coupled with the
"blessing" of IBM in the form of the original IBM PC.  The Z8000 was a 
very clean design, as was/is the 680x0 family, but technical merit has
rarely
been the deciding factor in marketplace decisions (remember betaMax?).

Lots of embedded systems people got started with 8080/8085/Z80's  and
Z80's and derivatives are still being used in lots of NEW embedded designs.
The chips are dirt cheap and for people who are already comfortable using
them, and have a stockpile of tools (crossAsms &c) , it can be a very good
decision.  But not for general purpose end-user computers in today's
market.

I figured it might be, those older chips tend to be produced
forever for imbedded applications.  There were a whole slew
of "almost" Z80 chips developed with memory included, EPROM
and mask programmable prom included, ports of various sorts,
etc., etc., etc.

Nowadays, though, I doubt that anyone is putting a Z80 into
a real computer as a CPU, though I would not be surprised if
various controllers are somewhat Z80 based.  I programmed
the Z80 on several occasions for instrumentation, as it was
very popular as a base chip in things like cash registers and
weigh scales. Input of a button array and output of an LED
display is somewhat different than Cobol<G>.

I still have a card in service that is a Z80 based card talking
to a printer port on one side, and turning high voltage lines on
and off via optical decoupling  and heavy duty relays on the
other.  The code reading the printer port is Z80 assembler,
while the code writing it is an 8086 Cobol assembler subroutine.
