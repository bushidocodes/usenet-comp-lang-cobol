This morning I was sitting in my armchair writing code (COBOL and C#, so 
this post is not completely off topic:-)) when I noticed that the rug near 
the entrance to my lounge had moved.

It does this as a slow creep from left to right (the house faces North).

Usually my housekeeper picks it up and bangs it around outside then replaces 
it in the proper spot but on this occasion it seems to have moved faster 
than is usual.

I cannot account for why this happens and as I am not of a superstitious 
disposition I was wondering if the collective intelligence and experience in 
this group could provide an explanation?

I thought it might be the Coriolis effect but that seems a bit far fetched. 
The rug is made of a solid hemp (it is designed for heavy traffic areas), is 
not "large", say,  4.5 feet by 2.5, and is sitting on a wall to wall carpet 
that is 90% wool (not a polished floor or slippery nylon).

Here are the questions:

1. Why does the rug creep of its own volition? (The movement is 
imperceptible; if you watch it, you cannot see it moving. Over days, the 
movement becomes apparent.)

2. Does anybody else observe this behaviour in their homes?

3. Does it creep from left to right in other places too? (If it went from 
right to left in the Northern Hemisphere this might strengthen the case for 
Coriolis...)

All suggestions received with interest.

Pete.

It moves under the action of walking on it and pressing down the pile
of the carpet.

The pile will fall one way underfoot, as you step on the run the
friction will be highest between your foot and the top of the pile, as
the pile is pressed down the ends will move the way it falls taking
the rug with it.

When you lift your foot the pressure is released and the rug is held
in its new position by the rest of the area of contact.

A carpet has a pile direction. The pile does not sit upright but
points a little in one direction, giving a slightly smoother feel when
stroked with the pile rather than against it. When something like a
rug is placed on a carpet and walked upon, the pile bends in that
direction, carrying the rug with it. With the release of foot pressure
there is no longer the same amount of traction between rug and carpet,
so as the pile returns to an upright position it does not drag the rug
back with it.Traditionally, carpets are laid with the pile towards the
door. This is from the pre-vacuum cleaner days when carpets were
brushed clean and it was easier to brush the dirt out of the door in
the direction of the pile.  Don't ask me how I know this.  It could
get ugly.
