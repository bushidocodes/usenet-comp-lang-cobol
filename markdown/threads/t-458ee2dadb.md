› I have a very limited understanding of Turing and what you are talking
› about. Can you give the clueless (me) a little background?


Turing invented the "Turing test" ... the standard test for
artificial intelligence. The basic premise is that if you want
to test if a program exhibits artificial intelligence, then you
talk to it over a telephone (or an newsgroup for that matter).

If you cannot tell whether it is a program or a human that is
responding to you, then the program (or computer) is intelligent.

Turing was studying the nature of computability, and wanted
to know if artificial intelligence was/is possible. He was not able
to decide, but he was able to prove that if a problem is solvable,
then it is solvable with 14 basic instructions. Those instructions
were hardwired, and became the first computer. Damned if I can
remember the full set, but I will take a stab at it.
No-op, and, or, not, call, return, goto, gotoif, move, set, test, true,
false (??? anybody [stop?])

Quick summary:

1) If you are getting that message, then you are using the old (very old)

compiler ("COBOL for VSE") has different options).

2) When you go to MVS, do you know what compiler you will be using? If you
are going to the OS/VS COBOL compiler, then you will see very few changes in
your code. (The major differences are in the SELECT/ASSIGN statement - and
UPSI switches. Also, you JCL will be VERY different, but you only asked
about COBOL.)

3) HOWEVER, the "OS/VS COBOL" compiler is (and has been for several years)
unsupported by IBM. HOPEFULLY, the people who are doing your conversion are
moving you to the COBOL for OS/390 & VM (or almost as good COBOL for MVS &
VM compiler - but maybe the VS COBOL II compiler). With these you will see
LOTS of differences from the compiler you are used to.

4) LANGLVL(1) supports all of the ANS'68 COBOL Standard and any of the
ANS'74 Standard that doesn't conflict with '68. Similarly, LANGLVL(2)
supports all of the ANS'74 COBOL Standard and any of the ANS'68 that doesn't
conflict with it. (Therefore, with either option you get a "mish-mash" of
'68 and '74 support.)

The most common places that you see a difference are in
- COPY Statements where you are copying in a complete 01-level
- Combined abbreviated conditions

The complete list (and there are ONLY 10 items in it) are in the back of
your COBOL LRM.

5) If you are moving to any of the currently supported MVS compilers, then
you can forget all about LANGLVL(1) and LANGLVL(2) and start learning about
CMPR2 and NOCMPR2. CMPR2 is (mostly? completely?) an ANS'74 compiler (like
DOS/VS COBOL with LANGLVL(2) but with NONE of the old ANS'68 stuff (and some
other extensions removed). Most noticeable changes (and there are LOTS and
LOTS of them) will be missing
- EXHIBIT
- TRANSFORM
- bunch of file status code changes
- and enough other things to write several books. (I wrote one - and IBM
has several migration guides that talk about changes)

If you can get to NOCMPR2, this is the strategic direction for IBM (and in
fact all that they support with their PC and UNIX products). This will give
you ANS'85 behavior and syntax and open up a brand new world of many, MANY
new COBOL features.

*********

So much for being "quick" - but I hope that this helps.

P.S. Check with your management and see if the conversion is really to VS
COBOL II. If this is the compiler that you are going to, make certain that
you management knows:

A) IBM has already announced that they will drop support for it at the
end of 2000.
B) It is missing MOST of the Y2K features that their current MVS COBOL
products have.

Bottom-line: If you are doing a conversion any way, this is a really BAD
choice to move to (in my opinion).
