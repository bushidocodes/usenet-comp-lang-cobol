WILLIAM M. ULRICH

Remember Cobol? If You Don't, Get Reacquainted
  
When I mention the word Cobol to IT people, they look at me as if I just awoke
from a 20-year coma. Many IT professionals consider Cobol, like Latin, to be a
dead language. But rumors of Cobol's death have been greatly exaggerated.
Companies can't ignore their Cobol software assets and need to incorporate them
into their IT strategies. 

IT people treat Cobol like a pariah. Most universities have dropped it from
their curricula. Vendors shun the word Cobol in their marketing literature,
even when Cobol tools are responsible for much of their revenue. Programmers
who know Cobol tend to de-emphasize it on their resumes. 

In spite of its reputation, Cobol remains a resilient force in IT. Dale
Vecchio, research director at Gartner Inc., says there are roughly 180 billion
lines of Cobol worldwide. This isn't surprising, given that Cobol has been
around for more than 40 years. What is surprising is Gartner's comment in a
February research note stating that 15% of all new application functionality
through 2005 will be in Cobol. 

In the same research note, Gartner stated that 80% of all deployed applications
through 2004 will include legacy extensions. These extensions involve Web-based
applications triggering legacy system transactions that perform operational
business functions and access legacy databases. In January's issue of "The
Cobol Report," Scott Ankrum wrote that CICS transaction volume grew from 20
billion a day in 1998 to 30 billion a day this year. This 50% growth in
mainframe usage has largely been driven by Web-based systems accessing back-end
Cobol applications. 
...
• Protect your Cobol talent. The number of Cobol programmers is shrinking,
with many headed for retirement. Consider bonus programs for them. 
...
• Recognize the need for new Cobol skills to upgrade existing systems and
build new functionality. You may need to enhance in-house training programs and
rethink hiring plans. 

IT may need to launch an awareness campaign to gain funding support for these
action items. One way to do this is to remind management that the replacement
cost of these systems, conservatively estimated at $25 per line of code, is
typically in the billions of dollars. 

Cobol has outlived newer programming languages, hardware platforms, operating
systems and Y2k. It's not going away and must be leveraged to meet strategic
business requirements in a timely and effective manner. So, revisit the role
Cobol plays in your corporate IT plans.

Thanks for pointing this out.  I'm seeing more and more positive press on
COBOL.  It's amazing how the pendulum swings.
I think when the web first hit with a vengance, COBOL compilers and
Enterprise level systems in general weren't ready for web related
technologies.  And since the mission critical nature of those systems
require very long, slow and carefully planned and executed changes - the
Mainframe technologies couldn't keep up with the "apparent" pace of the
"just build something, throw it on the web and let the end user test it"
Web.

Now, however, companies and developers are discovering that:
1. Mainframe technologies and especially COBOL have caught up - and can
integrate and even drive newer technologies.  In fact, some notable dot com
failures have taught the industry that there is no substitute for careful
planning and design, production control, and (horrors) testing!  We have
also learned that when you need to process a huge volume of transactions
behind that web site - there is no substitute for big iron.
2. We have returned to the old principle of "use the best tools for the
job."  If you want to write event driven code, such as an operating system -
use C++.  But if you want to write a business applications that can be
easily understood and maintained for generations, use COBOL!

And if you want to move from an older, expensive to maintain Mainframe to a
newer midrange or even super Micro system - why recode.  COBOL actually runs
under UNIX and NT.  AMAZING but TRUE!  And a code migration to a new
platform is nearly always MUCH cheaper than recoding.

In fact, we have a good case study coming up in next weeks issue of
www.aboutlegacycoding.com about an insurance company that did just that.

Pardon the rant - I know I'm preaching to the Choir here *grin*
