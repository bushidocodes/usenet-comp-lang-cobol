```
Don't know if this answers any questions, but here's my "how I became a
programmer" story.

Between sixth and seventh grade a took a summer computer course.  I learned
to program BASIC on a TRS-80 Model III.  I then took another BASIC course
in seventh grade.  I took Pascal in eleventh grade (UCSD Pascal on an Apple II).
I also bought Borland Turbo Pascal to use at home.  Later I bought Borland
Turbo C and a few books and tought myself C.  I majored computer science
in college.  We used, I believe, Pascal first, and then C.  Also took one
semester of 8086 assembler.  I dropped out after 1 1/2 years of college
(CU-Boulder).  Sometime later I bought Borland C++ and a few books and
taught myself C++.

I worked at a retail electronics store (Fry's Electronics) for two years.
The cash register, purchase order, and inventory systems were all computerized
on PC compatables.  It was actually rather incredible because the company
only had one programmer!  I'm not sure if they bought the system, but he
made quite a few changes in the time I was there.  Anyway, I am a rather
timid person so, although I would have loved to program on their system, I
don't think I ever even brought it up.  Still, I didn't lose my passion
for programming, even though I was working 60 hours a week and didn't even
have a computer at the time (parents having owned the one I used prior to
this) so I didn't do any actual programming for the whole two years.

After that I got a job at a bank.  After a time I was able to get my own
computer.  I converted a partially written text editor that I had written
in C in my college years to C++.  I then did a very stupid thing.  I made some
major changes to it without saving a previous working version.  I then broke
it and was never able to get it working again.  So I simply gave up.  Pretty
sad, I know.  I didn't do *any* programming at all for at least another three
years.

During that time, I was working for this bank.  I did teller, new accounts,
bookkeeping and customer service for five years.  Then I saw an internal job
posting for a COBOL programmer at our corporate data center.  I applied and
got an interview, but I had never even *seen* COBOL at the time.  I didn't
get the job.

What I *did* get was a great incentive to learn COBOL!  I purchased
Micro Focus Personal COBOL for DOS and "Teach Yourself COBOL in 21 Days."
After I completed that book I purchased "COBOL By Command," which filled
in a lot of the blanks that "21 Days" missed.  I also bought "CICS for the
COBOL Programmer (Part 1)."  Less than six months after not getting the first 
job I applied again.  I didn't have one single day of actual job related 
programming experience.  What I *did* have was five years bank experience and 
a "know-it-all" kind of attitude.  I got the job!

That was less than 2 1/2 years ago.  I learned JCL on the job, and some
CICS.  I also took a one week CICS course paid for by the job.  (It was
*very* useful, btw).  I'm now the primary programmer on a purchased 
ATM/card processing system.  Late last year and early this year I wrote 
a 10000+ line CICS online program from scratch.  (Actually five different 
programs, but all interrelated).

In the time I've been in the programming department we have hired about
six experienced programmers and four programmer trainees (which is what
I started as).  Every single one of the trainees were already employeed
by the company (one was in accounting, one was in audit/loan review, one
was a computer operator, and one was already in the programming department
as a tester/admin asst/user services person).  Three of the four are
still with the company.  Three of the six experience programmers did not
last more than two months.  One just disappeared one day, we know not
where.  One job a higher paying job elsewhere.  One, who allegedly had
30 years experience, couldn't program his way out of a paper bag!  He
quit before he could get fired.  (The other three are still productive
members of society...err, the department.)

Anyway, of the programmers who were there when I started, I believe five
also were hired from within the company.  Two have since quit, but two
remain in mainframe applications and one has transferred to the client/server
(PC) programming department.

What's my point?  One main point is that it seems a very good idea to work
for a company who has a programming department, even if you don't start there.
Learn everything you can there.  Any company, I'm sure, is more likely to
hire a programmer trainee from within the company rather then from outside.

A new programmer trainee is actually starting next week, but as far as I can
tell all of the other programmers were either hired from within the company
or were already experienced programmers from elsewhere.

One other thing I'd like to say is make sure you *like* programming, and
seem to be good at it, before deciding to take a job as a programmer.  I
mean, this guy with 30 years experience obviously had to be able to
program to some degree, but I don't think he was a programmer at heart.
Sure, you can make a living simply being "OK," but I sure wouldn't want to
work with you!  :-)
```
