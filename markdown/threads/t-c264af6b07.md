Hey,
I have a (true) story about that

In the early eighties I designed a system for life-insurance. CICS, Cobol,
DL/I. We spent many hours with the customer to design screens, and we did
that with spacing charts
(24 lines and 80 columns). The customer was unhappy about one of the
screens - he needed more information and we couldn't sqeeze one single byte
extra on that awful screen.

We told him that he had to accept to split the information into 2 screens -
no deal. He told us to come back next day. He would consult his
brother-in-law (a self-made guru).

When we came bach the next day, he stood at the end of the table saying:
'Look here, this is what I want - it shouldn't be that difficult to
understand for proffs like you'!!!!!

We found out that his brother-in-law worked at a TV station. The screen
layout form he had given to our customer was 60 lines and 100 columns. We
had a hell of a time to declare the design impossible - 'Look here - it's a
screen - isn't it'.

Fun with all that memories.

Regards

Think they might be difficult to find. Unless you need them specifically
for designing a layout with a pre-printed form, suggest following. Use
any good source editor to lay out your lines as you want them to appear,
then take each line and turn it into a COBOL print line, e.g. :-

*..........Number : xxxx Name : xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx

01 Line1.
   05 			pic x(10).
   05 			pic x(09) value "Number : ".
   05 L-AccountNumber 	pic x(04).
   05 			pic x(08) value " Name : ".
   05 L-AccountName     pic x(20).

01 Line 2 etc.....

Thane:

The only problem with coding forms is that they're kind of short if
you want to use them as a printer spacing template.

The simplest approach I can think of...assuming that the printed
output is going to be fixed pitch fonts at 10 characters per
inch......

Just create a Word document first adjusting the margin to create an 8"
by 10' printable area, then create a table of 80 columns by 60 rows.
Darken the borders and you should have your template!
