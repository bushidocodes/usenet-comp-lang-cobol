Patrick wrote:
> 
> Cobol 2002+ is OO too no? Are there good books and tutorials discussing this?


Rui Bivar de Oliveira's book _The Power of COBOL_ (ISBN 0-620-34652-3) 
has some chapters on OO COBOL, using ISO (standard) syntax.[1]

You might be able to find a copy of Ray Obin's _Object Orientation: An 
Introduction for COBOL Programmers_, published by Micro Focus (ISBN 
1-56928-001-0). It's based on the first Micro Focus OO COBOL, from 
long before there was a standard,[2] so the syntax it describes is not 
COBOL 2002. But it's short and readable and a good conceptual 
introduction to OO COBOL, though programmers familiar with OO concepts 
will know a lot of the material already.

There are some examples of ISO OO COBOL programs on the web - if you 
use a sufficiently specific search ('"configuration section" 
repository "class-id"', for example) you can find some.


However: I don't believe OpenCOBOL supports OO COBOL of any sort, so 
you won't be able to try out OO COBOL with it. I don't know that there 
are any free compilers that do have OO COBOL support. (The free 
edition of Micro Focus Enterprise Developer only does syntax checking 
- it doesn't produce executables - and you can only get the Academic 
edition through a partner institution. I don't know what's currently 
available from Fujitsu.)



[1] There are several versions of OO COBOL. There's the standard 
syntax and semantics from ISO 1989-2002, the current published 
international COBOL standard. It's a bit verbose (in keeping with 
COBOL in general). There were older, pre-standard OO extensions to 
COBOL from some of the COBOL vendors, including Micro Focus. Then, 
after the 2002 standard, Micro Focus introduced alternative, 
simplified OO syntax for the managed environments (.NET and JVM); 
other vendors may have created something similar. The 2002 version of 
OO COBOL is standard; the simplified syntax is more convenient.

[2] Obin's book was published in 1993, so it predates the ISO standard 
by nearly a decade. He wrote it not long after the X3J5.1 committee 
produced its technical report, the first step toward the eventual 
standard.

I think you have to place the "dire warnings" in context.

I used COBOL successfully for over 30 years. It was very good and mostly did 
what was required.

But the world moved on.

The way that most people and corporations use computers today is NOT the way 
we did in the 1960s.

Networked operations need small pieces of code that  do clearly defined 
functions and require nothing more (they are "self-contained"). Objects meet 
this reqirement perfectly. Objects derived from Classes are encapsulated and 
they have their own properties (data) and methods (behaviours) which enable 
them to deliver functionality directly and wherever it is needed. They can 
respond to events happening in the real world and can even decide to provide 
more clones of themselves to handle unusual loads. It is a different 
paradigm and different concept from processing linearly and controlling 
everything from within a program.

Object Orientation is too important to be ignored and the world has not 
ignored it.

By the time COBOL was enhanced to support OO, the world was already moving 
to other languages that were DESIGNED to support OO. There is very little 
that you can't do with a modern COBOL compiler that supports OO, but other 
languages do it "better". (how and why they are better is too much to go 
into here, but if you talk to people who are familiar with both COBOL and a 
modern language like C#, Java, even VB.Net, they will tell you about 
conciseness of code, better tools than have typically been available for 
COBOL, and infinitely better support and help with learning.) You CAN do 
stuff with COBOL just like you CAN make fire by rubbing sticks together, but 
there are easier, faster, much more efficient ways to achieve a result.

Since moving from COBOL to C#, my personal productivity has more than 
doubled (and I was no slouch in COBOL...:-))  The cost of maintaining code 
has more than halved (but some of that is due to better design techniques 
and moving to largely component based development.)

COBOL is expensive; other languages are free.

The world hasn't gone off COBOL because everybody hates it; they have done 
so because it is much less relevant in today's world than was once the case.

http://primacomputing.co.nz/cobol21/COBOLRelevance.aspx     (Has taken just 
over 1000 hits)

http://java.dzone.com/articles/cretaceous-cobol-can-spawn   (Has taken 
around 11,500 hits)

You should also be aware that nobody has fully implemented the COBOL 2002 
specification yet.

(Having said that, it is an excellent spec.; probably too late to catch the 
last train leaving COBOL Town...)

"Better" is debatable.

I'm pretty proficient in COBOL, including various versions of 
procedural COBOL and OO COBOL. But my background is in CS and 
programming languages; before I learned COBOL I was proficient in 
BASIC and Pascal, and since then I've done serious work in C, C++, 
Fortran, LISP, Scheme, ML, OCaml and F#, x86 assembly, VAX assembly, 
Java, C#, and more scripting languages than I can count, with a 
smattering of others. I've studied a good bit of programming language 
design, written lexers and parsers and compilers, created my own 
domain-specific languages. I've followed language standardization 
processes and studied standards and their peripheral documents 
(rationales and the like); at one time I nearly had the C90 standard 
memorized. (The majority of my professional programming has been in 
C.) I've presented at academic conferences on how programming language 
design affects software development. So I have some experience in this 
area.

Here's the thing: there's good reason to believe that for most 
programming, the largest economic advantage comes from *readable* 
code. For most organizations, maintenance is the largest part of 
software-development costs (some studies estimate as high as 80%); and 
in most of those, a large part of maintenance is understanding 
existing code.

Consequently, "concise code" is a false idol. *Expressive* programming 
languages are good, because they let the source code correspond more 
closely to the algorithm, and make it easier to convey the program's 
purpose to the reader. But many programming language designs favor 
concision over clarity. OCaml (and other languages in the ML family) 
is a marvelous language in many ways, but it uses idiosyncratic 
punctuation for so many operations that it becomes nearly unreadable 
if you don't use it frequently. The champion of concise source, among 
languages that are used for serious purposes, is APL - infamous for 
its "write-only" nature.

C and C++ aren't as bad as OCaml (or anywhere near as bad as APL - but 
then nothing is) in this regard, but they make writing awkward, 
unclear code easy, and they've accumulated programming cultures that 
encourage it. I've worked with a lot of C and C++ developers, and read 
and changed a lot of other people's C and C++ source (closed and 
open), and the vast majority of it is terrible, from a readability 
point of view. The Java-family languages are better, but there's still 
a lot of dreadful source there.

COBOL has its own problems with readability (despite being designed in 
large part just for that - but under constraints that no longer 
apply). Procedural COBOL encourages huge monolithic programs, and its 
division-based structure creates huge reference problems: definitions 
far from point of use, flattened hierarchy, lousy encapsulation and 
abstraction. Even OO COBOL with simplified syntax (like MF's managed 
COBOL dialect) still suffers from artifacts of traditional COBOL, such 
as level numbers (pointless in OO COBOL). But COBOL at least 
acknowledges readability as a goal.

(There have been any number of attempts to address the readability 
problem over the years, from COBOL's documentary features like the 
identification division and the note statement, to in-code 
documentation systems like Javadoc, to coding standards like "picture 
on a page", to Knuth's "literate programming". In-code documentation 
is moderately popular; the others are mostly confined to niches. 
Literate programming, ironically, may have failed in part because the 
syntax Knuth developed for it is awful - a bunch of arbitrary 
sequences of punctuation.)

Programming languages certainly affect software development, and some 
are certainly better than others, particularly in specific domains. 
But the languages that can be called "worse" in an unqualified sense 
are *clearly* worse - they lack basic features or impose unnecessary 
limitations. Those languages aside, it's very hard to justify the 
claim that one programming language is better than another in any 
absolute sense.

I take your points about tooling and resources - but there again 
things are mostly subjective. Much of my early training used IDEs, but 
I've never cared for them, except sometimes when experimenting, for 
example; so as far as I'm concerned, the tools available for all the 
major languages are pretty much equivalent. And while there are a lot 
more examples and discussions and whatnot for C, say, online than 
there are for COBOL, the quality tends to be abysmal; if you're not 
reading material posted by someone who's intimately familiar with the 
C standard, it's likely wrong, or at least non-optimal.

I don't love COBOL; even the streamlined managed dialects carry a lot 
of old cruft. But I can find plenty of faults in any language I've 
used, even old favorites like Scheme or OCaml. C# is pretty nice, but 
interest in non-Windows implementations seems to be declining, and the 
political climate at Microsoft has swung against .NET and managed 
code. Contrary to some rumor-mongers I don't think .NET is going away 
- if COBOL has taught us anything, it's that once a language reaches 
decent penetration it'll be around for a long time - but I wouldn't 
want to be a C# specialist right now; I have a feeling that would 
involve a lot of arguing for my pet language in new projects. Java is 
very widely used, but its generics mechanism isn't very well designed, 
the framework's looking a bit dated, and Oracle's introducing security 
holes faster than they fix them. And so on.

My advice would be to learn a lot of languages, languages from 
different families and with different features and approaches. If 
you're comfortable with CS, I'd suggest studying language design a 
bit, maybe by reading something like the Lambda the Ultimate blog once 
in a while.

You seem to ignore the COBOL CALL statement. While it was standardised
in '74 it was available in some form much earlier. The dynamic CALL
was significant for several reasons. It catered for creating a
structure for code that gave isolation; along with the CANCEL it gave
memory management (I had multiuser systems on 8085 with 256Kb RAM);
being dynamic it allowed system updates of individual programs
eliminating the need for complete recompiles with associated system
testing.

It also allowed the CALL to use a textfield for the program name which
meant that the system could be very dynamic, it could run different
subprograms based on various parameters or conditions. It was also
possible to structure COBOL programs so that one CALLed subprogram
could perform various functions (methods) depending on the parameters.
For example a 'function' parameter could direct the CALLed subprogram
to execute different code, just as an object performs different
methods.

For example I developed a base system originally CIS COBOL for DRS20
and MP/M which included a file maintenance program that was driven by
text files specifying the file layout and the screen layout. Each file
had its own sub program with the record FD which it would read, write,
rewrite etc depending on the function code passed. It interacted with
a standardised linkage record by moving the fields as required. The
files were designed to make this easy by having only a few variations
of record size and layout into which the data was arranged. Those same
file subprograms were also used in the report program which was driven
by scripts.

The only real restriction on CALL is that there can only be one
instance of each subprogram. If the report program was to use 2 main
files then they could not use the same sub-program, in fact I had to
have a separate subfile program to cater for this. With OO there can
be multiple objects of the same class. However, COBOL systems tend to
be structured in a way that requires serial reuse rather than multiple
objects. Processing an invoice, for example, tends to be done by
reading each invoice line in turn, calculating and printing it and
then moving to the next. This processing is also record orientated so
grouping of data by level numbers is appropriate.

What's supported by a language, and what's used in the culture that

COBOL is by no means the only language to have some of its best

apparently don't understand critical features like the proper use of

It's generally implemented as a subroutine call (with some hidden

Although my initial response here is to Michael, I'm going to explain sme

general response for anyone who is interested...

Parameterization IS important, but way back in the piece (about 15 years 
ago) I gave this a lot of thought and realised the following:

1. If you parameterize method signatures, you are creating a possible pain 
when it has to change.

For example:

I create an object, objX , from Class X and it has a method which takes 4 
parameters:   objx.method1(p1 p2 p3 p4)

This gets invoked by 100 programs.

But now I find that some of these parameters are not required or used by 
some of these programs.  And some of the programs require a 5th parameter, 
but if I add that to the invocation, I have changed the signature of the 
method, so I have to change every one of the 100 programs where it is 
invoked, or create alternate interfaces for it.

We end up with cases where some of the parameters are set to NULL, or 
omitted if the language allows that and it all gets a bit hairy, in my 
opinion.

I want a nice clean method signature that never changes and is consistent 
for the life of the component. I want to know that if I require the 
functionality of method1 of objX, I can just instantiate objX and invoke 
method1. (objX.method1).

2. Like I said, when I was first coming to grips with OOP, I gave this a 
good deal of thought. It seemed to me that parameters are really just ways 
of modifying a fundamental behaviour so that it can be more generalised. I 
saw two solutions:

1. Write a different Class method for each parameter or combination of 
parameters and set properties within each method before invoking it.
2.  Let the parameters be Class (Factory) or Method Properties and eliminate 
parameter passing altogether. (2 actually derived from 1, because 1 was not 
at all an attractive proposition.)

So, instead of passing parameters in and out of an object method, just SET 
the parameters as Properties before invoking the method, then GET the 
Properties after invoking the method. This way, the invoker only deals with 
the specific parameters it is interested in to modify the method's 
behaviour, and the "parameterization" is removed completely. Method 
signature is clean and minimal and there is no problem with remembering it 
or getting it wrong, or passing unused or null parameters back and forth. 
Each invoker knows what it wants and it deals with the set up for that 
particular invocation by SETting the appropriate Properties, invoking the 
method, then GETting the results if it needs them. (In some cases, the 
invoker is not really interested in the invoked process, just seeing that it 
has happened so something further down the line has what it needs.).

This approach has worked very well for me using component based design. I 
don't parameterize methods unless it is a very simple set of parameters that 
are ALWAYS likely to be needed.

I DO create within the method public Properties that would have previously 
been passed parameters.

(I have carried the same principles into my C# development and it works very 
well. C# supports the GETting and SETting of properties with simple dot 
syntax and the setup of public Properties within a Class has been 
abbreviated to a single line in the later versions of the language.)

Compare:
 (COBOL):
01   MyProperty   pic x(256) PROPERTY. *> a public property which should be 
set by the caller...

("early C#"):
        private static string _MyProperty;
        public static string MyProperty    {
            get
            {
                return _MyProperty;
            }
            set
            {
                _MyProperty = value;
            }
        }

Kludgy 11 line definition of a Property. Although it shows the detail of 
what happens, we are not really interested in that. We just want our 
Property to be safe...

("current  C#"):
public string MyProperty { get; set; } // a public property which should be 
set by the caller...

One line definition as for COBOL. (The private static property is subsumed 
under the covers, so it is still "safe"...)

However, the crunch comes when we need to GET or SET the Property.... Here's 
the respective code in the invoking programs:

COBOL (Fujitsu):

      INVOKE  objX  "SET-MyProperty" *> SETs object property to value of 
ws-MyProperty
          USING  ws-MyProperty
      END-INVOKE

      INVOKE  objX  "GET-MyProperty" *> GETs object property into 
ws-MyProperty
          RETURNING  ws-MyProperty
      END-INVOKE

... and, in C#:

objX.MyProperty = ws-MyProperty;      //SETs object property to value of 
ws-MyProperty
   ws-MyProperty = objX.MyProperty;   //GETs object property into 
ws-MyProperty

(This also demonstrates my argument, made elsewhere, that once COBOL guys 
start understanding OOP they move away from COBOL...
  The C# is just as "readable" in terms of clarity and it takes much less 
writing. Apart from reducing RSI in programmers, if you write less you can 
do it quicker... :-))

If a new parameter has to be added, I can add a new Property to the Class 
and ONLY the invokers that need the new parameter are affected. No 
regression testing is required; I can just test the ones that use the new 
property and be assured that the ones that don't will continue running as 
they aways have. (If the new parameter was passed through the method 
signature, it might be necessary to amend and recompile all 100 invoking 
programs...). Of course, if some funny things started happening after the 
new Property was added, THEN it might be necessary to regression test and 
debug; so far, in 15 years, I have never had to do that.

When I first came up with this I was pretty excited and thought I had 
discovered something that could revolutionize the maintenance of program 
code... :-) (I was still pretty much in COBOL mode... :-))

> 1. If you parameterize method signatures, you are creating a possible pain

Sure. This is the standard coupling problem. OO languages typically 
try to solve this with polymorphic methods (or inheritance, when the 
changes are more extensive), so you can extend the API without 
altering existing code.

> 1. Write a different Class method for each parameter or combination of 
> parameters and set properties within each method before invoking it.
> 2.  Let the parameters be Class (Factory) or Method Properties and eliminate 
> parameter passing altogether. (2 actually derived from 1, because 1 was not 
> at all an attractive proposition.)

This isn't eliminating parameterization; it's encapsulating it. Since 
object methods all have a hidden parameter that represents the object 
that's being acted on (the "this" or "self" parameter), you've simply 
encapsulated the other parameters in the object itself.

Often this is a good technique. Instead of identifying parameters by 
position, it in effect identifies them by name - which means 
additional metadata is available to avoid certain classes of bugs. It 
lets parameters persist across methods, which can reduce source-code 
duplication, a common source of errors. And, per your original point, 
it makes the API more flexible and easy to extend.

It also has drawbacks. The first main one is that the compiler can't 
automatically detect missing parameters (since it doesn't know what 
properties the method expects to have set) - if you want contract 
enforcement, you have to implement that manually. The other is that 
sometimes a parameter doesn't logically "belong" to the object that's 
being invoked, so making it a property is incongruous. (Some people 
would refer to this as a "code smell".)

But this technique can be very valuable. It's especially useful in 
dynamic OO languages, particularly allotyped ones, where you can 
assign any property to any object and create types on the fly. Some 
Javascript[1] programmers habitually specify anonymous objects as 
method parameters, like this:

	thing.setColor({blue: 55, red: 123, green: 97});

Then the "setColor" method is written to take a single parameter and 
look for properties named "blue", "red", and "green" in it. It could 
be an on-the-fly anonymous object like the one in the example, or some 
other object that happens to contain those properties.

Yes. I edited a longer remark about the lack of parameterization with 
PERFORM, and the relative awkwardness of using external programs for 
small functions, down into a single comment that as it stands is 
incorrect. Ideally, you want to be able to decompose processing into 
small pieces that perform a single, coherent function (these may be 
called "subroutines" or "functions" or "methods" or whatever). When a 
bunch of these are related, it's convenient to be able to edit them 
together, in a single unit of source (source file or source member or 
whatever it may be).

There are in concept other ways of organizing source code - the "file" 
metaphor has been with us for an awfully long time, and there have 
been attempts to organize and view the source hierarchy in other ways. 
But for the most part we're stuck with files, and not being able to 
put a bunch of related small, parameterized routines in the same file 
makes it inconvenient to create modular programs.

> Recursion is never actually needed, iteration can be used with equal
> effect, it is simply a matter of maintaining your 'stack' in an array.

I didn't say it was needed. In fact there's a theorem which proves, by 
construction, that any recursive algorithm can be transformed into an 
iterative one with a fixed stack requirement, regardless of problem 
size. (In other words, the "maintain your own stack" approach is 
really just manually implementing recursion, and there are more 
sophisticated ways to eliminate recursion.) I said recursive 
algorithms are "difficult to implement" without support for recursion 
in the language, and in general I believe that's the case, though of 
course what counts as "difficult" is subjective. Certainly it's more 
trouble than letting the language do the work for you.

> I did write a quicksort for arrays, which is usually recursive, in CIS
> COBOL which is/was '74.

Iterative Quicksort is relatively straightforward; the Standard C 
Library's qsort is often implemented as an iterative Quicksort. 
(qsort, despite its name, need not be Quicksort at all - the standard 
only imposes a few requirements on it.) If you limit the size of the 
input (and in practice it'll be limited by address space, for an 
in-memory sort), you can do it with a fixed number of "stack frames" 
and avoid dynamic allocation. In fact, with some clever coding, you 
can ensure that you'll never need more than N frames to sort up to 
2**N records - so, for example, you can implement iterative Quicksort 
with an array of 32 frames for 32-bit programs.

Simple self-recursive algorithms can be transformed with this sort of 
keep-your-own-stack logic, mostly a matter of writing housekeeping 
code. Things get more complicated with mutually-recursive algorithms 
or cases of indirect recursion where your call graph has cycles but 
they're not trivial. Those do show up in the real world.

For example, I have a Telnet server implementation written in COBOL - 
fortunately in OO COBOL, with support for recursion. During the Telnet 
negotiation process, it's quite common to start processing a Telnet 
command sequence from the peer, call a subroutine that handles that 
particular command, and while processing the command data find an 
embedded Telnet command sequence. The cleanest way to process that is 
to call the Telnet-command subroutine, which is already on the call 
stack, so you have indirect recursion.

It's a question of affordance. Older COBOLs did not make it impossible 
to write modular programs, but they offered more resistance to, and 
less encouragement for, modularity than later languages (including 
later COBOLs) did. Consequently, COBOL developed a population of 
programmers and a culture that favored monolithic programs with 
relatively high coupling (side effects in one part of the program can 
affect distant parts) and low cohesion (the program isn't broken up 
into "modules" that each have a relatively narrow, specific purpose).

Richard wrote:
> On Jan 30, 8:51 am, Michael Wojcik <mwoj...@newsguy.com> wrote:

> I am not sure why you think that the 'same source file' is important.

It's a habitual unit of work for the programmer. A majority of 
development environments - from punching card decks to vi running in a 
Unix shell to ISPF to the first workstation IDEs - have had 
programmers work in source files or members of some sort. That's what 
the programmer has to hand when reading or editing a program; so it 
becomes natural (in the sense of unexamined, without further thought) 
to treat a source file as a functional unit.

Some languages even enshrine this division, as Java did with its "one 
(top-level) class per file" organization. Others, such as C, use 
source file divisions to control visibility; this originated in the 
simple linkage process available to early C implementations, but was 
carried forward into the language standard for reasons of compatibility.

That makes programmers strongly inclined to let the source-file 
organization reflect the organization of the program as a whole. 
Grouping associated pieces of functionality into source files helps 
keep the program structure aligned to the programmer's mental model of 
the program's architecture.

Also, in many environments (particularly before the advent of the 
modern IDE and "messy-desk" windowing, both of which support viewing 
an arbitrary number of files), switching between source files is 
significantly more work than remaining within a single source file. So 
in those environments, aspects of the programmer's workflow - reading 
and editing code - penalize breaking related functionality up among 
multiple source files.
