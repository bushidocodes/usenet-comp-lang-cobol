- expiration of the Bush era tax cuts,
- expiration of the payroll tax holiday,
- expiration of the Alternative Minimum Tax fix,
- sequestration of domestic and defense spending,
and probably more.

Coupled with the above is the (partial) implementation of the Affordable

increase in paperwork). Most experts predict an upheaval in employment as 
businesses make Draconian adjustments hoping to guarantee they won't be 
adversely penalized by the ever-changing regulations.

I don't even want to think about problems in our foreign relations (Iran, 
Afghanistan again becoming a haven for terrorists, etc.)

All these terrors will crash down upon us during the first six months of 
2013. The government will see some of the difficulties and try as hard as 
they might to alleviate them. This endeavor will mostly fail for two 
reasons. First, there are those who insist that the problems are transient, 
that the fix IS INDEED better than no fix at all, even if the good result 
won't be apparent for years (think environmental regulations). Second, most 
of the time a rapid response is worse than no response; sometimes a rapid 
response just makes things worse !

Continuing with this analysis could take pages; suffice it to say there's 
trouble looming in River City. Most agree that things are tough; what they 
fail to note is how bad things can actually get.

We can expect myriad consequences in the short and medium term. All of the 
following are possible. And likely, to one degree or another:
- Higher unemployment
- Higher national debt
- Significant inflation
- Downgrading of national bonds and T-bills with concomitant higher interest 
rates
- As a weakened nation, more testing of us by our adversaries
- Microsoft and Boeing moving a few miles north to Canada (just an example)
- Instead of letters to the editor, vigorous objections, from marches to 
riots, will pop up

Can this mess be fixed by the folks in charge?

Of course not.

As the nation circles the drain, the majority party will say and do 
everything short of waving chicken claws over the body.

All those on the other side of the current administration have to do is to 
be the "loyal opposition," make contrary arguments to the bizarre proposals 
of the Democrats, and, most importantly, continuing to argue "I told you 
so."

At some point, those subsisting on turnip leaves dug out of dumpsters will 
have an epiphany.

I think things are definitely going to be hard (not just in the US, but 
everywhere) as the World reaps the whirlwind of debt it allowed to blow up, 
and the "now" generation were indulged and allowed to buy goods and services 
without having to acquire the value represented.

Europe is on the verge of financial collapse and even here many people are 
starting to plant their own vegetables and hunker down for a long period of 
austerity.

As Kipling remarked: "For the sin that ye do by two and by two, ye must pay 
for one by one..."

I had an experience that brought it home to me a few days ago.

I was in the supermarket (and noted with some alarm that prices seem to have 
risen noticeably and significantly) over the last few months. I was looking 
for some limes (I like fresh lime with my Jack Daniel's and coke) and they 
are usually imported into NZ from the pacific islands, so while they are not 
"cheap", they are not outrageously expensive either.

A young woman with a small child was also examining the limes and looking 
for a pack that were cheaper. (they were all pre-packaged by the 
supermarket, rather than loose.)  I commented to her that it was good to see 
limes back in stock (they have been off the shelves for a week or two) and 
she agreed but noted that they were expensive. Then she turned to me and 
said: "There's more here than I really want, would you be prepared to go 
halves with me in a pack?"

This is something I have never experienced before and I was a bit 
non-plussed. The saving would have been around $1.50 but, obviously, to her, 
that was worth doing. There are people out there who are becoming 
increasingly  "desperate".

Fortunately, where I live it is unlikely that anyone would need to starve as 
there is a rich abundance of fruit and vegetables, the oceans are still full 
of edible fish and there is wild venison and pork, not to mention ducks and 
other game birds, but there are people who are old and infirm and others who 
have no interest or proclivity in providing for themselves, and, as jobs dry 
up and the economy constricts, it can be tough..

I agree with you that no Government is going to find a quick fix. It is just 
going to be a tough time for everybody for a while until there is again real 
value in the economy and there isn't too much worthless cash chasing it.

Negative growth is tough for everybody, but it is a necessary correction to 
years of living beyond our means, with successive governments lacking the 
balls to risk the unpopularity incurred by addressing it.

Pete.
