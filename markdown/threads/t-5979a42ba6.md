Now, what is wrong with the alphabet soup of standard bodies (j4, g4, 9x
etc.) appropriating the zippiest features of all existing languages and
adding them to COBOL? Well, we already have a cautionary example in fairly
recent history. IBM and a few others thought it would be great to combine the
best features of COBOL, FORTRAN, Algol and even assembler to create the Swiss
Army knife of programming languages. They called it PL/I and it was launched
with all the power of IBM's marketing machine. This was back in thedthe days
of real account control by IBM. They said defecate, and the adoring customers
asked how high and what color.But despite the formidable power of IBM's
marketing machine behind it PL/I laid an egg with commercial accounts. It was
too big, had too many features, required too much machine resource. People
with COBOL type problems stuck with COBOL and folks with FORTRAN type
problems stuck with FORTRAN.

The new COBOL will face the same problem. The enthusiasts who want to cram in
every available feature from all these other languages will in fact use the
other languages in preference to COBOL when the situation calls for their use.
Lets face it, it is easier to write C++ in C++ than it is in any possible
version of of COBOL.


What exactly IS the COBOL market? It is defined by three factors, legacy
programs, legacy programmers and the failure of more modern languages to
address directly the commercial programming problem. The new features won't
assist legacy programs. They won't convert legacy programmers to object
oriented methods (can't teach us old dogs new tricks - as many firms have
discovered.) And they won't address better the problems that COBOL already
addresses very well accounts receivable etc. The 9x standard will be the one
that finally puts COBOL under. All the manufacturers will feel the need to
keep up with the standard, and the resulting compilers (and their manuals!)
will cause mass confusion in programming shops around the world. We already
have a generational split, where new graduates are surprised to find their
employers ignoring large chunks of COBOL 85. With COBOL 9x we will have the
tower of Babel. New programmers will be writing code (per their CS professor)
that older programmers	can't begin to decipher. And the language will
finally die.

John Culleton, CDP
Ancient COBOL Crank

Having made my living using both, I can honestly say that I MUCH prefer PL/I to
COBOL and it's a vastly superior language. 
 
Why then did it fail to catch hold? 

IMHO: 

1. They charged too much - monthly maintenance was about ten times more than
COBOL. This is an error that continues to this day on the Windows platform -
Visual Age For Java is about $29 after rebate and runs on WIN 95; Visual Age
For PL/I is $2000+ and only runs on NT.

2. They forgot the business programmer - SEARCH or SORT verbs, the bread and
butter of business programming, were omitted in favor of such obtuse constructs
as 'CALL PLISRTx'.  

3. It was a little slow - optimized PL/I ran about twice as slow as optimized
COBOL due to such features as dynamically allocated variables. This counted
back in the old days (as many a dismayed Y2K worker can attest to!). 

4. Lack of education/books/PR/salesmanship - you'd never know that essentially
everything you could do with COBOL could be done with PL/I, and usually much
easier (e.g., inline DOs, 'real' initialization of variables, variable length
records/strings, string handling). In addition, there were some very useful
features that still aren't available (or available in a half-assed form) with
COBOL (e.g., controlled storage, recursion, macro preprocessor, put
list/edit/data stream output, in-line subroutines/functions).

First, some new features have assisted in making legacy code better.
