Peter,

A number of people through this forum have expressed concern that many of
the books on COBOL are WAY out of date and give a very misleading impression
of the Language.

Thane Hubbell is a fairly frequent contributor here, and his book "SAM's
Teach yourself COBOL in 24 Hours" has had unanimously favourable reviews.
(Despite the misleading title, it is NOT a quick and dirty day course in
COBOL. <G>)

I am currently working my way through Wilson Price's "Elements of
Object-Oriented COBOL" (Second Edition).

I cannot recommend this book highly enough. It is simply outstanding. In
fact, I am so impressed, I am trying to interest my local Polytech in
considering it as a standard text.

Wilson has produced a book which is simple, filled with excellent examples,
and follows a logical progression which introduces Object Oriented
programming in a COBOL context. It is completely up to date, and uses real
modern COBOL sample code. I see this book being of use to existing COBOL
programmers who want to understand the Object Oriented paradigm, and to
people like Java programmers, who already understand OO concepts, and are
interested in extending their skill set to include COBOL.

Some while ago I considered writing a book on OO COBOL, but after reading
this one I am persuaded there is nothing I could add...<G>

It is published by ObjectZ and can probably be obtained form the
www.cobolreport.com Web Site.
(No, I am NOT on commission...<G>)

The only other book on COBOL which I have ever been really impressed with
(and I have read, proof-read, and reviewed many) was written by Dr. Fritz D.
McCameron who taught COBOL at Louisiana State in the late sixties. I should
think this book is now long out of print, but, as a young programmer I found
it explained the concepts of things like nested IFs, complex PERFORMs, and
the need for structure (before the famous papers on Structured programming)
in simple language, with copious easy examples. In fact I still teach (and
use) his rules for nesting IFs to this day. The book, should you be lucky
enough to find a copy, is called "COBOL Logic and Programming".

Hope this helps,

Pete.
