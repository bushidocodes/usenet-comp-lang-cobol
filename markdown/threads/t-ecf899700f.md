I know that this is not related to the newsgroup but I really don't know

The reason I am writing this letter is because the consulting company

its verbal promises that I was told of on the phone (these promises were

Now I have two choices. I either take whatever (this can be: tech
support or ANYTHING - not necessarily a programming job) job they find
me or I will have to quit the company and pay $10,000.00 for the

repay the loans for the college tuition.

in the WEST after training.
I was promised of a paid apartment and airplane tickets back to my home
on the weekends (if placed nationally) or quarterly in every year (if
placed internationally). This was a really important thing for me
since my Dad has heart problems and I wanted to keep an eye on him
(last year I had to take him 3 times to
the emergency room).

I would also appreciate if any of you could place an answer to this
newsgroup since I am not leaving my real e-mail address.

Thanks

This message is bound to get a lot of 'look before you leap' advisories,
all with proper respectful intentions.
Unfortunately, I've seen situations where the contractor pays close
attention to the contract. And it appears that all bases are covered.
However, the firm's lawyers can claim that the contract says something
other than what it does. You end up with your lawyer vs. theirs, and you
finally 'win', and the lawyers all get together for drinks at the Ritz
afterwards on your money.
One other typical threat is that 'you can't work for a different
consulting firm at the same client' because you signed a 'no-compete'
agreement. Usually that phrase of the contract actually says you can't
work for the client as an employee for awhile after you leave the firm.
Sometimes this is escalated (their claim) to say you can't even work in
the same city.
Luckily, this is one area where you can be adamant and never sign a
no-compete agreement of any kind. If they have the client asking for
your services, they'll give in to the visions of you making $$ for them.
Oh, they'll push and try to intimidate you when you're joining the
company, but hang tough on that one.
I could tell you more stories.

My current consulting firm has not exhibited any of that behavior, which
makes me a happy camper. But my previous one used the standard arsenal
of threats, especially after I told them I was leaving.

I'm really sorry for you. You found out too late. Don't think you're
not the only person this has happened to, I've met others. Entry level
persons are easy prey. But let's not dwell on it.

Your commitment is probably two years, if you don't know, find out. Try
to do the best work that you can do, and setup a meeting with your
(consulting firm) boss. Try to get the best atmosphere for this meeting,
then stress your needs, and that you want to be a team player. Perhaps
you don't want to be a team player because you've been unfairly treated,
but just tell him that!

Look at it this way, that firm has got you by the balls. In every
business situation, the other party's main interest is WIIFM: What's In
It For Me. If you bitch and moan, he won't do what you want. He'll ship
you of to Alaska to shovel shit for two years, and he will not let you
out of your commitment.

Do good work for the client that you are at. Don't whine about your
problem to him, it's not his problem. After you have establish a good
relationship with your client, perhaps then you could discuss it with the
client, but be careful. Make sure it doesn't sound like whining, make
sure he WANTS to hear it, and make sure it's in your best interest that
he knows. But in most cases, the client just wants good work from you
and that's it.

You might try and contact the firm's office in the city you want to work
at. Set your vacation plans to visit there, and try to arrange a meeting
with the office manager or marketing reps (find out who's the best one)
to explore the possibilities of transfering there. If they receive good
reviews from the other office or client, they will want you. If they
receive reports of you complaining and whining, or your work is
substandard, they won't.


Tim Oxler
