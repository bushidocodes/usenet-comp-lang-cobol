Samuel Taylor Colerdge's poem, The Ancient Mariner, is about an aged sea
man who in his travels crossed spiritual boundaries forbidden to ordinary
men, and I seem to recall a parody, "The Ancient Programmer", in
Datamation a number of years ago.

But the Ancient Mariner has negative elements of the Daemonic as well as
elements of the god-like about him. Andrea Nye, professor of philosophy
at the U of Wisconsin (Eau Claire) writes "logic in its final perfection
is insane." The French critic, philosopher and outright fraud Lacan
wrote of the psychosis of rigor, and Foucault wrote "genius is rigor in
despair" (one of those marvelous French phrases, like Sainte Juste's "you
may now scatter our limbs to the four winds: republics will rise from
them!" that manages to be evocative and senseless.) These tropes are
useful correctives to the sunny American expectations that logic will be
"clear."

And I am one of those gods, or daemons, for I started writing code for
the ancient mystery, the IBM 1401. A compiler I developed in 1985 while
recovering from experiences too terrible to relate on a family network is
still muttering away under simulation on an RS 6000. And I did a lot of
Cobol in the 1970s and perhaps one of my date comparisions will end
civilization on 1/1/00!

Racine wrote "Minos juge aux enfer tous les pales humaines": Minos judges
pale mankind in Hades. When Phedre came to Minos, he was reduced to
rooting about for a suitable punishment for that queen and for her lust
for her stepson (there was no Ophrah Winfrey for Phedre, only l'enfer.)
One can only wonder what will happen to the Cobol gang.
