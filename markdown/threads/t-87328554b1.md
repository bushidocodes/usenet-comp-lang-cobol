For those who have failed to see any relevance to COBOL in the
recent discussions concerning morality and law, let me clarify.

Should object-orientation be included in the COBOL standard?

In the absence of other considerations, the nominal answer is:
"Yeah, sure, why not! After all, if I don't use it, it won't affect me."

In the presence of considerations of morality and law, other
questions must be answered before a conclusion may be
presented.

Is object-orientation an essential or necessary part of business-
oriented programming? Would its presence enhance the ability
to create business-oriented programs?

How will the inclusion of object-orientation affect the ability of
implementors to provide compliant translators?

Will the inclusion of object-orientation create a barrier to the
entry of new implementors? If yes, how will that affect the
cost of translators from existing implementors?

If a new implementor does not include object-orientation,
how might that affect their right to call their implementation
"compliant?"

Is it morally correct for me to support the inclusion of object-
orientation even though it may increase the cost to those
who do not need and will not use object-orientation? Or
should I care?

Now, should object-orientation be included in the COBOL
standard?

Morality is not simply "How will it affect me?"; it is also "How
will it affect others?"

Now, think about Screen Section and Report Writer. Should
they be mandatory, optional, or excluded? Please do not
answer without thinking.

are not.

And nothing is really off topic or dis-allowed in COBOL either.
If one just reviews the 7 or 8 different types of PC cobol systems
available, then one looks back at the various mainframe cobol
systems which have been offered over the last 35 years,
one can see there is a STANDARD amongst all that time which
is observed.

But when they started putting statements which are proprietary to
the OS you are using such as "PC_SET_DRIVE" or perhaps
the multitude of variations they have incorporated for seeking
a Julian Date or how about how the launching of programs in
new windows with a GUI, there is damn little standard or going
to be standard in that.

And because of this, the whole standards issue is thrown in the
toilet.

And I think that's a shame.

I remember about a year ago starting a large debate on the
interserv news group about what is a julian date.
There were 37 responses to my new question, in E-mails,
there were about 12 posted, and out of all of these only
2 seemed to agree on what it was.

In order to adopt a standard you have to presume there is to
be a NON-moving target to put a fence up around which we
can validate will not change.

OOP is still evolving.  I don't know if you know this or not but
there are 3 types of OOP out there right now.

And virtually everybody has their own answer to a GUI.

Rather than put our faith in standards, PC compiler manufacturers
are using MF compatibility as the issue as the consideration
of which compiler to use has gone beyond the old STANDARDS.

And what of people like me who use no OOP and use no GUI;
who are engaged in converting Unisys V COBOL code to a
server???  Well, we can have another STANDARDS discussion
there.

In the Unisys world you can have things like a 9(1200) comp.
Their HEX representation of their number is like this
@01FFFC@ and not X"01FFFC".  Where is the standard in that?

Or how about blocking factors, sectoring, and all that old time
file I-O stuff used on mainframes and not used on PC's.

Even 25 years ago, when standards were rigidly enforced,
you still couldn't sucessfully take a cobol package from
and IBM system and port it to another mainframe without
some re-coding.

You can't take something written in MF and move it to Fujitsu without
some re-coding.

Here's another one for you to laugh at.
In the Unisys digits math world, a 9(5) comp value 12345 looks
in hex like this @12345@.  They don't do bytes.

The MF equivalent of this is 9(5) comp-6 value 12345 which looks
like this @012345@ as PC's work only in bytes.  Henceforth you
end up rounding all your numbers up one digit to a 9(6) for example.

And let's not forget that wonderful set of phrases,
Entering Symbolic here.

Entering Cobol here.


I think standards are important when it comes to upward compatibiltiy
with a COBOL vendor.  I think it would be even grander if they all
used an adopted standard for everything encompassing a PC.
But they do not.

They do not for several reasons.
   #1.  PC hardware is changing the scope of what you can do almost daily.
   #2.  Major software manufacturers I.E. Microsoft are changing their day
to day
          business coding philosophy with every version; they are changing
old
         commands and at the same time adding more commands to do different
         things which eventually spill out into COBOL source code.
   #3. Competing COBOL manufacturers have a tendency to put special things
         in their compilables which make it hard to port COBOL code to other
machines thus
         forcing a major re-write.

So therefore,
I have no objections to them adding OOP as a part of the standard.
Why not.
