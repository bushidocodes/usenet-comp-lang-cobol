Bill, I am going to take issue with this one.  Before starting to scrap,
though, I will say that I agree the mainframe is a totally different beast,
and used for totally different things.  A PC CANNOT replace it, and I do not
agree that you can do the same thing as a mainframe with a bunch of micros.
The two are a completely different thing.  "Distributed processing" is a
buzzword that you and I  both take with the grain of salt it deserves.

However.  Stability (not size, not number of transactions) is another
matter.

I have a server beside me, used exclusively for software development and
testing.  It has not so much as hiccuped in five months. It was reset five
months ago because we had a power outage. It is running Win98 on a plain 233
PC with 64 megs.  It cost $1200 bucks, and if I wanted to put a $800 power
backup on it, I could let it sit for years.

What you say about legacy devices is true.  However, there is nothing that
requires you to install such devices.  The stability of a PC goes way down
as you load up various crap.  Just do not do it. This peer-to-peer stuff is
not suitable for anything beyond casual network use. It is designed for
email and small device sharing.  That is not the job of a file server.

If, on the other hand, you take a dedicated PC, load the OS, equip it with a
quality disk drive and a quality Ethernet card, it is not going to
spontaneously change.  It is going to sit there and do disk IO as long as it
has decent power.  If you want to do something else, put another PC on the
Ethernet bus, and do it.  We even do backup on a station. And it is still
*way* cheaper than getting a mainframe.  The load cannot justify a
mainframe. (I am only talking a hundred transactions per minute).

A large reason for the stability of the mainframe is that it is designed to
be used exactly this way, and nobody even tries to do otherwise. Take your
MVS system, and put a GUI on the operator's console. Remove all software,
and power it down.  Then give a twenty page manual to someone that just
failed out of an accounting course at the local college, and tell them to
install/setup/manage the mainframe. Give them no programmers, and a budget
of $1000 do get all the software they need.  Allow them to load up as many
games as they can collect on the internet into the OS area.  Do you
seriously think you will get a solid system?  I would bet money that they
could not even get the system to say "hello world".

That is how 99% of all PC's are set up.  It is a small miracle that they run
at all.  Expecting stability ... well ...
