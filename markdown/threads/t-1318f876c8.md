```
PLEASE, please send an email note to (both)

        Ann Wallace                   NWALLACE@u.ibm.com
       Don Schricker                 doncobol@mediaone.net

If you agree with me that it is critical that the "composite language
skeleton" be kept in the next COBOL Standard.  For several years now, a few
members (but so far not enough to carry the day) of the J4 (ANSI-ish) and
WG4 (ISO-ish) COBOL committees have repeatedly tried to get rid of the
composite language skeleton (from the next Standard).

I am not the best person to represent their views (because I think it is so
wrong), but they think it is hard to keep current and that no one use it!
(Furthermore, one person actually told me in private, that "well, you don't
need it in the Standard, because each vendor will provide their own."  What
that person thinks is supposed to happen to people developing books and
training that are SUPPOSED to be cross-vendor or delivered before real
products, I have never been able to figure out.)

The following is the report of the discussion at the last J4 meeting, (from
J4/98-0544, "Selected CD 1.4 quality review comments for WG4
consideration") - viewable at

  http://people.ne.mediaone.net/doncobol/98-0544.doc

8. (98-0543)  Composite language skeleton
The composite language skeleton is considerably out of sync with the body of
the standard, as noted in other comments and previous input documents to J4.

The skeleton is now manually duplicated, or manually updated, and will
always be a maintenance headache.   Framemaker is able to replicate parts of
documents in multiple places, but the result of that was not esthetically
pleasing because of extra space generated around the diagrams in the body.
Lee spent some time trying to work around this glitch, and the only way he
found was to cut and paste the diagrams rather than replicate them.

I don't know what technique is being used to maintain the skeleton now, but
obviously it is not easy to keep this error-free.

This redundant presentation of syntax diagrams is not worth the effort it
takes to maintain it.  There are much more important things our project
editor can be working on, and there are much more important things for
reviewers to spend time on.

It is time to bite the bullet and get rid of the composite language
skeleton.

J4 notes:   The J4 vote on removing the Composite language skeleton was 5-4.
J4 does not have a recommendation.
```

The standard is a reference.  Not a user manual.  User manuals have to 
be more "user friendly".  Standards do not and should not be that way.  
They have to be technically correct and exact, which makes them hard 
to read.  Were we to say things like "when you use this one way such

talked to doesn't like them (lots of this at Tandem where we changed 
our manuals to use railroad tracks because IBM did).  The Committee 
voted that they did not like them either.  Also, you can't represent 
as much with railroad tracks as you can with the tried and true 
methods.
