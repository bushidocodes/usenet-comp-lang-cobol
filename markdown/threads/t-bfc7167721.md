Jimmy and others:

As someone whose skills are in COBOL, Assembler, JCL, and various
proprietary and script languages, I take the statistics quoted with a
grain of salt.  I suspect that they were possibly realistic in the
1980's but the world has changed drastically in the past 10 - 12 years. 
I cringe when I read of C/C++ being used for business purposes and I
cringe because C/C++ doesn't have native decimal arithmetic, not because
of the other issues with the language. This also ignores the various
strengths of the language.  Much of the newer programming is being done
with a variety of tools.  One business package which is doing the job
for a small business I know of looked to be written in Access Basic. 
What languages are the ERP's (enterprise resource programs?) which
replaced vast amounts of code in various organizations written in?  

I saw a reference to UML which I believe stands for Universal Modeling
Language.  If such a language can be used to accurately describe te
business processes, then would not the next step be to enrich that
language (if needed) and use that language to generate the running code?

In short I suspect most statistics you see on language usage are at best
questionable and of dubious validity.  The year 2000 remediation and
replacement processes caused a lot of code to finally be sent to the bit
bucket.             

Clark Morris, CFM Technical Programming Services Inc., cfmtech@istar.ca
