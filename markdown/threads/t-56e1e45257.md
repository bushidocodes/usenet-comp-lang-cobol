I agree with the above posts, but I think the real important thing is a bit
more fundamental.  What I appreciate is the right attitude.  If they lack
knowledge you can teach them, if they lack experience they can gain it.  But
if they don't have a good attitude, it doesn't matter what they know, they
aren't worth the gunpowder to blow them up, as my granddad used to say.  When
a programmer has the basic talent and the right attitude, the rest is just
a matter of time.  There is an attitude in academia which elevates academic
knowledge above real-world experience, and this attitude seeps into many of
the students.  This is a very bone-headed attitude which often severely
limits the graduate's willingness to learn from experience and those with
experience.  I suspect that, like myself, this is what the posters above
really object to.  Not the education itself, which is a good thing in itself,
but the bone-headed attitude that education alone is sufficient, and even
superior.  As Mickey pointed out above, which would you trust, a pilot with
one year's experience, or a person who had never flown, but read about it
for four years?  Only a moron (or a bone-head) would choose the latter.
