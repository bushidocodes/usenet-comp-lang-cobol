Actually, computer professionals don't get wraped up in their own world as
often as they should.

Visual Basic is a fine piece of software, and I've praised it many times (I
don't know whether I have done so on Usenet, though, as I don't recall VB
coming to my attention before in the newsgroups I read). It is excellent
for rapid prototyping of front ends. That doesn't mean it's /necessarily/
the right solution for production systems.

Why not? Well, for a start it's not portable (I see this is cross-posted to
comp.lang.basic.visual so let me clarify that I mean it's not portable to,
say, Unix or mainframe systems). Ha, I hear you say. Who cares? Well, maybe
you don't care today. And maybe not tomorrow. But after you've left,
perhaps a couple of years after, your client is going to ask himself "how
can I get this to run on my DeathStation 9000?"

It is the job of computer professionals to point out the alternatives. The
customer should be the one deciding the importance of issues such as
portability, not the programmer. But it is the programmer's job to
highlight the issues of which the client may not be aware, should the
client be sufficiently naif to not be aware all by himself.

If a client says "I only want this to take an hour to develop, performance
is not an issue provided it's not stupidly slow, and I will never need it
to run on my DS9000" then fine, by all means do it in VB.

I like Java better, but VB has a couple of advantages:

1.  It's better tuned to do Windows specific tasks.
2.  VB is (almost) the macro language used by MicroSoft products such as
Excel, dropping the learning curve for people whose requirements
overlap.

Java works on more platforms which I use, and is better suited for
client-server.  It's also cheaper.  I still think in COBOL, and find it
harder to learn all the words in library based languages.

There's no way I believe the claims made for OO languages that testing
is reduced.  In an enterprise environment, I want to test everything
which uses a component which gets changed.  That's not efficient at all
with widely reused components.  Although I have to admit, in real life
reuse isn't nearly what OO advocates claim.

The product which I would love to play with, but am too cheap to get is
Visual Age Cobol.  I imagine if it were available cheaply for people to
use non-commercially, programmers who try it out would recommend it to
their companies where appropriate.  But, who spends that type of money
to play with it?
