```
A writer named D.B. Grady recently penned a piece in The Atlantic holding up 
to contempt and ridicule the sorry business which allowed the panty bomber 
to make it all the way to the U.S. without being confronted. I dug out Mr. 
Grady's email address and sent him the following note:

--- begin quote

In your most recent article for The Atlantic, you wrote:



"When 'Abdulmutalab' was typed in, did the computer not ask, 'Did you mean 
"Abdulmutallab"?' "



The computer shouldn't have to ask.



Since even before the advent of computers a primitive system of rooting out 
homophones has been used. It's called "Soundex," and is used, among other 
places, by the Social Security System. According to Wikipedia, it was first 
patented in 1918! Had the terrorism people's computers made use of Soundex 
or similar technique, the double-l difference would have surely been caught.



Now maybe you assumed a double-l difference to illustrate the possible 
triviality of the situation. Maybe the name used had a lot of Xs and Zs and 
Alephs and Omegas. Still, it would be interesting to track down just how the 
terrorist-seeking factotums deal with potential misspellings.



Buncha twits, you ask me.



Anyway, keep up the good work,



--- end quote



Mr. Grady was gracious enough to reply:



--- begin quote



Jerry,



Thanks for the very, very insightful response! (It's a pleasant contrast to 
the hate mail.)



I wish I'd have spoken to you yesterday. I will certainly mention Soundex in 
columns to come, because it's a really sharp point.



Thank you, and all my best,



David



--- end quote
```

```
Soundex is pretty cool. I added it to a large database at an Insurance 
company and the tech people were horrified. Unnecessary overhead. 
Fortunately, it was my call and I insisted on it. Some time later they had 
an exchange  scheme where clerks from Scandinavia were swapped with people 
at the home office for a period of time. Entering English surnames was 
difficult for these people and they used Soundex all the time. I showed them 
a demo using "Shakespeare, Shakezpeer, Shaykespere" etc. It was also very 
useful for detecting duplicate mispelled entries.

Probably no surprises to anyone but I do have a Soundex component which I 
wrote years ago in COBOL... :-)
```
