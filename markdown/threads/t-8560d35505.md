That's what's so wonderful about COBOL: it slices, it dices, it looks at
data any way you want it.

Yes, in the LINKAGE SECTION is where you'd place items that are to be
passed. For example...

LINKAGE SECTION.
01  LS-DATA PIC X(70).
01  LS-ARRAY REDEFINES LS-DATA.
    05  LS-ARRAY-ITEMS OCCURS 10 TIMES.
        10  LS-ARRAY-ITEM-X PIC X(05).
        10  LS-ARRAY-ITEM-9 PIC S999 COMP-3.

There, you've got your mixed strings and packed decimal items defined. Now,
in order to be able to address these data elements, you're gonna need the
following statement...

PROCEDURE DIVISION USING LS-DATA.

This makes everything in your LINKAGE SECTION available to your code. Now
you can go about quite happily referencing LS-ARRAY-ITEM-9(WS-ITEM-NBR) with
impunity... assuming that the value of WS-ITEM-NBR always falls between +1
and +10.

One calls using data in working-storage (usually), and sees the data within
the subroutine as being in the linkage section.   The linkage section, as
you would expect from the name, is really a way of linking the subroutine
procedures back to data that exists in the calling program.

An array is data the same as any other record.  It exists in the calling
program, and is seen through the linkage in the subroutine.  A common way of
handling that, and ensuring that the descriptions are the same in both
modules, is to make it a copied file.
