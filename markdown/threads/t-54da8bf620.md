### (Next) COBOL Standard - is it dead or just in serious need of CPR?

```
I attended the (still going on) J4 meeting in Carmel, CA Sun, Mon, Tues, and
half of Wednesday.  The meeting (along with the co-located WG4 meeting) goes
until Friday - so anything I say in this note is based on "tentative" at
best information, but ...

On Sunday evening, I was convinced that there never would be another
approved/official COBOL Standard.  By the time that I left on Wednesday, I
had been swayed to the belief that there will (possibly - maybe even
probably) be an approved Standard some time between 2002 and 2005.  It is my
expectation (and fear) that anything new and "hard" that was planned for
(and possibly even included in earlier drafts of) the next Standard:
  - will be removed
  - will be made implementor defined
- will become "processor dependent" (which actually means optional)
    or
- will simply be specified WRONG (incomplete, un-implementable, with
contradictions, completely NOT upward compatibly, or a combination of any of
the above)

OK - before giving some examples of what has caused me to reach this
depressing and depressed state, let me say (again) a little so that the
"reader will beware" of my opinions.

A)  I have a real Cassandra complex (OK - Doc, want to explain about
Cassandra to our non-classically trained readers?).  I keep on seeing awful
things happening to the Standard; I keep warning them about them; they keep
thinking I am crazy; and my worst fears keep coming true.

B) I am clearly "out of touch" with the accumulated knowledge and opinions
of the majority of BOTH the J4 and WG4 committees.  Almost every time that I
voted (everyone - even non-members - can vote on their "straw votes") I
would end up on the "losing" end of the debate.  Normally, I tell others
when they keep on being on the wrong-side of discussions with a large body
of experts, that such a person should at least CONSIDER that maybe it isn't
the rest of the world that is wrong.

C) Although it is a real simplification of a complex situation, it is J4
(the ANSI-ish group) that does the "nitty-gritty" technical work and WG4
(the ISO-ish group) that sets direction.  As I (intentionally so as to not
be bound by US positions or to "endorse" a consensus of countries "knows
best" policy) did not attend the WG4 sessions, I was not privy to either the
discussions or decisions of the "policy" and "direction setting" side of
this meeting.  Maybe, if I had seen this side of the "debate" I would have a
more hopeful view of the future of this Standard (but I don't really think
so).

D) and final "preface" statement, we all know what they say about statistics
and statisticians (no, Doc - I don't think you need to explain this one).
Therefore, I will freely admit that the votes and decisions that I include
in the following section are selected NOT at random - but to justify my
pessimism.

    *****

OK, what were some of the things that happened from Sunday to Wednesday in
Carmel or previous to this meeting. (wording may be completely off - see the
actual minutes for correct "details".)


  NOTE WELL:
     Some (but not many) of the following are things that I actually agree
with.  The point of including them all is to show you how many items are
STILL changing and how basic some of the changes are (and some of the
"reasonable" things that are being rejected).

1) Straw vote:
   Users are one of the Standard's primary target audience:
       Lost about 12 to 4 (I don't have the exact vote)

2) Remove the Composite Language Skeleton
      J4 member vote  -  Yes-9 No-1 Abstain-2

3) Remove allowing mixed alphanumeric and national characters in an
alphanumeric literal
  A) remove T-literals - consensus
  B) remove mixed data in traditional literals (HAVING such data is what
makes it impossible to allow all 256 bit-patterns as valid alphanumeric
characters)
    J4 member vote: Yes-1 No-8 Abstain-1

4) After several hours of J4 discussion, Vote on remove the
File-Sharing/Record-Locking definition from the Standard
   Yes-6 No-4 Abstain-2
(WG4 ended up keeping file sharing, but removing some of the more complex
aspects of record locking)

5) GLOBAL in USE statements (not for files or data) to be made OBSOLETE
  (consensus - I think)

6) Don't add a new relational operator "<>" to mean "not equal to"
   (consensus to reject the operator - because it really is too late to add
even "small things")

7) Hours and hours were spent on figuring out what types of groups could and
could not have "bits" and where they were aligned.  By the time I left,
there was still no resolution on this.

8) Remove new "dynamic tables" feature

9) Remove dynamic setting of SORT/MERGE collating sequence (they had already
removed setting other dynamic collating sequences - these have now been
replaced by using _locale processing)

10) Keep CALLs and INVOKEs with object data to "default" to BY CONTENT (as
opposed to everything else that defaults to BY REFERENCE).  This was done
because inline function references and method-invocations can't specify any
BY-phrase - therefore, CALL and INVOKE were changed to be consistent.

11) PADDING CHARACTERS were made archaic

12) The entire PICTURE clause is being re-written (hopefully compatibly -
but if you have ever read those rules, you will see the challenge)

13) NOT phrases and "positive" conditional phrases can now be specified in
any order, e.g.
    Read ABC
       NOT INVALID KEY  perform xyz
       Invalid Key perform lmn
    end-read

14) Allow "empty parenthesis" for empty function lists, e.g.
       Function Random ()

15) There needs to be another INTERNAL review of the document after the
current problems are fixed.

16) Allow GLOBAL on 77-levels
   rejected by consensus

17) Allow UNSTRING on referenced modified items
   rejected (postponed until "next" Standard)

18) But Object Orientation and National Character support in the processor
dependent list.
    Both of these were rejected

19) CANCEL should be defined as releasing storage
   rejected (although it was recognized this is what most users expect)

20) In the current J4 agenda, there are over 50 items that are STILL not
completed from the LAST "official" CD review (which ended some time in '97 I
think)

21) There are over 800 individual items identified during the current
"informal review" - and over 80 recommendations already made for changes
from this review (and still over 60 papers that haven't even been processed
yet).  This review doesn't even end until January of next year.

22) During this J4 meeting, it took usually over a half hour just to
"phrase" (word) straw votes (not motions which have some "binding nature" -
but straw votes that are simply intended to explore committee sentiment.)

23) The current draft (before already decided upon changes)
  - includes 20 USAGEs (some with options) - 13 are NEW
  - 14 formats to the SET statement  - I think 10 are new
  - 35 items identified as "Substantive changes potentially affecting
existing programs"
 - 140 items identified as "Substantive changes not affecting existing
programs"
 - 226 items in the implementor defined list
 - 60 items in the explicitly "undefined" list
 - 30 items in the "processor dependent" list

24) The following are major features that are still (or again) being
revised:
  - internationalization (use of locales)
  - how to process bit items
  - file sharing/record locking
  - how and when COPY, REPLACE, and compiler directives are processed (as
opposed to syntax processing)
 - how to define the new features for allocating and freeing memory
 - several bits and pieces related to object orientation
```

There is a saying that it is better for those who eat sausage not to
see it being made. :)  I admit that I have never had the opportunity
to participate in the standards process, and have only recently had
direct exposure to any of the people and processes.  But I wonder if
it won't be better for everybody if this proposed standard does *not*
become finalized anytime soon.  As many of you, I have spent the last
several decades mostly designing and writing COBOL systems, and I have
developed a pretty good idea of what is important or not, particularly
in small to medium sized business and local government, where about
80-90% of all people are employed here in the U.S. (66% work for
companies with fewer than 20 employees).  I see three areas which are
important in any significant system I have ever worked with.  The user
interface, the internal processing, and database access.  The proposed
standard adds the Screen Section and associated Procedure Division
constructs, a long overdue plus.  But the last time I looked, there is
no real consensus across the computer profession even that OO is a
'Good Thing', though its proponents are vociferous.  And even if OO is
a 'Good Thing' in general, it is certainly not clear to all that OO is
a 'Good Thing' for COBOL.  Much of the OO community do not understand
the long time strengths of COBOL in the first place.  Even so, a truly
enormous amount of effort has been expended on adding OO constructs to
COBOL.  At the same time, standard COBOL has almost ludicrously limited
capabilities in Indexed File access.  For example, I do not have one
client who would be satisfied with a program which could only allow
them to search through a file forward but not backward.  Before I had
the construct available in Micro Focus COBOL, I actually wrote code to
store viewed records in a temporary relative file so the users could
at least back up to the beginning point of an inquiry search.  My
clients had demanded it.  "Why on earth can't we go backwards as well
as forward?" they asked.  I have been very excited about the proposed
new capabilities in that area.  But I understand that there is some
sentiment to actually remove the READ PREVIOUS construct from the new
proposed standard.  I cannot imagine that anyone even suggesting such
a thing has actually ever written a real system using Indexed Files!
And Bill is reporting below that J4 actually voted to remove the file
sharing/record locking from the standard.  If I correctly understand
what that sounds like (please correct me if I am wrong), I am appalled
by the sheer lunacy of such thinking!  Do these people actually *use*
COBOL, or is it simply some intellectual exercise for people who merely
talk about it?  When I consider the long term ramifications of bringing
out a poorly considered standard which we will probably have to live
with for decades, and live with much of the stuff forever, I really
have my doubts.  I am looking forward to reading what the rest of you
have to say about this.

Well, I pretty well agree with you Judd.  The areas that are
most important to me are standardization of file locking,
and a standardized Screen Section. With those, plus
perhaps a few very minor changes (select clauses come
to mind), I would be quite content.  As far as a GUI goes,
one could easily have a module that replaces a standarized
screen section and extends it.  That can quite easily be
a third party tool.

The Isam read previous is not nearly as important, in
my opinion.  I seldom read a file backwards, and the
few times that I have needed to, I have been able to
fake it. It would be a pleasant addition, nothing more.

The rest, to me, is fluff.  The only other thing I would like
to see (and it is not even proposed) is an absolutely
standard calling procedure.  I think *any* Cobol program
should be able to call *any* Cobol subroutine, using
a standard call, regardless of the compiler.  Obviously,
the data would have to be in a compatible format, but
that is trivial.

compilers will again come into conformity and the great compiler

standard.
