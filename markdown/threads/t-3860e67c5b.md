compute result <s9(09V99)> rounded = (days-used  <9(02)> / days-of-month
<9(02)>) * amount <s9(09)V99>.

399,75 (?!!)

calculate 16 divided by 30 = 0,533333333333 .... multiplied by 750 = 400 !

This means Fujitsu also rounds intermediate results based upon the picture
of the resulting item (+1 decimal place).

sample. The code ran fine in NCR/ITX COBOL and later in RM/COBOL followed by
RM/COBOL85.....

solve. Just use an intermediate field for result (with 6 or more decimal
places depending on its environment), skip the rounded phrase in the compute
statement and multiply the result by 1 giving the actual result
rounded.......sigh.....

From Fujitsu's documentation:

"If the intermediate result value exceeds its precision* at execution time,
the intermediate result will be truncated. Therefore, if a longer data item
is specified in the arithmetic statement or arithmetic expression operand on
the in the function argument, an unexpected truncation may occur during
result evaluation."

*precision is 1 greater than the maximum significant digits of the operands.

In your case:

16 / 30 = .533  (result has three digits of precision)

0.533 x 000000750.00 = 399.75

whereas, by re-arranging the computation:

16 * 000000750.00 = 00012000.00  (8 digits of precision)

12000.00 / 30 = 400.00

In other words, intermediate calculations are NOT done at the maximum
precision possible by the computer; calculations are done with reference to
the precision of the operands.

Most of the time, you lose precision when dividing. Division should be done
last where possible.

Others have replied related to "work-arounds" - but I wanted to reply to the
issue of whether or not this is "non standard behaviour" (see subject line).

The answer is that it is TOTALLY implementor defined (in the '85 Standard -
and by "default" in the 2002 Standard) how they "evaluate" arithmetic
expressions (such as the "right side of the equal sign in a compute").

Therefore, (and this is TOTALLY extreme and no vendor would ever do this),

If the vendor defined that no matter WHAT "expression" or value was on the
right side of a COMPUTE statement, they would "return" the day-of-the-week
at run-time, this would be STANDARD CONFORMING (but rather user-unfriendly
<G>)

    ***

In actual fact, I know of one vendor who had a "feature" (e.g. BUG) in a
"long unsupported" compiler release where they TRUNCATED before doing
fractional exponentiation, e.g.

  Compute NumField =   OtherField  ** .5

was treated as if the program had

  Compute NumField = OtherField **  0

Needless to say, this gave RATHER unexpected results when fractional
exponentiation was used.  This compiler passed all the Standard
certification tests (when they were still in use) - and the vendor
(correctly) maintained that this product was Standard-conforming in its
behavior.

LUCKILY, they eventually "fixed" the behavior to be "more accurate" !!!

Intermediate results are not being rounded, they are being truncated. 

You should have written: 

days-used * amount / days-of-month

The rule of thumb is 'create big intermediate results before creating smaller
final results'. I groan every time I see someone compute percentages this way:

compute a-percent = (a / b) * 100

No, no, NO! It should be:

compute a-percent = a * 100 / b

Fujitsu is not the only COBOL compiler to do this. What's the alternative, store
intermediates as floating-point? That would be too slow and raise other
complaints. 

The 2002 standard tries to enforce Standard Arithmetic, which addresses this
point. I'm not conversant on Standard Arithmetic. Perhaps WMK, Chuck Stevens or
one of the other committee members will expound on it.

The 2002 Standard does NOT try to "enforce" Standard Arithmetic.  It
provides an OPTION for "standard arithmetic" - which is in many/most cases
more accurate than "native" arithmetic, but is in all cases (where it
applies) "portable".  The default in the 2002 is fully upwardly compatible
with the '85 Standard - and is so because NATIVE rather than STANDARD
arithmetic is the default.
