You might like to consider the question from two different
angles (1) Do they mean the future of COBOL - as in does it
have a future at all - OR - (2) Do they mean the future of COBOL
- as in what enhancements are planned to be made to
COBOL in the future.

If you look at this from the perspective of point number (2)
then the new COBOL-97 standard that seems to be talked about
on this news group would probably point you in the right.
Maybe someone on this group could provide a draft copy of
the standard or at least point you in the right direction.

If you look at
companies like IBM who are bringing out an object oriented
version of COBOL this year as well as MicroFocus who have
already announced such a product then you might get an idea
of the future direction for COBOL, ie, it seems to imply
that both companies think (or hope in MicroFocus's case) that
COBOL has a future and what direction that future will take.

It could also be that the
future of COBOL lies not at the front-end but at the back-end
peforming the database/transaction server functions with other
languages being used at the front-end to do GUI presentation.

Looking at it from the perspective of point (1) there is no one
who could categorically answer this question. You are asking this
question in the COBOL forum, obviously most people are going to tell
you that COBOL has a future. Perhaps you should post your question
to the comp.lang.c forum and see what response you get. (most
likely it will be diametrically opposed). Clearly ex-COBOL
programmers like myself would like to see COBOL stay around
for a long time. We make a living out of it, we feed our kids and
buy our houses. We don't want that taken away from us just
because someone wants to write a payroll system in C rather than
COBOL. Proponents of other languages would like to see
COBOL dead and buried because the make a living out of writing in
that language.

One angle to help you work out the future of COBOL from
perspective (1) might be to look at the number of permanent
and contract job ads in Tuesday's Australian Newspaper
(the mecca for AP jobs) and take a count over the last few weeks
of the number of A/P jobs advertised for COBOL programmers
versus say Visual Basic or C or C++ etc. Then look at the number
of COBOL jobs advertised and see if they indicate that the job is
a maintenance job or a new development job or a conversion job.
This may give you some idea as to whether companies
(not lecturers) think the future of COBOL is.

You could probably make an interesting comparison between the
future of leaded petrol cars and unleaded petrol cars in
Australia. Yes, there are still a lot of leaded petrol cars
in Australia and all petrol stations still sell leaded petrol
and they will need to for some time to supply the old cars.
However, eventually those leaded petrol cars will disappear
(except for a few collectors items). Clearly there is no
future for selling leaded petrol in Australia or being an
expert in fixing leaded petrol cars.

There is however one big difference in this analogy and that
is that the government outlawed the production or importing
of leaded petrol cars into Australia thereby guaranteeing
the demise of such cars. This obviously will not happen
with COBOL. Companies will decide whether COBOL has a future
and it will be based on simple economic facts like the cost of
programmers, the cost of conversion, the cost of legacy systems
and a bit of guess work and thumbing in the air.

People are still free to develop COBOL applications and many
still choose to. So for the foreseeable future COBOL is
assured of its existence, in the long term the business
application community will decide the future of COBOL. In
other words - who knows. (If I were a student I would be
hedging my bets and learning COBOL only because it might
help to get me a job in the short term but I would make sure
I focussed on other languages).

Good luck in your research.

DISCLAIMER. I had nothing better to do in my lunch time.

Brad Swan
