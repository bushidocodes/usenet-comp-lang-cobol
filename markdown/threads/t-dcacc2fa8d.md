Saw your post after responding to Judson, Alistair.

As I'm not sure whether it should be clockwise or counterclockwise, I
decided to do some experiments with the kitchen sink. It is a standard
stainless steel sink, with a waste disposal outlet situated more or less in
the middle of it, and supplied by a "swingable" mixer tap. ("faucet" for our
North American friends.)

I found some "interesting" results.

If the faucet is swung to the side of the sink so that water flows IN
anticlockwise, when the plug is pulled (always carefully and directly
upwards so as not to impart any spin), the water flows OUT anticlockwise.

If the faucet is swung to the side of the sink so that water flows IN
clockwise, when the plug is pulled (always carefully and directly upwards so
as not to impart any spin), the water flows OUT clockwise.

I quickly realised that the slightest inclination towards a particular
direction affected the outcome. So I repeated the trials (4 in each
direction) and ensured that the body of water in the sink (always about the
same amount; half an inch over the plug) was completely at rest before
removing the plug. (I used tiny bubbles to detect any movement in the body
of water and was satisfied that within minutes of filling, it was at rest.)

I got exactly the same results! It was as if the water somehow remembered
the direction it HAD BEEN flowing in... (Coincidence? I guess it has to
be...)

So then I tried doing it with the water flowing in directly over the plug,
so there was no pre-disposition to swirl in either direction.

In this case it went anticlockwise 3 times and clockwise once.

Draw your own conclusions.

(My conclusion is that this is an indeterminate experiment and proves
nothing. It MAY show a preferment to wards anti-clockwise, but this is
impossible to confirm.)

'documentary', you must mean 'mockumentary'.

The Coriollis force is a result of the different speeds at the surface
of the Earth at different lattitudes.  At the equator it is
approximately 1000 mph.  At the poles it is zero.  Here is it roughly
700 mph.  This means that if I walk to the equator I will have to
speed up by 300 mph.  Or to be exact I will have to be sped up by the
surface.  It will take me approx 1000 hours to walk at 3 mph without
stopping, thus the surface will accelerate me at 0.3 m.p.h. per hour,
or approx 0.0001 ft per sec per sec.  This is 1/300,000 the force of
gravity.

When air or water moves north or south it will tend to lag or lead
slightly based on those figures.  The result is that for a statically
sized flow there is a tendancy to reult in a circulation. The final
result will be a rotation with a rate of up to once per day.  Rates
faster than this are the result of the rotation being reduced in size
by other forces or effects and the conserving of angular momentum
(just as a spinning skater can speed up by bring the arms in to the
body).

If you were to carry the sinkfull of water with you when walking to
the equator, and given no other forces are applied then eventually it
will be swirling away at some fraction of one rotation per day.  If
the plug is then pulled then it will speed up as it drains due to the
reduction in radius lower down the sink.

While the sink is fixed in your house there is _no_ coriollis force
acting on the water at all, this is entirely the result of moving
north or south.  Any residual tiny rotation in either direction is
amplified by the conservation of angular momentum as the water reduces
in radius.

Good explanation, Richard. It is the conservation of angular momentum
as mass moves either closer or farther away from the axis of rotation
(ie. the raidus of rotation changes) that results in the Corolis effect.
The same angular momentum results in  faster or slower rotation as the
radius of rotation decreases or increases, respectively. The radius of
rotation (distance from the axis) for any latitude is proportional to
cosine(latitude). Since the radius of the earth is relatively uniform,
we can omit that for discussion. Since it is the change in distance that
causes the Coriolis effect, you can get the rate of change as the first
derivative of cosine(latitude), which is (conveniently) sine(latitude).
When you calculate the difference in rotational velocity between the
opposite sides of a hurricane hundreds miles across, it is enormous.
As the heated air at the center rises, causing the hurricane to contract,
the effect is overwhelming. Both the contraction of the hurricane as
it rotates about its own center, and the contraction and rotation of the
hurricane as the earth rotates, work together to increase rotation. But
the difference between the sides of a whirlpool a few inches across is
infinitesimal. Almost any movement in the water could be greater than
Corolis at that level. However, once rotation begins, and the movement
is toward the center, the change in radius of rotation as the water moves
inward toward the drain becomes very significant. Like an ice skater
who spins, then pulls in their arms to rotate very rapidly. Whatever
rotation is present at that point will swamp any effect from the earth's
rotation. The water would need to be very still in a symmetrical bowl to
get a good test. In those conditions you should see counterclockwise
(northern hemisphere) and clockwise (southern hemisphere) rotation,
pretty consistently. Because of the small Corolis effect at this scale,
the system is somewhat chaotic, so you may not see absolutely uniform
results. :-)

Coriolis effect

The effect of the Earth's rotation on the atmosphere and on all
objects on the Earth's surface. In the northern hemisphere it
causes moving objects and currents to be deflected to the right;
in the southern hemisphere it causes deflection to the left. The
effect is named after its discoverer, French mathematician
Gaspard Coriolis (1792-1843).
