As Thane said, it's all device dependant; but you can forget about 512
bytes per sector on the mainframe. The physical entity is the block,
which contains one or more logical records. IIRC, the 3380's & 3390's
have 15 tracks per cylinder. The no. of bytes used per track depends on
the block size (i.e., there's a fixed interrecord gap). BTW, MVS
supports allocating datasets in MB nowadays, too.

Very rough rule of thumb (like give or take 200%!!!)

1 block = 512 bytes (if you're referring to a hardware block - a block of
records could be anywhere from 1 byte to 64K)
1 track = 40K
1 cylinder = 20 tracks

Like I say, very rough, totally device dependent and these numbers don't
represent any known device!

No, not reallly...

the size of a track is depending on the physical architecture of the disk drive, i.e.
track size is different on 3330, 3350, 3370, 3380, 3390 and all the other disk drives

A Cylinder is the set off all tracks that have the same distance from the spindle.  In the
338003390 disk drives 15 tracks make one cylinder.

The blocksize, then, is depending on the record format:

Recfm=F	Blocksize = LRECL (which is logical record length)
RECFM=FB	Blocksize is some multiple of LRECL
RECFM=VB	Blocksize is 4+multiple of (4+LRECL)

Hi Andrew, in my shop I use the following estimated sizes:
one page of output (file or report) = 8,712 bytes (characters per line *
lines per page.  In my case, 132 cpl * 66 lpp)
one track = 121,968 bytes (roughly 14 pages per track)
one cylinder = 1,829,520 bytes (roughly 15 tracks per cylinder)

As for block sizes, they are usually determined by record lengths and are,
therefore infinitely variable.  I hope this helps.
