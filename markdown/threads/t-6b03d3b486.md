Martin Nabbs <mnabbs@hotmail.com> wrote in article
<6t2qkp$89h$1@reef.news.technocom.net>... 

> If you have access to the A Series you can use the command PD <filename>
> which will tell you what type of object code it is.
 
This isn't a fail-safe approach, though.  Those people who use WFL to do
all their compiles, or use the "WITH" modifier in their CANDE "C" commands,
might well have saved their files as type COB instead of C74 (or, for that
matter, C85).   

Some commonly-used items that are clues indicating COBOL (68) rather than
COBOL74:  
	$ ANSI74, $B2500, $USASI, $S360, $B5700
	USAGEs: 
		COMP-1, COMP-2, COMP-4, COMP-5, INDEX FILE
	`SELECT ... 
		ASSIGN TO integer-1 [* integer-2] 
		[INTERCHANGE] 
		[DIRECT] 
		[OR] 
		[SINGLE [BY AREA/CYLINDER]] 
		[SAVE]
	CONSTANT SECTION. 
	
COBOL74 and COBOL85 may largely be distinguished one from the other by the
ABSENCE of COBOL85-specific constructs like EVALUATE, in-line PERFORM, and
END-IF.  Moreover, COBOL74's USAGE BINARY is equivalent to A Series
COBOL85;s BINARY EXTENDED; COBOL74's BINARY TRUNCATED is equivalent to
COBOL85's BINARY.   Thus, if you see BINARY (COMP-n is a dead giveaway for
COBOL(68) on A Series) items, and no sign of END-IF, END-PERFORM, EVALUATE,
nested programs and the like, it's probably COBOL74.  

And it's probably COBOL74 anyway. Though we do have a growing customer base
for A Series COBOL74, the vast majority of existing COBOL programs on
Unisys A Series systems are in COBOL74, and the vast majority of A Series
users continue to develop their programs using this compiler.  And there is
a sizeable group of unconverted COBOL (68) programs floating around.  

Note that if indeed the programs are COBOL(68), many users attempting
conversion to another compiler run into a data alignment problem.  It's not
clear whether this was a standards issue or not, but GROUP items in Unisys
A Series COBOL (68) actually take on the USAGE declared for them.  I
suspect the relevant standard was silent on that issue.  This causes
problems for COMP-2 (packed decimal) groups which are permitted in
COBOL(68) to begin on an even-digit boundary.  For example, given the
COBOL(68) declaration 
	01  THE-RECORD. 
		03  FIELD-1 PIC 9(3) COMP-2. 
		03  GROUP-1 COMP-2. 
			05  FIELD-2 PIC 9(3) COMP-2.
			05  FIELD-3 PIC 9(3) COMP-2. 
		03  GROUP-2 COMP-2. 
			05  FIELD-4 PIC 9(3) COMP-2. 
			05  FIELD-5 PIC 9(3) COMP-2. 
		03  FIELD-6 PIC 999 COMP.  
		03  FIELD-7 PIC X.  
the record will occupy a total of 20 digits, or ten characters.  

COBOL74 (and COBOL85) standards require that GROUP items be treated as
ALPHANUMERIC, and thus the equivalent declaration in COBOL74 (COMP in
COBOL74 is equivalent to COMP-2 in COBOL(68)) will cause the compiler to
insert a one-digit filler before GROUP-1 as well as another before FIELD-7
because neither is permitted to begin on a "non-byte" boundary.  The length
of this record will thus be 22 digits, or eleven characters.  Clearly this
will present migration headaches for those attempting to migrate multiple
record declarations under an FD or REDEFINES records in WORKING-STORAGE.  

Mechanisms exist in A Series COBOL(68) to assist with identification and
conversion of such records and the files that contain them in a form
"palatable" to A Series COBOL74 and COBOL85.

Technically speaking, A Series COBOL (68) is no longer upported in the
usual sense; trouble reports from "casual" customers have been rejected out
of hand since 1997.  Enough demand for this product was generated, however,
for Unisys to allow individual customers to negotiate for continued support
for a limited period of time to allow them to migrate their programs to
COBOL85, but such users are on a "short list" and they pay (dearly, I
suspect) for that privilege.   

And while the impending demise of COBOL was indeed widely rumored through
much of the latter half of the 1980's, negotiations between the software
development managers and the various marketing arms of the company did not
reach enough of a consensus to allow the formal process to begin until
early 1992; the patch to the compiler that began to notify users of this
impending support posture change was applied to the compiler in mid-1992.  

Probably the major reason Unisys has continued to support the product for
so long is that there was sufficient demand and sufficient pressure from
enough large customers to warrant keeping it going on financial grounds. 
From the customer's standpoint, there are a few features -- features that
some of the current architects of both the system and the compiler feel
were perhaps ill-advised to begin with -- for which the later compilers do
not provide universally-satisfactory solutions.   Most current COBOL(68)
users are, I believe, reluctant to change for that reason.  We've provided
solutions to some of them, over time (the SYNCHRONIZED attribute as a
substitute for direct I/O, for example), but some stumbling-blocks remain.

not a compatible member of that family, even though it was marketed (as the
B5700) alongside that series as a cheaper alternative well into the 1970's.

> The COBOL 68 compiler existed well
> before the A Series was introduced, the A just inherited it.  

True.   But it also inherited the COBOL74 compiler, written for the B6700. 
Both had been widely distributed before the A Series proper was introduced.
 Optimizations for A Series in COBOL and COBOL74 date from the early
1980's; a copy was made of the symbol file of COBOL(68) in the late 1970's,
and development of an ANSI-74-compliant compiler begun based on that
symbol, in response to DoD requirements for an ANSI-compliant COBOL
compiler.  

COBOL85, on the other hand, was written ab initio without a backward look
at the predecessor B5900/B6500/B6700/B6800/B6900/B7700/B7800/B7900 systems,
and without incorporating any part of the symbol files that make up
COBOL(68) or COBOL74.  It's a whole new compiler.  

> My guess is
> that most currently running A Series programs are still COBOL74, simply
> because there has been no compelling reason to convert them to the slower
> COBOL85 compiler.

A Series COBOL85 compilation is very often slower than COBOL74, but
execution-time performance is more often faster than not (depending, of
course, on the construct being measured).  This was not always true, but
over the years COBOL85 has undergone significant improvements in this area.
 There are some areas that are extremely difficult in COBOL74,  and the
developers of COBOL85 have done a really good job of avoiding a number of
those pitfalls.  There are also some architectural limits to the COBOL74
compiler that the COBOL85 compiler does not have (maximum record sizes, for
example).  

While Unisys fully supports the A Series COBOL74 compiler, they also
provide a migration tool for free to assist our customers in migrating to
COBOL85 should they desire to do so.  This tool also assists in COBOL(68)
migration.   And the migration tools from V Series COBOL (of any flavor) to
A Series all target COBOL85 on the destination system.  Don't sell A Series
COBOL85 short!  Unisys has some large A Series customers who have migrated
or are migrating from COBOL74 to COBOL85 and have been pleased with the
execution-time performance.

And interestingly enough, *very early* in C74's career (we're talking

diverged significantly, so providing an A-series-compatible C68 compiler
was not all that onerous a task.

Series to inform users programmatically that a feature is going away and

existing features don't go away on that system.  A Series C68 had access to

Two updates for previous responses to this comment: 

<<(This was back around '84-87 and COBOL80 had been introduced.)>>

I finally ran across somebody who thought they might know what this was. 
We had a "COBOL8X" compiler on our BTOS/CTOS machines that had some of the
features included in earlier drafts of what became the COBOL85 standard. 
Best guess is that you had some B36 systems (or their predecessors, or
their CT equivalents) floating around the shop or among your users.   

And a further comment on migration from B1000 or V Series COBOL(68) to A
Series COBOL(68):  The first two products were very similar to one another,
and both were VERY different from the third.   

The V Series (and the Medium Systems that preceded it) were
"string-oriented" machines specifically designed for the express purpose of
running COBOL applications.   COMP was packed decimal, and the systems
handed decimal operations on such fields of virtually arbitrary length.  

The B1000, being a "soft" machine with dynamic language interpreters, to
large degree "emulated" the Medium System in its COBOL mode.  

The A Series, by contrast, was originally designed to run ALGOL (and
similar word-oriented, block-structured languages like its philosophical
descendant Pascal).  Thus, A Series COBOL was built around word-oriented
arithmetic, without a second glance at compatibility (with smaller
Burroughs machines, or with other manufacturers' machines either, for that
matter), and the defaults (like the meaning of COMP) were "skewed"  toward
that word orientation.  

Medium Systems COBOL (68) users could migrate their application systems
without all that much difficulty to B1000.  Going the other way presented
some problems because of the file naming convention differences between the
two machines, but was otherwise just as straightforward in most cases.

But migrating COBOL(68) programs/application systems from either B1000 or
B2/3/4000 systems to A Series presented significant challenges.   

These very architectural differences were the primary reason that when
COBOL74 loomed on the horizon, corporate management mandated a *single*
approach to salient characteristics of that language on *all* systems, to
ensure compatibility, not just of the programs, but of the files
produced/accessed by them.   To large degree, that architecture was more
closely allied with the existing B1/2/3/4000 systems approach than it was
the B5/6/7/A Series approach.  

Further, that common approach is why the conversion aids focused on COBOL74
(the latest and greatest at the time) as a target as well; migration to
COBOL74 increased the chances that the resulting source file would be
compatible with minimal changes across the entire Burroughs product line. 
  

And a further comment about the B1000 COBOL74B compiler discussed in some
of these threads:  COBOL74B was developed primarly to provide support for
TADS (Test And Debug System).  COBOL74B was out for only a short time
before new  development on B1000 system software products was discontinued
and did not enjoy widespread use..
