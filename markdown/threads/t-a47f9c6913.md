"William M. Klein" <wmklein@ix.netcom.com> wrote in message
news:a4rpe7$st3$1@slb1.atl.mindspring.net...

> I do NOT *personally* agree with the "conclusions" - but for those
> interested in the (long lasting - but apparently resolved) issue of
> "processor dependent" vs "obsolete" in the draft of the next COBOL
Standard,
> I would suggest you read the J4 paper at:
>
>  http://people.ne.mediaone.net/doncobol/02-0052.doc

From what I can gather, Bill, this is a "long lasting" issue only to those
who are opposed to the idea of "processor dependency" in the current draft.
The question has been brought up several times in my presence in J4
meetings, and each time the point is raised, it has been pointed out that J4
is producing this draft on behalf of ISO/IEC JTC1 SC22/WG4, that the
appropriateness and correctness of the term "processor dependent" in the
document they are asking J4 to develop has been repeatedly reaffirmed to J4,
and that the correctness of "market position" for a compiler as a
determining factor in processor dependency is likewise repeatedly
reaffirmed.

And the point is not ' "processor dependent" vs "obsolete" '.  There is only
one "optional element" in the Draft Standard:  the Communications Facility
and all the syntax associated with it.  There are, however, elements of the
syntax that are optional to the user -- for example, the ON SIZE ERROR
clause of the COMPUTE statement is optional (to the programmer, not to the
compiler).

The terminology controversy is between "processor dependent element" vs.
"optional element", and whether the same term should be used for both.  I
would argue that it should not.

Whether you call it "processor dependent element" or "blivet element" is not
particularly material to me personally, but at this stage in the development
of the Draft Standard, it is a little late in the game to change it to
something else, and to change the term to make it more ambiguous rather than
less does not strike me as an improvement.

Now, I understand the need for a conforming program compilable by one
vendor's conforming compiler without using any of that vendor's extensions
(which must in any event be flagged) to be compilable on any vendor's
conforming compiler.

Let's take USAGE BINARY as an example.  The current standard requires that
"a radix of 2 is used to represent a numeric item in the storage of the
computer."  in all conformant implementations.  The draft standard places
USAGE BINARY in the "processor dependent" category, because the authors felt
that it was not appropriate either to require that any hardware supporting
COBOL *must* support radix-2 arithmetic, or to require that the implementor
of a conformant compiler provide software simulation of such a feature!  I
would support a compiler implementor providing such a feature *if they
wished to do so*, but I am not at all convinced that a COBOL compiler on a
decimal machine would be worthless without USAGE BINARY!

There are other "processor dependent" features that are arguably more
divorced from the nuts-and-bolts of the Central Processing Unit on which the
program ultimately is to run, and that have been discussed over and over and
over again at both the US and the international standards body level.  I
think what's going to be "in" and what's going to be "out" of the draft
standard is pretty well set by international consensus at this point; we are
down to the final editing for typographical errors preparatory to submission
of the draft for an international ballot.

And, at this point, to pick some names relatively at random, I think the
formal opinion of the standards body of, say, the Netherlands or New Zealand
can reasonably be said to outrank the formal opinion of Bill Klein in this
matter.  J4 is responsible for the *development* of the standard, but
ISO/IEC JTC1/SC22/WG4 claims ultimate responsibility for its content, and I
believe they have spoken, loud and clear, on this issue, to J4.

The document you reference is a discussion defending the existence of
"processor dependent" (call them "blivet elements" if you prefer) in the
Draft Standard.  I strongly support the idea of a compiler implementor
providing all features that *can function* on the target machine *if they
wish to do so*.  I also believe the mandatory elements (like object
orientation and standard arithmetic) are appropriately mandatory for
conformant compilers even if some implementors find them onerous as
requirements.   But I do not believe the presence, or the contents, of this
two-page list represents a catastrophe for the standard.

>
> I have not (yet) seen/read the minutes of the meeting where this paper was
> discussed, but it is my IMPRESSION that this paper DOES accurately reflect
> the view of the MAJORITY (not unanimous) of members of J4 and WG4.

Discussions that do not have concrete outcomes are not typically recorded in
the J4 minutes.  The response in question has been discussed in at least two
meetings at which I was present.  The original text was made public in a
draft recommended response on April 19, 2001, in J4 document 01-0309, in
direct response to US public review comment #52 of the same date, reflected
in J4/01-0306.  This response was discussed at Meeting 230 in Roseville, CA,
at which you were present, and at which TWO straw votes were taken to
reaffirm the current definition of "processor dependency" in the FCD (the
straw vote count was seven in favor of that reaffirmation, one opposed).
The response discussed then has not changed much since then.

While you may not have read the minutes for that meeting, they have been
available on the web in draft form since last May and in final form since
last June.  The response, and its refinement, is reflected in several
editions of the tracking document for US responses since that meeting.  It
was made into a separate document in part because of the sheer length of the
comments that stimulated the response, and in part because a clear
explanation as to why J4 (and from all I can gather WG4) felt it unwise to
change the document in response to the comment required a lengthy answer.

You may not agree with, or even like J4's answer.  I think reflects not only
the consensus of J4 members over the last several meetings but also the
direction I believe J4 has received from the international standards body
for whom J4 is producing the document.   The Draft Standard will stand or
fall as an International Standard on the consensus of participating national
standards bodies in the upcoming months, and I believe J4 and the members
that participate in it have done their level best to provide a document that
will warrant that consensus.

    -Chuck Stevens
