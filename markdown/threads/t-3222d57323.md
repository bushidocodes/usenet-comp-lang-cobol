Hi:

The use of GO TO is universally despised these days. Instead,
people are forced to use the arbitrary 'structured' style.

There is absolutely nothing wrong with GO TO which I use
constantly. (I do not use structured style) At the risk of infuriating
everyone, I would say that structured programming was an invention of
people who didn't know how to program in the first place, it was
not because of anything wrong with GO TO.

People moan about spaghetti code riddled with GO TOs.

However, the author and subsequent maintainers of that spaghetti
code would probably have written equally incomprehensible code
using a structured approach. Structured methods arose becaue
people didn't know how to program; they started a program, they found
out about the things they didn't know when they started to test
and the code just got worse and worse as they attempted to fix each new
bug they encountered.  If the program had been properly designed in
the first place, there would have been no problem.

I get yelled at all the time because I make the distinction between
programming and coding. Programming, to me, is the design of a
solution to a problem, independent of any language, which recognizes
EVERY eventuality and deals with it properly. I guess that COBOL
courses these days deal with coding and syntax and stuff but never
address the design of the logical solution.

I would go further to say that the structured programming style
creates a new set of problems in place of the problems people
had with non-structured programming. It all boils down to whether
one knows how to program or not.

Earlier this year, in another thread, I wondered if anyone drew
flow-charts any more. Someone said, 'No, we just sit down and
start typing'.  If you are confronted with a problem which you
have never faced before, try drawing a flow-chart to solve the
problem and, when you are finished, and you think that it is OK,
code it.

Drawing flow-charts will teach you how to program, once you know
how to program, you don't need structured style.

With apologies in advance. . .

Thanks

TOny Dilworth

In response to this particular query, and I think Don has already
answered it - my answer is a DEFINITE NO ! I wouldn't waste my time on
flowcharting any more than I would on Horizontal Flowcharting. Certainly
as an introductory process to learning, flowcharting can be a great
benefit to students. They are faced with a blank sheet of paper and now
they are challenged to show a design. As a tool it becomes superfluous
as you become more proficient; just like any other skill, does the
trained carpenter go about it the same way as the beginning amateur.

Whether or not you are talking systems analysis or program design - they
should both start the same way. Keyboard idle and you go into 'think
mode' - some of mine occurs down by the river when I'm walking my dogs,
or even when my wife says to me, "Did you hear what I just said !!!!?"

You will appreciate that in systems analysis the problem is even larger
- you are faced with automating/enhancing an existing system, or taking
a topic in the abstract and coming up with a new design. At least in
pure programming terms, your thinking is restricted to one module
(program) in the production process. VARs of course have to do a dual
job.

The simple stuff is a bit like writing the school essay - Beginning,
Middle and End - you can purloin from other programs. Some new,
reasonably or very complex problem comes up, you sit back and give it a
lot of thought. You get ideas, bits and pieces. (I'll use Decision
Tables if necessary to clarify the problem I have in mind), and as Don
said you start coding those pieces, (sub-routines) to see if they work
out in code as you saw them in your mind. Then you test them. In your
case they are PERFORMS or PARAGRAPHS, to me they become OO methods -
same thing.

Having got that squared away in your mind, now you can wrap your
traditional program around those proven/tested complexities.

There is actually nothing wrong with GO TO at all.  It is a simple and
clear statement with a well defined action.  When looking at a piece of
code in a larger context it is always obvious what a GO TO will do, and
why.

The problem actually arises at the other end, the staement label that is
the target of the GO TO.  When examining a piece of code in a larger
context and there is a label (paragraph name) then it is not obvious how
the logic flows in, around or through it.  That label could be the
target of a GO TO, a PERFORM, or the code could drop through from above
or a PERFORM could terminate on reaching that label.

One solution to the problem is to maintain a complete and detailed flow
chart do that the flow of logic around that label can be determined. 
Another way is to examine the whole program attempting to find how the
logic arrives or leaves that label.  The only safe way to maintain the
program is by _knowing_ all possible paths.

Those that developed programs using detailed and expansive flow charts a
few decades ago have learnt to tame the label problem and may not see
the 'point' of changing to something that is easier to deal with.  In
fact they can deal with these types of programs because they are used to
drawing new lines on their flowcharts.  Show them a structured program
and their brain would seize from being unable to just draw a line from
where they are to somewhere else without the program collapsing.

But this all arises from the problem that the label causes, and the ways
of getting to it.  It labels are constrained so that some actual rules
apply to them then one has assurance about what happens at any label.

For example if one adds the constraint that the only use for a paragraph
label is a PERFORM with no THRU, and that SECTIONs are never to be
referenced then when one looks at an isolated piece of code on is
_assured_ how the logic flows through it.  While it may then be nice to
have a general appreciation of what the overall program flow is there is
no longer any need for detailed descriptions such as flow charts, they
can be completely discarded.  Every piece of code can be isolated,
changed, moved, replicated, without breaking the whole program.

However these benefits are not gained until one has _fully_ converted to
structured GOTOless programs.  Half hearted attempts at trying to mix
semi-structured styles, such as allowing GO TO an exit paragraph do not
gain the benefits of fully GOTO less programs because there remains a
large overhead in creating the structure and maintaining its integrity
(such as ensuring that the GO TO is to the correct Exit).  While some of
this integrity can be automated (ref Judson) this requires the
imposition of fixed elements of style, such as naming or numbering of
paragraphs which introduces its own overheads.

Any style that has GO TOs reduces the opportunities for code reuse, or
introduces overheads in doing this.  The whole point of structured is to
allow reuse and thus savings in rewriting.  A flow-charted program makes
it almost impossible to reuse code, or so expensive that it is not
worthwhile.  The proponents of this style have probably never tried to
reuse code (though they may recycle the flow-charts) and so are unaware
that these benefits may exist.  When they try to change to a structured
style they simply get frustrated that their established techniques don't
work, can't work and thus claim that structured (or later OOP) are
worthless.  I know this - been there done that, bit the bullet.  Now I
know that I have to do it again to get to OOP.

In respect of labels, other languages have developed _different_ types
of labels for different purposes.  In C, eg, you cannot goto a function
or call a label.  One is assured that flow is contained within the
structure in a rational way.

Which can be done with PERFORMs too, with just as disasterous results.

> If you use GO TOs sensibly,

> nested logic sections, there's nothing wrong with them.

You are halfway there.  You see the benefits of having 'routines' but
you implement them as PERFORMed sections with a GO TO Exit.  This does
not fully reap the benefits of structured code and you will probably get
very frustrated with OOP.  The way forward is to dump the GO TO Exit
style, only perform paragraphs (not sections, no through), then dump all
those numbering schemes which were only required to give you control
over the sections.

Once you have got over the frustration of not being able to GOTO out and
discovered that you can just break down paragraphs in a free flowing way
you will be ready to look at OOP.  Until then you will probably think
that OOP is just another hype, just as 'flow-chart' programmers think
that structured is just hype with no benefits.

That depends on the context of the statement and the style required at the
site.  If the imperative is predicated by a condition then a period would
alter the flow of executed statements:

IF (cond-1)
    PERFORM (paragraph/section)
    COMPUTE (variable = formula)
    PERFORM (paragraph/section)

... will *not* necessarily execute in the same manner as:

IF (cond-1)
    PERFORM (paragraph/section)
    COMPUTE (variable = formula).   <== period/full stop/dot
    PERFORM (paragraph/section)

... as the punctuation mark removes the second PERFORM's predication by
the IF.

be one-and-only-one '.' per paragraph, e.g.

PARA-1.

Murphy's Law     prov.     The correct, original Murphy's Law reads:
"If there are two or more ways to do something, and one of those
ways can result in a catastrophe, then someone will do it." This is
a principle of defensive design, cited here because it is usually
given in mutant forms less descriptive of the challenges of design
for lusers. For example, you don't make a two-pin plug symmetrical
and then label it `THIS WAY UP'; if it matters which way it is plugged
in, then you make the design asymmetrical (see also the anecdote
under magic smoke).

Edward A. Murphy, Jr. was one of the engineers on the rocket-sled
experiments that were done by the U.S. Air Force in 1949 to test
human acceleration tolerances (USAF project MX981). One
experiment involved a set of 16 accelerometers mounted to different
parts of the subject's body. There were two ways each sensor could
be glued to its mount, and somebody methodically installed all 16 the
wrong way around. Murphy then made the original form of his
pronouncement, which the test subject (Major John Paul Stapp)
quoted at a news conference a few days later.

Within months `Murphy's Law' had spread to various technical cultures
connected to aerospace engineering. Before too many years had
gone by variants had passed into the popular imagination, changing
as they went. Most of these are variants on "Anything that can go
wrong, will"; this is correctly referred to as Finagle's Law. The
memetic drift apparent in these mutants clearly demonstrates
Murphy's Law acting on itself!
