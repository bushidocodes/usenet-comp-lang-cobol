This looks like a truncation problem. You seem to be indicating that the
original value is stored as a single precision floating point numeric data item
in VB. The value 4.2 actually can not be recorded exactly in binary. The .2 is
1/5 which in binary is a continuous fraction.

The value stored can be very nearly correct and as long as you stay within one
language discipline storage and retrieval will present the value approximately
correctly, if favorable rules of rounding are in force.

It seems that you may have the effect of a truncation when you are moving to
the comp-3 (BCD) field.  I am fairly confident that CICS is not doing this, but
confess ignorance about your COMTI. Most likely candidate is VB, as the
truncator. 

You may be able to influence conversions with compile (or runtime) parameters,
but the scope would be broad, possibly fixing this field and damaging others.

You may need to wrap the specific field with rounding functions.

It is not obvious that this is a bug, maybe you should just look at it as an
inconvnience that has lots of ramifications.

Not being familiar with the objectives of the specific application, one can not
criticize your data definitions.  But it is worth mentioning, that if you are
sensitive to the difference between 4.1 and 4.2, you MAY need to preserve
atleast another digit of precision.  A COBOL definition of 9(x).9 instructs the
computer to ignore hundredths. Yet you are telling us you care about the effect
in data format conversion of hundredths.

There are two basic solutions at the field definition level. Code the other
digit in the interfaces, as in 9(x).99, or multiply the number by ten before
shipping it about, and then demodulate it at the last moment before
presentation by dividing by ten, (perhaps in COBOL with exact control per field
with ROUNDING phrases).

You can combine this with compile (runtime) parameter controls to get the
overall effect that you want. But from what you have presented this does not
look like a bug or a strange thing. As you become accustom to it you will
recognize that somewhere in this mix you have actually instructed the
compilation tools to truncate. 

The value 1/5 can not be represented exactly in binary. Same effect in 4.2, 8.2
and 12.2, ad infinitum.

There are many such problems. I can see with the mix of software that you are
no novice; sometimes newbies are amazed at this simple difficulty, and
sometimes we each have to be reminded.  Generally, you should carry around a
bit more precision (more digits) then you think you need, to compensate for
rounding/truncation problems. When problems arise still, you should isolate
them and apply the parms or clauses that fix the specific behavior.

Can you state exactly where the single precision data item gets converted to
BCD, and by what software?  Can you inform us of the parameters you are using
to control rounding?

I think we all agree that tools have to be selected and used in a manner that
retains the precision required by the application.  Yet I do not fully agree
with the poster that the problem decribed at the origin of this thread nor the
technical issues engaged when trying to hold fractions in various formats leads
necessarily to the conclusion that VB is not good at the front end (in GUI
programs).

I have used BASIC for many dollar-and-cents, and engineering factor fields
holding non integral values. BASIC works fine for this. You just have to be
aware of the internal technology. 
Such data can easily be stored in integer fields by multiplying and dividing by
appropriate powers of 10.

Engineering calcs are very sensitive to initial values, and it is both very
important to preserve the exact original input and to exploit floating point
technology to enable large scale interation during which ranges can vary
dramatically (that is, beyond the integer field facility).  In short you
sometimes have to move data back and forth from integer to floating point
fields.

This is not unlike what the COBOL compiler does for us automatically when it
converts a display numeric filed to integer or packed for efficient calc, and
then automatically converts it back to display for us.  In BASIC you need to do
some of the house keeping yourself. Which doesn't of itself argue conclusively
against BASIC.

BASIC has many advantages. In Windows contexts BASIC, like Perl in UNIX, allows
for rapid deployment, ease of transfer to the next programmer, and direct
simple hooks to the presentation managers.

The importance of this is that you can get a show to the user much quicker and
keep projects on track with earlier reviews, compared to COBOL. Speaking in
economic terms, you can also use a different class of worker.

As relative processing requirements grow, the stronger discipline of  COBOL,
though slower in deployment, gains comparative advantage in project control. So
it is not surprising to see BASIC up front, and COBOL in the core.

It is no more difficult to understand precision loss in a movement from single
precision floating point to integer, than it is to understand the loss of
precision in a movement from a PIC 9(n).99(2) to a PIC 9(n).9(1). We just have
to pay attention to the tools.

Also, I might add that the mainframes are finally coming around to the PC IEEE
floating point format in hardware. That will coerce COBOL into a more standard
floating point computational type.  At which point the roosters come home to
COBOL as a GUI deployment toolset.

When you move data from a field of one type to a field of another type, you
must be conscious of the potential for loss of information (precision, sign,
justification, editing).  It does not matter what tools you are using, it is
the technology implicit in the field type specifications that counts. 

What does happen to some users of BASIC in their early experience, is that they
assume that a non integer must be stored in either a single or double precision
number format.  Not really.  You can get these things into integer format
without much trouble, and it is simply required when you are sensitive to
specific limitations of floating point format.

(On some occassion you can also preserve precision by carrying data as
character).

In quite a few situations storing the number in single or double precision
format preseves enough information. It depends on the application specifics. 
But if you have a challenge with a few fields, that is not enough reason to
abandon BASIC as the tool of choice. In many situations it has economic
advantages.

The only floating point values which can be represented with 100% accuracy are
equal to an integer (positive or negative), the mantissa, multiplied by 2 to an
integer(+/-) power (exponent).  It may not be represented that way internally, but
could be derived if necessary.  For example 48 could be expressed as 11 (binary) *
2**4 but may be 0.110000 (binary) * 2**6 in the computer.

Since only a finite number of digits are available for the mantissa and exponent,
only a finite number of floating point numbers can be represented on a computer.
For numbers such as 1/3 or pi, something close is all that can be done.
