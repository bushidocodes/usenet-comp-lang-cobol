Correct. Most COBOL code is, however, coded in a business environment
where a n organisation pays us programmers to write and maintain the
code. As David said, none of these businesses will release their code
into the public domain because a massive proportion of it could reveal
trade secrets to their competitors.

After all, the programs merely reflect the business process as carried
out in the particular business. Inventory, database functions and the
like are part and parcel of corporate applications which are written in
COBOL and other languages. So, again, the software is part of trade
secrets.

We all sign contracts and confidentiality agreements to promise not to
reveal anything of what we learn about anything to do with our
employer's or client's business to any third party.

In some ways, this is a sad thing because you'll just have to put up
with the generally crappy examples that academics dream up rather than
what goes on in the real world. It should, however, be understandable
that we cannot put real examples of COBOL source code into the public
domain because we, the professionals, would lose our jobs.

It's also usual for us, when we get off-site, to try to forget about
work and indulge our other interests. Some employers even stipulate
that _any_ code produced by their employees is the intellectual property
of the employer - regardless of when and were it was produced - for the
duration of the employment.

A lot of do not, therefore, have any great incentive to produce COBOL
code outside of our workplace.

Charles
