>Try the ANSI COBOL 85 standards manual.

Or X3.23-1985 as it's know to its friends.  Or ISO 1989:1985.

But Jud, he asked for *succinct*.  The COBOL standard has an organization
which leads to redundancy, and it is also verbose and sesquipedalian.
Anyone programmer who can hack his way thru X3.23-1985 will be better for
it, but he'll also profit to read the mainframe programmer reference
manuals, which are far more accessible to the common reader.  I think the
best one I read was IBM COBOL for OS/VS Release 2.  Release 2 was
Cobol-74, release 1 was Cobol-68, and there was a world of difference in
the writing of those manuals.  IBM discovered tech writing in between.  I
liked all the IBM manuals from the mid to late 1970s.

The draft of COBOL-200x is better than X3.23-1985 in nearly every respect,
but what it lost in redundancy, it has gained and then some in features.
Unfortunately, the language standards (especially COBOL) are not written
for the programmer; they are written for the implementor.  This is just an
inherent part of the ANSI/ISO process.  It makes sense for everything but
computers.  The screw-thread standard is written for the people who make
the nuts and bolts, not the people who use the nuts and bolts.  To milk an
old analogy, a race car driver may know every bolt in his car, but does he
know the thread angles?  (A good race driver knows a machinist who knows
that answer.)

But with a language standard this isn't the case.  Absolutely every point
that is relevant to the compiler writer is also relevant to the compiler
user.
