"15 U.S.C. 2: Monopolizing trade a felony; penalty

"Every person who shall monopolize, or attempt to monopolize,
or combine or conspire with any other person or persons,
to monopolize any part of the trade or commerce among the
several States, or with foreign nations, shall be deemed guilty
of a felony, and, on conviction thereof, shall be punished by
fine not exceeding $10,000,000 if a corporation, or, if any other

or by both said punishments, in the discretion of the court."

A 'monopoly' is a condition of status. A monopoly cannot be illegal or
legal: it just is.

The Act cited pertains to 'actions' not status. Further, only certain
actions tending toward a monopoly are illegal, others not. For example, the
Constitution protects certain acts in furtherance of a monopoly (trademarks
and patents). It is not the end result (monopoly) that determines whether
someone violated of the Sherman Act - the violation occurs totally based on
the path taken.

Let me say it again: whether the actions in question lead to a monopoly is
irrelevant; the main determinant is whether the actions were designed and
carried out with eventual monopoly status as a goal. Of course, if a
monopoly results from the actions, a reasonable person may presume intent.
BUT, if it can be shown that a company reached it's monopoly status by legal
means (such as internal growth or the protected method of patent), the
company is blameless. There is no crime in just BEING a monopoly.

In Microsoft's situation, since they already WERE a monopoly, and since
there's no penalty for acting to PRESERVE a monopoly position, the case can
be made there was no foul.
