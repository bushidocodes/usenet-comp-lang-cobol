```
This is about the 2002 COBOL Standard, so I hope y'all don't mind my
bringing up here. <g>

I am confused by the following rule,

COPY statement, 7.1.2.2 Syntax rules, "11) The length of a text-word within
pseudo-text and within library text shall be from 1 through 322 character
positions."

A careful reading of the preceding section states that a 'text-word' may
be a literal. "7.1.1.4 Text-words, 2) an alphanumeric, boolean, or
national literal including the opening and closing delimiters that bound
the literal;".

An alphanumeric literal is limited to 160 characters.
    Alphanumeric literals, 8.3.1.2.1.2 Syntax rules, "1) The length of an
    alphanumeric literal, excluding the separators that delimit the literal,
    shall be greater than zero and less than or equal to 160 alphanumeric
    character positions."

A boolean literal is, likewise, limited to 160 characters.
    Boolean literals, 8.3.1.2.3.2 Syntax rules, "1) The length of a boolean
    literal, excluding the separators that delimit the literal, shall be
    greater than zero and less than or equal to 160 boolean character
    positions."

A national literal is also limited to 160 characters.
    National literals, 8.3.1.2.4.2 Syntax rules, "1) The length
    of a national literal, excluding the separators that delimit the literal,
    shall be greater than zero and less than or equal to 160 national character
    positions."

In each of these cases the total, with delimiters, is 162 characters; so,
where does the "322 character positions" come from?


But, I also noticed another potential problem.

COPY statement, 7.1.2.3 General rules, "10) ... c) ... 3. Except when used
in the non-hexadecimal formats of alphanumeric and national literals, each
alphanumeric character is equivalent to its corresponding national character
and each lowercase letter is equivalent to its corresponding uppercase letter,
as specified for the COBOL character repertoire in 8.1.2, COBOL character
repertoire."

Notice that, "lowercase letter is ... uppercase".

However, in COBOL character repertoire, 8.1.2.1 General rules, "3) ...
a) Except when used in the non-hexadecimal formats of alphanumeric and
national literals, each uppercase COBOL basic letter is equivalent to its
corresponding lowercase COBOL basic letter, if any." And, "4) ... c) An
uppercase extended letter is treated as though it were folded to its
corresponding lowercase extended letter in accordance with the case mapping
described in Annex D, if any."

Notice that, in rules 3 and 4, "uppercase ... letter is ... lowercase".

Is the wording in the COPY statement in error? Or, is it intended to be
the opposite of everywhere else in the standard?

The issued Technical Corrigenda (1, 2, and 3) do not address this discrepancy
and I have no interest in submitting an interpretation request.
```

I agree generally, but I think the standard is trying to
define how to achieve the equivalence. The standard is,
after all, "a precise specification".[1]

For the COPY statement, the rule says, in effect, that
x'abcdef' is equivalent to x'ABCDEF' and nx'abcdef' is
equivalent to nx'ABCDEF'. But the rule in character repertoire
says the opposite and neither rule addresses hexadecimal
boolean literals, that is, bx'abcdef' and bx'ABCDEF' are
not covered by a rule for equivalence.

For non-hexadecimal literals, 'ABC' is not equivalent to
'abc', nor is n'ABC' equivalent to n'abc'.

For other text words the undelimited XYZ is equivalent to
the undelimited xyz due to folding to lowercase.

At least that is where I am now in trying to understand.

COBOL character repertoire, 8.1.2.1 General rules, 3),
-----
a) COBOL basic letters, when specified in the non-hexadecimal formats
 of alphanumeric and national literals, are treated in a case-sensitive
 manner, unless specific rules require otherwise. COBOL basic letters
 appearing elsewhere within the compilation group are treated in a
 case-insensitive manner.

This change says, in effect, that boolean-hexadecimal literals
"are treated in a case-insensitive manner." Or, that b"a" is
equivalent to b"A"; but does not require the equivalence to
be established by correspondence to lowercase.

> Or, is it intended to be

No, the wording used in COBOL character repertoire was in error
and has been corrected in the proposed new standard. It is
possible that a correction to the current standard will appear
in Technical Corrigendum 4, whenever that may be issued.
