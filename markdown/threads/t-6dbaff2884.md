In the US, an employer must report the salary paid to an employee to the
IRS on a W-2 form. A person who pays money to someone who is not an
employee must report that payment to the IRS on a 1099 form. There is a
great deal of common-law and statutory baggage attaching to the word
"employee", and it would take thousands of pages to give an accurate
answer, but the following may begin to give you an idea:

An employer who hires an employee must withhold federal, state, and local
taxes from the employee's salary. What's left is "take-home pay". In
addition to the withheld taxes, the employer must pay federal, state, and
local taxes levied on the amount of the employee's salary. The employer
must remit all withheld taxes to the three governments at specified
intervals, along with the federal, state, and local payroll taxes owed,
or face severe penalties. When an employee submits an expense account
and is reimbursed, the reimbursal is not income and is not reported to
the IRS. At the end of the year, the W-2 reports the total withheld
taxes remitted to the government by the employer, along with the total
take-home pay of the employee.

Now let us suppose you are so unfortunate as to be sued. You retain a
lawyer to defend you, and he submits a bill for $7654.32 labor and
$2345.67 expenses. You do not withhold any taxes and remit them to the
government, nor do you owe any additional taxes on the amount of the
bill; you simply send him a check for the full $9999.99 and report the
full $9999.99 to the IRS on a 1099. The $2345.67 in expenses is reported
as income to the recipient and in order to deduct them he must
substantiate them to the IRS, not to you. He must compute and remit
federal, state, and local estimated tax or face severe penalties. This
is the essence of the 1099: no matter how hard the lawyer worked for you,
he is not your employee. You do not give your lawyer paid holidays; he
must take them for himself out of his earnings. You do not give your
lawyer health insurance; he must pay for it himself out of his earnings.
You do not give your lawyer anything except, of course, the $9999.99. He
works for himself.

When choosing whether you want to be an employee on a W-2 or go into
business for yourself on a 1099, you must carefully evaluate the extra
expenses involved in working for yourself before comparing the 1099 rate
with the W-2 rate. Most importantly, don't overlook the 15.3%
Self-Employment Tax!

The following information is from tax year 1996 because I'm too lazy to
go and find out about 1997.

The Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA) taxes consist of 6.2%
Social Security Tax plus 1.45% Medicare Tax. These rates increase from
time to time but the law must be amended in order to raise the rates.

For the Social Security Tax, your employer withholds 6.2% of the first
$62,700 that you earn, or $3,887.40. The rest of your earnings are not
subject to this tax. That's why your take-home pay goes up at the end of
the summer if you're on a W-2. The maximum amount subject to the Social
Security Tax goes up automatically every year without having to amend the
law.

For the Medicare Tax, your employer withholds 1.45% of your earnings.
There is no limit to the Medicare Tax.

The sum of these withholdings is remitted to the government and reported
in the FICA box on your W-2.

Your employer also pays Social Security Tax and Medicare Tax equal to the
withheld amount, plus Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA) tax of 6.2% of
the first $7,000 that you earn, or $434.00. These are reported on Form
940, which you don't see.

When you work for yourself, you must pay both the employer and the
employee taxes. That is, your Self-Employment Tax is 12.4% of the first
$62,700 that you earn, or $7,774.80, plus 2.9% of all of your earnings.
The self-employed are not (yet) liable for FUTA.

In order to be eligible for a Social Security benefit, you must have FICA
withheld in at least ten calendar quarters, or pay Self-Employment Tax in
at least ten quarterly Estimated Tax payments, or any combination of
these. Therefore, if you work in the US for less than two and a half
years over your lifetime, you get back nothing. The benefit amount is
determined by the amount of tax paid, with strongly diminishing returns.

An employer deducts all the payroll taxes he pays, and as the "employer"
of yourself, you deduct your "employer's" half of your Self-Employment
Tax on Line 25 of Form 1040.

W-2 and 1099 are the U.S. Federal tax forms on which income is reported
to employees and independent contractors respectively. A W-2 contract
position is one in which you become an employee of a contracting firm,
from which you receive your income. A 1099 contract position is one in
which you are an independent contractor, receiving your income directly
from the client. W-2 positions typically include a benefit package such
as paid vacations, retirement, health insurance, and so on -- all those
nice things that independent contractors don't get. Another significant
difference is that the effective tax rate is much higher on independent
contractors (1099) than it is on employees (W-2).
