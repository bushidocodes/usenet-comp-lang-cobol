Reports! Reports! Reports! So many places buy vendor systems these
days, that sometimes that's all a programmer will get to do (when
management learns that the vendor really ~didn't~ have one for every
need, and will charge 100 bucks an hour to write one- surprise,
surprise).

Control breaks. Summary pages collecting all the sub-totals onto one
page at the end. Things that combine information from multiple
files. (Grade them well if the report is are nicely centered, spread
across the page, and have the date and time in the header. Execs are
not impressed by the ones that are all squished into the left upper
corner....)

Form alignment is good. (We always stick a newbie with the 1099 tax
forms.) So is the string assignment - I've attained limited fame in
my office now and then for simply knowing string and unstring. I have
no idea why.

One thing that I notice seems to confuse almost all new programmers is
the difference between sequential, random and dynamic reads. File
I/O in general seems to be skimped on in classes - I know it was in
mine. (We mostly had to input our own data. On cards. Dark ages.)
We heard about indexed files, but never accessed one.

If you want to give your students something realistic, and if you can
do it in your school environment, have them copy their FD's from
files outside of the program, and have them put together a header
record and one or more detail records from indexed files you provide.
You could do that with the payroll program, with the basic employee
information in the header file, and the separate types of pay in the
detail - i.e.
1) employee number, name, ssn, address, department, store, job code,
hourly rate, salary or hourly, accumulated time off (9.9 hours per
bi-weekly pay period)
2) employee number, pay date, an array with the type of time,
(regular, overtime, paid time off), number of hours.

Extra points for remembering without being told to adjust the time off
hours when some get used, and displaying the adjusted amount.

A header / detail pair from my world (health insurance) might be:
1) a claim record - claim number, insured's number and name, patient's
name, date of service, Doctor's name and state license number, and ;
2) detail lines for each billable procedure for that visit - Claim
number, line-item sequence, what was done, amount billed by the
doctor, amount paid by insurance, amount that is patient's
responsibility.

Joellen Bloe took Junior to the doctor for a regular exam. They're
on 80% paid coverage for doctor visits. The doctor (1) did the exam
(medium office visit), (2) did a blood test. (3) cleaned a wound
(skinned knee) and (4) extracted a foreign object (green beans) from
junior's nose.

Joellen herself had an chat with the doctor - (1) brief office visit.
(diagnosis - stress-related Migraine headaches.)
The doctor did not bill for the cleaning and bandaging of his own
finger where Junior bit him.

I dunno, maybe this is more complex that you want for your students.
On the other hand, maybe you do this already. But this is about the
type of basic stuff that we would expect a new-hire to be able to
handle without too much supervision. (It's a grosso-mundo
simplification of the type and number of files they would be looking
at for the information, however.)

Good luck to you and them, HTH
Sak

you missed the point about OO. As an evolving language, any new
version of COBOL must also support the old versions. So any compiler
that supports ANSI '85 will be default support ANSI '74. Which means
that if the MF and IBM compilers support parts of the emerging
standand then they must also support the old stuff.
In fact the MF compiler is probably the most flexable compiler of any
language available today, supporting:
74, 85, OSVS, VSC2(2), VSC2(3), VSC2(4), SAA(1), SAA(2), Xopen, MF1,

So given that the MF stuff can do all of the old COBOL and all of the
new, why cripple students by only training them in the old?
