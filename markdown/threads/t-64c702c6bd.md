The whole syntax?  I assume you mean record layout/organization?  I'll also
assume you're talking about an IBM 3480 or related tape, since "standard
labels" are found there most often.

I can't remember the whole thing, but here goes . . .  (I'll omit discussion
of Tape Marks)

The first thing on an SL tape is the 6 byte volume serial number.  This
field should have the same value as the physical label on the outside of the
tape.
The second thing is the 80 byte Header One record.
The third thing is the 80 byte Header Two record.

These are collectively considered the "tape label", although there is more.

The last thing on a tape will either be an End of File or an End of Volume
record.  End of File for the end of a dataset, unless that dataset is the
last dataset on a tape (there can be more than one dataset on one tape, and
there can be more than one dataset on more than one tape; i.e. huge dataset
1 goes into four and half tapes, and dataset 2 goes onto the last half of
tape five and then onto a couple of more tapes, etc.), in which case you get
End of Volume.  On top of this, there is an End of Volume at the end of
every tape in a multi-volume dataset (i.e. dataset 1 from above gets an EOV
label at the end of tape 1, tape 2, tape 3, tape 4, and an EOF half way
through tape 5 where dataset two begins; thereafter, dataset 2 gets an EOV
label at the end of tape 5, tape 6, and tape 7, where it ends and leave an
EOV label because it is the last dataset on the tape).

EOF labels and EOV labels come in sets just like the Header labels.  There
is an EOF 1 and an EOF 2, and an EOV 1 and EOV 2, and they are always found
in pairs, just like Header labels.

The Header labels of successive volumes in a multi-volume dataset contain
information about the immediately previous tape in the series.  The volser
field at the beginning of successive volumes in a multi-volume dataset does
not change (that is to say, it sold have the same value as the physical
label on the cartridge).


If you need the exact record formats, I can look up where to find them and
tell you, or photocopy a complete description if you don't have access to
that information.  Email me if you need them.

See
http://www2.s390.ibm.com:80/bookmgr-cgi/bookmgr.cmd/BOOKS/DGT1M302/CCONTENTS?SHELF=DGT1BK23
for current version of "IBM DFSMS Using Magnetic Tapes", specifically
Chapter 2 on IBM Standard Labels and Chapter 3 on ISO/ANSI Standard
Labels.

Reasonable operating systems designed for commercial use automatically
create standard labels on output tapes and analyze standard labels on
input tapes to improve the reliability of data interchange via tape. 
When receiving a tape from a non-technically oriented user of another
installation, any information provided about the physical format of the
tape may be more wishful intent rather than actuality.  If the tape uses
standard labels, these provide the information needed to verify you have
the correct data set and to correctly access the logical records on the
tape, in spite of misinformation provided by its creator.  Correct
interpretation of fields within a record may still be a problem, but at
least you can get to the records.
