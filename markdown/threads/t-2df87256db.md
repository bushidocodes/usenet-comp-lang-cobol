This is to Richard:I don't see why this is a problem, let alone a major one. What's the difference between sections and
paragraphs here?. At most the compiler has some more problems in COBOL than in other languages: in COBOL the end of a
section is denoted by the beginning of another one or the end of a higher construct (the end of the linkage section is
denoted by the start of the procedure division f.e.) The same goes for paragraphs: they end where the next one starts or
where a section or other higher type of construct starts.
So two things:
* 1 * The end of a section is as much or as little a problem as the end of a section.
* 2 * As soon as the compiler knows the end, it has to do some "end processing", eventually resulting in some
return-to-caller-code, perhaps conditionally executed etc. My point is that this "end processing" is of the same kind as
in any compiler (any language) when an end of some unit is reached. (Like the } in C/C++, END in pl/1, pascal etc.) Only
the syntax parsing part of the compiler is a bit complexer in COBOL than it other languages. (Personally I suspect that a
lot of the simplicitiy in languages other than COBOL  is not built in for convenience of the programmer, but for the
compiler builders).

> In some implementations, especially older ones, SECTIONS were
> used for overlaying, I still have some programs that are
> overlayed on DOS.

Of course, in COBOL, sections were introduced as an extra divisional layer for the procedure division, to be used in
memory management. The syntactical possibility to add segmenting numbers etc. shows that. Even without explicit segmenting
it gave the compiler builders a way to segment the resulting code anyway. Lots of compilers used that, f.e. IBM
(DSECT/CSECT). I am almost sure that the developers of the COBOL standard had all this in mind when they choosed for
sections and paragraphs, besides their goal to mimic plain english and book-like structures. I would not have surprised
when they had invented CHAPTERs also :)
