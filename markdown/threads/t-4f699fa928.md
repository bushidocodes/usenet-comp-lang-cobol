A number of higher level managers have not yet seen that the market has
improved. There are more opennings, longer contracts, higher posted rates, and
more project management postions at this time indicating that the projects are
larger than during the worst of the recession.

Lower level decision makers are trapped between a market that is moving up and
away, and high level managers that are looking backward.

IMHO the current rebound looks like a sustained, modest, but significant
improvement. 
Rates are not going down at this time.

The upper midwest may well be the last to get the pulse, but honestly it is
hitting now in the Ohio Valley, and so there is not much left.

And for the big operations the mainframe talent base just relentlessly attrits.
Major corporations would be unwise to place there significant outsourced
resources in jeopardy at this time.

Hidden beneath the recession is the fact that a large portion of mainframers
now have acquired dual skillsets. Some people, after all, have had a lot of
time on their hands, and these are amongst the most educable.  If the boom
happens bigger in retail first, the net effect will simply be a flow of workers
away from the industrial information applications towards the applications
using the newer technologies.

As the recession eases, the major operations are at considerable risk of
loosing technical talent. And the switch to the newer hardware and software
technologies may leave the big guys with very little to choose from.

Intelligent mid and upper level managers should now be looking towards
substantial extensions to slow the talent loss which is inevitable.
