project with some affection. The hardest part for me personally, (and the 
most rewarding) was ensuring that the 21 people on my Legacy team were 
re-absorbed into the organization or retrained for the new Siebel system. 
All but 2 were salvaged and they didn't want to do the 20 mile commute that 
would have been necessary to keep their jobs.  I spent a lot of time 
lobbying managers to pick up the Legacy team people (who, of course, all had 
really good business knowledge). A senior management decision had been taken 
that Siebel would decide who worked on the new system and they apparently 
didn't want anyone with knowledge of the old system.I never understood the 
reason for that and, in the end, I managed to get 2 of the team onto the new 
project with suitable training.

It is a real managment challenge to keep people motivated and interested in 
coming to work when you are not allowed to spend any money on the "old" 
system and everybody knows their future is uncertain...Nevertheless, it was 
achieved and they threw an amazing party for me (which I still remember with 
fondness) when the project was completed.

The best way to handle the stagnation is by paying contractors to do it.  (I 
was brought in with the brief to "keep legacy running until we cut over", on 
the basis that I had done some previous successful projects at the same 
company.) I found a de-motivated and disspirited team who had suffered from 
some poor management, and were generally perceived by the Busness as "the 
Enemy" :-). When I left, their KPIs were the highest they had ever been and 
their perception in the company was completely changed.  (In fact, some of 
the Business managers were questioning why the Siebel system was being 
implemented when the current IT service was now so good...)

I honestly don't know how successful the new Siebel system was but it cost 
24 million and the IT budget for the Legacy system was around 9 million per 
year.

(I should add that the Legacy was NOT a COBOL system; it was all SQL 
procedures. There were some very talented people working on it.)

Universities CREATE media hype. (Well, to be fair, the media keep an eye on 
what is happening in Universities because it is often newsworthy.)

Nobody moved off COBOL because of hype. An entrenched community who had 
become reactionary and arrogant refused to change or adopt to a new 
paradigm, believing that the way they had always done things was the only 
way.

In it's heyday (60s through to end of the 70s) COBOL WAS the only game in 
town and "computer programming" was a mysterious Black Art that the general 
public regarded with awe and suspicion. The Priests Of COBOL protected their 
turf and new approaches were generally dismissed and ridiculed. Nobody 
believed there was any need to change. Nobody believed that anyone else 
(outside the Priesthood) could know anything about computer programming. 
Anything new was evaluated in terms of COBOL and immediately shown to be 
lacking. I saw it time and again across sites, countries, and continents; 
and know whereof  I speak.

But the rest of the world wasn't in the Priesthood. They weren't even True 
Believers. They just wanted to be able to make computers do what they 
wanted.

IBM (which was the major Faith at the time) shot itself in the foot by 
inventing a very cheap personal computer that was within the reach of 
everyone. It very nearly cost them the company.  You can clearly trace the 
decline of COBOL from the arrival of the PC in the early 1980s. A PC on its 
own was fun but not really a serious contender for commercial processing 
(and they were mainly programmed in BASIC at the time, which was still 
interpreted rather than compiled.) I can remember a certain programmer 
sniffing when he heard I had obtained a Commodore Pet: "Don't know why you 
waste your time with toy computers, you have access to the real thing..." 
(He gave up on me completely when I showed him my Sinclair ZX Spectrum 
playing casino Blackjack a few years later :-))

But as soon as you started connecting them, something magical happened. The 
synergy obtained was far more than anyone had expected. The Military 
(ARPANet), Academia (JANET) and Commerce (CyberNet, Dial 360...) all started 
playing with networked computers and it had impact on the way applications 
were developed. A language intended for a centralized processor with 
complete  sequential control of all operations (like COBOL) wasn't good 
enough. Hardware was advancing in huge leaps and it wasn't going to sit 
around waiting for software to attend to it. A new term started to be 
heard... "interrupt driven". Instead of software controlling hardware, the 
hardware simply demanded attention and expected softwere to service it. This 
was a completely new paradigm to COBOL programmers, who shrugged and 
dismissed it as "unnecessary".

But Networking wasn't going to go away and a new programming paradigm was 
invented to support it: Object Orientation. Encapsulated chunks of 
intelligent code which were not dependent on a single centralized processor, 
could be activated by interrupts, and could be transferred  as "objects" 
across systems to run where required in real time, started showing that 
applications didn't have to be huge, monolithic, core images compiled from 
thousands of lines of code. Functionality could be "distributed" and reused. 
The concept of component based applications evolved and Object Orientation 
was the perfect way to implement it.

COBOL was upgraded to support OO but most of the community were not 
interested and couldn't see the point. "It's just Modular Programming 
re-invented...".  It wasn't.  I tried on numerous occasions here to get 
people to recognise the subtle differences that had such HUGE implications 
(ITSA and ITSLIKE), and finally gave it up.

Java arrived. It was regarded with hostility by COBOL people who simply saw 
it taking their jobs. When Java projects failed there was glee in the COBOL 
camp. (When COBOL projects failed it was the fault of Management...) Again, 
I said that COBOL people should expand their skill sets to include Java. 
Some did and are still employed today; others didn't, and aren't.

Coming back to your point, Steve: NOBODY in Academia believed that the 
existing investment in COBOL code would be replaced with the "Language du 
Jour". People in Academia, simply DON'T CARE about that; it is the preserve 
of Commerce to decide what they will do about it. Academia is more concerned 
with developing and evolving better approaches, not with what happens to the 
old ones.

The famous "Billions of Lines" claimed by Gartner (which they subsequently 
revised and which they no longer quote) HAS been eroded by languages like 
Java, C#, VB.Net, and even C++. But, more importantly, scripting languages 
like Python and Ruby are cutting into what was once the preserve of COBOL. 
ALL of the modern languages are completely comfortable with the OO paradigm 
and deployment on the Network. COBOL just isn't.

Over a period of years the World's existing COBOL codebase WILL be 
refactored, replaced, and discarded. (the reasons I am confident of this 
would take a completely separate article, but believe it I do...I should 
also note in passing that I am currently employed in making this happen, so 
I WOULD say that :-))

for something better. The applications which are perfectly adequate today, 
may not always be. If the industry you are working in is competitive change 
is often driven by competitive advantage. If the competitor shows they can 
respond to market opportunities more quickly  (all they have to do is write 
a script), while your company has to prepare a spec get it reviewed, have it 
programmed, debugged, and acceptance tested, along with all the regression 
testing for things that might have been affected....well, you catch my 
drift. COBOL is not just a Language "problem" it is a system development 
methodology problem as well.

Objects are encapsulated. So immediately, regression testing is dramatically 
reduced or rendered completely unnecessary. Objects are small so they are 
easier to change and and can be rewritten completely, much more easily than 
a monolithic COBOL program. Objects can be reused, so your competitior can 
simply change the way components are connected instead of needing to 
undertake a full system review before changing anything. The list goes on, 
but the world has voted with its feet. COBOL is NOT declining because it is 
inherently a BAD language. It is happening because there are better 
alternatives available, and, in particular, the Obect paradigm supports

the main single reason was the replacement of the procedural paradigm with 
the Object Oriented one, in concordance with the rise of distributed 
(networked) processing over centralised. This, more than anything else, 
rendered COBOL redundant.
