› Can anyone explain W2 and 1099 to me?

For those of you outside the USA, a W-2 form which the United States
government requires employers to complete on an annual basis. Every
employer must complete one W-2 form for each employee. The form
includes the employee's total income for the 12 month period ending on
December 31st. It also shows the amount of taxes withheld from the
employee's pay during the same period.

A 1099 form is essentially the same thing, except that it is used when
the person performing work for the company is *not* an employee, but
an independent contractor.

Hope that helps.

There's a few more things you might need to know Richard. A W-2 is
sometimes referred to as an hourly employer. And as an employee, you
would have your withholding and social security taxes taken out of
your paycheck and handled by your employer. Also, as an employee, you
may be entitled to benefits such as health care, retirement savings,
vacation, etc..

As a 1099, sometime known as a subcontractor, you have to manage your
taxes which would included paying quarterly estimates and monthly
withholding. Any benefits, you have to manage and pay for yourself.
The next thing you have to worry about is a tax code know as Section
1706 (only 1099s, not W-2s). That section defines 20 guidelines that
determines, in the IRS's eyes, if you really are an independent
contractor.

There is a whole lot more to this. I'd talk to an accountant.

In <01bbff01$982c2260$9b76f482@agda>, "Richard Nettleingham" writes:
› Can anyone explain W2 and 1099 to me?

W2 and 1099 and US Internal Revenue form numbers. Form W2 is used to
report the earnings of an employee. An employee is a 'chattel', slave,
of the employer and the employer is responsible for paying 8.3% of the
employee's salary to Social Security; it's actually paid to the IRS.

Generally, an W2 employee receives holiday pay, medical insurance, and
other benefits.

Form 1099 is used to report the payment to a contract employee. A
contract employee is a free agent and pays both sides of his social
security tax. A contract employee also pays for his own medical
insurance, holidays, etc.

You can figure on roughly 50-60% more pay as a contract employee but
that's real loose. You also have a lot of extra expenses as a contract
employee.
