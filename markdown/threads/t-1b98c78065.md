All righty, gang... this one will be a little long and rambling but the 
point will be... in there, someplace, maybe.

There's been a bunch of talk here recently about the rigidity associated 
with a Fortress mentality; one of the things I've mentioned that might 
have gotten lost is that departmental mentalities rarely exist in a 
vacuum and an organisation which values precisely defined, rigid functions 
in all other departments will likely do so in DP/IS/MIS, as well.

Today I went for a hairy-ears interview with a pimp and afterwards got to 
take a web-based tech-test... on various aspects of COBOL '74.

(That has nothing to do with the story that follows but I figured folks 
might get a giggle out of this.)

Anyhow... they looked at my bragsheet and said 'It seems that your last 
few assignments have been consulting'; I responded 'My last few 
assignments seem to go back to 1987, then.'

Then I got... 'Are you open to coming aboard full-time?'... and I must be 
getting old because I did not use my Standard Reply of 'I am *always* 
willing to listen to an offer; if someone wishes to take me out for a meal 
I just might have an appetite.'

Instead I tried something different... I said 'I'm always willing to 
listen to an offer... it's just that the offers I get are so bad.'  The 
pimp frowned, slightly, so I went on.

'Let me explain... a few years back I was on a gig, making a hair under 
US$65/hr, and the project leader started making noises about taking me on 
full-time.  I asked 'What do you have to offer?' and he responded 
'US$67,500/yr to start.'  I said 'If that's all that you have then I can't 
understand what reasons there are for me to consider, seriously, an offer 
to cut my salary in half.'

The pimp nodded and said 'I understand...'... and I smiled, broadly, and 
said 'No, you *don't* understand, just as the manager didn't.  Does the 
company have a good insurance package to offer?  Fine, then explain it to 
me... does the company have a good tuition rebate program?  Good, tell me 
about it... does the company have excellent vacation schedules, maybe a 
travel-agent tie-in for cheap places to go?  Great, let me hear about 
them... but all he had to give was the figure for an annual salary and if 
that, indeed, is all he had... then he was *right* not to go on.  A job's 
a variety of factors and this guy could not, even to his own mind, compete 
with my then-current situation on price... and he had nothing to offer 
beyond that.'

Later on, as I was walking with Killer around ManMade lake, I mused on 
this... and I thought about how little I had seen of bargaing that 
includes playing salary off against other forms of remuneration.

'The salary is a little low... how about this, in addition to the salary I 
get full tuition rebate on *any* graduate-level course I take and get a B 
or better in?

'The salary's a little low... how about raising the company matching-rate 
for my pension contributions from 25% to 40%?'

'The salary's a little low... how about accelerating the schedule and 
amounts by which I accumulate vacation?'

It has been my experience that managers would respond to suggestions such 
as these with a smile and 'That's a good idea but Personnel wouldn't let 
me.'

(side note - if someone with sufficient Corporate Authority makes a call 
to Personnel *anything* can be done... but let's leave that aside for the 
moment)

'That's a good idea but Personnel wouldn't let me'... to some ears that 
might sound a bit Fortressoid, aye.

DD

I aced (100%) three such COBOL skills tests. Then I scored 65% on BrainBench
COBOL II. Lost a job opportunity because of it. 

Beware of skills tests. Most are ok but some are very poorly written. The one I
flunked was not about COBOL; it was about how to install a COBOL compiler,
written by and from a system programmer's point of view. It had many questions
about obscure compiler options. It asked "What's the difference between V3.6 and
V3.7?" MOST questions were totally inappropriate to assess an applications
programmer. 

Other BrainBench tests are ok. The one titled Micro Focus is pretty good. It's
not about Micro Focus (much); it's about COBOL 85. I wrote to the BrainBench
company explaining all this. As expected, their only response was boiler-plate.

Does anyone else have anecdotes about poorly written tests?

When I was hiring programmers, I did a lot of research on correlating test
scores with measurable aptitude. My technique was to numerically grade
programmers of known ability, give them the test, do a linear regression between
test score and ability. Commonly used tests (in the '80s) with titles like
Programmer's Aptitude Test showed predictive power not much better than random.
After evaluating two dozen tests, some far afield, one I wrote myself, I finally
found one that WORKED. Its correlation with known ability was better than 90%,
over a wide range of abilities. It wasn't about programming; it was about logic.
It had 24 questions and took 6-10 minutes to complete. That's what they SHOULD
be using.

We once had a board game around the shop called Twixt, which had very
simple rules but required strong analytical abilities to play well.
Very easy to learn; one game and you got the gist of it.

In a (relatively small) sample, we found that programming ability

If an individual has good analytical skills, it's no big deal to teach
