We have a COBOL Crash course which is based on the best selling "Teach Yourself
COBOL in 21 Days" book. The course has an extensive study guide that takes you
through the course, lesson by lesson and includes additional information and
lessons for example on how to use the compiler and editor, so it gets you off
to a quick start. It includes a compiler and electronic study aids to help you
locate hard to find errors in your code.

The course teaches the basic of COBOL to an entry/intermediate level. Once you
are done with it we receommend the technical handbook "COBOL, Dates and the
Year 2000.

The course is being used by government agencies, corporations and education
establishments to train entry level programmers for their Year 2000 project and
for maintenance work. Some companies are giving a test on our materials and if
the student passes they will hire the person and train them further.

Many of the programmers I speak to, who want to train programmers are of the
opinion that COBOL is a language that particularly lends itself to entry level
work, and a "work/study program". Once a novice has the basics under his belt,
you can give him a simple programming task and have him to it until he is able
to do it with ease and then give him more difficult work to do and so on.
There is lots of entry level work to be done.

You wouldn't expect such a person to emmediatly be doing design work, or making
decisions about what needed to be done, and how.

As any programmer will tell you, programming is a profession wher the study is
never complete. there's always more to learn.

Once you have the basics down, however, you will have a frame of reference to
work with, to go on to learn more advanced concepts. But it's important to
study and practice your programming skills by programming.

I hope this helps.
