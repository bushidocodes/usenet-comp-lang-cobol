Well, this thread is exciting as it involves thinking.
To add my 2 cents, here are some memories.

A vendor set us up with a method for running our applications.  We used it
extensively.
We built procedures around it.

The vendor sold us another model of hardware for another site.  They did not
support the
same method for running our applications thus setting up a set of problems
we had to overcome.
One way would have been to design a new set of procedures which meant that
people at one site could not understand the methods at the other site
without training.

So, we said, even if your different hardware does not easily support the
method you provided on the first computer, we insist that you find a way for
that method to work on the other computer.

It took awhile, and market influences, but they figured out how to do it.

Another vendor built a system with RPN (reverse Polish notation) as the
hardware method.
Their marketing thought that the system would appeal to a certain group of
potential users.
The market proved to be driven by Data Processing types, and the hardware
while good, was not
as good as it could have been in it's handling of COBOL programs. So, when
the next generation came along, their Engineers, and Marketing determined
that it was in their best interest to revise the system in other ways.  As a
result, the COBOL move was accomplished with one instruction vs. 90+ in the
prior model of the computer.

The computer industry is replete with examples where user "influences" via
independent development or market pressure of one sort or another made
significant changes in the hardware
and the software.

This has happen a lot to accommodate COBOL even in the attempted hardware to
reduce instruction sets.

So, I believe, and wish that users would see that the things that they now
have to go to VB, C or other languages to get are reasonable features to add
to COBOL.  And based upon the OOP discussion currently going on, I see not
reason to add the OOP to the name of the language because certain behavior
results from features added to the language.  The tower of Babel has to stop
somewhere.

Warren Simmons

When I and others use OO or OOP in combination with a language
name, I mean (and I understand others to mean) "(use of) the OO
features of the language." Hence the term OO COBOL refers to
the OO features of COBOL or to the use of those features.

There are more than 2300 language names, including this interesting
jewel.

A0 or A-0 - Grace Hopper's team at Remington Rand, 1952, for the
UNIVAC I or II.  Possibly the first compiler ever.  Later internal versions:
A-1, A-2 ("The A-2 Compiler System", Rem Rand, 1955), A-3, AT-3.
AT-3 was released as MATH-MATIC.  Sammet 1969, p.12.

A list is available from:
    < ftp://wuarchive.wustl.edu/doc/misc/lang-list.txt >.

As long as there are computers, new languages will be invented
and re-invented to suit the needs of the inventor. A few of these
languages will also fit the needs of users. Many of these languages
will have a name and each will add one more brick to the tower.

The open source movement is not about having others make
changes for us. It is about us being able to make changes for
ourselves. The license for open source code generally requires
that changes made to open source code also be made freely
available to others. It is the Others who will decide whether any
individual's extension to the language will appear in the Other's
open source implementations. As such, each individual or group
that maintains an open source implementation is equivalent to
a vendor. Those who use the implementation unchanged are
equivalent to customers, with no assurance of support.

This lack of assurance of support is what has prevented large
users from embracing the open source movement. Red Hat
and other companies are beginning to overcome this reluctance
by providing that support. In the absence of organizations that
are willing to guarantee support for various implementations
of open source COBOL, there will be no wide-spread adoption
of the language extensions among large users and no pressure
for current vendors to accept those extensions into their
implementations. Without this wide-spead adoption, there is
little chance that the extensions will become part of the COBOL
standard.

I think open source code is a great idea; well, I thought it was
a great idea, until I downloaded the GNU distribution. Maybe
I could work with open COBOL source code; but I couldn't
work with the GNU C source code. Obviously, it works and
works well, I even ported flex (equivalent to Unix lex) to VC 5.0;
but I couldn't get past the programming style of the source
code enough to determine how it actually worked. I made a
decision very quickly. I decided that since that style was a
requirement of participation in GNU, I would not participate!
Considering how much reaction coding style opinions create
in this newsgroup, I wonder how many different open source
groups would need to be created just to satisfy those style
differences and how many more to cover implementation
differences.

A few points on this thread (not including anyone's posts so far - but
starting on a "fresh" tack).

1) Whatever problems I have with the members of the various Standards
committees, I often repeat that they are ALL remarkably "unbiased" in their
input and votes.  I do *not* think that it is either trying to "protect"
their proprietary products or extensions that delay the Standard (and its
enhancements).

2) One of the biggest "problems" in COBOL development is the STRONG (almost
absolute) requirement that "new things" must be "upwardly compatible" with
the last Standard.  I understand the reason for this, but for anyone who has
worked with the existing Standard, it is INCREDIBLY full of bugs,
inconsistencies, and ambiguities.  Every time ANY of us try to write a
proposal for "new stuff," we run into problems with the existing Standard -
and its errors.  Unfortunately, not all vendors have made the same
assumptions about what the (existing) Standard means in these situations -
so each time we try and "fix" a problem (while adding new stuff) - we run
into at least one implementation where the users would KILL us if we went
that way.  (Compare the frequent posts in this NG about IBM and File Status
39. This never SHOULD have been a problem, but users had grown accustom to
and learn to code to a "loop-hole" in earlier IBM implementations.)

3) Since the '85 Standard, there have been a  number of "consortia" created
which attempted to create new COBOL Standards.  If anyone in this NG ever
read the X/Open COBOL Standard, you would just about "die" trying to figure
out what it was trying to say (I know that the implementors did).  With the
US Federal Government (FIPS and NIST) getting out of the COBOL business, I
don't know what force will be able to give an appropriate "stamp of
approval" on any variation of a new specification to make it popular.
(Personally, I just do not think that major corporations are going to "bet
their business-critical" applications on a programmer-driven definition ...
But I could be wrong.)

4) Once upon a time and far away ... When IBM ruled the Mainframe world
(well sort-of) and where COBOL ruled the IBM mainframes (well sort-of), it
was possible for a single vendor to create a "de facto" Standard.  That is
why COMP-3 means Packed-Decimal in so many implementations and why GOBACK is
a useful verb in those same products.  Again, I don't see this happening
with COBOL now or in the near future.  (Remember my post a few weeks ago
where I was asking if *ANY* reader of this NG knew of a company with more
than 5 programmers that used Fujitsu COBOL?  So far, I have yet to hear of
ONE.  On the other hand, IBM, CA-Reallia, and Micro Focus seem to have just
"written off" the individual COBOL developer.  Likewise, I haven't seen
PerCOBOL making a huge dent yet - but time may tell.  Without a market
leader (dare I say it - verging on a monopoly), I don't see any one
implementation (sold or free) taking the lead in defining the language.
Again, I could be wrong - but that is my impression.

5) Finally, we do need to talk about the "reputation" that COBOL has among
senior management *and* academics.  The former view it as archaic and the
latter view it as irrelevant.  (A generalization - but not far from true).
Again, I don't see this changing and as long as it doesn't, the future for
COBOL is pretty similar to that of Assembler - it won't die, but it will
hardly thrive.
