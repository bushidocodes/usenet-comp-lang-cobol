That depends on a lot of other factors: what mainframe? what kinds of 
file access: indexed files or databases (leading to what type of 
database); what type of terminal interaction etc. Some combinations of 
such factors cannot be debugged or run on a PC (at least not with a 
reasonable amount of work).

The first question you need to find an answer for is whether there is a 
compatible COBOL compiler for the PC for the mainframe environment you 
are targeting... OPENCOBOL might do the trick, or Fujitsu COBOL or MF 
COBOL, but so much depends on the mainframe capabilities you wish to 
emulate.

Having read the thread, and not wishing to take sides, I did want to offer 
the following observations on this point from Alistair:

1. A person who works as a freelance is likely (and, in my opinion, has a 
perfect right to...) promote the position that if you want something done 
professionally, there are professional people available to do it. I don't 
think that constitutes "begging".

2. Being between contracts is normal for most contractors and does not imply 
a level of "desperation" to be re-employed. In a contract career which 
lasted from 1975 until 2005 I was out of work (when I would have preferred 
to be working) for a total of around 21 days. Although this mileage may vary 
for others, I believe it is fairly normal for competent contractors (and 
there is no question o f the Doc's competence) to take time out and to be a 
bit selective before diving back in to the stresses of the workplace. We all 
need some time out and contractors don't get paid holidays.

3. If a contractor WERE anxious to be employed, it is unlikely that frequent 
posts to this forum, reminding people that professionalism comes at a cost, 
would be a good way to obtain offers. I am therefore persuaded that this is 
simply the Doc's position on what should be paid for and what should be 
free, and has nothing to do with his current employment status.

4. I believe the REAL issue here is what can be given away for free and what 
should actually be paid for. Over years many people have contributed and 
received help through this forum. Sometimes even very capable people can use 
a different perspective and just discussing approaches to a problem can be 
helpful, sometimes there are people here with specific expertise in areas 
which are not generally covered in text books and manuals and they are 
prepared to generously give their time and advice. The question arises: 
Should people be paid for giving advice here?

My own feeling is: "No"  My reason for that opinion is that you have a 
perfect right to post here or not and it is an individual choice. No matter 
how capable you may be personally, there may come a time when you could use 
some help. Doc's stated position is that you should buy that help from a 
professional and I don't see that as being "wrong", but neither do I see it 
as being the only possible response. Rather, I see times when some free 
advice would be appropriate, even if simply indicating a direction for 
further exploration, or pointing out some of the less obvious implications 
of a particular solution, but I also see times when it would be appropriate 
to offer paid services. If the solution being requested is likely to 
generate revenue for the requestor, and requires information or skill which 
they patently don't have, then they should be prepared to pay something to 
obtain the solution. Similarly, if a provided solution can save someone a 
good deal of time, then they should recognise that value. For myself, I try 
to acknowledge where something has been helpful and will do this with money 
if the provided advice helps me to generate revenue. Just because there is a 
forum with some generous spirits in it doesn't mean we should take that for 
granted and EXPECT free advice.

(Sometimes, even in a commercial environment, it isn't about money. I 
recently had a client who got into a major project and simply ran out of 
money. PRIMA continued support and never turned off the tap because he 
couldn't pay us. We supported our tools, and provided COBOL expertise 
because we said we would, not because there was a maintenance contract in 
place (there wasn't; he had no money to pay for one...). Today this same 
client is a valued customer who pays his bills on the nail, extols our 
company and services to anyone who will listen, and hasn't forgotten that we 
supported him. I can't buy that kind of loyalty. The point is that there are 
times when you should do somethng for nothing and there are times when you 
shouldn't.)

I guess all of us can relate to seeing someone struggling with a programming 
problem and most of us would want to help (maybe based on the conscious or 
subconscious realization that we could be in the same position ourselves).

But there are always the freeloaders who are looking for a free ride, 
contribute nothing to the forum, and usually don't even say "thank you" when 
they get what they came for.

The trick is for all of us to recognize the difference,and that is a purely 
subjective call.

Pete.
