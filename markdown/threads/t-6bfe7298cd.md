```
Hi guys,
  I'm in a discussion with a colleague (we're both currently academics)
about the proper roll of the project leader (or any manager) in
motivating the team members, in particular, program developers, beit
they programmers, designers, analysts or anywhere else in the SDLC.  My
view (and I know my view has holes in it, I just don't know where those
holes are, that's why I am posting this question to CLC) is that the PL
shouldn't have to mess with this stuff.  Programming is a highly
profession position, and a person should have their act together before
they get to that level in the organization.  Now I'm not talking about
showing appreciation for a job well done.  I'm all for that, but there
is a fine line between appreciation and manipulation.  Nor am I talking
about hygenic factors, I am all for those too, for example, giving the
programmer her own printer even though the cost-benefit analysis shows a
slightly negative bias.   What I'm talking about is motivating the
programmer who doesn't even want to be there, someone who would rather
be home watching TV, or someone who just doesn't like doing that kind of
work and can't bring himself to focus on the task.  My opinion is that
the PL shouldn't waste his time on it and just get rid of the damn
person and try his luck with someone else.
  My colleague's response is that I am living in a dream world.  To her
IS work is a joke.  All group members are un-motivated.   She says I'd
only be wasting my time because the chances are so great that I'd only
get someone else with similar problems, and even if I did get someone
with a good work ethic, the rest of the group and the company would
bring that person down.  I guess her's is the "an eagle can't fly when
surrounded by turkeys" theory and my response is, in that case shoot the
damn turkeys.
  I be interested in reading practitioners views on this.
  Thanks,
John Grznar

P.S.  And no docdwarf, this isn't a homework assignment.  :-)
```

```
Let me try and explain this.  In college the only thing a student can learn is how
to learn.  We cannot provide students with knowledge, because all knowledge comes
from within (the person.)  The process of learning begins when deep inside of you,
something slowly forms and rises up to the surface of your conscience where you can
express it.  Once you can express your idea, you then should put it to the real
world for verification.  Once it has been verified to YOUR TRUE SATISFACTION then
you have LEARNED it.  The idea may still be wrong! but you have LEARNED it.  Of
course, nobody likes to learn wrong stuff.  But whether what we learn is right or
wrong depends on the verification process.

There are two valid ways to verify an idea.  The best way is through empirical
experiment.  The other way is through no-holds-barred, in-your-face, debate.  The
first way is preferred, but it is by no means foolproof.  The second method can be
done using polite sweet talking terms (more on this later.)  But the
no-holds-barred method is better between persons who yet gained mutual respect of
each other (more on this later, too.)  Here is why,  the degree to which something
is LEARNED depends heavily on the confidence the person has in the verification
process.  The probability of whether or not the idea is correct depends on the
performance of the debaters.  Now nobody likes having their ideas disproved,
because when an idea is disproved it becomes worthless.  So one moment someone is
holding on to an idea he thinks is very valuable, then someone else pokes a hole in
it and it vanishes. The person's loss causes his feelings to be hurt.   So instead
of showing someone their idea is wrong, we try to lead him to conclude himself that
his idea is wrong (Thanks Thane.)  How this is any better, I don't know, but it
certainly is a more difficult and time consuming approach.

Now once the person has full confidence that his debators are not holding anything
back, and the other debators are confident he isn't holding anything back (because
they too will indubitably be testing some of their ideas at the same time) the
gruffness of the conversation can be mellowed out and we then say they have gained
a degree of respect for each other.

Now it is far best to select a worthy opponent to debate with. (Plato and Aristotle
both realized this at the precise moment of their most vehement disagreement.)
If the adversary is too weak, then little confidence is there in the method.  If
the adversary is too strong, the debate may not be understood, thus no real
conclusion can be drawn from the verification process.

Of course, the same idea and be tested many times.

use that.
```

```
Actually, no one can motivate anyone else!
Motivation comes from within.

Motive:  something that causes a person to act.
Motivate: provide a motive.

You can provide all the reasons you think will get me to do something,
but if I don't want to do that, I won't.

Now LEADERSHIP, getting others to WILLINGLY do what you
want, is something the Military, (Army and Marines especially)
does well.

That said, even the Army in its training manuals on Leadership,
states that if a sodier is not doing a task correctly or acting
improperly, you must first determine whether it is due to a lack
of PROPER TRAINING and if so provide that.  If not it may
be due to personal problems that are taking too much of the
person's time / energy.

If the person can't do ther job after proper training, BUT is
trying to do it correctly, then you evaluate for a different
job.

If the person has personal problems, those must first be
addressed before the person will be trainable.

Deming, a genius in Management and the application of statistics,
says (essentially) all work problems are MANAGEMENT'S FAULT.
This is because of unclear or conflicting standards / goals,
failure to recognize good work, etc.

Most individuals WANT to be respected for the contributions made,
WANT to be told occassionally 'Hey, that was Great!  We had a really
tight schedule and you came thru.  OR We were all skeptical that this would
work, but your idea saved us X dollars / weeks, etc.'.

Surveys consistently show that we will take a job with less pay than another
if we think we will be treated better, given the credit due us.
'You can't pay me enough to be insulted or cheated'.

The Leader who finds out what is the issue with Joe the Dud, and
maybe splits his current work with a couple of others and says
'Joe take a couple of days off and get some rest'  or whatever is
reasonable will (arranges the training for the new tools that Joe
is afraid to use for fear of looking dumb, will
  a)  have earned Joe's and others' respect
  b) have saved the company time and money by keeping
    Joe onboard
  c) have shown the workers that Management DOES care
  and thereby enhance morale.

Also beware the 'self-fulfulling prophecy'.
Jack or Jill has worked at company X for n years.  Has always
been on time, done consistent good work.  Never had a serious
problem, etc.  Yet he/she has never or not in a long while, been
told " / we appreciate the good work you're doing!".  Soon, Jack or Jill
begins to think " They don't really care about me?!" and begins to show
less enthusiasm, be less carefull, stops volunteering to come in early
 or stay late to get that problem solved.  THIS OF COURSE GETS
NOTICED.  Jack or Jill now gets the "Jack, you've been sloppy
lately,(OR you haven't been a team player lately), if this keeps up,
it won't be good." speech.

Of course, Jack or Jill is REALLY P'D off now, and starts looking
for excuses to tally up every little 'slight'.  Soon Jack or Jill either
gives notice, or is fired.

All that was ever needed to PREVENT this is a regular review of the
work each person is doing to reinforce positive actions / attitudes and
reduce negatives.

To correct the problem once the reviews weren't done, all it would have
taken was an informal talk.  "Jack/Jill, I wanted to talk with you because
you are a VALUED member of the team and for the longest time, you've
never needed anyone to check on your work, you were always punctual,
etc.  And lately you've seemed to not be the same.  Is there anything I can
help with?".
```

```
I have read this thread with interest.

For the past 15 years I have made my living (on the rarer and rarer
occasions when I feel like working...) as a Project Manager/Consultant,
rather than a Programmer.
However, having been a programmer for 35 years, I have some inkling into the
mindset...

Points about motivation/leadership and whether or not it is actually
possible to motivate other people have already been discussed, so I'll
confine myself to some "do"s and "don't"s, and some basic "rules".

I offer the following observations, John, based, not on textbooks, but on
real world experience (mainly outside America).

1. NEVER publicly admonish or criticise a team member.  A quiet private
chat, with a genuine desire to put issues on the table and address them, and
listen to what the other person says, is the ONLY chance of reaching the
real problem.
The Corollary: Never beat up the Team as a whole. If they are failing that
is MY fault, not theirs.

2. NEVER lie to the team or individual members of it. Keep your promises. If
there is something sensitive which cannot be divulged "yet", tell them that
and assure them that as soon as you ethically can inform them you will. Give
an assurance it will not affect the outcome if you can honestly do that. If
you can't, then tell them that we will operate on what we have at present,
until we hear differently.

3. The ones your colleague says don't want to be there...rather be home
watching TV...

They get ONE chance.

You tell them what the project is hoping to achieve, tell them they will be
treated fairly (no matter what their Management have said or done to them in
the past), express your own enthusiasm for the project (and if you don't
have any, don't be there...), tell them you want them as part of it and it
will be satisfying and fun.  Having fun, as well as working hard, is an
essential part of the mix for successful Project Management and, sadly, too
many Managers overlook this, concerned that the people they answer to will
not approve. See next rule...

If the team Member fails to respond to your offer of a place on the team
with a "clean slate" (4 weeks is a fair period to see what happens), tell
them frankly there is no place for them and ask them what they want to do.
Get them removed from the team as quickly as possible.

I learned this one the hard way. As a new Project Manager I really didn't
want to fire anybody (still don't) but I had some problems with a husband
and wife team where he was really competent and she was about as useful as a
chocolate firescreen.

Unfortunately, the two of them were extremely disruptive as she made bullets
for him to fire...Very difficult situation. I discussed it with my Boss who
was older, wiser, and much more experienced. His verdict: "Fire them both.
Do it now. It's your call; I'll support you." I was shocked and decided to
persevere... little chats with both of them together and separately.

My attempt to be fair and avoid unpleasantness was perceived as weakness,
and the attitudes and destructive activity hardened. Six months later I
fired them both and apologised to my Boss for not doing it sooner. I worked
out later, my bad decision had cost us around 20 man weeks of work and an
unquantifiable bad effect on morale in general. Once they were gone, things
rapidly improved and we got it in on time (just...).

Your colleague is right on this one, John, although I believe her cynicism
about the impossibility of ever getting anyone who is (or, arguably, can be)
motivated is unjustified.

4. NEVER fear, attempt to propitiate, or gain Brownie points with the people
you report to. Develop an honest working relationship, keep them informed
and get their help when things are stalled. The attitude should be: "My
management are there to support my project when I need help, in exactly the
same way as I support my team when they need it. There is no "US" and
"THEM"...we are working for the same overall goals."

There are many other points which are really common sense and can be summed
up as "treat people the way you want them to treat you".  I believe the ones
above are the MOST important but I certainly don't minimise things like
encouraging and acknowledging individuals.

I usually do some "team building" from time to time and allocate a
percentage of the overall budget for this. It is not just pizza but can be
something a bit special when particular project milestones are achieved. I
always make the point that it is the Company's way of acknowledging what we
are doing. On some occasions I have paid for this out of my own pocket. (the
team had done an outstanding task and there was no money in the budget for a
party, or Company policy was that they didn't do this for employees). On
those occasions I simply say that I really appreciate the efforts being made
(which is true; see 2 above.)

BOTTOM LINE:

Programmers will respond to an environment where they are (ideally)
acquiring new skills, being recognised for the effort they put in (not just
with a paycheque, but in a more personal way), and can feel that they are
part of something that WILL be achieved and will be recognised by the
Company. They should also feel that their Project Management is 100% behind
them and can be approached for help and advice on anything.

Project Managers should have a sincere desire to "grow" team members and
provide the kind of environment where this is possible.

I have a number of people who have worked with me on past projects and have
indicated they would always be happy to do so again.

Whether this comes down to "Motivating Programmers" or not, is not for me to
say.

It works for me...

Pete.

PS. An eagle will fly whether it is surrounded by turkeys or not. If the
turkeys get in the way, the eagle goes right through them...
```

```
I came into this thread late, and if I have the time I'll read all the
messages that have already accumulated, but in the meantime, let me throw in
my two cents.

I'm a Systems Manager in a mainframe COBOL shop, over 3 teams, with one team
doing e-commerce as well.  Each team has a team leader, and consists of 3-5
additional programmers.  The team leaders report to me.

I would not characterize any of my people as uninterested in their work.  On
the contrary, they are all very interested.  Certainly, some are more
interested than others, but none could be said to be distinterested.

We have had good times and bad times, of course.  The bad times have been
characterized by a lack of assignments, and general uncertainty in the
office (we were being bought out, changing offices, a number of resignations
occurred).  That period of time is now past.  Things seem to have settled
down, there are plenty of assignments, and I'm trying to get some more
interesting development work for my people.  I think that's one of the key
things for people in the IS field: work that is challenging and holds your
attention.  When forced to do mundane, uninteresting things, it seems to
send the message that "you aren't good enough for the hard things, so go do
this."

In the book "The Soul of a New Machine" (by Tracy Kidder) it's recongnized
that people will work long hours, developing their own solutions to
problems, but the work has to be interesting and compelling.  Rote
maintenance, for most people, becomes mind numbing (though there are
programmers that prefer maintenance to development).

Hygiene factors (salary, time off, benefits) often make a difference if they
are not sufficiently present.  But there comes a point where if the work
isn't rewarding, then the hygiene factors don't matter.  It becomes the
project leader's job to guide his people in the right direction, fostering
the natural enthusiasm of creative people.

Paul
```
