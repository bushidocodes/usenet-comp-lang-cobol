See... the question is not "how many resumes?" but what kind of resume...
Last two times I've been in job searches, I had radically different
results. The first time, 45 resumes, 5 responses, 3 interviews before I
took the job...the last time 40 resumes, 37 interviews, 31 job offers...
What changed wasn't my qualifications, it was my presentation.

When you consider the fact that employers usually get from "several" to
"hundreds" of resumes, it makes sense that they look for 1) qualifications,
then 2) standout presentation. If you have 50+ candidates with the same or
similar qualifications/background, you call first the people whose resumes
have a presentation that seems a) more professional, and b) like they put
some thought into it.

A good cover letter doesn't hurt either...

I've been temping as a secretary for the past month. I work for the
president of a $350 million engineered products firm. BYW, they're
turning off their S/360 MVS box next year. Replacing it with HP-UX
running c/s apps: MFG/PRO and PeopleSoft are those of which I know.
But that's grist for another thread.

Two things I've learned: a lot of mail makes it to the outer office and
gets thrown away by a clerk (me). It's absurd, really. Their firm hires
clerks to mail it out, and the firm to which it's addressed hires clerks
to sort it and throw it away.

I read several resumes every day. They all seem the same after a while.
My boss is looking for a "few, good" managers, so he glances at them,
but they get grouped together and placed at the bottom of his inbox.

I also frequently receive and read circulars from the run-of-the-mill
recuiter/placement agencies. They send out little four-page or single-
sheet brochures the describe a job-seeker in about four or five lines:
it reads like a real estate listing, more or less. Or a personal ad.
Of course, the product being hawked is usually referred to by a code
number. Easier to substitute with what's on the shelf, that way. Or,
if it's just a troll, to know what to look for.

If they give names, you know that they have job-seeker bound by contract
enforceable at law.

The firm also deals with executive search firms. They have two with
which they deal. The VP-HR takes their calls. With the rest, I just
take a message. These will meet a job-seeker for dinner, talk with
him, and then write up their impressions and submit the report along
with the job-seekers resume. The last one of these that I saw included
a bit of subtle impression-making. The headhunter offered two candidates.
They're both coming in on Monday. The favored candidate got a glowing
write up: height, weight, "professional appearance, confident manner,"
etc. And his resume was nicely printed. The other guy got no write-up
and just a scrachy, faxed resume, with the fax glyphics still visible.

That's why it takes so many resumes -- most of them get thrown out by
a temp before anyone with authority even sees them.

Want to get a job? Do your own legwork. Read What Color Is My Parachute?
and, if your serious or desperate, read Where Do I Go From Here With My Life
by the same author. Then follow the program, step by step.

In What Color Is My Parachute?, it's reported that, on average, nationwide,
the job-seeker using the mail out resumes method must mail 1470 resumes
before accepting a job offer. Of course, in IT, today, the case is different.

Practice breathing meditation.

God's grace to all.

HTH.
