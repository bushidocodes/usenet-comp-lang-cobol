```
It's easy to dismiss fundamental concepts (state machines) as some kind of
academic junk, and to dismiss those who mention such concepts as academic
types who know nothing about the real world (*), but this is really just
a way of excusing lack of knowledge.

THe fact of the matter is that abstraction is a crucial element of ALL
programming, and models of abstraction (state machines are just one of
many), are crucial in doing abstraction in an organized way. Probably
you have used the concept in a half baked unorganized way often (it is
hard to imagine ANY application domain in which the concept of state
machines is not relevant). Understanding the formalism behind such
concepts is one of the important elements in making the transition
from software mucking to software engineering.

By the way, my comment that GOTO's may be reasonable in some cases for
implementing complex state machines does NOT at all mean that since
state machines are ubiquitious, I think gotos should be ubiquitious. The
great majority of state machines are very simple, with very simple
transition diagrams, and can be programmed in a conventional structured
manner easily.

Here is a thought, whenever you finding yourself writing REALLY complex
conditional statements, at least give a thought to the model behind the
code you are writing!

(*) I am always amused to be accused of being an academic person who knows
nothing about the real world. I would guess that I have written more
commercial COBOL stuff than many people who contribute to this group, and
I have certainly wandered around in the COBOL world pretty extensively
(it is hard to write a COBOL compiler without having SOME knowledge of
how it is actually used :-)

Don't sell COBOL short, it is by no means a language restricted to business
applications, or rather perhaps a better way of putting it is that business
applications covers such a broad range of problems that any language that
can handle all of them is bound to be pretty much a general purpose language.

THe only real area I can think of where COBOL is not naturally comfortable
is in the embedded real-time world, and I am sure that someone out there will
tell me that they have successfully used COBOL in some flight control
computer :-)
```
