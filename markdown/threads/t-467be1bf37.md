I think the free compilers will be a major challenge to the
closed source compilers, though the strong ones will survive.
They will survive where they add extra value - useful support,
productivity tools, etc.

The history of computing has shown many times that 'good enough'
at a lower price defeats soemthing better at a higher price,
especially if the lower price is 'free'.

Database is more difficult for free developers. To really test
databases, you need large machines, which are expensive. It is
also very complex technically to deliver good performance
combined with fast restart, robustness, data integrity,
transactions, row level locking, lock escalation etc.

Postgresql and mySQL are not really competitive with Oracle etc
at the high end IMHO, though they are very useful for the right
application.

Tim Josling

Tim, that's my point (apologies for the loaded question). your statement
applies with as much force to large COBOL applications (especially on the
higher-end Windows/UNIX boxes and the 'frames -- which coincidently is where
the larger $ deals are done). there is nothing better than COBOL for these
types of applications (tho' i think Java/J2EE will also [ultimately] be
successful in this space) -- that is what it was built for. the run-time
environments necessary to support execution of these applications in a
mission-critical manner are extremely complex ("run-time" is somewhat an
understatement -- "Application Server" as Micro Focus brands it is much
closer to the mark; and i don't think we'll see Weblogic or WebSphere going
"cheap" any time soon, if ever -- same "large systems" reasoning applies
here too).

now, an interesting point of view (and i'm not sure it's one i totally agree
with even if it might seem that way ;-) is that it is rather unfortunate
that COBOL is visible and popular outside of the "large systems" domain.
perhaps what the vendors need to do is recognise this as an opportunity and
provide a COBOL-oriented toolset that fits the "Visual [insert language
moniker]" paradigm.

years ago, Micro Focus attempted this with a product called "Visual Object
COBOL" that was priced at $499. in 6mths, over 3500 people registered &
downloaded the beta software. given the lack of "publicity" that was quite a
surprise (perhaps word of mouth spreads as fast as foot and mouth :-). but
the end of this "fairy tale" ended up being more like a "horror story"; at
least commercially. after release, relatively few people actually ponied up
and bought it. the successors: Net Express and it's UNIX counterpart Server
Express, were priced much higher; and have both done (and continue to do)
very well.

i don't think it's a coincidence that Fujitsu are now a good bit more
sensitive about protecting their revenue.

Gazaloo.
