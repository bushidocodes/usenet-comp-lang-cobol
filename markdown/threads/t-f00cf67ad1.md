All righty... there's been some talk 'round these parts of late as to what 
is the criterion which makes something 'humo(u)rous'.  Having spent, in my 
days, a fair amount of time in any of the four (or more) positions of a 
joke:

As teller
As audience
As member of group being spoken of
As member of other-than group being spoken of

... I feel eminently qualified to supply what any right-thinking person 
will be able to conclude is The Ultimate Definition.

It is, in essence, a position which Socrates put forward, as reported in 
various Dialogues.  Plato reports that Socrates held an object had a 
quality insofar as that object reflected or partook of the Essence or Form 
(An. Gr. 'eidos') of that quality.

An action did not show 'justice' because it fit in with laws or 
situations; such things change over time and place and yet the concept of 
'just' remained constant; this was, Socratically, because there was an 
Essence, a Form, an Eidos of Justice, which existed outside of space and 
time and remained constant (as the concept of 'just' did).

Likewise with beauty... something was not beautiful because it was seen to 
be such, it was beautiful because one recognised it partook of the Eidos 
of Beauty.

(A later lover of wisdom - An. Gr. 'philosophos' - would call this 'mere 
reified concept', an attempt to make a thing - L. 'res' + L. 'faciere', 
'to make' out of something which did not otherwise exist, that it makes as 
much sense to speak of 'beauty' in the absence of things to be called 
'beautiful' as it does to speak of 'sound' in a vacuum... but what do 
these latecomers know, anyhow?)

With this concept of Forms in mind, then, it becomes readily and 
immediately apparent:

Something is humo(u)rous because it partakes of the Eidos of the Funny.

DD

IMHO - all of them stumbled over the problem of definitions in every
language and situation: concepts can only be defined in terms of other
concepts - words can only be defiined in terms of other words - and sooner
or later you regress to the ultimate, totally fundamental concepts or
words - which cannot be defined.  I, Philosopher, have to accept (or hope)
that everybody knows what they mean, regardless of the lack of definitions.
Exactly analogous to mathematical axioms.  Thus Socrates speaks of the Eidos
because he can't define it but everybody will have their own understanding
of it anyway; so he evades the problem while sounding profound!
