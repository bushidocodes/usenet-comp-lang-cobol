```
After seeing lot of arguments on COBOL , I just thought
of typing something :

* Easy to make a change in a 10 year old COBOL program, 
  rather than a C program, even if both are not
  documented ( or no indentation ) at all. 
* A person with little programming knowledge can make 
  some sense out of a COBOL program. ( This is 
  where COBOL lacks some so called sophistication as 
  a programming language )
* COBOL is slowly giving place to C++, VB..etc...languages
  for the front-end on-line applications, but, this is 
  because people are moving to more GUI based front-ends
  rather than old 3270 ( 80 x 24 ) character based front ends.
  Lesser number of new developments are done today ( say, in 1998 )
  using COBOL/CICS front end.
* BUT, COBOL is still the number one language for batch 
  processing, at least in IBM world, and which is still
  a big world.
* COBOL is going to survive as long IBM OS is there in the 
  market ( MVS, OS390..). And looks like IBM OS will be the
  there for some more time, as they have come up with 
  Mainframe based httpd servers and products like net.data
  for writing macros to access DB2 data for Web display.

Kalpataru Barman
```

machine.

Have you ever wondered why companies are so often little interested in
graduates with no experience?  It is because programming ability, as
absolutely vital as it is, is only part of what a well rounded programmer
should know.  Anybody who has ever trained newbie programmers, no matter
how hot-shot they feel they are, knows that they are almost useless until
they learn a bit about what should be programmed, and why, and even how.
Most good MIS shops have coding standards and libraries of routines that
the new programmer must become familiar with.  All MIS shops have running
systems that the newbie knows nothing about.  Nobody in their right mind
would trust a newbie to start modifying critical applications without
some time to assimilate what is going on in the system.  Even general
knowledge is often lacking in a newbie.  For example, how to handle money
and batch balancing.  While I value my technical programming skills, I
could write code technically as good as anybody could want 25 years ago,
clean, efficient, etc.  I've mastered a pot full of languages during my
career.  But what I have learned about business, the software development
process, and the judgment I have gained with years of experience, I value
even more.

I'm not sure exactly how schools can best address these issues, but I am
convinced they could do a better job than now.  Nothing can substitute
entirely, for experience, but I believe we could be turning out more well
rounded programmers than we often do.  Academic folks are enamoured with
the 'science' of computers, so we have scads of CS degrees offered.  But
how many 'computer scientists' do we need?  For every computer scientist
we need, we need twenty or fifty programmers in other areas.  I majored
in physics and math at university, so I'm not anti-science.  But I have
had many occasions to wish I had taken more accounting and business type
courses.  Others may disagree, but I would like to see more emphasis put
on these areas, and other practical business issues, in the majority of
computer curriculums.
