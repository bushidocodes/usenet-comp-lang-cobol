```
Mark, I suspect the closest PC based COBOL would be MERANT Micro Focus
Net Express 3.1; you can even do CICS.  But it is expensive.  You can
get version 3.0 of Fujitsu COBOL free from www.adtools.com, which is

compiler on CD.  All the examples in the book are on the CD and work
with the Fujitsu compiler.  Thane also frequents this newsgroup.  You
should be able to get help with either compiler in this newsgroup.
```

```
edit CoBOL programs.  I prefer the ISPF editor for my coding.

Where SPF falls short is in hypertext links (not counting the ZOOM and
SHOWPROC commands).  But to get this to work on the PC, would involve
copying every module you might want to your PC to get this to work.  I
don't like counting on copies of modules to be current.  When I work on
the mainframe, I can quickly look at the real McCoy - not via hypertext,
but I know where to look anyway.
```

```
I have read both Judson's and Volker's replies.  I would like to stress that
IBM VisualAge COBOL *is* closer to IBM's COBOL for OS/390 & VM - because
they actually use the same "front-end" source code.

Having said that, IBM does NOT offer any (PC) solution for older OS/VS COBOL
code or even newer code compiled with CMPR2.  Therefore, if you have either
of those in your "mainframe environment" - the MERANT solution is "closer to
what you want".

There is also an issue of whether or not you have either Assembler or PL/I
modules in your mainframe environment.  If you have Assembler, then MERANT
provides a better solution; if you have PL/I, then IBM probably does.

Another option to look at - is CA-Reallia which also provides a "mainframe
emulation" environment. Like MERANT and IBM, it is NOT cheap.
```

```
Howard,
  I don't think ANYONE would (today) suggest editing on the PC - but doing
all testing on the mainframe.  As someone who worked for BOTH a mainframe
debugging vendor (ADS - who developed and distributed Xpediter) and a "PC
vendor" Micro Focus (developer and distributor of Workbench - and then
Mainframe Express), I can tell you "pros and cons" of both environments -
for development and maintenance.

Note1:  Both companies have changed names/owners since I worked for them.
Today, see Compuware and MERANT.

Note2:  There are now products that do "remote debugging" which allows you
to use a PC "front-end" to testing of programs running on an IBM mainframe.
This note doesn't (directly) deal with those options.

The bottom-line is that it varies based on company environment and
individual preference as to WHICH is a "better" environment for you/your
company.

Doing ALL development up to and thru "unit testing" on the PC *may* be more
productive if you shop either has significant resource constraints or
bureaucracy.  As mentioned in another note, working on the PC means that you
don't have to go thru "systems programmers" to set up CICS, IMS, or DB2
"tables".  You can do them for yourself and modify them - quickly and
(semi-)easily as requirements change - or as your "design" needs
modification.  If you are in an environment where "turn-around" for compile
and tests can be slow - or where "foreground debuggers" are prohibited
during "normal work hours" - then doing development on the PC can REALLY
save time and frustration.

OTOH, doing development/testing/maintenance on the PC requires SOMEONE to
set up a "mirror" environment for your mainframe.  In some shops, this is
more of a problem than the benefits that you gain.  If your shop does NOT
have "good" test data - but instead uses copies of multi-gig files
(databases) for testing, then you will probably have problems getting them
on the PC (LAN) much less multiple copies for each developer or group of
developers. Similarly, if you don't have a "good" source control system, it
is possible that your PC copies of copylib members can cause you serious
problems.  There are also issues of "converting" lengthy/complex JCL streams
to PC equivalents; some products include JCL emulators or converters - but
certainly NOT all PC products.

It is ABSOLUTELY true (IMHO) that you MUST do your "final testing" on the
mainframe itself.  However, for shops that work with and gain experiences
with PC development environments, it is possible to gain and maintain
confidence in doing all your development THRU unit testing on the PC.
```
