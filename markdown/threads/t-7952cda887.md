The advice you received about proving it on the command line first is good
advice. As a small additional hint: consider carefully what you mean by the
"Portuguese chars", If the enye, or non-English "American" puncutation marks
are of interest to you (what we silly folks would call the upside down question
mark and upside down exclamation mark) then these may have a code in Extended
ASCII of the IBM PC universe of 127 or higher (that is, a value of 128 requires
the eight bit to be on). 

Some UNIX software may damage such encodings (although I would be surprised if
that survives into LINIX, and would welcome comment from more knowledgable
folks on this as a general issue).

If there is anywhere the 7-bit ASCII code legacy would be most likely to
surface it would be in the telecommunications functions.

The word American in the title American Standard Code for Information
Interchange is somewhat bigotted, being heavily 'English' oriented. And though
there is both French and Spanish on the Northern continent as well as Portugese
on the Southern continent; the original code always needed workarounds for the
additional non-English ciphers.

The real truth is this simply lead to mispellings which no doubt were insulting
to some.

You may also want to give consideration to what will happen at the other end
(outside of your LINUX box) if the terminfo is in any way to actually be
transmitted. Your LINUX telecom software might soar like the grandest eagle
(once configured correctly) but transmitted ciphers could get masked to 7-bits
on the wires or masked to 7-bits at the receiving end (especially if the remote
site has older hardware or older OSes).

If it is not such sensitive information, could you tell us what fields you are
encoding that leads you to use problematic ciphers in the terminfo file.

Someday we will all go to Unicode and this kind of thing will ease up, but the
"A"SCII text config files will be the last trees to fall.

A minor footnote, in some cases the Extended ASCII ciphers encoded with value
128 through 159 get transformed into the ASCII control characters 0 through 31
by errant 7-bit mask operations. Thus, you have particular ciphers that might
be more than just mangled into other characters, but will instead transform
into telecommunications control characters.
