```
1.  Verify with your boss why you are doing the interview. 
Sometimes it is "can you work with the guy?", sometimes it's "Does
to guy know his stuff?" and frequently, both.

2.  Try asking stuff about where your shop's leading edge is.  Has
he coded nested programs?  Can he explain what different compiler
options do?  Tell you what data format is good for indexes, why or
why not 01 levels are contiguous in memory.  How much storage does
an Occurs Depending On really take?  What are the migration issues
to the next version of the compiler (if you aren't there yet).

Can he explain "Block contains 0 records" and how that affects
file size?  Does he know how to access VSAM or Oracle or what ever
data base you may have?  Can he work with the data base program
outside of COBOL (like SQL/PS or Oracle) or DB2 import/export
utilities?

3.  Find out about his coding philosophies.  I've seen fights in
shops over things like packed data vs binary data, paragraphs with
a single period, performing sections instead of paragraph thru
end-paragraph.  If you have a rigid program layout, have him look
at a sample and see if he can live with the standards you
enforce.  This is most important because he is also interviewing
you to see what he will have to put up with.

4. For IBM, does he use Expediter, Abend-Aid, Smart Test, File
Aid, StarTool or what ever tools your shop uses?  How about the
general utilities on your platform?  Sort?  ICETOOL?  

Has he run with LE/370, and can find the failing program in the
LE/370 formatted output?  

How about JCL?  Can he create a file, suggest performance
strategies like more buffers, bigger blocks?  Can he code JCL to
implement these?  How about JCL IF/THEN/ELSE statements?

Are you CICS, DB2, or IMS?  Can he run CICS enough to get his
program linked and loaded to replace the currently running code? 
Can he translate an error message from a CICS or IMS abend back to
a COBOL error (e.g. CICS ASRA back to 0C4 back to "bad index
value"?) 

Can he debug from a dump if he has to?  I keep a couple of old
dumps (easy problems) around and show people.  I see if he can
find the registers, figure out which statement failed from the
load module offset, find the data, etc.  Does he know that the TGT
is?

5.  Since you are in insurance does he understand the vocabulary? 
How about the basic math for calculating reserves or the possible
programing changes needed to meet XXX regulations.  How about
Interest Sensitive Life, and fund based annuities?

Can he learn?  Did he study anything in school or do some LOMA
courses?  (The FLMI and CLU designations are LOMA sponsored.)

6.  Find out generally what else he thinks he is good at besides
COBOL.  For example, could he code an extract program, move data
to Access or EXCEL and massage it there?  If you are IBM, does he
understand FTP and other things of "Unix" origin?  If he is Unix
or PC, does he understand that IBM keeps files as records, not
byte streams?  The idea is to see if he might have something else
that could be useful in the future.  For example, what if you were
going to start posting rates on a Web Site?  Might he be able to
contribute, not that you have anything to do with web sites today.

7.  The one thing I find difficult (other than by watching a
person work) is to figure out how they approach problems.  Do they
spin wheels trying all combinations?  Do they stop and look up
something they don't know?  Can they get reasonable test data
together?  Do they accept errors and changes gracefully, learn
something, fix the problem, and move on?  Or will they fight to
the end to do something their way,  ignore advice from others, and
be possessive of the code?

Hope this gets you started thinking!
```
