```
So... I get a telephone call the other day and it is the usual, some 
unfortunate newbie at an agency was given a stack o' papers and told 'see 
who's available'.

She tells me that there's a possibility for jobs in two places, mainframe 
COBOL CICS DB2 work, MQ Series would be nice, in Raleigh, North Carolina 
and Merrimack, New Hampshire.

'Ah,', I say, 'that must be with Fidelity.'

Silence.  Sound of a jaw being dragged off a table.  'How... how did you 
know that?'

'Ma'am, when you've been doing it for a few decades and you *don't* know 
who has shops where... *then* you start asking questions.  What kind of 
rate are they offering?'

She was so off-balance that she gave me an honest response, 'We can do six 
months on a corp-to-corp for US$40 - US$50/hr.'

I laughed and confessed that I didn't know what they thought they were 
doing.  For a few dollars less they could outsource the job to Bangalore 
and here they expected, for this piddling amount more, to find someone 
willing to drop everything and on their own nickel show up in a 
suit-and-tie in Raleigh or Merrimack for that kind of money?

She commiserated and said I was the nicest response so far, usually she'd 
get these really old guys cursing at her... not that there's anything bad 
about being really old, of course.  She asked what I'd consider 
noticeable.

My reply was about how difficult this was... the rate Fidelity was using 
was so low-ball that it'd be hard to come back with anything that would 
not get tossed out.  I finally said 'What the heck, tell them you found a 
gem of a guy, show them my papers and tell them that if they come back 
with US$95/hr we can then begin to discuss sign-on bonuses, expenses and 
completion bonuses.'

There was that jaw-rising sound again... and I effusively reminded her 
that she was not responsible for these idiots setting figures that were 
guaranteed to bring in either second-rate talent or nobody at all.  I 
believe we parted on good terms.

DD
```

```
US$95/hr seems like a reasonable rate to me for them to get someone who 
knows what they are doing. The perceived wisdom is that there is a shortage 
of COBOL talent available and so, as in any marketplace, the rate rises to 
meet the demand.

Here's some thoughts on strategies I would consider were I looking for a job 
in this market.

1. You know Fidelity are looking for people. Why not shoot off a CV to their 
IT director and say:"I have the experience and skill you need and I can 
guarantee the work I do. As you don't know me, I would suggest we have a 
chat and if you are still interested I will work for you for three months on 
a low market rate (say $75/hr). At the end of this time when my credibility 
is established and you know I can be relied on, we should look at it again. 
Obviously, there is no Agency overhead or markup if we work directly and 
that saves both of us money."

(The idea of a "trial" is a fair one and I did this once on a contract that 
required me to move to another country. There were risks involved for them 
and me but it turned into a two year stint and the rates were excellent. I 
could have extended that contract indefinitely but my wife was hankering to 
get back to England and we had saved a substantial sum after two years...)

2. As a general principle, working through a pimp sucks noodles. But to go 
direct when you are an unknown quantity with the client, means you have to 
spend some time and shoe leather. It also means some outside-the-square 
thinking to encourage them to take a risk with you.

3. Outsourcing to Asia is becoming recognised as fraught. For people who 
know little about actually developing computer systems (usually COIs) the 
idea of getting program support for $8/hr is just too good to be passed up. 
It takes some months before the real damage in this approach becomes 
apparent. Although the work is done diligently, and to an acceptable 
standard of competence (in most cases I have seen) it is seldom what is 
actually required, due to poor communication and lack of  knowledge of the 
actual business. It takes more months to get it fixed up and and if you 
actually factored in the costs to the business caused by these delays, you 
find out that nothing was really saved and you have a poorer quality product 
than if it had been done at home.  Nevertheless, the lure of cheap labour 
remains very attractive and that may be why the rates offered by pimps are 
not what could reasonably be expected for a skilled professional.

There ARE some cases where it really DOES make sense to outsource some 
specific code cutting. (where the requirement is simple and very clearly 
defined and a very large amount of code must be produced), but there is 
still no alternative to the on-site professional who knows the business and 
is properly experienced in the tools and infrastructure being used.
```

```
I've worked for a couple of clients who employed off-shore resources
but were under the supervision of on-shore management.  Let me just
say you get what you pay for.  You pay $8-10 an hour for off shore
folks, then expect the level of expertise and ability to be in the
same range.  More and more IT offices are finally realizing this and
are not renewing off-shore contracts.  I'm involved with one such
group right now, i.e., they hired off-shore people to do their testing
and java code.  They must interface with the mainframe which means
they write java code which generates XML which gets processed by an
interface program on the mainframe which formats the request for the
proper application gets the response back from the application and
sends it back to the XML which feeds it back to the initiator.  

Whenever something goes wrong, it is immediately the mainframe's
fault.  Why?  Because the off-shore people have no debugging abilities
whatsoever and until you prove to them that the mainframe wasn't at
fault for the failure, they won't contact their counter-part java
programmer at the client for help.  So far, in 8 months, there have
been 10 "failures"  all of them deemed the mainframes fault and all of
them finally traced back to bad java code.  But it is a real pain to
prove the mainframe is not at fault.

This stuff does get reported up through the various layers of
management and they are seeing that what they pay for does not result
in good productivity or a good product and if it wasn't for us higher
paid consultants, they'd not have any product at all!.
```
