1) Full and complete expensing of everything possible (hotel, car,
gasoline, etc.) to a Corporate Credit Card so that there is *no*
possibility of your getting taxed for reimbursement of expenditures.

2) Every city (just about) has a per diem rate, permitted by the IRS,
where a certain amount per day (per diem) can be paid to you without taxes
being withheld.  As to whether this is in addition to your salary or taken
from your salary is another matter entire.

3) At *all* times do not hesitate to make it *perfectly* clear that 'I do
*not* travel on business in order to spend my own money'.

Now, let me speak of my own situation... I'm travelling a good bit for my
client right now but I am not an employee, I am a contractor/consultant
and an employee of a miserable pimp-agency.  The way I work things, since
the pimps are lazy and do not want to split out per diem, is that I use a
combination of 1) (everything that can be charged to a CorpoCard, is)
and... something else; I have made it know to *everyone* involved, client
and agency, that every week I toss several extra hours onto my timesheet
to make up for 'miscellany'.

This keeps everyone happy here.

A lot of this depends on whether you believe that your bosses think that you
are doing a good job, quite often a given for people sent to visit clients,
whether the travel is your choice or your companies choice (i.e., do you WANT
to travel or do they MAKE you travel) and whether you have the necessary
confidence.

If the company is making you travel, it is important to remember that
traveling is work. There is no need to travel in off hours unless you get
money/time for it.

If the travel is your choice you are not in a good negotiating position. They
can tell you to fly Sunday or forget about it.

As a side point to your off topic question, it is my belief that a person
traveling on company business should not spend more than one would on personal
travel, with the exceptions of schedule flexibility and business needs. 

In other words, if one would stay at a Holiday Inn on personal travel, one
should not demand a Hyatt Resort for company travel. If one would normally fly
coach class, one should not demand first class. 

The exceptions being that if one would normally stay over a weekend for the
cheaper fares one may choose to return earlier for a higher fare. If one would
normally rent a compact car, one may rent a full size if driving clients.
