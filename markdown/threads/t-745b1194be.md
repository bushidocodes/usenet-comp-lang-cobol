I'm not sure of sequencing, but one of the old timers can probably put it into
correct timeline  - I think Richard is probably wised up on this.

Before the PC took off, all that there was here in Calgary in the late 70's was one
small hobby shop in a back street. Enthusiasts used to go in and buy bits and
pieces to assemble their own computers. I think the Altair was the next thing to
happen.

Late 70's again,  Datapoint came out with their computer series - but this wasn't a
Mickey Mouse approach. A PC unit (computer, screen and disks - old style platters).
Metal casing and a heavy bugger to lift. I worked on their 32K Model using their
own language DATABUS - something like half way between Assembler and COBOL and
integrated with their own Operating System, which allowed you to do some neat
tricks. (At the time their support guy here in Calgary, who was a classical music
buff - could go to concerts and then phone the office to trigger the machine to get
software updates automatically from back East). They also had a 64K (which had a
COBOL - don't know who wrote it). I subsequently upgraded my application to their
Model ??? which used a true hard disk unit.

Radio Shack (aka Tandy), introduced their Model I - from his description, seems to
me like Volker had a souped up version. Out of sequence they then introduced the
Model III, (32K) one unit (screen, computer and *diskettes* - I think). Then they
introduced the Model II, (64K) again one unit - but with platters - on which I used
RM/COBOL (ANSI 74). As DD is always saying, 'my memory is porous' - one platter
(disk) size around 8 or 10 inches held 0.25 Million bytes. I was into it big time,
I had an auxiliary disk unit which took three more platters. These machines were
all, I think, what I refer to as battleship grey.

The Shack also around this time introduced their Color computer. Had a very loyal
following which hung on, way after the machine went out of fashion. Meanwhile I got
involved with a small fertilizer company that wanted to go multi-user - four
clerks.
I switched to their Model 16 (can't remember memory) but it was a nice White and
the OS was MS Xenix - their version of Unix. Now I'm using the RM/COBOL multi user
version. The pig was I had to develop programs at home on my Model II, then at
weekends when the office was empty, transfer from my Model II platters to the Model
16 platters and re-compile and test on the Model 16. The 16's platters, to the best
of my memory, were 1 MILLION bytes - Wow !

I think somewhere in all this the first PCs were becoming available. But at the
same time there was the Commodore 64 (coloured I think), smartly followed by the
Amiga and very popular. There is still an Amiga Club going strong in Calgary.

Oops. Don't forget the Apple series which had an extremely loyal following, dented
somewhat by the introduction of the "PC".

All the above becomes like Arthurian history with the arrival of the PCs - and we
know where it went from there.

Question for Volker - your COBOL on the Model I - who wrote the compiler ?
