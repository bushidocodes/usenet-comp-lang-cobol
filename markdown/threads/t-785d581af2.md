Hopefully my experience with a moderated group will be helpful.

Some years ago I was an active participant in rec.food.veg (RFV), a high-volume
forum about vegetarianism. One guy entered and pissed off everyone with
irrational arguments, many posts, unresponsiveness to rebuttal, dogmatism, etc.
He was Jai Maharaj, who was listed at the time in Top Twenty Netkooks. 

Animal rights activists, who call themselves Ethical Vegetarians and others
call The Religious Right, normally stand on the brink of intolerance.  Jai
pushed them over the edge. They lobbied for and eventually got
rec.food.veg.moderated. I say eventually because it took 3-4 weeks of USENET
bureaucracy -- writing a formal proposal, calling for votes, etc. 

Everyone should understand that the process does not "make CLC a moderated
group." It leaves the original CLC intact and adds an additional group named
CLC.moderated. Users may read and post to either or both.

What happened is a few of the goodie-goodies went off to RFV.moderated, where
they exchanged banal pleasantries and 'supported' each other by preaching to the
choir with no possibility of contradiction. The moderated group had no
substance. Its volume was less than 10% the original RFV, which kept going as
before. When I checked a year or two later, RFV.moderated was gone.

In my opinion the only appropriate reason for moderation is when the topic is so
abhorrent to the general population that the group will be overrun by hecklers.
Censoring reasonable opposition is not a valid reason. Silencing kooks is not
even a valid reason. USENET history shows that such attempts are inevitably
short-lived. Legitimate topics are:

..Religion -- atheism, witchcraft
..Quasi religion -- astrology, paranormal, metaphysics, new age
..Sexual preferences -- gay (nearly all are moderated), pedophelia
..Business models -- multi-level marketing
..Diseases associated with sex -- herpes
..Paranoia -- conspiracy theories
..Emotion-based legal/social -- abortion
..Borderline legality -- drugs, 2600

Physics and math attract more kooks than most people realize. They used to be
moderated, but are no longer. The only moderated programming forums are C, C++
and Perl. The unmoderated/moderated ratio of messages in C++ is 2:1, C is 267/5,
Perl is 20/3 (your newsserver's retention may vary). The latter two will be gone
soon. 

Without pontificating about the ethics of censorship, my argument is pragmatic:
it is unsuccessful unless the topic warrants it. COBOL is not such a topic.
