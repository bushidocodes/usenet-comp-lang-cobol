That is a clever idea, but it doesn't compile (at least under COBOL II).
Surprisingly the nastygram indicated that the group level (TOTALS) could
have a non-numeric literal for a value.

05 WS-TEST OCCURS 14 VALUE +0.
10 WS-TESS PIC S9(13)V99.

IGYGR1118-S A "VALUE" clause literal for a group item was not a
non-numeric literal. The "VALUE" clause was
discarded.

Putting the value, occurs, and pic in the 10 level will work, at least
in COBOL II.

I.e. 10 AMOUNT OCCURS 14 PIC... VALUE...

Don't forget the old 'overlapping move' trick (I must admit I forgot
exactly how to do this and had to ask a comrade). This method can be
used to quickly initialize _all_ table entries to _any_ specific values:

01 TABLE-1-INIT.
05 FILLER PIC 9(05) VALUE 12345.
05 FILLER PIC X(05) VALUE 'ABCDE'.
05 TABLE-1.
10 TABLE-1-ENTRY OCCURS 5 TIMES.
15 TABLE-1-ITEM-1 PIC 9(05).
15 TABLE-1-ITEM-2 PIC X(05).

then...

MOVE TABLE-1-INIT TO TABLE-1.

Just make sure that PIC's on the FILLER items match the TABLE-1-ENTRY
items exactly. Also, you will most likely get a compiler warning message
such as this one:

IGYPA3013-W Data items "TABLE-1-INIT (GROUP)" and "TABLE-1 (GROUP)" had
overlapping
storage. An overlapping move will occur at execution
time.

Who says you can't teach a new dog old tricks? (or something like
that...)

I disagree with the advice not to use the INITIALIZE verb. Early in the
days of Cobol II ('90-'91) I read lots of advice to avoid INITIALIZE
because there was a big performance penalty. However, we use it all over
the place and haven't seen any penalty - we're running on a BIG mainframe
with lost of MIPS & our system is I/O contstrained, not CPU constrained. I
would suggest doing some testing to evaluate the INITIALIZE verb - if you
see a penalty, avoid it. If no, use it - it's a great tool to avoid
creating maintenance nightmares.

Ok, Let me be specifically clear on when I recommend not to use
INITIALIZE.

When you have say a Cobol program that is processing say 10 mil records and
the program logic has an internal Cobol table of say 1500 occurrences. If
you use INITIALIZE on the 01 group level for the table, the program eat up
CPU cycles like candy. If you move spaces to the 01 group level for the
table. the program will also eat up CPU cycles.

How do I know this from experience in fixing performance problems. Yes, if
you have a small number of records or the logic is not trying to clear an
internal table, then go ahead and use INITIALIZE. But if you have a large
number of records to be processed with a large internal table, then get
ready to pay a higher bill for CPU cycles!

Try this test with the following internal table:

01 SOME-TABLE.
05 SOME-FIELD1 PIC X.
05 SOME-FIELD1 PIC X.
05 SOME-LARGE-OCCURS OCCURS 1500
INDEXED BY SOME-INDEX.
10 SOME-MORE-FLDS1 PIC X.
10 SOME-MORE-FLDS2 PIC X.
10 SOME-OTHER-NEST-TAB OCCURS 50
INDEXED BY NEST-INDEX.
15 NEST-FIELD1 PIC X(30).
15 NEST-FIELD2 PIC X(30).



test #1 - INITIALIZE SOME-TABLE.

test #2 - MOVE SPACES TO SOME-TABLE.

test #3 - Do neither!

Also, I am not referring to Joe Schmo's Made-in-the-Garage Compiler, I am
talking about a large scale MVS mainframe.

OH, I AM NOT JUST TALKING INCREASED CPU! THE ABOVE PROBLEM WAS A REAL LIFE
PERFORMANCE PROBLEM AT ONE OF OUR CLIENTS THAT WAS CORRECTED BY REMOVING
INITIALIZE. THE JOB WAS RUNNING ABOUT 5 HOURS AND AFTER REMOVING ONLY THIS
INITIALIZE THE JOB NOW RUNS 30-40 MINS. YOU DECIDE!
