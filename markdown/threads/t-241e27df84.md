One of those imponderables when you try to re-cast how history might have turned
out.

In another message Jerry picked up on an article that suggested Chamberlain's
1938 appeasement gave Britain breathing space to build up the RAF. Out in a
political wilderness, Churchill was sounding dire warnings about "NAH-zism" back
in 1935 and was well acquainted with the lack of readiness of the RAF through
friends in the Air Ministry and Professor Lindemann. The UK government had
exactly the same information.

The collapse of France, Belgium, Netherlands etc. was a pushover in 1940. Mistake
#1 - halting the Panzers when the depleted BEF was encircled at Dunkirk and
allowing the British and allies to escape to Britain.

Mistake # 2 - the RAF was exhausted by the end of the Battle of Britain, both
lack of aircraft and perhaps more importantly, trained fighter pilots. On a whim,
Luftwaffe attacks were directed at bombing London, and others, rather than
knocking the RAF out of the game.

Mistake # 3 - Hitler had to be a poor student of history. By attacking the USSR
he allowed himself to get between the vice of the British in the West and the
Russians in the East - exactly what Napoleon did, with the same results.

Remember there never were any sea battles fought in either the English Channel or
the North Sea. Any naval actions like the Bismarck or Graf Spe were carried out
in the Atlantic. The Admiralty would never commit to a concentration of ships in
the English Channel, (without superior air coverage, and the U-boat threat), as
evidenced by the small naval support at Dieppe. There was only a concentration of
big ships, given VAST air superiority on D-Day June 4, 1944.

(*** Dieppe : Canada, prior to 9/11 anniversary just coming up, has just
remembered the 60th anniversary of Wednesday August 19, 1942 - in some six hours,
our greatest loss of troops in WW2).

Given that scenario, the Nazis COULD and probably WOULD have won, had they
invaded England (Operation Sea Lion), shortly after Dunkirk. The shortest route
to conquering Britain had to be roughly Calais across the Channel to English
South East coast. Once the threat was known, the RN would have dispatched
cruisers and battleships from Plymouth and Portsmouth - but too late to be
effective - and sitting ducks for both U-boat and aerial attacks. With Britain
conquered then the Nazis could have dwelt in some depth with what they wanted to
do about the Soviets. (That's accepting the fact that their war machine was
dependent upon Eastern European oil supplies).

The USA was providing lend-lease plus a major food supplier for UK. Had Britain
been defeated, and one can assume a grab for British colonies in the Far East by
Japan, and possibly looking enviously towards you folks <G>, would the US have
been provoked or even felt a need to jump in ?

I still contend the US didn't 'save any butts'. They were in the game for their
own interests - and don't knock them for that.

Jimmy, Calgary AB

> In another message Jerry picked up on an article that suggested Chamberlain's

These are not new ideas, wee A.J.P.Taylor 'Origins of 2ndWW'.  It has

> The collapse of France, Belgium, Netherlands etc. was a pushover in 1940. Mistake
> #1 - halting the Panzers when the depleted BEF was encircled at Dunkirk and
> allowing the British and allies to escape to Britain.

They had no reason to suspect that the BEF could escape.  They
expected a strongly defended stand and did not have sufficient forces
to attack such a large force.  A few days were required for the
Panzers to assemble sufficient men and arms into the area - they were
committed elsewhere already, and it was expected that a seige would
get results.
 
> Mistake # 2 - the RAF was exhausted by the end of the Battle of Britain, both
> lack of aircraft and perhaps more importantly, trained fighter pilots. 

Not true. The RAF was less exhausted than the Germans.  In fact the
RAF had _more_ aircraft at the end of the BoB then they had at the
beginning.

> On a whim,
> Luftwaffe attacks were directed at bombing London, and others, rather than
> knocking the RAF out of the game.

It wasn't a 'whim', it was a reaction to the carefully contrived raids
on Berlin.  Of course Churchill would never admit to deliberately
manipulating the bombing of London, but it saved the country.

> Mistake # 3 - Hitler had to be a poor student of history. By attacking the USSR
> he allowed himself to get between the vice of the British in the West and the
> Russians in the East - exactly what Napoleon did, with the same results.

The whole point of the war was to recover the land that Germany had
'won' in the first war before being forced to give it back.

> Remember there never were any sea battles fought in either the English Channel or
> the North Sea. Any naval actions like the Bismarck or Graf Spe were carried out
> in the Atlantic. 

June 1940.  Glorious sunk, Scarnhhorst and Geisenau damaged.  In what
way was this not a 'Naval action' ?

> Given that scenario, the Nazis COULD and probably WOULD have won, had they
> invaded England (Operation Sea Lion), shortly after Dunkirk. 

Nonsense.  'Shortly after Dunkirk' they had no air superiority and had
not even established the bases they later used for BoB.  They also had
no transport that could invade at that time.  They never had such
things as landing craft (as used by allies) but managed to modify
ordinary river barges for use, but there were not sufficient of these
until months later (if in fact there ever were enough).

> The shortest route
> to conquering Britain had to be roughly Calais across the Channel to English
> South East coast. 

They couldn't just drive their panzers over the channel.

Any casual glance at the SE Coast opposite Calais shows it to be
cliffs (White Cliffs of Dover).  There are only a few easily defended
access routes in that area.  Without air superiority (which the BoB
proved they didn't ever have) any invasion attempt would have been
easily defeated ...

> Once the threat was known, the RN would have dispatched
> cruisers and battleships from Plymouth and Portsmouth - but too late to be
> effective - and sitting ducks for both U-boat and aerial attacks. 

  ... without need for any RN involvement beyond the existing channel
based forces.

There never was any opportunity for an invasion.  The Germans had no
capability of transporting and supplying an invasion force at any
time, by the time that they had built up a supply of river barges it
had been shown that there was no air superiority and the deteriorating
weather would have wrecked any attempt, river barges with tanks on
them needed extraordinary calm seas and a protected harbour to unload.

Even loading and reloading the barges would have been a significant
problem because the required harbours were easily attacked.
