Chad,

You are correct about the 68 and 74 versions, but the 1985 standard does have
an intrinsic function for current-date (FUNCTION CURRENT_DATE) that does
return the current system date with a 4 digit year. Because there were
millions of programs using "MOVE CURRENT-DATE TO ..."; that special register
was left as is with 6 digit dates.

The COBOL-85 standard makes provision for the 4-digit year. I'm not
sure if you've really grasped the concept of COBOL being defined as it
is. The "sophisticated development team" was drawn from
compiler-writers and computer manufacturers - not the compiler-user
community.

Those of us who programmed in COBOL at the time were not one-language
people, and many of us worked in industry sectors that were conscious of
the need to process the century in some way or other. For instance,
anyone who needed to process vital dates (birth, death, etc.) were only
too aware that the standard date options on systems (not just in the
COBOL language) were insufficient and we had our own standard date
routines, usually written in Assembler.

Of course, that was when most sites wrote all of their applications
software. When they came to purchase software packages, e.g. accounts
software, from vendors who did not have the same awareness of vital
dates, that was when the trouble began. The packages had to seem to
provide everything at lower cost - not a difficult thing to do - and
they were sold on the basis of support and regular improvements. A
sound concept. Of course, if you needed to interface with the packages,
as most places did, then you had to provide a 6-digit date.

Add to this the fact that most systems provided only a 6-digit date and
you can see the need to process anything larger fell by the wayside.

Consider 1968, there were 32 years to the end of the century. What are
you going to be doing in 2029? Is a computer that you manufacture still
going to be in use then? Hell, my 486DX50-based PC is on its last legs
now and it's only five years old, though I do intend to keep it until it
becomes impractical to use any more but I still reckon that it won't
make it to its tenth year.

Anyway, I digress. Most people write dates with a 2-digit year. Check
out your own personal notes - how often have you written a 4-digit
year? I find myself doing so more frequently now but when I write a
cheque I still use the 2-digit year. Take a look at what happened at
the beginning of this century, people would only use a 2-digit year.
You have to consider this mindset being transferred into hardware and
software development in environments where the change in century was not
a major consideration to most people.

So don't try and put the blame on COBOL, or the Committees, or the
programmers and designers. It's a waste of time and effort to conduct a
witch-hunt. We have a situation that many of us are dealing with and we
don't have time to seek out the perceived culprits.

If you want to, though, how about looking at those who pay us for our
work? Those who dictate that a project must be implemented within
certain financial constraints, who won't pay for storage devices, who
won't pay for good programming skills, who won't look further than three
years ahead, who won't be in the same jobs in three years time and do
not, therefore, understand the concept of true investment.

Why not ask the guys who want Windows NT on their desktop because it is
the latest thing that they have read about? Why not ask those who
decided to go the client/server route with traditional mainframe
applications for no better reason that CS was fashionable? How many of
those guys were really impressed more by the worth of their current
investment than by the fashion of the day? How many of those guys
ordered sweeping system changes because they understood what they had
got, rather than from the angle that they had to show some tangible
reason for their employment and fat salaries? Believe me, I've seen
more waste of corporate funds on hair-brained "upgrades" that I have
seen wasted on the Y2K problem.

No, don't try blaming a language that has a hard-enough time getting
credibility in ignorant academic circles (that influence the grauates
each year), look to the products of your own institution and how they
are primed.

Meanwhile, we'll just get on with the job in hand because that's what
we're paid for. You want a client-server system? We'll write it but
don't blame us if it costs a bomb. You don't need the century taken
care of because the system's only got a projected lifetime of five
years? We'll write it but don't blame us.
