Yes, I've worked on sites where they employed cardboard programmers. They
are ideal employees, never demand a raise, never bitch about workloads,
never argue with management, and best of all, they are incapable of
independent thought.

The trick is to have ONE human being on the site (who has to be paid), let
him think he is part of a team (but in fact the rest of the team are
cardboard programmers) and he'll actually solve his own problems by
interacting with his cardboard colleagues.

Leaving the above whimsy aside for a moment, there was a time in the 1980s
when IBM used development teams comprised of 'brains' and 'bunnies'. There
would be one 'brain' and a number of 'bunnies'.  It is a similar concept...
'brains' working in isolation were not as prodctive as those working with
'bunnies'.

(Of course the talent employed at IBM was such that some of the brains HAD
to work in isolation; they simply weren't aware that there were other people
on the planet...)

Pete.

PS. I actually support the technique of explaining something to someone as a
means of analysing it for yourself. I've had two marriages... A cat or dog
make much better cardboard programmers...
