Charlie's recent post about the last indignity made me think some more about 
Management and Staff.

Good managers realise that staff are the most important resource in any 
organization, and value them accordingly, but most of us have experienced 
Managers who don't share that view and see staff as easily replaceable 
cannon fodder who can and should be treated shabbily, bullied, intimidated 
and back stabbed whenever possible.

While I'd like to think such "managers" don't have much of a career path, 
there are enough of them around to give cause for alarm.

So what can people do about it?

Does bad management persist because we allow it to?

Suppose people just refused to accept bullying, unfairness, and 
intimidation? (I have done this on a number of occasions but as a skilled 
contractor who can easily move down the road to another place, and actually 
expects to do this, even on the "good" sites, when the project is finished, 
perhaps it easier for me to take a stand than for permanent company 
employees who are signed up for the duration.)

How can Staff raise issues with their Management in a way that will ensure 
their cards are not marked for the future? How do you deal with a petty, 
vindictive, mean-spirited manager, without actually destroying any chance of 
you having a future in the organization?

Doesn't this present a political minefield that IT guys are generally not 
trained, by job or nature, to be good at?

The first step is to realize that your pocket Napoleon is answerable to 
somebody. He may think he's God but there are higher powers.

In fact, here's a few thoughts I remembered, that might come in handy if you 
are working for a bad manager:

1. Nothing is true unless it is written down. When he asks you to do 
something that is blatantly stupid, send an email to him and request 
confirmation that you have understood correctly what he wants. If you see 
problems, observe politely that you have reservations (list them) but you 
respect his right to manage and will do whatever he requires, as soon as he 
has confirmed it by reply. If he doesn't respond, don't do it. When he pulls 
you up because it isn't done, tell him you are awaiting confirmation from 
him. If his voice starts to rise and he tells you to "damn well do it", play 
the broken record: "Just as soon as you confirm in writing by responding to 
my mail." Point out, without becoming angry, that you have severe 
reservations about it and therefore need it to be clear that he is 
requesting it. This is a fair and reasonable position. If it comes to an 
impasse, see point 2.

2. You have an impasse. It could be about anything, but it has reached a 
point where neither party will budge. Tell him you require the issues to be 
raised to the next level of management. DO NOT go behind his back; make it 
clear that, as no further compromise seems possible, the only way to resolve 
the issue is by a directive from above. Understand that the predilection of 
his Boss will be to support him, not you (if his Boss is any good - good 
managers support their staff, but the really good managers support ALL their 
staff and are more concerned with what's right rather than who's right.) 
Nevertheless, if the guy is being an arse and trying to enforce something 
that is obviously unreasonable, his Boss will realise it and you can win. 
NEVER do something you feel is morally or legally wrong, just because you 
were told to. The Nuremburg defense didn't work in Nuremburg and it won't 
work in your office. However, state clearly that you cannot comply and why 
you can't. Genuinely seek to find a compromise, but if you can't, raise the 
issue to the next level.

3. It is natural for Human Beings to like and dislike each other. You are 
allowed to dislike your Boss, but he is the Boss. He has a right to manage 
(or even mismanage, as the case may be...). Don't be rude to him (or anybody 
else - heightened emotions are no excuse for bad manners), but accept that 
you won't always be able to stay calm, objective, and polite, even though 
you should strive to. Try and minimise lapses in this regard :-)
As a general rule, don't hit anybody... :-)

4. NEVER apologise unless you are sorry.

5. If you are working for a bad manager, don't look to him for approval or 
praise. He is not a role model you need to emulate or really have any truck 
with. Polite, honest, but distant. Don't denigrate him to others (in fact, 
it is really bad practise to denigrate any of your colleagues and it will 
come back to bite you if you do. Applaud what they do right, no comment on 
what they do wrong, except in the sense of analysing to prevent a 
repetition.)

Looking back over a long career I can honestly say there is a case for the 
proposition that we generally get the managers we deserve. If you allow 
yourself to be bullied and intimidated in the workplace, or accept stupid, 
pointless assignments, how can you then blame your manager?

All comments looked forward to... :-)

Pete.

Thanks for the posts, Charlie. There are some perfect examples of poor 
management in what you wrote, but there are things you could do better too.

For example, NEVER allow a manager to stress you out to the point where it 
could affect your health. I've had bullying managers who said to me: "I want 
this on my my desk by Thursday" (Tough guy, street smart showing the plebs 
that he was not to be trifled with.) My response became different as I 
acquired more experience...

1. As a young, eager-to-please programmer: "I'll do my very best."  (work 
all night for several nights and have it there by Thursday).
2. After some years of doing the above and realizing it actually made no 
difference to anything: "I don't know if it can be done by Thursday, but 
I'll get as much of it done as I can."
3. After actually becoming a professional and realizing that the difference 
between amateurs and professionals is that professionals pace themselves, do 
not allow themselves to be over stressed and work steadily through problems 
making sure that proper sleep is obtained to keep the engine running and 
realizing that very few problems in commercial data processing involve 
people dying if they are "late": "Well, good luck with that. Hope you find 
someone who can do it by Thursday. I can't."

This last usually evinced: "What do you mean? I need it by Thursday."

"Why? Will the world end if you have it for Monday? I'm telling you that I 
cannot do it by Thursday and I can't. I can't do it because.... <list of 
real reasons>. Now, if someone else is prepared to accept it and undertake 
to do it by Thursday, then good luck to them. Would you rather I said "Yes, 
Sir" knowing full well it won't be there, or would you rather have an honest 
appraisal that allows you to manage it?"

This is part of the process of "training" your manager. He needs to 
understand that demanding something won't make it happen, that you can be 
trusted to be honest with him, and that you will always do your best to 
meeet REALISTIC requirements.

The other side of that coin is when YOU become a manager. As you are not 
stupid, and have been a programmer for a good number of years, you know much 
better than to demand something by Thursday. "This is pretty urgent because 
<whoever> needs it completed so that <whoever> can do the <whatever>. When 
do you think you could reasonably have it ready?"

Unfortunately, there are poor employees as well as poor managers and some of 
them will try and sandbag you.

"Awwww... that's gonna take about six days." (You know full well it is three 
days work...)

"Are you sure?"

"Yep."

"OK, don't worry about it. I'll do it myself." .. or... "OK, I'll give it to 
<eager young programmer>.  Now, perhaps you can start on this <piece of 
tedious, boring, shit work, that is not time critical>"

This is part of "training" your staff... :-) They have to learn that you are 
not a COI and you won't be bullshitted by them. Note that there are no 
emotional responses involved in any of the above. It is just taking care of 
Business.

As a manager, I have never demanded anything from anybody by a certain time. 
I have explained why it needs to be done and allowed them to negotiate a 
time period that will allow them to do it successfully. (You should want 
your team to succeed and support them in their efforts to do so. Make sure 
tools, environments, access permissions and everything needed to do the job 
are available, then make an agreement with them about the work and help them 
to keep that agreement. If it looks like it is not going to be there, they 
should tell you as soon as possible so you can address the implications of 
that, manage the impact, and get them help. Don't even begin to tolerate a 
blame culture; don't blame people for failure and don't allow them to blame 
others. If things go wrong, seek to understand what happened and make sure 
it doesn't recur.)

I think you may have been a bit intimidated by the CARES project, Charlie. 
You are actually much more capable than you may think you are :-). Of 
course, if you are poorly managed, that doesn't help your morale or your 
capability. Attitude is a very important part of anything and part of the 
management role is to foster that attitude (by making sure that people 
succeed and thereby growing their confidence). Just imagine for a moment if 
you had had a manager something like what I have outlined. Do you think 
you'd still be retired?

This is the waste in industry. Good guys like you get shoved aside by 
managers who have never been trained as managers and don't know the first 
thing about managing people. They percolate up the hierarchy because they 
have to get salary increments and the only way that can happen is by 
promoting them. The Peter Principle states that people get promoted to their 
level of incompetence, but it is actually much worse than that. In some 
places people get promoted way BEYOND their level of incompetence and end up 
failing to do jobs that have a significant effect on the organization as a 
whole, and a devastating effect on the teams they are supposed to be 
managing.

I was doing some management mentoring with a manager here a few nights ago. 
He is a good person and a good manager (not in the IT industry) but was 
having some trouble with one or two team members.  He came to the 
realization that he simply wasn't trained to deal with people (in this case, 
women :-)) and had never seen it as part of his job requirement. He WANTED 
to be a good manager but he was at a loss. We chatted for a while and I 
suggested a few courses of action. He said it sounded a bit "touchy feely". 
(This is a Rugby player...) I thought about it and he was right. But the 
fact is that sometimes you need to be "touchy feely" in order to relate. I 
asked him if he was capable of doing it or would he just feel embarrassed. 
He agreed to try and we'll see what happens during the coming week :-). In 
fact what he refers to as being "touchy feely" is really just being honest 
and up front. But many people (especially poor managers) have problems with 
that. Instead of being real they promote an image of themselves that they 
learned from movies. They see proper communication as "weakness".

I think there is a place for a "Management Handbook" to help people who find 
themselves escalated to the point where they need to manage others and 
haven't been trained to do so. With the drop off in the economy and 
consequent lessening of work for me I am working on a novel that will take 
the rest of this year. But after that I'll review and update the previous 
handbook I shelved and see if I can get all this encapsulated wisdom into a 
hard copy before I kick off to big Computer Room in the sky... :-)

Enjoy your retirement, Charlie. It is the start of a new phase in your life.

and satisfaction in achieving goals. "Success breeds success." It is an old 
adage, but a true one. In reality, success breeds confidence and confidence 
breeds further success...

the applications; it is in the Data Access Layer which is where it should 
have been all along...

Both DATA CONVERSION and CODE CONVERSION are fully automatic with the
