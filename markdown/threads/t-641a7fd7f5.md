This is a wonderful short list of why I so much like New Zealand.   My most
recent visit was 1999.  Need to return for a Haka fix.   Nothing like a rousing
dance-along to Ka Mate to get one in the right frame of mind for taking on the
world. You did forget to include immigrants from the Planet Salo in the Crab
Nebula.   Must not overlook those peculiar but enchanting inhabitants.  :-)

Agree with those who understand that an employee's personal life is not his
work life.   It especially does not matter about a person's politics, ethnicity,

sexual prefererence, planet or country of ancestral origin, physical handicaps
unrelated to job performance, or even minor mental disability.  With regard to
the last category,  programming actually attracts people with certain mental
problems, and those same people are often able to turn that seeming disability
into an asset when programming computers.

The real test is for the manager to understand how to skillfully leverage the
differences into an effective team.   This is essential because so many projects

these days do require a programming team, and the members of the team must
learn how to work together.   Sadly, most managers are not competent enough
to do this well.   As the struggle to come to grips with their own inadequacies,

they often substitute stereotype and unconscious bigotry for those shortcomings.

The attempt to be humorous about certain categories of people by some authors
in this forum (nipple rings,  liberals,  vegetarians, etc.) only highlights the
deeper
issue that began as a discussion of "age discrimination."    The perception that

older workers will not be as productive as younger workers has long since
been discredited by serious research.   It has been correctly observed that
younger managers are often threatened by the idea of managing those who
are older and more experienced.    Many of these young managers are
inexperienced
as managers let alone as managers of those with more life behind them.
Therefore,
it is important for the older worker, and older worker job candidate, to put
them
at ease and help them understand that they are willing to be team players.  The
older worker must put that maturity to work on his/her own behalf instead of
taking a defensive stance early in the recruitment process.   Unfortunately,
when
one expects to be rejected on the basis of some anticipated prejudice, that
expectation often comes through during the interview.    It is the classic
example of a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Almost all managers want people who can do the job, especially in programming.
Even younger managers will hire older workers if they believe they can sustain
their own control needs and get good results.  We older workers must be careful
not to demonstrate a kind of reverse discrimination by assuming that younger
managers are not competent to evaluate our abilities correctly.   They will
see our prejudice toward them just as we might think we see theirs.

So, in addition to all the other people who we discriminate against, let's
include
those young programmers, young managers, and young entrepreneurs who might
just have the job opening that is perfect for you, if you demonstate your
maturity
instead of your resentment.

Peter. Kia kaha.  Kia menemene.  Kia manawanui.

Lao Xiao Hai
