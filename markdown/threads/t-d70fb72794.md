>I also tried writing a TCAM control address space in PL/I using the 
>Transient/Pending support, ended up doing it in assembler, better TCAM

My first PL/I project was similar.  I wrote a TP control program in
Assembler using a German queueing system called GENA (roughly
equivalent to TCAM).  I wrote the terminal control, messaging and
database management in Assembler, but the application layer was all in
PL/I.  So I learned an awful lot about generated object code before I
even wrote my first PL/I program!  (Later I worked on a PL/I compiler
for a micro system).

My first "real" PL/I program was a statistical analysis of the
performance, transaction and error logs I'd built into the system.  I
remember revelling in the power of PL/I compared to Cobalt, and I
still have fond memories of a single IF statement which flowed across
4 pages of the listing -- just to decide whether or not to skip to a
new page.  The things we did as kids!

All of this ran under DOS on a 360/40 with 128K.  Response time was
sub second and I still ran two batch partitions and full spooling.

I ran the upgrade to a 370, but left before the app was converted to
CICS.  I heard later that response time increased to 3 seconds, which
really made my day!

>I wrote a TPUT/TGET interface for PL/I, a couple pages of assembler, and
>used it to write a full screen desk calculator in PL/I; did the 3270 
>attributes, SBA, etc. in PL/I.

At one point I wrote a series of Assembler macros to handle the 3270
data streams under CICS.  It worked much better than IBM's BMS,
allowed for much easier screen definition and totally minimized the
actual data stream.  Remember when _that_ was important -- you know
when 2400 bps was _fast_?  Wasn't that when we had to worry about
saving a couple of bytes here and there by . . .  Oh _that's_ how this
Y2K date-rape business got started <G>.

>Good discussion Infomagic, :Dave, thanks for the trip down memory lane.
>I've decided to ease up on the denial-heads, you two and I have too much
>work to do... and certainly they've figured out that there's a couple
>dozen or so c.s.y2k'ers who are cranking code.

In case you hadn't noticed, I don't follow on to many of them either.
It's not worth the effort any more.  Evolution in action!

I've seen the same sort of denial when I teach multi-engine flying.
One of the things I like to do is simulate an engine failure
immediately after liftoff from a runway with trees at the end.  The
flight manual says the plane will climb on one, and it does.  But not
fast enough to clear those trees.

You have no idea how difficult it is to sit there watching the trees
move UP in the windshield (meaning we're not going to clear them).
Eventually a look of sheer and abject terror appears on their face as
they realize that there's _nothing_ they can do to keep us from dying
in a great ball of fire.  When I see that look I bring the power back
on the "dead" engine and let him climb on out.  Don't say anything,
though.  The silence says it all.

(Note to other instructors:  Don't do this without proper preparation
and safety procedures.  I'll gladly send you mine).

_Next_ time I _guarantee_ the student knows in advance that he can't
make it, he accepts this and lands on the remaining runway, safely,
even if that would mean running the plane off the runway and perhaps
even totaling it.  But the occupants would survive.

Y2K pollyannas are a lot like this.  They're flying into the trees on
one.  After all it's still flying, and they know the procedures for
dealing with the problem (i.e. fixing bits of code).  They just can't
understand that losing 50% of the (processing) power means losing 80%
of the climb/economic performance.  And they just won't listen to a
more experienced instructor!
