Dear CLC'ers,

I'm back. Extended absence due to the passing of my wife last December, and managing the estate.

I appreciate the comments to (OT) Software License Contracts and Trade Secrets from last November, and now I am picking it up again.

I will recap, update, and then ask if anyone wants to participate in my mini Independent Verification and Validation. It has to be pro bono, but we can use it get out names out a bit, and it would not be very much work for most.

The update will include info on how a local government has shown egregious behavior in this procurement. Which needs to be ameliorated.

Those interested in participating, contact me offline at klshafer-at-att.net.

To recap from last time:

Consider these two examples:

County One -

"Customer may not distribute any of the Documentation for use outside of Customer's primary place of business except to the extent required by law, including the Georgia Open Records Act. Vendor hereby asserts that its user documentation and training manuals are trade secret information of vendor and should not be disclosed under the Georgia Open Records Act. Vendor will provide Customer with an affidavit regarding such trade secret information."

County Two -

"Customer may not distribute any of the Documentation for use outside Customer's Dispatch Center."

Note that the first example is explicit in justifying the restriction as protection of trade secrets. Note also that the second example is even more restrictive location-wise than the first - circulation of materials is confined to one building, and probably one room.

Seeing as how these restrictions apply to overt behaviors of the system, presumably the user interface, and the visible manifestation of how the system appears to its users, and is NOT referring to software source, technical architecture, database specifications, interface definitions, or the like, it seems to me that is is egregiously overly-restrictive.

Consider also that in Example Two, the County did not even issue a formal RFP (Request for Proposal) with accompanying list of "required features" on how the system is intended to perform. How could anyone outside the dispatch center even inform himself as to how the system should perform, let alone validate that it indeed is performing as it should?

Update:


The system in question is an E911 Emergency Response Computer Aided Dispatch System. The end users are law enforcement, firefighters, and ambulance. The system is working pretty well for law enforcement, but has been buggy for the firefighters. Quite a number of features that were present in the old, legacy system, were NOT carried over into the new system, and they were and are features the firefighters are counting upon.

Note the fundamental problem above - local government did NOT issue an RFP, and they did NOT come up with a list of requirements or specifications.

The contract that resulted has a section for Specifications, but it is blank, and Freedom of Information Act requests failed to turn up ANY specs.

With no list of requirements, no set of specs, no RFP, and the inhibiting effect of the trade secret protection, how is anyone outside the dispatch center able to determine what the system is supposed to do, let alone to determine if it is doing it correctly!

Furthermore, additional FOIA requests were made for typical documents such as -
- vendor testing results
- government user acceptance testing results
- spreadsheets of defect statuses, severity, priority, resolution, etc.
- evaluation criteria used to "score" competing vendors and the scoring method
- technical specifications
- system architecture and design documentation
- specifications for customization (of which there was some)
- documentation on procedure to convert "configuration items" from old to new system
- any project management documentation, such as work breakdown schedule

The project was unable to provide ANY of that! ANY of that! One would think they might not have all of it, but surely they would have SOME of it, but NO!

What they did provide was a list of defects already reported by the township fire departments TO the dispatch center. A list that I had already indpendently obtained FROM the township fire departments.

I appeared multiple times before County Council meeting, pleading with them NOT to approve a budget for $1M in maintenance fees (on top of $1.3M of licensing fees already paid) UNTIL an IV&V had rooted out all the bugs and provided documentation. They ignored my ples, but worse than that, the County Commissioners had signed a maintenance contract for TEN YEARS OF MAINTENANCE PREPAID IN ADVANCE! And the County Council approved the appropriation!

To add insult to injury, the "discussion" centered around the county's liability for "breaching" the contract if they did not pony up the money. One Council member pointed out, "The vendor has already said they were going to use the money for financial expansion, and they've alreaady hired new salesmen."

Incredibly, local government was more "sensitive" to the business expansion of the vendor than it was to verify that any maintenance, during the warranty period or after, was being done! So send them another $1M, during the warranty period, even though there is no WRITTEN documentation as to how they have done any maintenance at all!

It was all I could do to contain my apoplectic fit.

Throughout there were a number of mealy-mouthed explanations by the usual cadre of suspects (politicians and managers) such as "bugs are the nature of software", "in applications like this the vendor dictates the testing", "these projects always have these types of problems", "you can't expect a new system to immediately have all the features the old system had", and other management-speak of the most stupid variety I have ever encountered.

I simply cannot abide this. I CANNOT ABIDE THIS. If I do only one more computer thing in my life, it will be to do my best to correct this completely unacceptable situation. The E911 system has already had some performance glitches, and in emergency response, minutes, even seconds, mean the difference between life and death. It is "mission critical" in the true sense of the word.

I eagerly await your comments.

Ken
