Barbara:

I am glad that someone from IBM is participating in the news group. The
variety of questions, concerns and comments are real issues to many of
us. I'm pleased as punch to be able to participate with a group of
people with similar problems and interests.

Even though IBM is a major player in this business (bigger than alot of
Microsoft advocates would like to give IBM credit for), I sometimes feel
that IBM has lost touch with an important group, as is represented in
this news forum.

IBM does an outstanding job keeping the COBOL products at the highest,
current ANSI standards. But I do not feel that IBM is devoting the time
to COBOL that it does to other languages. PL/1 seems to get more
attention than COBOL, in terms of sopistication. For example, you can
use PL/1 for MVS operating system uses. Why cannot IBM make their COBOL
do the same. Granted, ANSI standards determines a great deal of COBOL's
functionality and those standards can be credited for a great deal of
COBOL's success. But I also feel that 10-14 years between standards is
hurting COBOL, too.

While products like Visual-Age are powerful, almost everything created
for a COBOL implementation outside of the mainframe uses something else
to accomplish the task. Other vendor's products use scripting languages
or Basic-like languages to create GUI's, for example. The vendors who do
use a COBOL approach are hard to find and are the products I use. Why
cannot the COBOL vendor community create a common syntax for COBOL to
keep up with current trends as an extension to the ANSI standards? Why
can't COBOL have native capabilities to do things as easily and routinely
as 'C' or PL/1? Pointer, bit manipulation and the like should not even
be an issue of discussion because it is laking in COBOL after this many
years.

While I would agree completely that COBOL is first and foremost a
business programming language, the industry has changed, nevertheless.
Artificial constraints on COBOL's flexibility need to be removed.

I am not an advocate of doing everything from scratch. Good tools are
very important. I don't have the time to do everything the hard way.
But Cobol has to be promoted as the language of choice for business and
kept current as quickly as possible. I feel much more can and should be
done. After all, the number of Cobol programmers and Cobol code out
there does say a lot about Cobol's importance.

I would very much like to hear your personal thoughts on this issue as
well as IBM's position. My sincere apologies for any brashness. These
comments are not intended as criticisms towards you, IBM or any other
person. I must admit, however, that I have been very frustrated about
this issue for some time.

Thanks,

Mike Dodas

Mike,

I appreciated your lively post!

IBM is very concerned about keeping COBOL up to date with the industry.
There's a huge investment out there in COBOL, and we want to make
sure businesses can -- and want to -- continue to leverage that
investment. We want to make sure that businesses can take advantage
of new technology with the language that they've relied on for the last
twenty or more years.

We are active in development of the future COBOL standard, and are
working to make COBOL a more general-purpose language. We are working
hard to expedite the standards process. However, we are quite willing
to precede the standard when we have strong requirements from our
customers to do so. For instance:
o We supported pointers and floating point well before the standard.
o We recently delivered OO extensions to COBOL, based on but preceding
the standard. This has been a major win in terms of giving businesses
a viable path into object-oriented programming, as well as making a
clear statement that COBOL is still alive and thriving.

You can also do some pretty cool things with our VisualAge COBOL. For
example, you can use COBOL as a scripting language for creating GUIs.
Your COBOL program can use DDE to talk to other desktop applications
like Lotus 1-2-3. And COBOL applications can use SOM-enabled class
libraries written in other languages.

So the upshot is: we are committed to promoting COBOL as the language
of choice, and we're very interested in the opinions of you and the
other contributors to this newsgroup.

Barbara Price, IBM
