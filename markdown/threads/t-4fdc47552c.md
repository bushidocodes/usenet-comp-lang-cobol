A simple example of the need for good resolution of transactions might be a
security system that is defending a mainframe across a firewall.  How rapidly
are the high-speed lines presenting us with traceable hacker attempts to break
in? 

Such a system must have a resolution as fast as current technology, period. The
next day the time resolution must be faster. This responsibility never eases.

Assume that the hacker is coming in on a line connected to the demand deposit
accounts of the poster. Does the poster want the financial institution to be
able to keep up with the hacker? Does the poster want the defensive system to
be as precise in its resolution of sequence of events as the assaultor is? Does
the poster care if we protect the account?

Would the poster prefer that we look at inbound hacker transactions as
duplicates?

Does the poster care about the solvancy of an institution holding that account
even if it is some other account holder(s) that get hurt? Do you want your
money in a bank where the managers are nor concerned about clock resolution on
there machines?

I assure you that hackers can present more than 18.2 assaults in a second on a
single account, and on thousands of accounts! I assure you that they realize
that the clock resolution IS a weak point.  I assure you that they realize many
folks are not up to speed on this issue. They like your approach. Please tell
them the location of your machines. Let us know the shops you worked in in the
past, too.

We like look-alike time stamps, they're fun. You can tell its Matel, its swell!

Some one in one of these notes in this thread, claimed that you can not
synchronize CPU's to anything more than  something like .047 seconds (or
hundreths of a second). I deleted that post so can't quote it exactly. I'd like
to give proper attribution because the notion is important for time critical
functions.

You actually can get devices nearly synchronized if you use triangulation with
an intermediate location, and iterate through approximations. You then hit some
threshold in the technology you are using to blip the clock ticks, but that
certainly could be a very small increment.

 The need to do that in business apps is surely rare. But you could do it.  A
more problematic issue is that the semantics of time on different devices gets
difficult if you resolve very finely. Since the capturing of an event takes
finite time, then it matters if we stamp before, during, or after the cyles for
the capture (or any other processing). 

Most applications need uniqueness either to record actual sequence, or to
impose a sequence to maintain the order of the information. Exact time to an
exreme resolution has very little meaning for a human perceiver. No one is
claiming that the exactitude is useful in itself in a single instance; it is
the sequence that matters - either the sequence as things occur, or the
sequence we want the information ordered by.


Very often for recordation of human actions, a resolution down to nothing more
than a second, with an artificial extension that just counts whatever a machine
might enumerate, is fine. But not in a multiprocessing system which permits
concurrent access to related information.  

It is especially true that large data stores connected to communication lines
need very precise unique identifiers.

The sequence in which things are done is of the essence in finite state machine
behavior. If you can not detect sequence, you are quite vulnerable.

No clock is as accurate as the master clocks, this means that to keep
as accurate as possible each clock must be adjusted to the
reference.  This adjustment could happen at any time and could mean
that seconds, or parts of seconds are skipped or repeated.

: Very often for recordation of human actions, a resolution down to nothing more

Which is why a timestamp should not be used and a sequence generator
should be used.  The actual time of these events is usually
irrevelant, the sequence is critical.  Clock times accross several
different machines, server farms, distributed systems, multiple
comms controllers, is not a guaranteed sequence.
