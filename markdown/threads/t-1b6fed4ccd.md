I wish that I had caught the beginnings of thisthread.It isobvious
that someone hasn't a clue about contracting. I have been contracting
since 1989 and I think that I have earned the right to compare
contracting to employment.

1) In contracting, you never know where you will be working or for how
long. They could get rid of you any time.

Can you say 'lay-off'. I feel that I have more control over what I do
now that I contract. I know the minute that I walk in the door that
my days are numbered and I shall be leaving. Many cases, I know
exactly when -- until I am asked to extend. My trouble is getting out
of the client.


2) Contractors make ton's of money.

And we have expenses too. My medical insurance alone is more than
$5000 per year. Then there is general liability insurance, business
licenses, corporate registrations, self employment tax (otherise known
as FICA-matching). Employess can base there wages on 2080 hours per
year. I use 1800. 2080 hours in a year minus 80 hours for holidays
that the client takes, minus 80 hours for vacations, minus sick time,
personal time, down time.

If I don't work, I don't get paid.


3) Brokers and agencies take a BIG cut.

True, unless you have a good broker. I have a few that once were
programmers and don't rob the contractor. They provide a valuable
service to me and are worth a fair cut ... but not everything.

If you are a JDE type of person and want a great agent, call David
White at 1-800-494-6843. He'll treat you more than fair and a large
part of the money belongs to YOU!

4) You have to wear a tie.

Most of the time that is not true. I am in a contract now that doen't
want me wearing a tie. I explain to them that "you get the best that
I have to offer ... every day". Also, I could be called out to
another client or potential client ... I must be prepared.


5) the contractor gets no benefits.

Baloney!!!! I get anything that I want to buy. I have medical,
dental, short and long term disability. I have life insurance and
pension plans. Much of what I have, I got to choose. Employees may
be stuck with whatever is offered. My benefits are better than what I
ever had as an employee.


6) The contractor better make sure that he gets along ...

Again, baloney. People must get along. PERIOD. Doen't make any
difference if you are a'temp' or perm. Can you say "YOU'RE FIRED".


7) The contractor has risked his family's livelihood ...

True, but with risks, there are rewards. I gross triple what I did as
an employee. That out for taxes and benefits and I am far better off
as a conractor than I was as an employee.


Folks, contracting is a way of live. I liken it to being a
'gunfighter' in the old west sense:

A job needs to be done and it doesn't matter if you like them or they
like you. No politics, no crap. When the boss wants you to pull the
trigger ... you had better be able to do it. If you can't, then don't
take the money.

Having been a contractor off-n-on for about 2+ years, I have to agree with
Gary Watson here... kinda. I'm about 50/50 on which one is better. If you're
looking at compensation only, then, yes!, being a contractor is definitely
more lucrative (by far!). On the other hand, being an in-house geek does
have its advantages... just s'long as you're prepared to be a PUSHY in-house
geek who knows how to play politix.

Some of the points to consider are...

Contractors - pros: you get more money (sometimes, lots more money!);
because you're dealing with clients and not employers, the attitude is more
respectful & professional; you have the relative freedom to move from
company to company without having it reflect badly on your resume; less
office politix (there are still politix to contend with, just not quite as
petty); because you're constantly moving from one working environment to
another, your horizons are definitely broader.
Contractors - cons: you have to provide your own technical training; you
have to keep up with all the 'new' stuff out there at your own expense (and
I'm not just talking technical expertise and/or money here!); basically,
you're out-of-the-loop in most instances: if you like being on the decision
making side, forget it; in many cases, when the ax falls, you're the first
to go (no job security when you're a contractor); there's nothing permanent
about your seating/office arrangements; sometimes you have to deal with
considerable animosity on the part of permanent employees (here's where
petty office politix *do* come into play); you usually get to do all the
'donkey work' no one else wants to be bothered doing.

Employees - pros: you can feel 'part of' the company culture; there are
benefits, such as there are (not all the benefits are worth the while
though); there is a certain measure of job security ('certain measure' being
the operative phrase here); if you whine enough, technical training can be
provided at no expense to yourself; if you know how to play politix, you can
find yourself not only in-the-loop, but also part of the decision making
process; play your cards right, and YOU get to be the one who hires and
fires contractors (as well as permanent people); if you're lucky, you get to
work on all the fun, new applications.
Employees - cons: low pay, with little or no reprieve; constantly being
bombarded with petty office politix and other crap; lots of hard work, for
which YOU have to take responsibility (sometimes you get to take
responsibility for stuff that you never had any part of in the first
place -- see petty office politix); due to company culture, horizons are
fairly limited; in many cases, management's attitude towards you tends to be
patronizing and demeaning.

Now, I'm sure there are any number of individuals out there who will want to
take me on point-by-point (that's why this is a free discussion forum!).
But, from what I've experienced over the years, there are good and bad
points to each side.
