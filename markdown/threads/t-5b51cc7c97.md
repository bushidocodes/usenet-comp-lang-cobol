I am a really BAD person to be answering this - because I have some "issues"
with the current Standards groups.  On the other hand, I can at least point
you to some starting places.

For J4 (the ANSI-ish COBOL group), look at:
  http://www.microfocus.com/Standards/

For WG4 (the ISO-ish COBOL group), look at:
  http://anubis.dkuug.dk/jtc1/sc22/wg4/

For NCITS, the parent body to J4, look at:
  http://www.ncits.org/

For SC22, the parent body to WG4, look at:
  http://anubis.dkuug.dk/JTC1/SC22/

A few other "bits of information" related to your question (or at least how
I read it):

1) The next COBOL Standard is being developed as an ISO Standard, not as an
ANSI one.  The assumption (hope? expectation) is that once it is adopted
thru the ISO procedures, that ANSI will also adopt it - with minimal or no
modifications.  (This is the reverse of previous Standards which were first
ANSI Standards and then adopted by ISO.)

2) I think that your reference to "database" was probably to the CCC JOD
(COBOL CODASYL Committee - Journal of Development).  At one time, J4 (then
X3J4) did no (or almost no) "development" on the COBOL language.  Instead
this separate entity (CCC) did development and X3J4 took "snap-shots" of the
JOD and "adopted" it to make a new ANSI Standard.  (This is highly
simplified - but gives a clue of the OLD procedures.)  The database
structure was a part of the JOD but has never progressed to the ANSI (or
ISO) Standard.

3) Different companies have joined and then left different ones of these
committees (CCC and X3J4 - also some "consortia" such as X/Open and MIA).
My impression (VERY personal - not official) is that a number of companies
have "written" off COBOL and do not feel it is worth their
time/effort/resources to participate in the COBOL development and
maintenance procedures.   ISO (as stated in another message) has delegates
from COUNTRIES and *not* from companies.

4) FYI - another entity of interest is FIPS which issued a US government
"COBOL Standard".  Because of the "money involved" (he said cynically) the
FIPS Standard was often quite significant.  For example, it REQUIRED the
Intrinsic Function Module - while the ANSI Standard had it optional - and
few (if any?) vendors provide "HIGH Subset" COBOL compilers *without* the
Intrinsic Function Module.  There was also another group (related to FIPS -
but not quite the same) which USED to offer "certification testing" of COBOL
compilers.  It was *relatively* easy to pass these tests (and still have
many "bugs") but I think that group is no longer doing COBOL certifications
(but I could be wrong on this - it has "come and gone" a number of times in
the last few years).

5) Membership in the J4 committee is VOLUNTARY and so when people write
about "kicking people out" or "who decides who can join" these are really
irrelevant questions.  Similarly, both the ANSI and ISO rules and procedures
for creating and adopting a new Standard apply to *everything* for which
Standards are made - for example the COBOL Standard goes thru (mostly?) the
same steps and procedures and screw-drivers, plumbing interfaces, C++, and
SQL.   Therefore, if you want to suggest that J4 do something (officially)
think about how it fits into the ANSI (or ISO for WG4) "world".

I hope this helps (a bit) and I would be happy to see others add to my
(relatively) brief explanation.

There was also an X3 committee which worked on a cross-language database
standard in the late 70s and early 80s.  They were trying to make a
database which cold be accessed simultaneously from multiple ANSI standard
languages.  This couldn't be done without standardizing cross-language
data types, which no language agreed to do, so the project collapsed.

There was a later committee just to develop cross language data standards.
It may have been a spin-off of the database committee.  Each language
group was interested in changing the other languages to match their own
data types, and no one was interested in supporting anything new.  That
collapsed, as well.

> There was also another group (related to FIPS -
> but not quite the same) which USED to offer "certification testing" of COBOL
> compilers.  It was *relatively* easy to pass these tests (and still have
> many "bugs")

They sent you all the program sources you had to compile and run.  In
theory, you could write a "compiler" which recognized each of those
program sources and spat out the correct output.  There were some
compilers which passed certification with a release which was completely
unusable, i e, which failed to compile most ANSI programs.  You couldn't
sell computers to the Feds until your compiler passed certification.  It
let you sell computers to the government, while you got the compiler
working.

In the Burroughs COBOL-74 compilers for Medium Systems (V Series) and
Large Systems (A Series) the early release of Indexed files was intended
only to pass ANSI certification.  Every customer who used it was warned by
their Burroughs rep not to.  But soem customers stubbornly used it anyway,
and kept reporting bugs to Burroughs until it was fixed.  Neither
implementation was ever efficient.  Burroughs had a strict poilicy against
taking performance issues as bugs.  There were very rare exceptions, like
when a feature was suddenly running at one tenth its former speed, and
even then you had to fight your way thru the bureaucracy.
