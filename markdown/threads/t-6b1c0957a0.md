For those of you interested in COBOL and XML, you might want to see an
"initial" paper proposing full (???) XML support for a future COBOL
Standard.  This paper may be viewed at:

     http://www.cobolportal.com/j4/files/03-0094.doc

Notice particularly the statement,

"This paper is based on a working implementation.

Since WG4 and J4 are evaluating alternative approaches for XML, I have not
identified the pages on which the changes should be made, but left this to
the imagination of the reader.  I am willing to develop this working paper
into a formal proposal if this is the approach that is chosen."

As I am sending a copy of this note to IBM-MAIN, it should be noted that
this is a QUITE different approach than that currently available in
Enterprise COBOL.

I would think that if "you" have input on this (or another) approach to XML
support in "native" COBOL, that J4 and WG4 would be interested in it.  You
can contact the J4 Chair, the WG4 convenor, and the author of this paper
(all of whom are the same person) by emailing:

I picked up on Don Schricker's draft prior to Bill's posting, in the course of
looking for something else. I know nothing of XML, (which I don't currently have
a need for), other than a cursory read of Will Price's second book on Web
Programming.
However, I *did* like Don's overall approach, and of course this will be
'dotted' and "I'd' through J4 discussion.

My only suggestion to Don was his return error or status codes - he had some as
negatives. For 'familiarity' why not group those codes in the same fashion as
file-status.

If COBOL is to survive, and not be seen as a dinosaur by the youngsters, then it
has to play catch-up with new concepts as they arrive. This can't happen as soon
as somebody 'splashes' new technology - we have to wait for maturation to see if
it is going to last.

The problem is, when did XML first surface - i.e., when was it acknowledged as a
generally accepted technology. Having got to the stage, 'This is worth a look',
it's then in two phases, Phase I - J4 discusses then goes concrete on a
proposal, (emphaisis - within a targetted timeframe), and Phase II when do
compiler vendors make it available. Some vendors get *real* courageous and jump
in with an intermediate extension - I can think of two <G>.

As the opposite to the above, there are those who contend, "Nah, nah, nah. Don't
screw around with COBOL. You can call language/software X or Software Y  to do
the same thing". But - the same argument could have been made for (R)DBMS and
SQL - to my mind the ability to include SQL statements gelled into COBOL source,
makes complete sense - the two dovetail beautifully together.

Before Pete explodes on seeing the magic characters "J4", let's see if I can get
him to reflect <G>. (a) J4 is the only game in town, and (b) J4 is not 'Us"
(self appointees) and 'Them" (the compiler vendors). It *IS* the vendors. Just
did a quick check on current membership :-

Status:  October 10, 2002

J4 has presently 9 voting members and 5 advisory members.  The voting members
are:

(Compiler Vendors) - 6

- Fujitsu Software Corporation; Hewlett-Packard Company; IBM Corporation; ITSCJ
(Japan's own 'ISO/J4' and he works for Hitachi); Micro Focus Inc., and Unisys
Corporation;

("Independents") -  3

- EDS, Karlins'Korner; and Victor Consulting.

Changes since last year:

Membership dropped from 11 to 9.  HP bought Compaq. Individual member Stephen
Spiro did not pay his INCITS Service fee, which was too high for him as an
individual to bear. (I sympathize for Stephen - it cost me a real bunch of money

Thought you and others might find the following bit of 'history' interesting. The
source is Jerry Garfunkel's 'COBOL 85 Examples' (real neat little paperback for verb
syntax) :-

1. CODASYL 1959 - Secretary of Defense, USAF, US Steel Corp., DuPont Co., Esso
Standard Oil, Nat. Bureau of Standards, USN, Sperry Rand (Grace Hopper), IBM Corp.

2. It was probably from the spec done by their Short Range Committee that you  first
used as COBOL - N.Bureau of Standards, USAF, Honeywell, RCA, Burroughs, USN, Sylvania,
IBM, Sperry Rand

2. CODASYL 1985 - Burroughs, Control Data, Data General, Deloitte Haskins and Sells,
Digital Equip, Gen Services Admin,Honewell Info Sys, IBM, Jerome Garfunkel Associates
, (his brother is the 'Garfunkel' in Simon and Garfunkel), Micro Focus, Nat. Bureau of
Standards, Nat Computing Centre UK (my buddies in Manchester), NCR, Norfolk Southern,
Sperry Corp, Tandem Computers, US Army, USAF, USN, Wang Laboratories

3. ANSI COBOL Committee 85 - Burroughs, Control Data, Digital Equip, DECUS (DEC Users
Society), Hewlett-Packard, Jerome Garfunkel Assoc., Martin Marietta, McDonnell
Douglas, Nashoba Systems, Nat. Bureau of Standards,  NCR Corp, Online Systems Support,
Perkin-Elmer, Ryan-McFarland, Sperry Corp, Travellers Ins. Co., ( ** see below on
Travellers), US Army, USAF, Wang Laboratories

4. ISO COBOL Committee as of 1986 - Austria, Canada, France, Germany, Netherlands, UK,
US

One all prevailing thing in this is that from a US perspective they had to come up
with something acceptable to DofD to obtain military contracts - hence getting the
good housekeeping seal of approval via ANSI..

I highlighted Travellers Insurance above - that's hilarious. Without gong into the
nitty gritty, their head honcho in DP, (but of course the SOB was a bloody-minded
Irishman <G>), strongly objected to changes proposed, which he contended would put him
through massive re-writes. He went on a paper campaign which finished up with a couple
of ballots, plus he threatened to sue each individual member of what we now call J4 -
that made them do a double-take. That is one of the reasons that there is always that
backward compatibility in new features introduced to the language.

The jazz about that secretive mob in Geneva - ISO - Again something Gerry briefly
covers in his book. This is speculative on my part, having read his text. It wasn't
that the Americans were arrogant but naive. Following on from the success of COBOL
under the ANSI umbrella, they got the idea why not take it to the Europeans, (Hey ! It
works in the good 'ole US of A !), and suggest it as an ISO standard. Rather like the
EU is currently contemplating a tit for tat tax of US goods, (things like the US tax
embargo on foreign steel imports), I get the impression they said "Screw you. We will
look at it and do it in our own way", What the actual detail was I don't know, but
Gerry was one of the US presenters, I believe  - and he summarizes that by writing :-

"See Computer World March 1984.........." and concludes, "Some anti-COBOLists
referred to the significant action taken by the Vienna group as the Valentine's Day
Massacre, 1984".

No wonder ISO/WG4 is a pain in the ass !.

I've not used the XMLParse from Enterprise Cobol (as my shop likes to be 
a few releases behind on the compilers for stability).  But I do use SAX 
all the time -- it makes great sense and is easy to use.

While DOM is great for small documents, it simply cannot handle large 
documents.  SAX excels at both.

For those not familiar, SAX (Simple API for XML) is a event call-back 
model.  The parser is provided with a program (or paragraph in IBMs 
case) that can accept events.  They are simple and limited in number:

  Start Document Event  -  No parms, raised at beginning of doc
 
  End Document Event    -  No parms, raised at end of doc

  Start Element         -  Parms are element name with namespace
                           info and any attributes
  
  End Element           -  Parm is element name

  Character             -  Parm is character data (CDATA,
                           Comments, Whitespace or plain chars).
  
  Processing Instruction-  Parms are target and Data
   
It reads sequentially, firing events as they are recognized, and allows 
for unlimited document sizes to be processed.

While SAX says nothing about writing XML, the methods needed are similar 
to the ones used to read XML.  Although, XML is so simple and the rules 
so well defined that a utility to generate XML is a trivial programming 
task. 

There is a corresponding description for an XML data writer, which I 
jokingly call SOX (Simple Output for XML), available from the creator of 
SAX, David Megginnson.  See www.saxproject.org for more detail.

My other concerns about the DOM model (which I have also never used) 
described in the posted document are two.

The record oriented IO style of READ/WRITE/DELETE/etc and the record 
description seem designed to build very flat, record-like documents.  I 
tried to imagine how to describe an XD record that would process the 
schema for schema and the complexity worried me a bit.  I don't really 
have enough familiarity with this product to make an accurate judgment 
on that, just gut instinct.

The second, and more serious concern, is that the described DOM model 
violates XML standards in several small, but serious ways.  It applies 
the namespace of group items to subordinate items; it allows multiple 
root elements; it ignores PIs outside of root elements; it treats the 
xmlDecl as a processing instruction.  Also, while strictly optional, 
this spec does not mention comments at all.  Are they not supported?  
They can sometimes be quite useful.

All things considered, I think I might like this DOM approach.  But I'm 
not crazy about it being the ONLY thing available.  SAX has great 
utility as well for a different set of problems.  I would like to see 
them both in the next version of Cobol.
