So let me get this straight... you wan to replace the Same Old Questions
with some New Questions... which, rapidly enough, become the Same Old
Ones?

'It doesn't matter if the horse is dead... we can still get somewhere if
we beat it hard enough!'

>How do you determine if
>a person is skilled at being interviewed or skilled at programming?

It might just be the same way you determine that *anyone* is better at
(doing x) than at (talking about doing x).

Now permit me to ask a question... how was it determined that 'they
couldn't code'?  There's a world of difference between being able to
generate a program and being able to settle down, productively, into a new
shop.

(I was once asked Interview Question Number 17 (DB2 sheet) of 'What do you
do to get a COBOL source-code file which contains SQL statements to run?';
my answer was 'Well, what *I* do is go into my library, grab the right
compile skeleton, do a global replace on the member-name and SUB the
result... but you're probably looking for the Textbook Answer of
'Precompile, Compile, Link and Bind', right?'

I got the contract... but imagine what would have happened if the
interviewer was not familiar with the steps of 'go into my library, grab
the right compile skeleton, do a global replace on the member-name and SUB
the result'.)

Yes, testing is a good option and you don't need formal tests, just be
creative.  For example, I once had an interviewer push a COBOL listing
across the table and leaving the room he said,  *Tell me what you
think this program does, I'll be back in five minutes*.   The program
was just a simple application menu driver which called other programs.

When he returned, he was very polite and explained that he had been
*burned* before and felt it was necessary for his own sake to test my
knowledge of COBOL, regardless of how strong my resume was.   (I later
discovered he had once hired a COBOL programmer that knew very little
about COBOL even though the person's resume said otherwise)

He picked up the listing and the interview was over.   This is a point
worth noting.  He chose to test at the very end of the interview
process and I think that should you try this, you should do the same.
