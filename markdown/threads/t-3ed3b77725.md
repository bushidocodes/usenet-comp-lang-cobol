Just a comment on the implication being put forth here...

The way that these questions are worded one could read them
as "...so I can create the really 'cool' or 'neat' or 'slick'
programs that make use of them..."

Your posting also indicates that you are a student. So here is
my two cents...

While programming around "tricks" and "undocumented features"
may be an interesting academic exercise...
In the *real* world people do not do this and for good reason.
Consider the case of a real workd production program which utilizes
tricks and undocumented features (UDFs), a upgrade to the operating system,
compiler, whatever comes along and viola! your program breaks (remember--
you haven't changed the code!) have fun finding the problem!
Remember, most times undocumented behavior is undocumented for a reason...
The vendor reserves the right to change these behaviors at any time and
without notice.

Again, just my 2 cents.

What "interesting undocumented features"? What "tricks for programming
COBOL"?

Pie··.@pi··g.be (Pieter Geyskens)?? If you're looking to write
games, you can forget about using COBOL. COBOL is designed for writing
business oriented applications. That's all, nothing else. As far as I
know, there aren't any undocumented features or neat tricks involved with
COBOL/400. This is one computer language that's about as straightforward
and uncomplicated as a computer language could possibly get. Everything
you need to know about writing programs in COBOL/400 is right there in the
LRM and the Programmer's Guide.

Of course, knowledge about DSPFs and PRTFs, and how to use the QCMDEXC
call can only help. You may also want to get your hands on the System
Programmer's Guide to learn about creating vectors in COBOL (check out the
QLRSETCE call), or utilizing USRSPC to allocate up to 16Mb of private RAM.
