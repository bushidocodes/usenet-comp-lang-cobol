Yesterday there was a Broadcast in German Television

"BundesKriminalAmt", and it is the equivalence of
the United States FBI.

installed in 1972. Complaint was expressed that the
vendor (Siemens) is not anymore capable of
maintaining this old system because most programmers
retired or even died.

The Systems networking capability: one. Compatibility
with modern databases: none. Power and real estate
consumption: quite impressive.

Data-Exchange with the other departments of the
police in Germany has to be done by reading from one
terminal and typing into another terminal.

An interview with somebody of the ministry of the
interior (BundesMinisterium des Innern) was broadcast
which showed that the ministry of the interior
has no idea in what bad state the computing
equipment of the BKA actually is in.

Considering this, it is evident that terrorists
may move quite freely in this country, and we all
hope that the US do not consider us as
"harbouring terrorists" because Germany has such
insufficient resources. After all, we do not want
to be air-raided.

As for the relevance to this newsgroup: I do

After all, there is an opportunity for a new,

I had a look at the site, Herwig. (My German is a bit rusty, but I think I
got the gist of it)

It is a promotion for Object International. They are quoting sites and
Applications which utilise their OO product. ("Reference" sites...)

As the proposed INPOL system has to run across many countries, they are
saying that the use of components, developed using their product, will
guarantee platform independence, reuse and all the other benefits of
component based technology.

Here's an explanation of the terms you (and they) quoted:

>     Java, C++, CORBA, DBMS Oracle 8x, Application Server Weblogic
>     BEA, OS/2, SUN Solaris, HP Unix,
>     Swing, Java Beans, LDAP

Components will be developed in Java and C++ (note OO version of C). Some
components will be developed as Java Beans. All components will utilise the
Java Swing Class (a Java specific GUI Class) to provide a standard look and
feel, irrespective of the platform they are running on.

The server side will be handled by BEA's Weblogic Application Server. (BEA
are an American company who are rapidly establishing a reputation for
e-business expertise...). This supports Windows, UNIX, and just about
anything else, but it is primarily oriented towards Java based applications.
(Makes sense as they have chosen Java as the primary application development
language, with C++ also being utilised. (Note that the language in which the
components are developed is not really important, although they are
favouring Java... This bears out what I have been saying for over a year
now...<G>)). You can find out the whole story on this server architecture
at: http://www.bea.com/products/weblogic/server/index.shtml

The target OSes are OS/2, Sun Solaris, and HP Unix. This is simply a matter
of documentation because the components don't care what OS they are running
under. It has NO bearing on the development; that's why they are using
components.

CORBA is the Common Object Request Broker which allows the components to be
remotely served and shared. In effect, it defines a component interface or
wrapper which means that all OSes can deal with the component. (This
includes the capability to broker MS COM objects nowadays, although it looks
like the proposed system is studiously avoiding Windows...)

DBMS Oracle 8 is the database software. It is not clear whether they will
use intrinsic Oracle capability for access and reporting or will use JDBC. I
hope they don't tie it directly to Oracle, but as they seem emphatic about
platform independence it is more likely they will use SQL and Java Database
Connectivity (JDBC).

Finally, LDAP is the Lightweight Directory Access Protocol which utilises
TCP/IP stacks rather than OSI. This means the system will communicate with
the TCP/IP protocol which is used on the Web. As its name implies it is a
lighter weight, and therefore less resource hungry, protocol and it permits
an OS independent Directory structure view.

All-in-all, I think this is impressive. They have to meet a European (maybe
World) wide requirement for a system that can run on a number of platforms
and exchange data instantly. I note that they are planning for 20,000
clients.

They have opted, quite logically, for a component based, platform
independent, solution.

Makes sense to me...

final comments below...

The FBI isn't likely to answer this question. However, having worked on some
US military systems, here is my estimate.

US Military purchasing is a complex and time consuming process. Getting a
critical item may take six months or a year, unless it is already in a
warehouse. Getting a new computer system (except for PCs) may take several
years. Some military systems use computers that are 15 years old or more,
especially special purpose systems. These old systems are well maintained
(as the B52s which are about 40 years old), and serve their purpose.
However, they aren't GUI systems with all the bells and whistles.

PCs are handled differently. Since the cost is fairly low, getting a PC is
much easier. Consequently, the replacement cycle is probably much less than
15 years, but it is probably longer than the industry standard of 2-3 years.

Being a government organization, the FBI suffers from constraints similar to
those of the US Military.

I suspect the FBI has both old and new systems--about the same as the US
Military. That is, mainframe systems up to 15 years old and PC systems that
are relatively new, both hardware and off-the-shelf software. They must have
an staff of computer professionals who continually maintain and enhance
their proprietary systems. As is the case with private business, mission
critical projects will be done and others will be delayed.
