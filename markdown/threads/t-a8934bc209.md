Not to throw gas on the fire, but to provide some perspective:

In 1959 when the COBOL concept was being developed the short range
committee did NOT have an assignment to do a business oriented language.
Personally, I never thought it was to be limited except by user
requirements. In my mind, the name COBOL because the short range committee
which was made up of a super majority of Math Grads, they felt that naming
their product could not include the kinds of things done by existing
languages.  I believe personally that the EC at that time were only
interested in the documentation benefits derived from the effort. They
already had experienced half a decade of binary and other machine code
listings, Fortran listings, etc.  All of the problems Judson attributes to
C and C++ already existed, and the software industry and use of computers
instead of card equipment had suffered a great learning cost over and over
again.

In my dotage, I must include these are my opinions, and do not represent
the thinking of the EC or the short range committee at that time.  I
believe that if the software people at the vendors sites would reflect the
users needs (which includes their own) computer instruction sets would more
nearly represent the programmer and thus the user needs.  I am aware that
hardware has evolved since that time to accommodate COBOL.
However, not without some very big errors on the part of vendors.
To sell any product, the salesman needs to get a foot in the door.
