Chip,

I can't think of a polite reply to your question. About the nicest
thing I can come up with is 'get stuffed'. The question is way
off-topic for the NG, of no interest whatsoever to me, and the subject
matter is best handled by those annoying headhunters that post to
comp.lang.cobol. Try http://www.careerbridge.com or visit the web sites
of any company in the high-tech directory that sparks your interest.
However, I'd be amazed if anybody is actually publishing rates.

A bit of unsolicited career advice:

Find a company you'd really like to work for, convince them they need
you, then NEGOTIATE your compensation. Notice I did not say 'wages'.
There are all sorts of compensations that don't directly involve
dollars, like the option of working from home, stock options, hardware,
a health club membership, whatever. Being flexible about compensation
will also help to convince your future employer that you've got a better
grasp of the 'big picture'.

For what it's worth, I work from home for a company 500 km away (that's
300 miles for those in that country to the south which couldn't cope
with changing to the metric system like everybody else on the planet) so
I don't care about local rates. Thanks to the internet and call
forwarding nobody needs to know that I'm not in the office. I get a
competetive wage but I don't have to commute so I don't need a second
car and I can have a hot lunch without the cost of dining out. Every
now and then I have to hop on a plane to visit a customer or head
office. Funny enough, it takes the same amount of time for me to get
from Ottawa to Toronto as it did every morning when I was commuting.
Now that's compensation!

While I'm ranting, the whole concept of 'rates' ticks me off. Companies
looking for somebody with a shopping list of skills are interested in
filling a position, not in providing a career. It's indicative of a
short-sighted or heavily bureaucratic organization. If you want that
kind of a job go to work for one of the 'body shops'. I swore never to
work for a company that hires for what people already know. I'll only
work for a company that hires me for what I am capable of learning. The
only certainty in the high-tech sector is change. If you're not learning
at least one new thing a day then you're falling way behind.

Rantingly yours,

Karl Wagner

But it's not the same to the employer. An increase in salary means an
increase in other costs like unemployment insurance, payroll taxes,
health care benefits, long term disability premiums, etc. So your extra
dollar may cost the company $1.50 or more.

A new notebook computer might cost $5000 but the company can depreciate
that asset to reduce taxes so a dollar in wages is not the same as a
dollar in compensation. As well, the company may get volume purchasing
discounts which you as an individual could not get, meaning the company
spends say 70 cents to give you what you perceive to be a dollar's
value. You get a new toy, the company isn't out as much money and
you're both happy.

Same for work-at-home... the company can reduce overhead by maintaining
a smaller offices since they don't have to provide a desk for everybody
in the company. There are some tax advantages to the employee which
should be added into the compensation equation that don't cost the
company anything except the time it takes to sign a tax declaration.

Stock options and other variable pay schemes are based on the company
doing well. They have to pay out in good times but can reduce their
costs in bad times. This reduces the employer's risk.

You get the idea...
