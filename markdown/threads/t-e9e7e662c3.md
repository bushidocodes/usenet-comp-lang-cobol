```
   I have a question relating to professional development that I'd like to
pose. I've entered the IT field after a 20 year career in the military.
After retiring from the Army, I returned to school to get a degree in
Applied Computer Science (which was mainframe oriented) and have spent about
18 mos programming in COBOL, with limited exposure to DB2, IMS and CICS.
   I am very concerned with expanding my skill set and realize I do not have
decades to do so. As the job market evolves, however, I am a bit unsure as
to which would be the best direction to proceed. I took one course in a
Masters in Software Engineering program via distance learning but was very
discouraged by the lack of teacher student feedback and won't be taking any
more courses from that school..
   I am now considering an MS in Info Systems from Drexel. The degree would
take 4 years to complete. My feeling is that this program would be
applicable in a wide variety of settings or sytems.
   I am also aware of the fact that the technical lines of distinction
between mainframe and client server seem to be blurring. Therefore I am also
considering taking about a year to get Java certified with the possibility
of following that up with a 2 year MS in E Commerce. I will be doing all I
can to develop my mainframe skills all the while.
    Any thoughts or advice concerning the benefits or drawbacks of these
possibilities would be greatly appreciated . It goes without saying that I
value the advice of the professionals who post to this group.
                 Thanks in advance.
```

```
Cobol, CICS and DB2 are the most important skills for the mainframe
market. Java does appear to be making progress but the demand for that
skill is patchy (Access and VB are in greater and more regular demand).
I wouldn't bother with the additional qualifications. It is rare (on
this side of the pond) for a degree or computer related qualification to
be required. More often 3+ years of relevant experience is asked for.
Personally, I would not employ any Ivory Tower Academics for a
commercial programming job, other than for compiler writing. In the long
run a degree in Business Administration or Project Management will stand
you in better stead.
```

```
Anything client-server based is a good way to go, I would think.  Like
Oracle, NT, VB, C++, Perl and Java.  And any business analysis or leadership
skills are a big bonus.

In my area, Toronto, the market for COBOL Programmers is now *VERY* bad.
One of the major banks (CIBC I think) layed off approximatly 300 COBOL
programmers just before Christmas.  There are so many older programmers who
moved back to COBOL and trainee's who picked up COBOL, both due to Y2K, that
applicants far exceed jobs.  And Toronto is still regarded as a high tech
city, with supposedly the 4th biggest stock exchange in the world and a huge
banking district.  One comment from a recuriter that worried me was that
when a company submits a COBOL job to the web, normal practice is to receive
at least 100 resume's by the end of the first day

Thankfully I have team leadership/project management skills, so I can try
the management positions.  Otherwise I would be really worried about work.
```
