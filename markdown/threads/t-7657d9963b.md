The title name is "Systemantics" as in 'system' + 'antics'.

A Wikipedia entry for the author is:
< http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gall_(author) >

The book title given there is "Systemantics: How Systems Really Work
and [Especially] How They Fail". (title corrected)

That site quotes Gall's Law,
"A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a
simple system that worked. A complex system designed from scratch never
works and cannot be patched up to make it work. You have to start over
with a working simple system. – John Gall (1975, p.71)"

One of Jerry's lists is at,
< https://groups.google.com/d/msg/comp.lang.cobol/EpsUkIy0KfE/z09rDT7ZLbcJ >

In any case, it appears that the implementation problems for the
Affordable Care Act were predicted by Gall's Law!

They can't just do what they want to do... everything is subject to review
and committee and there are many agendas. Apart from that, there are
physical hoops that must be jumped through, especially if they are trying to
procure from outside the USA. We have had North American commercial clients
who had absolutely no problem transferring funds to us once it was agreed
what we would deliver, but for Government departments there is no such
luxury. It is very difficult for them, even if they manage to get the
necessary approvals.

I would have loved to have their business because it would be very good for
us to have a Government client (from any government, really :-)), but it
didn't happen

Currently, I'm still trying to finish the COBOL21 side of the PRIMA web site
but it keeps getting pushed down a stack of priorities.

The immediate coding is all about replacing Embedded SQL with LINQ. I would
very much like to be able to offer LINQ as an alternative to ESQL in our
Data Access Layer in the new year, but it is a huge amount of work.

The indications from the pilot projects and experiments are that LINQ is a
quantum leap beyond ESQL and is superior in every way.

I've been worried about SQL for some time now and the fact that there is an
"impedence mismatch" between object oriented programming and the way we
manipulate relational data. LINQ addresses it very well and allows typed
Classes of results, which are subject to syntax checking and Intellisense in
VS. Everything happens at set level and LINQ mostly manipulates result sets
and Lists, although you can get down to singletons and individual columns if
you want to. I REALLY like the "universality" of LINQ; you can manipulate
obects from a database, objects in memory, XML files, and entity lists, all
using a single syntax, and you can mix and match them. It is years ahead of
SQL.

I am enjoying learning it but I have had to raise my C# coding game to
really understand some of the finer points of entity sets, typed database
collections, generic delegates and the shorthand form of them which is Lamda
expressions. Never mind, I'm gradually getting better at it and LINQ itself
is elegantly simple once you put aside 30 years of SQL and look at it in a
fresh light... :-)

As for 500 million lines of code... No I don't think so. I replaced 5700
lines of COBOL and ESQL the other day with just under 600 lines of C# and
LINQ... (And I think I can actually make it less when I learn some of the
more advanced stuff)

So, for me at least, it is all about coding... alternating between C# and
ASP.NET (which is mostly C# code behinds with a little CSS and Javascript.)

Interfacing C# to COBOL is another thing I'm doing quite a bit of...
expanding existing COBOL functionality by plugging new facilities written in
C# into it. There is a steady demand for this and I quite enjoy doing it. I
have just delivered a module that sends SMS texts to mobile phones, for a
Power COBOL package at one of our clients. Very interesting work.

I'm supposed to be retired but I don't see myself stopping writing code any
time soon. I do find the learning a little more strenuous as I get older...I
think Doc's description of a "porous" memory is pretty apt, but I believe if
it isn't exercised it would get decidedly MORE porous...

Now, let me remember where I put that cup of tea... :-)

Pete.
"I used to write COBOL...now I can do anything."
