Many secrets in this world are not published on internet. I haven't got the
ideal reference, but I have a good memory. Here is a few pieces of
information for you, and if you like, you ar welcome to mail me for further
questions.

The years from fifties through the sixties we had many different ways to put
programs into the computers.
Auto Codes were slightly formalized 'machine language' (or perhaps one pass
assembler)
Neat, which was NCR's Assembler
BAL, Basic assembler language for IBM 360/2x and 360-Assembler for 360/3x
and up
RPG was born in these years, but none of them were designed for a special
purpose.

Computing was a mystery to almost anyone outside the business, by even
within the business we had different categories of purpose for computing.
Technical applications and business applications were the 2 major
categories.

Technical computing was based upon Math, and very typical was that you
entered a very limited amount of data, and you received a small amount of
data, but that old rig was often
processing for days and weeks to give the result.

Business applications were made to produce accounting, invoices, delivery
notes and all that stuff an office appriciate.

When COBOL was born, we knew what 'business oriented' meant - and I think
you know it as well.

Soon after Cobol another language, 'ALGOL' was born. ALGgebraic Oriented
Language was for Math at Office. Insurance and statistics were the places
where that language were widely used.

But, you know,  after Cobol nothing of value ever ocurred (This will make
this thread grow beyond your wildest imagination)

Once upon a time the programming world was divided into
"Business" and "Scientific" (some also say "Light" and "Dark").

Business programming was characterized by a high volume
of input and output with very little computation (Read record,
compute PAY = HRS * RATE, put record. Repeat 5000 times).

Scientific programming was characterized by a low volume
of I-O but lots of computation (Read a matrix of values on
ten punched cards, compute the inverse of the matrix in
ten hours of solid computing, print ten lines of output).

These original divisions have today blurred and new ones
have been born (Sons of the Dark).

There are many aspects of "business oriented", and I am sure others will
mention more.  However here are a few starters.

Cobol is designed for easy *reading* rather than fast and dirty writing.
This is the exact opposite of languages like C, where you are dealing (in
theory) with academic code.  Business code evolves, rather than being
written.  Because of that, it is far more important to be able to write a
simple program that can be expanded by the next programmer than it is to be
able to whack out a large program in a hurry. A large proportion of Cobol is
documentary.

Cobol has far more sophisticated data typing than most languages, and is one
of the very few with intregal decimal data types.  Rounding and overflow in
business follows DECIMAL rules, not binary. This is crucial when dealing in
monetary units.

Cobol is, by far, the most platform independent language.  This is important
for both the longevity of programs, and for cost control within data
processing.

Cobol has, within the language, file structures that allow the building of
quite complex record structures.  While record keeping can be done in just
about any language, Cobol was designed for it.  That shows.

Languages for computers are generally designed to solve specific types of
problems.  C was designed as a replacement for assembler, and is ideal for
an OS, for example.  In fact, it is good for just about anything that I
would have written in assembler twenty years back.  Basic was written to
make it easy for post grad arts students to get a computer course.  Pascal
was designed for teaching.  Fortran was designed for formula translation.
Cobol was designed for accounts receivable, payables, and payroll.  The
above paragraphs and things like "PICTURE $$$,$$9.99" demonstrate that.
