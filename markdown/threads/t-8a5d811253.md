The Roman numeral system does not allow 5 like symbols in a row,
when that happens a new symbol is used, e.g.
5 = V not IIIII
50=L not XXXXX
500=D not CCCC
but there is no symbol for 5000.
Some people say that the maximum number is 3999,
because not even 4 symbols are allowed, thus
4=IV not IIII
40=XL not XXXX
etc,
but there is some doubt here because on every watch
with Roman numerals, 4 is indeed IIII.
The rule is a bit murky here, but certainly 5 symbols in a row
is NOT permitted, because there is a special symbol for 5, 50, 500.

Lief,

The "Roman" four on watch faces is a jewelry tradition and not a Roman
numeral.  Most clocks and watches will have "IIII" on the face only when
the numerals at the bottom of the dial are in the upside down position. 
Faces with Roman numerals right side up at the bottom of the dial are
usually shown as "IV".

Our watchmaker (my company is in the jewelry business) told me this
story.  According to tradition, a clockmaker had completed a clock for
his king but improperly fashioned the Roman numeral 4 as IIII.  He went
before the king to beg forgiveness and asked to be given the opportunity
to fix it before the rest of the kingdom viewed his mistake.  The king,
however, was so taken with the clock that he suggested the rest of the
clocks in his kingdom be made to look the same.  Hence the IIII remains
on clocks and watches.

Please correct this if someone is confident that it is wrong, but I believe
that the device of muliplying a symbol by one-thousand by means of an
over-score did  not apply to I. So that to list thousands, the basic technique
was to use M.

Also we impose a bit of our modern Western culture on this discussion, when we
discuss "... the rule ...".  It did not work that way.  There were no standards
committees. 

Instead there were competing traditions and during brief periods schools that
influenced practice. Roman enumeration is best understood as convention, not
rule.  I think that four I's (as in IIII) was not really uncommon, especially
in isolation as the value four. 

In combinations, in the position that we would think of the last digit (the
units digit), the IV was rather more consistent over time, perhaps.

But the reason for this would not necessarily be obvious to moderns. For the
truth is, the device of placing the smaller number symbol in front of the
larger number symbol is not subtraction. It is a counting down.

Just as we are currently counting down to the Y2K millenium event threshold,
the ancients would count up for a while, and then begin to count down to the
next group, or to the next start point.

By the time that a zero basis decimal numeric system took hold in Europe nearly
all evidence of the primitive counting culture had been obliterated. But the
earlier Roman numerals (and word specified calendaring of numeric day names) do
preserve the more primitive systems, though ever so vaguely. These folks,
however, did not have standards as we do.

Greek Phi was originally used for one-thousand. And that the Roman D, used for

The Nones was always Eight days before the Ides. That is, _we_ would call it
the 'eighth' before the Ides; we would also call the day before the ides the
'first' day before the Ides.

The difficulty here is that we moderns are being zero based in our ordinal
names.  The Ides being the zeroith, the day before the 1st day back, etc. Until
we get to the Eighth day back which we know they called the Nones.

The ancients who evolved these practices did more than not use a zero cipher. 
They had no zero concept. Counting is utterly corporeal. Zero is cerebral. It
is said by some scholars that the ancient actually distrusted the idea of
nothing.

At any rate, when counting with reference to the Ides, the Ides was the first. 
The day before was the eve, and the next day back was the third as the ancients
named it (which to us would be the second back). And so it is that the eighth
day back, was refered to as the Ninth.

Throughout the centuries of trial and error with the calendar, the relationship
between the Ides and the Nones remained constant.

The day before the Calends, the day before the Nones and the Day before the
Ides, were refered to as the Eve of that date.  And in each case the day prior
to each of these was the third.

As they experimented with the length of certain months either to meet the
demands of celestial mechanics or political expediency, the insertion
(deletion) point for new days was thus just past the Ides; that is, the middle
of the month. This, perhaps, should be accounted as the first random access
system.

TATAIUI:

The Julian Calendar was originally designed with Winter Solstice on
January 1, Spring Equinox on April 1, Summer Solstice on July 1, and
Autumnal Equinox on October 1.  To make this work as close as they could
according to their astronomical data, they made Winter 90 (or 91) days,
Spring 91 days, Summer 92 days, and Fall 92 days.   

But before this was implemented, one of Caesar's advisors observed that
many people held celebrations on the solstices and equinoxes, and they
kept celebrating for several days after.  The new calendar was for civil
not religious use, and they wanted to avoid having first of the month
business collide with these celebrations.  So the new calendar was set
seven days behind the actual seasons, which was actually eight days behind
the actual seasons, because it was based on 2nd and 3rd Century BC
observations.  By Pope Gregory's time the calendar had further slipped by
13 days, but the Greorian calendar was corrected by only 10 days to allign
it to some 4th Century AD event.

the 14th century.  He did correct the erroneous implementation of leap
years.
