The only reason I  can see why anyone would buy a very expensive CLR 
generating COBOL compiler is so they can run their COBOL as managed code. It 
runs slower than unmanaged code (which is native) and provides no advantage 
whatsoever apart from the psychological one of it being managed.

(Basically, this means that the framework will close it down properly if it 
misbehaves (primarily addressing exceptions). If code is properly debugged 
and has been running in Production for some time without subscripts going 
out of range, the risk is low. (Actually, if you're really worried about 
exceptions being caught and handled properly, you can specify TRY... CATCH 
blocks for unmanaged code just like you can for managed code (as long as you 
are using InterOP services.))

It doesn't get you away from dependency on COBOL and simply perpetuates the 
use of it, in an environment that COBOL is not well suited for.

I can't see a case for managed COBOL.

After a year or two the programmers realize it takes them far too long to do 
stuff in COBOL which they could do in a fraction of the time with .Net 
languages, and the management realize that these languages are free, so they 
have an expensive white elephant sitting on their hands.

Anybody moving to .NET will eventually move off COBOL, so why would you buy 
an expensive compiler for a language you are trying to get away from?

The usual answer is that people are told to buy the compiler, and don't 
understand the .Net environment or the facilities available in it, so they 
do.  (Or maybe they know .Net but don't know COBOL so they take the advice 
on that which they are given.)

Then they find there are no credits or refunds... I've had people actually 
offer me  NetCOBOL for .NET as a gift. (A legal transfer of licence, when 
they found they didn't need it and couldn't get a credit from Alchemy). I 
turned it down. No need or use for it. Ironic really. A few years back I was 
desperately trying to buy it, because, like everybody else, I thought it was 
necessary.

I have a free Ferrari in the garage. Why would I drive a model T with a

There was nothing here that made me go "Wow!" and all of the facilities 
(including the development tools) I already have, or can get with a simple 
download and plug in to VS 2008. (For free...)

It made me smile to hear the presenter getting excited about stuff that most 
.NET programmers take for granted and have been taking for granted for many 
years now. I described these facilites here years ago but I don't think 
anyone actually believed me... :-) I was also amused that the first sample 
app. was a post to Twitter (which didn't actually work properly, but was 
intended to show that COBOL is now "cool" (it isn't :-))

Nevertheless, it is a good integration, as we would expect, given it was 
done by MF and given that VS is designed to have such integrations plugged 
into it.

It is kind of nice to see COBOL getting a facelift but, in my opinion it is 
way too late. If this had been done 15 years ago we might have a different 
IT world.

I see two major problems with it:

1. It doesn't look like COBOL any more and programmers who have resisted OO 
will find it very strange indeed. Despite the "spin" that MF have "updated 
and trimmed down the language to support .Net" (in other words, tried to 
make it less verbose), what we have is something that will spook the 
majority of COBOL people who are deeply embedded in COBOL. (Shorthand forms 
of OO notation are STILL more verbose than the free languages, and although 
the presenter says it will be easy for C# and VB programmers to move to 
COBOL (true), I wouldn't hold my breath. It is MUCH more likely that the 
converse will occur...

Like so many of the COBOL community, this presenter has failed to realise 
that the underlying paradigm is flawed and suffers by comparison with other 
languages in a distributed environment. (But that is too subtle an argument 
for anyone who makes their living out of COBOL so I don't blame him for 
that.) Once people master OO COBOL they will soon find it is more tedious to 
write than the free languages. (This was very much my own experience, but 
I'd be surprised if others don't find the same.)

The fact is that with a highly advanced IDE, the choice of language really 
isn't that important (provided it can handle Objects). Nevertheless, 
languages designed around OO are always going to be less verbose and more 
facile at handling Classes and Objects, than a language that has had these 
facilities "bolted on".

2. It is competing with products that have all the same facilities (plus 
more) and are FREE.

I can't see a traditional COBOL guy shelling out thousands of dollars to get 
.Net COBOL (from MF or Alchemy) then finding that it doesn't look like 
COBOL, he has to master concepts and facilities which he already fiercely 
resisted when OO COBOL was first released, and once he has mastered it 
(given that he manages to...), realising he could have got the same 
facilities for free.

However, as I mentioned elsewhere, I get mail from people who want out of 
COBOL and most of them are companies (not individuals) who have bought a 
.NET COBOL compiler because they were told to and because they have always 
used COBOL. (Some are not; they are non-COBOL people who are interested in 
moving a COBOL site to .NET)

So much for an objective look, now let's view it as an existing COBOL shop 
might:

1. It provides a superb IDE which makes COBOL much easier and quicker to 
develop.

2. There is some learning required, but this can be easily absorbed into the 
cost of implementing the IDE.

3. COBOL guys now are prepared to take another look at OO (even if they 
wouldn't have a bar of it 15 years ago) because they realise it is a very 
successful paradigm. (What they usually don't realise is WHY it is, but that 
comes after a few years of using it...)

4. Existing COBOL shops are used to spending money for software and accept 
that a major upgrade will incur a cost, so the money probably isn't much of 
an objection. (It IS to small businesses and individual developers, but 
these are NOT who it is targeted at.) From what I gather, the average cost 
of implementing a .NET COBOL compiler, with support, maintenance, and 
licensing is around $US11,000 (This is REAL cost, not the quoted price.It is 
based on information from 3 sites, in different parts of the world).

To a small business, this is cause for pause, to a larger one it is petty 
cash in the IT budget. To be fair, If you decided to drop COBOL and go to 
the free .NET languages, there is still a learning cost involved. I assess 
it at as around $US2000. In my own case, I never paid a cent. I used free 
videos and taught myself, but many people won't want to do that. And today I 
employ people who are being taught C# at University or Polytech. (And they 
are easier to get and cost much less than COBOL guys;that is why I do most 
of the COBOL stuff myself.)

5. There is a tempting vision that mainframe (or other procedural COBOL) 
code can be simply compiled for .NET and all your worries are over. Anyone 
who buys that, really deserves whatever they get.

BOTTOM LINE (as always, one man's opinion):

1. MF have done a good job of integrating COBOL with VS.
2. It is really too late to change the perception of COBOL as a modern OO 
language, ideal for distributed and networked solutions, but it will give 
some encouragement to people starting to look at where to go next with 
COBOL.
3. It remains to be seen whether the COBOL community will now embrace OO 
COBOL, given there is a decent IDE for it. (They didn't when it was 
available for Fujitsu, but MF have a larger COBOL market share.)
4.I believe this will encourage some programmers and sites away from COBOL 
rather than persuade C#, Java, and VB.NET programmers to move to COBOL.

Pete.

To be fair, I probably provoked some of the bad reaction (although I never 
posted here with a mischievous intent to "stir") and although it was an 
unpleasant experience, I came out of it more mature and tolerant. I came to 
realise that it really doesn't matter; it is only computer programming, not 
life and death.

Having worked for decades on IBM (mainly, but also "other") mainframes, I 
well understood the things you mention in your post. There certainly WAS 
Management resistance, but it was largely a reflection of Programmer 
resistance, and all the reasons for NOT doing it that you mention above, in 
retrospect, were quite valid. (However, in some places, there was an 
unfortunate arrogance as well which saw PCs as "toys" and the Internet as a 
stupid fad that would soon go away...)

Nevertheless, I persevered with it for my own development and found it (at 
least when combined with a component based approach) to be very effective 
and useful. I wrote OO COBOL components for over 10 years and am still using 
some of them.

What few of us actually realised at the time was that the Network would take 
over the world, permeate in subtle ways so many different aspects of our 
lives, and usher in a completely new kind of society with different models 
for trading.

And THAT is the nub of it.

The network is about Objects and Layers.

It likes small, encapsulated pieces of functionality that can be easily 
invoked locally or remotely. and run on the nearest available processor. It 
is about sharing and redirecting workloads and resources dynamically.

The idea of a monolithic sequential process which directly controls 
everything it does, is just anathema to it.

Batch Processing is only a requirement in a certain mindset. It IS NOT 
essential if systems are designed differently.

(If your system reflects an analogue of the real world at every given 
moment, with all transactions complete or pending action at a certain 
specific time in the future, there is no need to "complete" transactional 
updates overnight because you didn't have the capacity to complete them at 
the time they happened.

This was one of the reasons that Batch Processing came into being; it simply 
wasn't possible to complete the processing for the volume of transactions 
occurring, as they occurred. The on-line system simply wrote down keys and 
they were picked up and processed overnight. It was like "finishing off " 
the transactions at a less busy time...

Another oft-quoted-reason is that we need to view things as of certain dates 
and the "only way" to do that is to accumulate and consolidate the 
transactions in the stated period, which requires overnight dedicated 
processing.

It doesn't.

Modern Data warehousing and newer retrieval techniques can consolidate the 
details as part of a retrieval request. Stored procedures on a database can 
offline this and work transparently.

We have better ways of doing things and much more powerful processors and 
subsytems to do them with. Batch Processing is simply archaic.)

So, if you eliminate Batch Processing, why would you need a centralised 
procedural processing system?

You don't, and most of the world is aware of that. More are gaining that 
awareness every day.

COBOL is ideal for the centralised Batch Processing model; OO is ideal for 
encapsulated functionality that can run anywhere.

You can figure out for yourself which of these models is most likely to have 
a future.

By-the-way, I have no regrets about moving to OO, not even about moving to 
OO COBOL. It was very useful and helped me transition to much more powerful 
stuff.

And I have no grudges over the flames that went down 15 years ago... :-)

Pete.
