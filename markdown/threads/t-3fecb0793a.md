This is the story of how I got my first Data Processing job.  (As it
was called back then).

I had been working full time (at Mc Donalds!) and going to a Jr
College.  My SAT's were good enough for me to go anywhere, but I could
not afford it.  And I was impatient.  I was taking my "general"
courses in college -- but working full time I was having trouble
staying awake.  

I had decided I wanted a job in computers - I could program the heck
out of my TI 57 and TI 59 calculators, I had a TI 99 4A I wrote
sort/merges on in basic, and I had a Timex Sinclair with 16K!  I was
hooked.  

I decided to take out a student loan and I applied to Computer
Learning Center in Anaheim CA.  I passed their entrance exam for
programmers - but they said I was too young to be a programmer (I was
20!) - so they shuffeled me into Operations.  

These tech schools provide the bare bones background you need to learn
DP (er IT).  If you are motivated you can learn more than they teach.
You will either love it or hate it.  The course started with an Intro
to DP - (Number systems how the CPU actually works etc).  The next
course was RPG II.  I learned how to program in RPG.  Then there was a
DOS clas and an OS (back then) class.  They used VM.  They ran the RPG
stuff, COBOL and BAL that they taught the programmers under DOS - so
OS was not up much.  But I learned the HASP commands and I ran the
operating system from the card reader to get my OS utility assignments
done while DOS was running.  You have to learn all you can.

So there I was - fresh out of operations school, ready to become an
operator - wanting to be a programmer.  I used their placement service
- and interviewed with several defense contractors - who while
interviewing tech school grads were looking for degreed individuals.  

Eventually they found me a job with a temp agency - I was contracted
out to Data Products and I worked in Quality Assurance.  I didn't do
much, but they paid me well.  They had a PDP 11 that they had some
BASIC programs on that they had trouble with - I fixed them.

I hated the drive though - 50 miles in LA traffic.  

At the same time I took the job, I saw a newspaper article talking
about a local (to me) business that was making it big.  The article
was an interview with the director of ops, and the paper showed a
picture of an IBM tape drive.  I got his name from the article and
called him.  He didn't have a job - but he thought they might have one
shortly.  I called him every day.  After a week he called me in to
interview - no promises.  I had no experience.  

I brought with me all of my RPG programs, my utilities and assignments
from school.  I asked them what OS they ran and it was a DOS variant
called MVT/VSE from Software Pursuits.  

He sat me down and had me explain the work I did - what meant what-
just to see what I understood and if I really knew my stuff.

A week later (I called every day) he told me I could start - but I
would not be paid much  $6.00 an hour.  Graveyard shift.  I took it.  

The first night I dumped a stack of paper boxes on the lead operator.
But I learned on the job.  I got very good at the job.  There was a
contract programmer there that worked nights sometimes - to get things
done.  I bought a COBOL book and he taught me COBOL - and the right
way to do things.  I wrote programs to make operations eaiser.  I
modified JCL to prevent problems - I automated processes - to give me
more time to learn to program.  

Eventually they hired more graduates from the same tech school - some
were great some were not - but I moved into being a
programmer/operator - for a short time - then a full time programmer.
They sent me to school at IBM for several different things including
CICS - I've never looked back.

So what's the answer to your job search.  Take ANYTHING you can get.
Prove yourself.  Take your work from school to the interview.  Be
persistant.  Someone will see your fire and take a chance on you.  If
you don't have that fire, or the desire to excel - I know of a Mc
Donalds that is still looking to replace their best swing manager.

When you hire a newbie it takes a considerable amount of time to get
them up to speed.  Just knowing the technical programming details is
only a part of what a working programmer must know.  They must learn
the systems (applications) they will be working on.  And beyond that
they must learn about systems in general; what makes a good one or a
bad one, etc.  Believe me, they teach you virtually zip in that area,
and what they did teach you, you will probably need to unlearn.  Most
of the academic crowd has almost no real-world experience.  Ironically,
shops which are short for people simply do not have the extra time to
hire newbies.  It's almost a 'catch-22'.  But don't be discouraged,
there are lots of jobs out there, you just have to look around a
little for a beginning position.  I believe there are some companies
and government agencies out there who have yet to really get serious
about Y2K.  When they do, it will be panic time, and they will be
hiring anyone who can spell COBOL.  May not help them a lot if it's
too late, but it may help get you in the door. :-)

Just to emphasize this, my experience with new programmers is
that each one reduces my output by 25% while I explain things to
them, while at the same time, that produce very little for at least
a year.  In effect, as a one man shop, if I hire four new grads
I close down for a year while I teach them.

Traditionally, newbies are hired to do junior jobs, like training
users in 123, installing software on PC's, maintaining small
networks, that sort of thing.  While they make themselves useful
doing grunt work (that the average office worker *cannot* do),
they get trained by inserting them into the programming staff
on relatively minor jobs.  As they pick up experience, they are
given bigger and bigger jobs.  It takes about 2-3 years before
they can do a decent job on something like designing a new
system from scratch, and that is for the better ones.

The bottom line is that the last thing you need in a crisis is
a junior underfoot.  The best place for a junior is in a stable
shop with lots of new work, and lots of grunt work like training,
installations, machine set-up, etc.

Hmmmmm... I am getting a beginning of an idea which I may give more flesh
to as time goes by.  I am familiar with the Argument Against Training, of
course:

To train a person both costs money and increases that person's value in
the jobmarket; once another company recognises this increase in value and
offers a larger salary the cost of the training is lost.  As a result of
this no training can be done.

Oddly enough this argument ignores a fact with which every businessperson
(and most property-owners) are familiar:  when the value of an asset is
increased there are usually associated increases in the costs of
maintenance and insurance.  What appears to be desired in the case of
training is an increase in the value of an asset (a better-trained
employee) *without* an increase in maintenance-costs (higher salary) or
'insurance against theft' (higher salary).

At Casegen we have the concept of a Technical Apprenticeship for new
unexperienced staff which readers may find of interest.

It is a two-year contract that includes some day release from work to study
towards a further computer qualification such as an MSC degree in Computing
(part time). During the two years, the employee is paid a fairly low salary
but we ensure that the work is suitable for the capabilities of the employee
thus providing the best environment to gain suitable experience.

The aim, from the employee point of view is to receive the best two-years
experience it is possible to get while still achieving high-level
qualifications part-time. The salary, although low, is still better than
being a student. After two years sucessful experience the salary capability
of the employee is very high.

From our point of view, we attract the best people who can bring strong
commitment to developing and supporting our products plus the "fresh ideas"
any technological company needs to survive.

There are no complicated contracts, just the agreement that should the
employee leave during the contract period that references sought by the
employee would be returned with "xxx broke his training contract".

This style of employment seems to work, we have been using it in one form or
another for five years.
