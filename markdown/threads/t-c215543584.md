```
Did you ever have a consulting firm's recruiter present a prospect
to you and then reveal really enthusiastically that it is a "Right-
to-Hire" arrangement? Wow! Whoopee!

Then you ask, "What's a "Right-to-Hire" arrangement

She says that the client, after employing you for six months will
put you on its payroll as a regular employee if the client likes you.

So you ask, "What will be the salary?"

She answers that she really doesn't know, but the employer will be
extending an offer six months hence.

So you ask, "Well, what you are expecting me to do is to commit
myself to a deal without even knowing the most pertinent detail of
that deal, right? And furthermore, you are asking me to commit to
a client who makes no commitment to me in return, right?"

Then she answers, "Oh, well, just make like you will accept the
arrangement, and you really won't be under firm obligation to
accept employment later on."

Now, think about it. If you are under no obligation (which is really
not true because the client will be operating under a different
impression) and the client is under no obligation to hire you, then
why even bother characterizing the whole thing as "Right-to Hire" in
the first place?

Consider the client now. If you are dealing with a client who thinks
that you are under obligation to accept employment without your even
knowing the terms of employment, especially the price, WHILE HE IS
UNDER NO RECIPROCAL OBLIGATION, then that client is a ridiculous
moron with whom you should have no dealing. The consulting firm on
the other end of your telephone line is likewise moronic, and you
should have nothing to do with it, either.

But the idiocy goes well beyond what was just presented above.

Invariably, when you are offered employment, it will be at a
wage that is roughly half of what your consulting rate is, so
after you work six months to demonstrate your worth, the reward
you get for sterling performance IS A DEEP WAGE CUT! Not a raise,
not a bonus, but a deep wage cut! That's backward, upside-down
logic, a total reversal of how incentives should work.

But think about it further. If an employer needs a six month
trial period in order to evaluate you, then that employer is
a goom-ba who knows absolutely nothing about his area of
endeavor. For crying out loud, the organization doesn't even
evaluate an outside candidate for the chairmanship of the board
for six months. If I have to be evaluated over such a period,
I would want to be awarded the C.E.O.'s position!!

This "Right-to-Hire" bull crap is just one more illogical construct
that the ding-a-ling, ding-dong school females have ushered into the
business world! Females have totally unreasonable, absurd expect-
ations. Flaming idiots are what they are!!

If "Right-to-Hire" is mentioned to you, just slam down the phone
in the idiot's face, just as you should do if they start mentioning
multiple middlemen arrangements!
```

```
I'm not so sure on this point. It may have something to do with the
fact that many projects that I have worked on are on accounting
systems. If I should accidentally happen to see the accounts payable
entry for my agency (possibly by their falling within the scope of a
system test), I would have a look at the numbers out of morbid
curiosity.

It has happened and I have never yet had grounds for complaint. The
guys at the agencies are generally differently focussed enough to not
realise that this knowledge is at my fingertips, indeed they express
surprise when I tell them that I could look at any time, if I was so
moved. Naturally, I do not feel the urge. It doesn't bother me that
when my project scope does not include accounts that I can't have a
look at this information.

I always find out what the agency is getting paid, and without putting
any effort into it. The boss often mentions it, either directly, or
by leaving an agency invoice lying around where I (and others) can see
it, and there are other ways. I have never deliberately sought this
information, it has always gravitated towards me. I suspect that this
is also true of many other contractors.

Like I said, though, the only trouble comes from those who make an
agreement and are happy with it until they discover the agency's take,
and then perceive this as being excessive, rightly or wrongly -
usually wrongly. Usually wrongly? Yes, they do not understand the
reality of cashflowing a deal where the agency pays the contractor
before they have even raised their own invoice, and waited months for
payment. If you don't believe this, work out the cashflow on a simple
spreadsheet and you will see that the agency's breakeven comes a hell
of a lot later than most people think - oh, and don't forget to factor
in the bank's charges.

Anyway, reverting back the topic itself, most contracts in the UK have
a Right-to-Hire aspect. If the employer wants to take the contractor
on as a permie, and the contractor wants to join up, then the employer
pays the agency a rather hefty fee, often 20%-25% of the first year's
salary.This isn't a lot different to what an agency would charge for
finding a permie anyway. This way everyone's had a chance to try the
deal and see if it works.
```
