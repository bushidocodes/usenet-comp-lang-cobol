After being "away" for a while I find that I look at the threads with a new
vision.

I have literally been too busy to even look at CLC for some weeks now and
when I can look in it has to be quick. That means I look for topics that may
be of interest and names that I know post good sense. There are some people
whose posts I always read.

I find less and less that I want to contribute. The arguments are hackneyed
and we've beaten them to death over years. The entrenched attitudes of the
fortress COBOL people (for the most part... not all) means that there is
little point in exploring the issues because no-one's mind is going to be
changed. The Client/Server people are mainly working on small or personal
systems and work the way they do because that's what they know and love and
they have always worked that way. The majority of the contributors here are
just not able to see the way the industry is going or why.

And it was all predictable years ago. The Market Place is speaking. As I
have remarked many times here, 200,000,000 people are not about to become
computer programmers. The tools they demand and need are being delivered,
and there is no longer any real need to have "Programming skills" in order
to develop commercial systems. The smug complacency of computer programmers
is no longer relevant. I see people every day doing it for themselves and
the concept of using a procedural non-object-oriented language is as foreign
to them as trying to persuade a European Tour Guide to learn Swahili as an
important or useful job skill.

Fewer and fewer of the young are taking up the Language so there is less
need for advice. Seven years ago I was tutoring around 17 students on the
Internet at any given moment. Today there are none. I get mail asking about
.NET COBOL, (which, ironically, I don't use myself <G>), Object Oriented
COBOL, and Project Management, Program Management, and general Management
issues, but nothing about the nuts and bolts of COBOL.

The COBOL topics are diminishing because the use of COBOL is diminishing.

There are still some of us who love the language and continue to use it, but
it is getting harder and harder to do this on a commercial basis.

For instance, I am unable to make any kind of case for introducing COBOL
into my current workplace. Five years ago I could have; today I can't.

Even my beloved OO COBOL is verbose and unwieldy when measured against Java
and VB. And these tools are arcane when measured against the many "Quick
Build" solutions that are becoming available. Most of these implement a
point and click development interface. Need a database? Three mouse clicks
sets it up. Another six or seven create your tables from a data dictionary.
Load it with test data from live flatfiles? A half-dozen more mouse clicks,
and some help from SQL Server and DTS. Or an ADO component... no code
required. Want to interact with it via the Web or the Company IntraNET?
Some more mouse clicks...XML, HTML generators, and pre-built templates
transport the data to the presentation tier for formatting and interaction.
Want to be able to access it from anywhere on Earth WITHOUT holding another
copy of it on a different server? Wrap it in SOAP. No code...no programming,
just tools and components that are becoming more and more effective and
easier to use.

The only real use of COBOL today is for building components.

Application development  in COBOL is just a "quaint" alternative for the
Cyber impoverished. (A bit like making lace at home instead of doing it at
the factory...you do it because you need some curtains, can't afford a lace
machine that would do it in a tenth of the time, and you won't buy the
manufactured lace on principle...<G>)

It is ironic that less than five years ago I could write 10 good reasons to
use COBOL for development. Today all of these reasons have been overtaken by
events.

Given this scenario it is inevitable that CLC will become a chat room.
(Nothing wrong with that, but I believe Bill's concern over the impression
people will receive is misplaced. There are SO many places where people can
get technical support on the Internet these days that it really doesn't
matter if they have to wade through some junk to get what they want in CLC.
Most are students who are just trying to get their homework short-cutted
anyway. They have no innate desire to learn COBOL, it is just part of the
paper chase. As significant as learning Classical Latin or Greek is today.)

Yes, there will be installations that are still running mainframe COBOL in
10 years time but they will be hard to find. The old Management are retiring
or dying. The next generation will have no compunction in removing
yesterday's technology. The trickle is becoming a torrent. I wouldn't mind
betting that everyone reading this knows of at least one installation where
they are dropping COBOL for future development, or has a friend or colleague
who works in such a place. I'll further wager that not one of you knows a
greenfield site where they are deciding that all future development will be
in COBOL, for any Company with a turnover in excess of $US15,000,000.

 Even the last bastions of fortress COBOL (Utilities, Banks, Insurance
Companies, and Airlines) are being slowly undermined. (As I mentioned
elsewhere this week, the Utility I am currently working with, a household
name in the UK, has NO COBOL in any of their divisions...I am told that
British Airways are moving to RAD DSDM and away from mainframe development,
the Halifax (a major Bank/Building Society) is also doing the same for new
development (ORACLE)... the landslide is starting; the pebbles won't get to
vote...)

I have previously advised people here to learn JAVA. That is still good
advice (mainly because it teaches OO concepts, and new commercial software
in the future will be object oriented). However, I would add to this that
the skills that will be required in the future are more and more SQL and
packages like ORACLE and SQL Server. Relational Database rules, and the
dream of the DB Evangelists in 1983 is today becoming reality.

Furthermore, if you get a chance at training on SAP, Siebel, or Chordiant
(JD Edwards), grab  it with both hands!

Someone asked me the other day why COBOL is dying (some believe it is
already dead...). I answered with the following:

1. No credibility. No proper standard and a farce of an organization
controlling it.
2. Indexed Sequential files. (More properly, a closed file system that means
data developed for COBOL systems is virtually inaccessible to standard
packages and tools. This was OK in an era where centralized data was the
norm. In the modern world data must be acccessible from anywhere. Therefore
it must be open. It is no longer acceptable to have to write a COBOL program
every time a new Query, Report, or downstream feed is required). )
3. A lack of understanding by COBOL people of the needs of the Market Place
and a lack of tools to get robust solutions delivered quickly.
4. The failure of the user base to pick up and embrace OO.

I'm sure many of you will disagree, but, as always, I call 'em like I see
'em...<G>

I won't say I told you so in 1994, but I did...<G>

requiring CoBOL to add this ability natively is not a limitation of CoBOL.

the way mainframes will consider OO CoBOL is not for the commonly described
benefits of OO (isolation is valued more than interdependency in all mainframe
environments that I have worked in), but they will consider it for the ease in
picking the appropriate language for a particular task.

For instance, we could have CoBOL doing the business logic, and have a 4th
generation Java tool doing the screens.   Objects are used as ways to
encapsulate the communication, similar to TCP/IP.   This can be beneficial even
without reuse.
