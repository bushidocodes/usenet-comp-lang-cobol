I have recently in this NG called for a vote on the issue
outlined below. Several of us are interested in seeing if
there is widespread support for the proposal. So far, we
have received about 10 votes. I would like to continue the
voting process for a few more days before tallying the
result. Please respond to the posting (after snipping its
contents) with "yes", "no", "don't care". Thanks.

yes  :  I would use a hypertext Cobol standards document
no    :  I would not use a hypertext Cobol standards document.
don't care:  I only read what my vendor feeds me, if even that

--------------------------------------------------------------

The new Cobol standard will guide us for the next 10-15 years.
There should be a hypertext version of the standards document
with lots of internal cross-references, in addition to the
normal text. An example is the HTML 4.0 specification at
http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-html40.

Other examples are:
http://imatix.com/html/sfl/index.htm
http://www.etk.com/etkfweb/etkfweb.htm

Imagine that you could click on a  word in a list of Cobol reserved
words and instantly be shown the definition of that word.
Everywhere within the definition another concept or Cobol word
is used, it in turn can be clicked on to show what it is and so on.

Clarifying examples might be included, or added at any
time (I know some standards people will shudder at this,
but the hyperlinks just extends the original document -
and don't alter it)

This newsgroup is full of questions as "what does the standard
say about this or that", so people have interests in that. Even
the vendors are beginning to be standards minded (e.g.. IBM).

There is backlash against proprietary extensions. The "write
once, run anywhere" strategy is valid for Cobol as well.

As a hypertext, the standard could be a sort of the Talmud of
Cobol, with the sacred standard text extended by layers and
layers of commentary, notes, tips, and links to vendors, and
such like.

You do not have to use the hypertext features. The document
should also read simply from top to bottom as normal text.
The hyper-text features are "on top" of the base document.
```

Would I use something like this?  Probably not. I keep a paper textbook
handy 2.5 feet away from my head.  It answers 97% of the problems I come
across and I never have to look for it.  For the rare occasion when I come
across something that the book can't answer I have good online documentation
(Bookmanager on MVS) that is just a few keystrokes away.  It has a decent
enough search facility.  If I run into anything really tough, I can ask the
company's technical specialists, who are a phone call away.  I regularly
read the posts here at comp.lang.cobol, so I have a pretty good idea what's
going on with my code.  For me, a hypertext standards/help system would be
just one more thing to keep track of and it wouldn't give me more usable
information than I already have and most important, I don't see it helping
me get my work done faster or easier.

Like you, I have always been fascinated by the potential of hypertext, since
before it came into vogue.  Its a charming concept we programmers just love,
somewhat like the COBOL to C interpreters folks are always asking about
here, or the heartfelt Dilbertesque urge of wanting to solve every problem
in the universe by building a database.

I guess I'm making a 'NO' vote.

As for guiding us for the next 10-15 years... Excuse me for blaspheming
here, but COBOL is a dying language.  Today you would be hard-pressed to
find a class in the Portland Oregon area, where I work and live, that
teaches COBOL programming.  The number of people programming in COBOL is
going to shrink, so you need to take into account the fact that the number
of people using and supporting this online hypertext document is going to
shrink over the next 10-15 years.
```
