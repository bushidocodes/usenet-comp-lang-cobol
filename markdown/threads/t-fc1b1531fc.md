Warning - the following posting contains what some might call Too Much 
Information (TMI) about a habit of my biology.  Be forewarned.

I suffer from what seems to be a moderately common syndrome... the 
workings of my lower intestine seems to be associated, by some as-yet 
undocumented neural pathway, with my eyes.  In short... things seem to go 
best - when dealing with the end-results of digestion - when I am reading.  
It doesn't matter what I peruse... I find that if my eyes are moving 
across words on a page the passage of, say, yesterday's dinner, is greatly 
enhanced.

(This causes me, at times, to spend enough time on The Throne so that my 
feet fall asleep.)

Anyhow... I found a copy of Government Executive at work to assist in my 
functioning; this issue (1 Jun 2004) has a cover-article about Todd Grams, 
the new CIO at the IRS (Internal Revenue Service, the tax-collecting arm 
of the United States' government).  I recalled that during my Y2K 
remediation-coding days much was being made of the then-new IRS 
Commissioner, Charles Rossotti, and his plans for using Y2K remediation to 
Do Better Things with the agency's computing infrastructure... evidently 
not enough came of this, as the article talks about Mr Grams doing the 
same.

What caught my eye, though, was the following few paragraphs, which 
described some of the difficulties encountered by Computer Sciences Corp 
(CSC) during some upgrade efforts.

(Full text available at 
http://www.govexec.com/features/0604-01/0604-01s1.htm )

--begin quoted text

In some cases, Cofoni says, CSC employees ceased working in the middle of
a system upgrade when they came upon some previously undocumented
modification made by IRS employees, perhaps years ago. Many were
work-arounds to comply with new changes to tax laws. Whenever the company
found them, data codes had to be rewritten and tested, putting work behind
schedule, Cofoni says. IRS employees didn't jump out of their seats to
help.

And that, says one 17-year IRS computer programmer, drove managers crazy. 
"We have total power over the machines. . . . Managers have none," says 
the programmer, who is fluent in the archaic ALC language in which the 
all-important master file is written. "It breaks their minds . . . because 
ALC is the most powerful language on Earth," says the programmer, who 
asked to remain anonymous. She describes her colleagues as "special 
people," "creative" and "nonconformist."

--end quoted text

'... ALC language in which the all-important master file is written.'

Wow!

DD

Files 'written in ALC' typically have multiple variable-length segments. What
would nowadays be normalized rows in a database are contained in a single
record.

This style can easily be handled by a Cobol program, demonstrated by tools I
wrote at Sears where the payroll system was 100% assembly language. It has since
been converted to PeopleSoft.

Assembly language programers thought the file structure gave them job security.
They didn't understand the dynamics of upper management decision processes,
where (mediocre) ten-year-old technical skills trumped obsolete technology.
