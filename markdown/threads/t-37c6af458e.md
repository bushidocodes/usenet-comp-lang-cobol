The Gartner Group says that COBOL comprises from 60% to 80% of all
existing operational source code, and about 3% is C/C++.  There's
probably more assembly language then C/C++.  So, not only is there
more code in COBOL than anything else, there is more COBOL code than
*everything* else put together, significantly more.  If the cost to
remediate that code took years and cost hundreds of billions, how
long would it take to completely replace it all, even if no more
COBOL were ever written?  Most of us probably won't live that long.
Considering that, using current 'non COBOL' technology, IS projects
are failing at the rate of over 45% from cost or time overruns and
failure to meet design goals, I have doubts whether it will ever
happen, until some radically new type of computer comes along that
doesn't use procedural code or the von Newmann model.  There have
been front page articles in major publications over the last few
years reporting that some companies are backing off significantly
from C/S-GUI and going back to the technology we have been using
for about 20 years.  They found it is far more reliable, far less
expensive, and can be developed much quicker.  They now carefully
weigh factors before jumping into a C/S-GUI solution.

Massively Parallel processors have been around for years. The last time I
looked (1994) there were systems capable of supporting up to 2048 processors
on a single system.
I think that the "mainframe" will grow, more processors, more storage. I
wouldn't be supprised to see a mainframe with 64 bit addressing, and storage
to justify it.
The main performance bottleneck is still Input/Output operations. If you can
remove the Physical I/O wait from the application programming process
throughput will increase very dramaticly. The physical I/O would still have
to be done, but not in a serial manner. Imagine the speed if your files and
Tables were all in memory while your application ran.

> Sorry about the Non-Cobol related question but I have to know.  I figure
if
> the mainframe ever meets its match the relevancy for COBOL will decline
> rapidly.

Businesses have a large investment in their COBOL systems, especially when
they have developed software to give them a competitive edge. If a Business
has to modify off the shelf software to recreate the functionality they now
have, they may back off the conversion.

The migration from COBOL would have to involve extensive testing (look at
the recent SAP conversion horror shows with millions lost). It may not be
cost justified to migrate the heart of Business Operations from COBOL
systems to ???.  GUI front ends are being developed in languages other than
COBOL, but the Business processing is still being done in COBOL.
Yes there has been a decline in pure COBOL systems, but this will just lead
to the demise of the people at the bottom of the ability ladder.
