```
Wow! What an attitude. I am impressed, I don't think I would have made such
a decision. However, programming is a wonderful occupation, I am most sure
you will love it. You should however remember a couple of things;

    1. Be patient. As with any craft, be it a carpenter, plumber or a
programmer. Becoming
        experienced and rewarded demands years of humble work.

    2. Do not be alone. Use either electronic forums like this or real
people sharing your interest to
        get hold of tips and tricks, discuss technics, find new ideas. When
I was at university, we could
        sit discussing programming techniques for hours. It is my opinion
that this kind of influence is
        way more important to your maturing process than any book and/or
lecturer.

    3. You *MUST* have a computer at home, and your wife and family *MUST*
allow you to spend
        HOURS in front of it. It's a hard world, you won't get anything for
free, thus hard work is the only
        way.

    4. Theory is important. The A - B - C introductory course do no good for
you aspiring to become
        a professional. There is a lot of theory in computer programming,
although you may rarely need
        the theory in everyday Cobol programming, it will lift your eyebrows
and give you an
        understanding  of programming that will easen your work. Compare it
to a mechanics at a gas
        station. He knows the engine, so, to service it, he knows what to
look for and what to do. I can
        service my car too, I can change oil to it, but what's next?

    5. Understanding what is behind the scenes. It is not strictly
necessary, just like many people work
        as drivers and knows nothing about an engine, it is perfectly ok to
use a computer and not have
        a single idea of what's going on in there. I would however recommend
you to get an idea. First
        of all it will improve your understanding of what may be the error
in case of runtime errors.
        Second, if you are up to date with the heart and soul of your
computer, any new technology
        is easier to understand.

    6. Find yourself an application to do and do it. Keep improving it,
everyday, find new applications
        to do. This is just like pilots, they need a particular number of
flight hours to qualify, so it is, also
        for you and your programming skills.

    7. Find yourself an adoptive company. Make a resume, do your initial
courses and go THERE.
        Sell yourself! And offer your services for free as an apprentice. I
believe you could do this after 1
        year of school. After the second year, you should argue for a decent
pay. Of course, if you get
        paid from the beginning, its perfect, if not, you are earning
experience.

Welcome to the wonderful world of software programming, I wish you good
luck!

Cheesle
```

```
 I am from the U.S. so perhaps the job market might be different -- but for a
while there was federal assistance for schooling if you were unemployed. Since
my temp assignment had ended I could claim unemployment and then be eligible
for 60% tuition off. Well sounds great but the curriculum at CHUBB was
primarily (80%) devoted to COBOL , 20% c,Vb4 Access and a million other
dabblings.
For absolute beginners Programming is not the field  you want to be the jack of
all trades and the master of none-- just be Careful about wieghing which one
you start with since your time is "leaned on" by the wonderful  forces of
practical survival.  Incidentally I wanted to learn C since it really opens up
a lot more avenues in programming -- it provides the foundation for everything
thats hot in PC world and beyond. Though Cobol may be somewhat practical,  I
personally find C much more rewarding even as a novice c programmer. The
disadvantage with C or languages based in it ( by this I mean if you master C
all the others fall in place:C++.Java and all other Object oriented languages.
) is that its pretty hard to get hired as an entry level. (incidentally its
just as hard with entry level  Cobol to get employed  if your school doesn't
place you -- because say you still have a balance with them) So if practicality
is wieghing on you, definitely investigate each tech school/or program and
whether there is government assistance for tuition. Then Find out the
conditions of their placement program. 
Well Good Luck from Brooklyn N.Y. - Turquemada@aol.com
```
