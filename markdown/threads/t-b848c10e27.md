Dear hkim,
You'll find COBOL the better choice because the code you write will
match very closely the real-world business process your automating. It's
exactly why COBOL is so neccessary today. A Mid-level language like C/C++
focuses on "power programming" and is barely legable to the laymen. COBOL
explains itself. The code is easily understood, modified, fixed and
maintained. With C/C++ there's alot more "overhead" coding and mind-bending
"translation" required. Why waste time with that when all your writing is a
straight-forward business application? Don't get me wroing, I like C/C++
very much. Heck, almost all the COBOL compilers on the market are written
in C/C++. Also, who wants to write in assembler when you have C/C++. I
could go on and on about the merits of C/C++, but, the bottom line is COBOL
wins out on business apps hands-down. It's a no-brainer. Of course, what
kind of response did you really expect on a COBOL newsgroup? *grin* 8)

trivially concerned with money, unless strongly constrained by other factors.

are where COBOL really shines, and also where C has very little to recommend

likely to do so with a C routine! Non technical people don't need to look at

Donning my asbestos clothing...

If you are developing on Windows, take a look at Delphi. In the 18
years I have been in this business, I have developed software in over 20
languages (including Cobol), and can honestly say that Delphi is by far
the best development environment I have ever used. Granted, I am a
degreed computer scientist; thus, I already knew Pascal when I started
with the product; hence, Delphi's Object Pascal base did not bother me.

Delphi supports Binary Coded Decimal (BCD) math (what people on this
forum referred to a comp or packed decimal), and is at least an order of
magnitude more powerful and flexible than Cobol. The only thing one
cannot do in Delphi is write VxDs.

Furthermore, Delphi makes Visual Basic look like a toy (I have developed
production software with both tools) . The entire development
environment and class library is written in Object Pascal; thus, it is
completely extensible.

Object Pascal also has the pedigree of a language designed to teach
students how to write "GOOD" as opposed to "WORKING" software. In
fact, it forces the developer to write good code. I have been able to
read and understand every single Pascal/Object Pascal program on which I
have placed my hands.


Last, but not least, Delphi supports three-tier application development
between disparate systems through the CORBA-based, Entera middleware
product as well as between MS systems using ActiveX (both servers and
containers). It also supports AS/400 client/server development with
Delphi/400 Client/Server Suite.


In closing, take it from a person who works in an organization with a
very large glass-house and over 30 million lines of Cobol code that is
doing zero new projects in Cobol, Cobol is a dead-end road. Yes, it
will not disappear overnight, but it will disappear; if not for the
simple fact, that our educational system is not turning out anymore
Cobol programmers. Demand is high right now because of Year 2000
conversion work, but when these projects are over, it will drop off.
Most of the young CS and IS grads I have met would rather be on welfare
than code in Cobol, and I am sure they mean what they are saying as I
have witnessed several of them quite when assigned to Cobol positions.
