I never thought about this until I read a recent article on Cobol and
Security & the Web.

Most COBOL programs pose little risk to the security policies of a
company.  They cannot override operating systems limitations and they
don't have hidden tricky code which may worm into existing controls.

As we now have the ability to use pointers outside of the code, and the
ability to use objects created by other languages, this is changing.   

I have to admit that I haven't worked with anybody who USES these
pointers - with the exception of a program which I downloaded and
modified which will find filenames in the JCL of the job which is
running the program.  Even here, I haven't spent enough effort to pore
through IBM documentation to understand the external pointers.  I think
this is typical of mainframe COBOL programers and maybe COBOL
programmers in general.  If we were into the bits, bytes, and hooks, we
would be using a different language.

Who has done COBOL Web applications?  Are there typical Web security
problems there?  How much difference does the language make?

Security has nothing to do with the language, it is a way of designing
applications. It is equally stupid to let company data wide open through a
VB application as through a Cobol application.

If you are setting up pointers to automated stuff in a Microsoft
environment, you are leaving the doors wide open for worms like the last
we've seen. Your .JPG files will suffer.

If you are using Cobol to interact through HTML to company data, you are
better off. However, the logon procedure must be controlled by you, and
privileges must come from accessing your data with your application, and all
logon details must be encrypted. Don't let anything of value be in the HTML
it must be accessed from somewhere behind you.

Accessing API's will be quit safe and if you really want to annoy
worm-makers, rename all pictures and soundbits and restore them with correct
extension after signon. Then your pictures and soundbits will be refreshed
even after a worm has entered your network.

I have no ambitions of making applications impossible to hack, but
protection for the impact of macro-virus (worms)  is easy. If somebody will
need to spend days and hours to hack me individually, I'm satisfied.

Regards
