The procedure is:

1. Set up a VM.  (Very easy with Virtual Box) You can do this on a Win 7 or

processes on networked machines. See:

It is tempting for people who love COBOL to simply ascribe the sea change in 
demand to fashion. "COBOL isn't fashionable, that's what the problem is." 
There is some truth in this but you have to ask WHY the fashion changed. It 
changed because the world became connected at a level that COBOL could never 
have imagined.

A processing paradigm based around sequential procedural processing on a 
single CPU (like a mainframe in the 1960s) can never deliver the distributed 
results that a series of objects with parallell processing capability can 
deliver across a networked environment.

A process which controls everything from a single CPU cannot compete with a 
process which is event driven, when it comes to networks. Event driven code 
works better with Objects than with procedures (although you CAN do it 
either way).

everything in C# is an Object...)

market and has to compete with people who have more modern solutions.

"Indexed Sequential flat files."

He loses the sale.

they want to know that the package has a future. (And they also want to be 
able to tell their competitors that they have a "state-of-the-art" 
solution.)

getting him to .Net and C# as a longer term solution. Most of his existing 
code can be salvaged using the PRIMA Migration Toolset and re-wrapped as 
objects for the new environment.

Despite what individuals here may wish, the world has voted with its feet.

There is no future for COBOL or COBOL programmers, UNLESS they embrace new 
technology and expand their skill sets.

For a short time to come, it might just be possible to make a living from

language.

be sidetracked into a non-productive career avenue.

I started thinking about this and in the late 1960s (when I started using

remember it wasn't taught at either University or Polytechnic. By the 
mid/late 1970s Auckland Polytech had a COBOL course, but the majority of 
people who were actually working on COBOL sites had learned it on courses 
run by the vendors, for which their employers had paid.

I remember being quite excited when my employers told me I would be 
attending a COBOL course (which they had paid for...). This was in marked 
contrast to a previous employer who had said: "We can't afford to pay for 
you to learn programming but we have borrowed the course audio tapes from 
NCR. (It was 500 programming in machine language). You can have them for 2 
weeks and study them in your own time. If you need access to the machine 
(which I did) it must be outside office hours...".

After I went freelance and travelled round the world (around 1976) I don't 
remember meeting ANY programmers who had learned COBOL at University. 
EVERYBODY I remember working with had either learned on the job (a 2/3 week 
in-house course was pretty usual and I actually ran some of these in a 
couple of places I worked) or been on a course run by the vendor.

Industry was training the people it needed, very much as noted above. These 
days vendors will still run a course, at their premises or yours, but the 
cost is high and there is a fair element of marketing in the content. The 
world has moved on and video self-instruction is a very effective way for 
most people to acquire the skills they need. (I learned VS2008 and C# in 
this way; Java mostly from books and colleagues, but the point is that the 
onus now is on the individual to get the needed skills, rather than on the 
employer or the University.) Universities paint with a broader brush  (still 
useful, but not necessarily at a specific coal face level) and that is why 
we often complain about new graduates "knowing nothing". There is no 
substitute for experience on the job, and in the particular environment 
where that experience is needed. SOME industries still need COBOL expertise 
(a very small minority of the number of companies trading in the world) and 
they will be forced to provide training for their own staff until such time 
as they move off COBOL.(It is the recognition of this which is often a 
factor in raising the necessity level for companies to get Migrated.)

Acadaemia simply cannot justify running courses in a language that is no 
longer relevant, whether it is because the Tutors don't want to (as Michael 
suggests) or because there is no real demand for it (which is what I glean 
from the Faculty people I talk to).

It is silly to blame Acadaemia for the decline of COBOL.

You might as well blame them for the decline of skills in lantern trimming 
and home-based lace making.

Some recent post have siad  "oh it is just GUI but it does not do anything". 
I think this is an incorrect view as most people are strongly visually 
oriented and do care about how their data is presented.

I once worked at The Library of Congress which uses MARC records to store 
information about books.  They had an inhouse file management system that 
programmers used to access information in the MARC records.  It was called 
Logic Library.  The library management wanted to replace Logic Library (LL) 
because they associated it with the "green screen" displays of CICS.  Of 
course the GUI was completely separate from (LL) and there was no off the 
shelf equivalent for LL.  The IT department created an web style GUI and 
interfaced it to LL and management and users were quite happy with it and 
the IT department was able to keep LL and continue to enhance it.

Both the GUI and the processing supporting it are important.
